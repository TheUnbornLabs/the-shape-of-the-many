# References — 59. Tipping points

## Confirmed sources

- Grodzins, Morton. *The Metropolitan Area as a Racial Problem*. University of Pittsburgh Press, 1958; and related 1957 writings on the neighbourhood "tip point." — Source of the social-science "tip point" concept in racial residential succession.
- Schelling, Thomas C. "Dynamic Models of Segregation." *Journal of Mathematical Sociology*, vol. 1, no. 2, 1971, pp. 143–186. — The mathematical model of individual thresholds aggregating into a collective flip; foundation for item 67 (Schelling segregation).
- Gladwell, Malcolm. *The Tipping Point: How Little Things Can Make a Big Difference*. Little, Brown, 2000. — The popular synthesis; source of the "Law of the Few" (connectors, mavens, salesmen), the "Stickiness Factor," and the "Power of Context."
- Centola, Damon; Becker, Joshua; Brackbill, Devon; and Baronchelli, Andrea. "Experimental evidence for tipping points in social convention." *Science*, vol. 360, no. 6393, 2018, pp. 1116–1119. — The committed-minority experiment; found a critical mass of roughly 25% able to overturn an established social convention, with the exact threshold depending on interaction structure and memory.
- Watts, Duncan J.; and Dodds, Peter Sheridan. "Influentials, Networks, and Public Opinion Formation." *Journal of Consumer Research*, vol. 34, no. 4, 2007, pp. 441–458. — The critique of the "Law of the Few"; cascades depend more on the susceptibility/state of the network than on special influentials. Underpins item 57 and the beat-7 critique of Gladwell.
- Granovetter, Mark. "Threshold Models of Collective Behavior." *American Journal of Sociology*, vol. 83, no. 6, 1978, pp. 1420–1443. — The individual-threshold mechanism underlying the tipping arithmetic (item 7).

## Further reading to verify before final publication

- CONFIRMED: Grodzins's "tip point" concept appears in "Metropolitan Segregation," *Scientific American* vol. 197 (October 1957), pp. 33–41, and is developed in the 1958 monograph *The Metropolitan Area as a Racial Problem*. The chapter's "In 1957 ... Morton Grodzins ... described what he called the 'tip point'" is accurate.
- The characterisation of the 1989 eastern European collapses as tipping-driven preference-falsification cascades draws on Timur Kuran's work (item 34, *Private Truths, Public Lies*, Harvard University Press, 1995) — cross-check the item-34 references for the primary citation.
- CONFIRMED (Centola, Becker, Brackbill & Baronchelli, *Science* 360:1116–1119, 2018): the experiment used 10 groups of 20 participants; committed minorities below ~25% of the group failed to shift the established convention, while at/above ~25% the population abruptly flipped to the new convention. The paper is explicit that the exact threshold depends on interaction structure and agent memory. The chapter's "clustered around one quarter," the "10 groups of 20," and the not-a-law-of-nature caveat are all accurate.
- Historical tipping cases cited illustratively (handwashing adoption, abolition, rights expansions) are used as familiar examples, not as sourced claims; if retained as specific assertions, attach primary sources.
