# References — 51. Dunbar's number

## Confirmed sources

- Dunbar, R. I. M. "Neocortex size as a constraint on group size in primates." *Journal of Human Evolution*, 22(6), 469–493, 1992. — The founding paper of the social brain hypothesis; establishes the cross-primate correlation between neocortex size and social group size.
- Dunbar, R. I. M. "Coevolution of neocortical size, group size and language in humans." *Behavioral and Brain Sciences*, 16(4), 681–735, 1993. — Extrapolates the primate regression to humans, yielding the ~150 estimate; the primary source for "Dunbar's number."
- Dunbar, Robin. *How Many Friends Does One Person Need? Dunbar's Number and Other Evolutionary Quirks.* Faber & Faber / Harvard University Press, 2010. — Popular synthesis of the argument, the nested circles of intimacy, and the recurring-150 examples for a general readership.
- Lindenfors, Patrik; Wartel, Andreas; Lind, Johan. "'Dunbar's number' deconstructed." *Biology Letters*, 17(5), 20210158, 2021. — Re-runs the primate regression with modern statistics; finds the human confidence interval so wide that a precise number "cannot be estimated." The central critique in beat 7.

## Confirmed on verification

- Lindenfors, Wartel & Lind (2021) report 95% confidence intervals for the extrapolated human group size of roughly 4–520 and 2–336 (depending on dataset/method), with point estimates ranging from the teens to the low hundreds across methods — hence their conclusion that a precise number "cannot be estimated." The chapter's "something like 4 to well over 500" and the reference note's "roughly 4 to 500+" are accurate summaries of the 4–520 interval.
- The Hutterite colony-splitting rule at ~150, with the explicit rationale that behaviour can no longer be controlled through informal peer pressure beyond that size, is confirmed as standardly reported (including in Dunbar's own work on optimal community sizes and in Hutterite demographic accounts). The "split before crossing 150" mechanism as narrated is well-supported.
