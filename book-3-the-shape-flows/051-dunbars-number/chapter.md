# 51. Dunbar's number

> There may be a ceiling on how many people you can truly know — and above that line, a society has to be held together by something other than knowing.

## The idea

Think of everyone whose face you could put a name to, whose history you carry, whose call you would take without hesitation — the people who are not strangers to you and not acquaintances either, but part of your standing social world. Now try to count them. Most people, when they attempt this honestly, arrive at a number smaller than they expected: not thousands, not even one thousand, but something on the order of a hundred and fifty.

Dunbar's number is the claim that this is not an accident of your particular life but a rough cognitive ceiling shared by our species — a limit, somewhere around 150, on the number of stable social relationships one person can maintain at once. Not the number of people you can recognise, which is far larger, and not the number you could list as contacts, which in the age of the address book can run to thousands. The claim is narrower and stranger: it concerns the number of others for whom you can hold a live, updated model — who they are, how they relate to you, how they relate to each other — and with whom you can sustain the ongoing investment that a real relationship demands. Beyond that ceiling, the argument goes, the personal bonds start to thin, and the group can no longer hold itself together by the sheer force of everybody knowing everybody. It needs something else.

The figure is not sharp. Dunbar himself gives a wide range, roughly 100 to 250, with 150 as the central estimate. And it comes wrapped in a set of nested layers — an inner circle of about 5 intimates, then rings of roughly 15, 50, 150, 500, and beyond, each larger and looser than the one inside it. The 150 is simply the outermost ring of people you would still call relationships rather than recognitions.

## The origin

The number belongs to Robin Dunbar, a British anthropologist and evolutionary psychologist, who arrived at it in the early 1990s not by counting human friends but by looking at monkey brains.

Dunbar was working on a puzzle about primates: why do some species live in small groups and others in large ones, and what in their biology sets the size? The dominant answers pointed outward — to ecology, food, predators. Dunbar's proposal pointed inward, to the brain. He argued that what limits the size of a primate's social group is the cost of running it: keeping track of who is allied with whom, who owes whom, who is to be trusted and who avoided. That bookkeeping is cognitively expensive, and the organ that pays for it is the neocortex. In a 1992 paper in the *Journal of Human Evolution*, Dunbar showed that across primate species, the relative size of the neocortex correlates with the typical size of the social group. Bigger social brains, bigger societies. This became known as the **social brain hypothesis**: brains grew large, on this account, to manage the demands not of the physical world but of the social one.

Then came the move that made the number famous. If neocortex size predicts group size across primates, you can fit a line to the primate data and ask where humans fall on it. Extrapolating that regression to the human neocortex yields a predicted group size — and the answer came out around 150. Dunbar laid this out most fully in a 1993 paper in *Behavioral and Brain Sciences*, and spent the following decades arguing that the figure was not merely a curve-fitting artefact but a number that keeps turning up in human life: in the band sizes of hunter-gatherers, in the estimated populations of Neolithic farming villages, in the sizes of military companies, in the membership of Hutterite religious communities, and in the personal networks people report when you actually ask them to count. His popular account, *How Many Friends Does One Person Need?* (2010), gathered the case for a general audience.

## The mechanism

The gears behind the ceiling are made of time and attention.

A relationship, in Dunbar's sense, is not a fact but a process. It has to be maintained. You keep it alive by contact — by conversation, by the small currency of attention that primates pay each other physically through grooming and that humans pay largely through talk. Let the contact lapse and the bond decays; the person slides outward through the rings, from friend to acquaintance to name you half-remember. Maintaining a relationship therefore costs something real, and the budgets are finite. There are only so many hours, and only so much of the mind's machinery for tracking other people.

That machinery is the second constraint, and it is the one the social brain hypothesis is really about. To hold a relationship you must hold a model of the person: not just their identity but their disposition toward you and, crucially, their web of relationships with everyone else in the group. Social life is not a list of individuals but a lattice of connections, and the number of possible connections grows far faster than the number of people. To navigate a group is to track this lattice, and the neocortex — on Dunbar's argument — sets how large a lattice you can carry. The 150 is where that capacity runs out.

The consequence is the part that matters for this book. Below the ceiling, a group can cohere on personal terms alone. Everyone can, in principle, know everyone; reputation circulates by direct acquaintance; a cheat or a shirker is known as a cheat because people have seen him cheat. Trust runs on memory. But push the group past the ceiling and this stops working. You can no longer know everyone, and neither can anyone else, so you can no longer rely on the dense mesh of mutual acquaintance to keep people honest and coordinated. Something has to substitute for it: rules that apply whether or not you know the person, hierarchies that route decisions, offices and titles and written obligations, shared stories that bind strangers who will never meet. The group must change not its size but its *kind* of glue.

## The anchor example

Consider the Hutterites, a communal Anabaptist people who have farmed the northern plains of North America since the nineteenth century, holding all property in common and living in tight agricultural colonies. The Hutterites have a rule, arrived at not from anthropology but from experience: when a colony grows to around 150 members, it splits. The community deliberately divides, sending roughly half its people to found a new colony elsewhere.

They do this because they have learned what happens when it grows larger. A Hutterite colony runs on no police and no formal law. It is held together by faith, by shared labour, and above all by the fact that everyone knows everyone — that behaviour is governed by the constant, mutual, personal watchfulness of a community small enough to be transparent to itself. A person who slacks or transgresses is seen, and known, and answerable to people whose faces are familiar. This works, the Hutterites found, up to a size and no further. Past roughly 150, the informal watchfulness frays. Factions form between people who no longer quite know each other; the sense of a single community, answerable to itself, begins to dissolve; and the colony would need formal enforcement to hold together what personal bond can no longer hold. Rather than cross that line, they split before reaching it.

The lesson is exact and it is the whole point of the concept: personal bonds do not scale past about 150. The Hutterites did not read Dunbar. They discovered, in the practical business of keeping a community coherent without laws, the same ceiling his primate regression predicted — and built their entire social structure around never crossing it.

## The flip

Dunbar's number is not, in the end, a fact about friendship. It is a fact about the *transition* — the line at which collective behaviour has to change in kind, and this book's whole argument lives on that line.

Below the ceiling, a crowd can coordinate the way the wise crowd does at its best: face to face, on personal terms, through reputation and direct reciprocity. This is the world of item 14, reciprocal altruism, where cooperation is stable precisely because people deal with each other repeatedly and remember. I help you because you helped me and will again; I do not cheat you because you would know, and tell, and the whole small world would know. Trust here is cheap and reliable because it rides on acquaintance. The conditions that make a crowd wise — that people can watch and check and hold each other accountable — are, at root, conditions of a group small enough for everyone to be legible to everyone.

Above the ceiling, all of that breaks, and here is the flip: a society larger than 150 cannot run on personal bonds, so it must invent impersonal ones. It must build coordination devices that work between strangers — money, law, hierarchy, and the shared fictions of item 83: the collective agreements to treat a piece of paper as wealth, a border as real, a stranger in a uniform as an authority. These are the technologies for cooperating with people you cannot know. They are what lets a society of millions cohere at all. And this is the seam the series keeps returning to, because the same abstract devices that let strangers cooperate at scale are also what let them be *dominated* at scale. A village needs no bureaucracy of oppression; everyone can see what is being done. It is only above the ceiling, where relations become impersonal and the glue becomes rules and stories rather than faces, that stratification (item 85) becomes possible as a standing structure — hierarchy as a scaling technology, a way of coordinating far more people than any of them could ever personally know. The wise crowd's face-to-face conditions work below the line. Scaling past it forces the invention of the abstract machinery that can sustain both cooperation and domination at once. The shape does not just grow. Past 150, it has to flow into a different form.

## The human weight

The stakes of this transition are the stakes of the modern world, because the modern world is entirely built above the line.

Every large society is, by definition, a society of strangers coordinated by impersonal means. This is an astonishing achievement — it is what makes cities, states, economies, and universities possible — and it is also the source of a specific vulnerability. In a village, the order is held by people you can check. If a decision is unjust, you can see who made it and why. Above the ceiling, the institutions that replace personal knowledge are, by their nature, things most people cannot personally check: laws written by people they will never meet, offices held by strangers, and shared fictions (item 83) — currencies, ranks, national stories — whose authority rests on nothing anyone can verify directly, only on the fact that everyone else appears to accept them. The very abstraction that lets a large society function is what lets that society's machinery be captured, made oppressive, or run on beliefs no one is in a position to examine.

This is where the ceiling touches the frontier this series keeps circling. The transition from personal to impersonal order is exactly where both the modern state and modern domination become possible. Stratification (item 85) is not a feature of small bands, where everyone is legible to everyone; it is a feature of the scaled society, where hierarchy substitutes for acquaintance and where an order can be held in place by shared fictions and imposed silence rather than by the direct mutual knowledge of a group small enough to see itself. Dunbar's number, read this way, is not a curiosity about how many friends you can have. It is a marker of the threshold past which a society must be governed by things larger and more abstract than any of its members — and therefore of the threshold at which those things can begin to govern the members in ways none of them chose.

## The critique

And yet the number itself may not survive scrutiny, and honesty requires saying so plainly.

The most serious challenge came in 2021, when Patrik Lindenfors, Andreas Wartel, and Johan Lind re-ran Dunbar's core calculation in *Biology Letters* using modern statistical methods. They fitted the primate neocortex-to-group-size relationship with proper attention to the uncertainty, and then extrapolated to humans as Dunbar had. The extrapolation gave a number, but the confidence interval around it was enormous — spanning, depending on the method, from something like 4 to well over 500, and in some analyses far wider. Their conclusion was blunt: a precise cognitive limit on human group size "cannot be estimated" from this data. The point estimate of 150 was always sitting inside a band of uncertainty so vast that pinning the species to any particular figure is not warranted by the statistics.

There are further reasons for caution. Critics have long noted that 150 has an air of numerology — a round-ish number that recurs partly because its wide range (100 to 250) can be made to fit almost any human grouping one goes looking for, and because the nested "circles" of 5, 15, 50, 150 are suspiciously tidy for data that are, in fact, loose and variable across individuals and cultures. The social brain hypothesis itself, the foundation on which the extrapolation rests, remains contested; the correlation between neocortex size and group size is real but its interpretation is disputed, and rival accounts explain primate brain size by ecology or diet. And the ceiling, even if it exists, may not be fixed: modern communication technologies, which let us maintain contact at a fraction of the old cost, might change the effective number, though whether they raise it or merely spread it thinner is unsettled.

None of this makes the concept worthless. There does seem to be *some* ceiling; personal networks are demonstrably finite; the Hutterites really do split, and small groups really do coordinate differently from large ones. The transition from personal to impersonal order — the part this book cares about most — is robust whether or not the line sits at exactly 150. So Dunbar's number is best held as a suggestive regularity, a memorable pointer at a real threshold, and not as a hard biological constant. The truth it points to is that personal bonds have a limit and that societies larger than the limit must be built out of something else. The precise location of the limit is the part we should hold most loosely.
