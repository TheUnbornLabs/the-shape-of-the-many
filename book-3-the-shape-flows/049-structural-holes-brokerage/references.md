# References — 49. Structural holes & brokerage

## Confirmed sources

- Burt, Ronald S. *Structural Holes: The Social Structure of Competition*. Harvard University Press, 1992. — The founding statement of structural holes and brokerage; the argument that positional advantage accrues to those who span gaps between otherwise-disconnected clusters, and the source of the information-benefit / control-benefit distinction.
- Burt, Ronald S. "Structural Holes and Good Ideas." *American Journal of Sociology* 110, no. 2 (2004): 349–399. — The supply-chain-manager study; the empirical claim that managers whose networks bridge structural holes have ideas rated more valuable and enjoy better career outcomes; the "creativity as importation across a gap" mechanism.
- Coleman, James S. "Social Capital in the Creation of Human Capital." *American Journal of Sociology* 94 (1988): S95–S120. — The closure counterview: dense, redundant, closed networks build trust, enforce norms, and constitute social capital; the pole against which Burt's brokerage is debated.
- Granovetter, Mark S. "The Strength of Weak Ties." *American Journal of Sociology* 78, no. 6 (1973): 1360–1380. — The precursor insight (item 45) that non-redundant information travels along bridging, weaker ties; the lineage Burt relocates from tie-strength to hole-spanning.
- Simmel, Georg. On the *tertius gaudens* ("the third who rejoices/benefits") — the third party who profits from the separation or discord of two others. The concept appears in Simmel's sociology of the triad; see *The Sociology of Georg Simmel*, translated and edited by Kurt H. Wolff (Free Press, 1950), the standard English source for Simmel's treatment of triadic relations and the tertius.
