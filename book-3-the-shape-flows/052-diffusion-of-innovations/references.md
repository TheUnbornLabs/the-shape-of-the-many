# References — 52. Diffusion of innovations

## Confirmed sources

- Rogers, Everett M. *Diffusion of Innovations*. Free Press, 1st ed. 1962; 5th ed. 2003. — The foundational synthesis; source for the adopter categories, the four elements (innovation, communication channels, time, social system), the five perceived attributes, the S-curve, opinion leaders and change agents, and Rogers's own critiques (pro-innovation bias, individual-blame bias).
- Ryan, Bryce, and Neal C. Gross. "The Diffusion of Hybrid Seed Corn in Two Iowa Communities." *Rural Sociology*, vol. 8, no. 1, 1943, pp. 15–24. — The founding empirical study; hybrid seed corn diffusion, the S-curve over more than a decade, and the finding that mass media created awareness while interpersonal (neighbour) channels drove adoption.
- Tarde, Gabriel. *Les lois de l'imitation* (*The Laws of Imitation*). 1890. — Early theory that society spreads novelty by imitation, with an intuited S-shaped take-up; intellectual precursor to diffusion theory.
- Coleman, James S., Elihu Katz, and Herbert Menzel. *Medical Innovation: A Diffusion Study*. Bobbs-Merrill, 1966. — Classic study of the diffusion of a new antibiotic (tetracycline) among physicians, showing adoption spreading through professional social networks; cited as a parallel diffusion case.
- Moore, Geoffrey A. *Crossing the Chasm*. HarperBusiness, 1991. — Business extension of the framework; names the "chasm" between early adopters and the early majority as the make-or-break gap in the technology adoption curve.

## Confirmed on verification

- The adopter-category percentages (2.5% / 13.5% / 34% / 34% / 16%) are Rogers's standard-deviation cut-points on a normal distribution of adoption times (innovators earlier than two SD below the mean; early adopters between one and two SD below; early/late majority the two central segments split at the mean; laggards more than one SD above). Confirmed as standard in Rogers.
- The S-curve / "imitation" intuition attributed to Tarde's 1890 *Les lois de l'imitation*: confirmed — Tarde described the spread of novelty as a "law of geometric progression" and is standardly credited as a precursor of the diffusion S-curve.
- Coleman, Katz & Menzel's *Medical Innovation* (1966) centres on the antibiotic tetracycline, studied among physicians in four Illinois towns (Peoria, Bloomington, Quincy, Galesburg), with adoption tracking interpersonal network ties. Confirmed.
