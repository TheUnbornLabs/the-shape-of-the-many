# References — 47. Centrality — locating power in a network

## Confirmed sources

- Freeman, Linton C. "Centrality in Social Networks: Conceptual Clarification." *Social Networks*, vol. 1, no. 3, 1978/1979, pp. 215–239. — The synthesis that separated degree, betweenness, and closeness as distinct concepts, each encoding a different theory of importance (activity, control, independence). Central to the chapter's core argument.
- Bonacich, Phillip. "Factoring and Weighting Approaches to Status Scores and Clique Identification." *Journal of Mathematical Sociology*, vol. 2, no. 1, 1972, pp. 113–120. — Origin of eigenvector centrality: a node's status defined recursively by the status of its connections, resolving to the leading eigenvector of the adjacency matrix.
- Katz, Leo. "A New Status Index Derived from Sociometric Analysis." *Psychometrika*, vol. 18, no. 1, 1953, pp. 39–43. — Katz centrality: counting all paths to a node with a distance decay factor; an early formalisation of attenuating influence through a network.
- Brin, Sergey, and Lawrence Page. "The Anatomy of a Large-Scale Hypertextual Web Search Engine." *Computer Networks and ISDN Systems*, vol. 30, no. 1–7, 1998, pp. 107–117. — PageRank as eigenvector centrality applied to the web, with a damping/random-jump term; the foundation of Google.
- Burt, Ronald S. *Structural Holes: The Social Structure of Competition*. Harvard University Press, 1992. — The theory of brokerage advantage from spanning gaps between otherwise-disconnected groups; supports the anchor example (high betweenness, low degree) and cross-reference to item 49.
