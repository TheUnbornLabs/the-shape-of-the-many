# References — 50. Echo chambers & filter bubbles

## Confirmed sources

- Sunstein, Cass R. *Republic.com*. Princeton University Press, 2001. — Introduces the "Daily Me" worry and the argument that filtered, like-minded information environments drive group polarization; the founding statement of the echo-chamber concern for the internet age.
- Sunstein, Cass R. *Republic.com 2.0*. Princeton University Press, 2007. — Updated edition extending the echo-chamber and polarization argument to Web 2.0 platforms.
- Sunstein, Cass R. *#Republic: Divided Democracy in the Age of Social Media*. Princeton University Press, 2017. — Latest restatement, applying the argument to social media and algorithmic curation.
- Pariser, Eli. *The Filter Bubble: What the Internet Is Hiding from You*. Penguin Press, 2011. — Coins "filter bubble"; the argument that invisible personalisation (search, feeds) narrows each user's information world. Preceded by his 2011 TED talk of the same name.
- Bakshy, Eytan; Messing, Solomon; Adamic, Lada A. "Exposure to ideologically diverse news and opinion on Facebook." *Science*, vol. 348, no. 6239, 2015, pp. 1130–1132. — Large-scale study finding the feed algorithm's filtering effect was smaller than users' own click-based self-selection; key counter-evidence to the strong filter-bubble thesis.
- Flaxman, Seth; Goel, Sharad; Rao, Justin M. "Filter Bubbles, Echo Chambers, and Online News Consumption." *Public Opinion Quarterly*, vol. 80, S1, 2016, pp. 298–320. — Web-browsing-history study finding social media and search raise ideological distance between sources but *also* increase exposure to opposing viewpoints.
- Sunstein, Cass R. "The Law of Group Polarization." *Journal of Political Philosophy*, vol. 10, no. 2, 2002, pp. 175–195. — Formal statement of group polarization: deliberation among the like-minded shifts the group toward more extreme positions.
- McPherson, Miller; Smith-Lovin, Lynn; Cook, James M. "Birds of a Feather: Homophily in Social Networks." *Annual Review of Sociology*, vol. 27, 2001, pp. 415–444. — Foundational review of homophily, the underlying sorting tendency (cross-referenced as item 48).
- Schkade, David; Sunstein, Cass R.; Hastie, Reid. "What Happened on Deliberation Day?" *California Law Review*, vol. 95 (2007), p. 915. — The primary write-up of the Colorado deliberation experiment. In the summer of 2005, sixty-three citizens from liberal Boulder and conservative Colorado Springs were sorted into like-minded groups and asked to deliberate on global warming, affirmative action, and civil unions for same-sex couples; deliberation made members more extreme and narrowed within-group diversity. The anchor-example source.
