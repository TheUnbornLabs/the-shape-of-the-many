# References — 57. Virality & network thresholds

## Confirmed sources

- Watts, Duncan J. "A simple model of global cascades on random networks." *Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences* 99, no. 9 (2002): 5766–5771. — The formal cascade model: threshold nodes on a network; global cascades possible only in a connectivity window; the "percolating vulnerable cluster" and the rarity of large cascades.
- Salganik, Matthew J., Peter Sheridan Dodds, and Duncan J. Watts. "Experimental Study of Inequality and Unpredictability in an Artificial Cultural Market." *Science* 311, no. 5762 (2006): 854–856. — The Music Lab. Confirmed against the primary paper and its supplementary materials: 48 songs by unknown bands; 14,341 participants total; two conditions — a single independent (control) world and a social-influence condition split into eight parallel "worlds" that evolved in isolation; two experiments (differing in signal strength) run Oct 2004–Mar 2005. Twin findings that social influence increases both the inequality and the unpredictability of success; quality sets loose bounds only.
- Watts, Duncan J., and Peter Sheridan Dodds. "Influentials, Networks, and Public Opinion Formation." *Journal of Consumer Research* 34, no. 4 (2007): 441–458. — The challenge to the "influentials"/"law of the few" thesis: under most modelled conditions, cascades are driven by a critical mass of easily-influenced ordinary people rather than by rare highly-connected individuals.
- Granovetter, Mark. "Threshold Models of Collective Behavior." *American Journal of Sociology* 83, no. 6 (1978): 1420–1443. — The individual-threshold logic (each actor flips when a critical fraction of contacts has flipped) underpinning Watts's cascade model. (Chapter item 7.)

## Further reading to verify before final publication

- Music Lab specifics — CONFIRMED against the primary 2006 *Science* paper and its supplementary online material: 48 songs; 14,341 participants (chapter's "more than fourteen thousand" is correct); **eight** social-influence worlds plus **one** independent (control) world (an earlier draft's "nine parallel/social worlds" was an error — the eight parallel worlds are the social-influence condition only; corrected in the chapter). Recruitment ran October 7, 2004 – March 8, 2005 across two experiments.
- Whether the Music Lab's extreme inequality and unpredictability generalise from the artificial single-signal setting to real, multi-signal cultural markets — stated in the chapter as an inference, not a measurement; verify against later replications and field studies.
- The contested status of "influentials" — the claim that well-placed individuals do reliably move outcomes in some domains (certain product categories, platforms, or bounded communities) should be pinned to specific studies (e.g., subsequent seeding and influence-maximisation literature) before final publication.
