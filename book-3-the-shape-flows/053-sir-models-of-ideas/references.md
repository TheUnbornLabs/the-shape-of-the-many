# References — 53. Epidemics of ideas — the SIR model

## Confirmed sources

- Kermack, W. O., & McKendrick, A. G. "A Contribution to the Mathematical Theory of Epidemics." *Proceedings of the Royal Society of London A*, vol. 115, no. 772 (1927), pp. 700–721. — The founding SIR compartmental model and the epidemic threshold theorem; source of the R0-style critical-density result.
- Goffman, W., & Newill, V. A. "Generalization of Epidemic Theory: An Application to the Transmission of Ideas." *Nature*, vol. 204 (1964), pp. 225–228. — Explicit transfer of epidemic transmission mathematics to the spread of ideas/intellectual influence.
- Daley, D. J., & Kendall, D. G. "Stochastic Rumours." *Journal of the Institute of Mathematics and its Applications*, vol. 1 (1965), pp. 42–55; and the related short communication in *Nature*, vol. 204 (1964), p. 1118. — The ignorant/spreader/stifler rumour model; establishes that rumour spread diverges from disease because spreaders stop on meeting those who already know.
- Anderson, R. M., & May, R. M. *Infectious Diseases of Humans: Dynamics and Control*. Oxford University Press, 1991. — Standard reference for the basic reproduction number R0, the transmissibility × contacts × duration decomposition, and the herd-immunity threshold 1 − 1/R0.
- Pastor-Satorras, R., & Vespignani, A. "Epidemic Spreading in Scale-Free Networks." *Physical Review Letters*, vol. 86, no. 14 (2001), pp. 3200–3203. — Shows that in scale-free networks the epidemic threshold vanishes (tends to zero), so hubs raise the effective reproduction number and let infections spread even at very low transmissibility; basis for the super-spreader / hub claims (cross-ref chapter 46).
- van der Linden, S., & Roozenbeek, J. Work on inoculation theory and prebunking against misinformation, including Roozenbeek, J. & van der Linden, S., "Fake news game confers psychological resistance against online misinformation," *Palgrave Communications*, vol. 5 (2019). — Basis for the "cognitive immunity" / prebunking frontier discussion; builds on McGuire's original inoculation theory (1960s).

## Confirmed on verification

- *War of the Worlds* (1938 broadcast) panic scale — the claim that mass panic was largely a newspaper-manufactured legend is Jefferson Pooley and Michael J. Socolow, "The Myth of the War of the Worlds Panic," *Slate*, 28 October 2013. Confirmed: they argue almost no one was actually fooled (a C. E. Hooper survey that night found roughly 2% of respondents listening to the program, and none described it as a news broadcast), and that newspapers, threatened by radio's advertising encroachment, amplified the panic story to discredit the medium. The chapter's "largely a myth" framing is well-supported.
