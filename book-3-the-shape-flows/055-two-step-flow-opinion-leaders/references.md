# References — 55. Two-step flow & opinion leaders

## Confirmed sources

- Lazarsfeld, Paul F., Bernard Berelson, and Hazel Gaudet. *The People's Choice: How the Voter Makes Up His Mind in a Presidential Campaign*. Columbia University Press, 1944. — The Erie County, Ohio panel study of the 1940 US presidential election; origin of the finding that interpersonal influence outweighed direct media effects and the first statement of the two-step flow.
- Katz, Elihu, and Paul F. Lazarsfeld. *Personal Influence: The Part Played by People in the Flow of Mass Communications*. The Free Press, 1955. — The Decatur, Illinois study; developed and tested the two-step flow across marketing, fashion, film, and public affairs, and established that opinion leadership is domain-specific and exists at every social level.
- Katz, Elihu. "The Two-Step Flow of Communication: An Up-To-Date Report on an Hypothesis." *Public Opinion Quarterly*, vol. 21, no. 1, 1957, pp. 61–78. — Named and consolidated the theory; principal citation for the concept.
- Bennett, W. Lance, and Jarol B. Manheim. "The One-Step Flow of Communication." *The Annals of the American Academy of Political and Social Science*, vol. 608, no. 1, 2006, pp. 213–232. — Argues the classic two-step model may collapse toward a one-step flow under fragmented, individually targeted contemporary media; source for the beat-7 revision.

## Further reading to verify before final publication

- CONFIRMED: the Erie County, Ohio panel of the 1940 election is reported as ~600 respondents re-interviewed monthly across roughly seven interviews during the campaign (a panel drawn from a larger initial sample). The chapter's "roughly six hundred" and "month after month" are accurate. (Note: "Erie County, Ohio" is correct — the book's own study chapter is titled "Erie County, Ohio, 1940"; some secondary sources erroneously say "Erie, Pennsylvania.")
- CONFIRMED: the quoted phrase "ideas often flow from radio and print to the opinion leaders and from them to the less active sections of the population" is from *The People's Choice* (1944), reported at p. 151, and is the phrasing Katz (1957) later restates. Wording as quoted in the chapter is accurate.
- Decatur study details (that fashion/marketing leaders skewed toward younger, gregarious women while public-affairs leaders differed) — reflects the domain-specificity finding in *Personal Influence*; verify the specific demographic characterizations against the source before publication.
- Agenda-setting (McCombs and Shaw, 1972) and cultivation theory (Gerbner) are referenced generically in the critique as the later revision of the "limited effects" era; add full citations if these claims are foregrounded in a later pass.
