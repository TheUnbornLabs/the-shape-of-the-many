# References — 54. Simple versus complex contagion

## Confirmed sources

- Centola, Damon, and Michael Macy. "Complex Contagions and the Weakness of Long Ties." *American Journal of Sociology* 113, no. 3 (2007): 702–734. — The founding formal statement of the simple/complex distinction and the argument that long, weak, bridging ties fail to carry contagions with adoption thresholds greater than one.
- Centola, Damon. "The Spread of Behavior in an Online Social Network Experiment." *Science* 329, no. 5996 (2010): 1194–1197. — The controlled experiment showing a health behaviour spread farther and faster in a clustered-lattice network than in a random network with long ties; the chapter's anchor example.
- Centola, Damon. *How Behavior Spreads: The Science of Complex Contagions.* Princeton, NJ: Princeton University Press, 2018. — Book-length synthesis of the complex-contagion research programme, including wide-versus-narrow bridges and social reinforcement.
- Granovetter, Mark. "Threshold Models of Collective Behavior." *American Journal of Sociology* 83, no. 6 (1978): 1420–1443. — The individual-threshold framework (each person requires some number of prior adopters before acting) underlying complex-contagion models; cross-referenced as item 7.
- Granovetter, Mark. "The Strength of Weak Ties." *American Journal of Sociology* 78, no. 6 (1973): 1360–1380. — The weak-ties/bridging argument that complex contagion partly overturns; cross-referenced as item 45.
- Watts, Duncan J., and Steven H. Strogatz. "Collective Dynamics of 'Small-World' Networks." *Nature* 393 (1998): 440–442. — Small-world topology and long-range shortcuts; cross-referenced as item 44.
- McAdam, Doug. "Recruitment to High-Risk Activism: The Case of Freedom Summer." *American Journal of Sociology* 92, no. 1 (1986): 64–90. — Field evidence that participation in high-risk 1964 Freedom Summer activism was predicted by dense strong-tie embeddedness rather than attitude, supporting the strong-ties account of complex contagion.
