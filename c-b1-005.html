Chapter 5 · The Shape Forms

Positive & negative feedback loops

Two loops run under everything a crowd does: one amplifies, one restrains. Their ratio decides whether a system settles or explodes — and the word "positive" is a warning, not a compliment.

The idea

Every collective system contains loops in which its own output comes back around and changes its own future. There are only two kinds, and the whole of collective dynamics is built from them.

A positive feedback loop feeds output back in a way that increases itself. More produces more. A little applause makes a few more hands join, which makes the sound louder, which pulls in more hands still. A rumour that a bank is failing makes people withdraw money, which makes the bank weaker, which makes the rumour truer, which makes more people withdraw. Positive feedback is the engine of everything that runs away: the snowball, the stampede, the viral share, the bubble, the panic. It is amplification with no sense of proportion.

A negative feedback loop feeds output back in a way that reduces itself. More produces less. A thermostat that senses the room getting warm shuts off the heat; the room cools; the heat comes back on. Rising prices choke off demand until they stop rising. A full stomach ends the meal. Negative feedback is the engine of everything that steadies: homeostasis, correction, the pull back toward some equilibrium. It is amplification's opposite — a restoring hand.

The single most important thing to understand, and the thing the everyday meaning of the words actively fights, is this: "positive" does not mean good, and "negative" does not mean bad. The signs are mathematical, not moral. Positive feedback is the destabilising loop — it is as happy carrying a lie to catastrophe as a truth to triumph. Negative feedback is the stabilising loop — the boring, unglamorous machinery that keeps things from flying apart. Almost every real collective system is a tug-of-war between the two, and the ratio between them — how much amplification, how much damping — sets whether the system will settle into a quiet equilibrium or tip over some edge and run.

The origin

The two loops were named and unified by the mathematics of machines before anyone thought to point them at crowds. The word for the whole enterprise is cybernetics, coined by the American mathematician Norbert Wiener in his 1948 book Cybernetics: or Control and Communication in the Animal and the Machine. Wiener took the term from the Greek kybernetes, the steersman — the one who reads the drift of the boat and corrects the tiller — and made the act of correction-by-feedback the founding image of a whole science of self-regulating systems, whether the system was a servomechanism aiming an anti-aircraft gun, a nervous system, or a society.

But the deep root is older, and it is engineering. The intuitive ancestor is James Watt's centrifugal governor of the late eighteenth century — the pair of spinning weights on a steam engine that fly outward as the engine speeds up, and in flying out close a valve that cuts the steam, which slows the engine, which lets the weights fall back. It is a negative feedback loop rendered in brass: speed is fed back to reduce speed, and the engine holds a steady pace it was never explicitly told to hold. The governor worked so well and was understood so poorly that in 1868 the physicist James Clerk Maxwell wrote a paper simply titled "On Governors," giving the first rigorous mathematical account of why some feedback-regulated engines settled to a steady speed and others broke into wild, growing oscillations. Maxwell's paper is, in effect, the moment feedback stopped being a knack and became a theory — the founding text of control engineering.

From there the lineage runs through the servomechanism engineers of the 1930s and 1940s, through Wiener's cybernetics, and into the study of whole systems of interacting loops. In 1961 the MIT engineer Jay Forrester, in Industrial Dynamics, extended the idea to organisations and economies, building models in which tangles of positive and negative loops, laced with delays, produced the booms, busts, and oscillations of real firms and supply chains. The tool Watt built to hold a steam engine steady had become a language for the behaviour of the many.

The mechanism

Strip a loop to its bones and it is a circle: a quantity produces an effect, the effect returns and changes the quantity. The only question that matters is the sign of the return.

If a rise in the quantity feeds back to produce a further rise — and a fall feeds back to produce a further fall — the loop is positive, and it is self-reinforcing. Whatever direction the system happens to be moving, the loop pushes it harder that way. Left alone, a pure positive loop grows exponentially: the bigger it gets, the faster it gets bigger. This is why positive feedback is so often invisible until it is violent. For a long time it does almost nothing; then it crosses some threshold where the amplification outruns whatever was restraining it, and the system leaps. The pheromone trails of the last chapter (item 4) are a positive loop of exactly this kind — a path with a little more scent draws a few more ants, who lay more scent, until one route out of many is suddenly carrying the whole colony. Applause is a positive loop: clapping recruits clapping. A bank run is a positive loop: withdrawal justifies withdrawal. The share, the retweet, the fashion, the panic — all are output feeding back to swell its own input.

If instead a rise feeds back to produce a fall — and a fall feeds back to produce a rise — the loop is negative, and it is self-correcting. It always pushes against the direction of change, which means it hunts toward a resting point and, once there, resists being moved. Price is the great negative loop of markets: scarcity lifts price, high price suppresses demand and calls forth supply, which relieves the scarcity. Satiation is a negative loop of appetite; body temperature, blood sugar, and predator-prey numbers are held within bounds by negative loops. Negative feedback is what makes a system have a "normal" to return to at all.

Two subtleties do most of the real work. First, delay. A negative loop that corrects instantly holds things steady; a negative loop that corrects late — that reacts to where the system was rather than where it is — will overshoot, correct too hard, overshoot the other way, and oscillate. Much of what looks like instability in economies is a negative loop lagging its target. Second, loop gain — the strength of the return relative to the damping. A system with strong negative feedback and weak positive feedback settles. Let the positive loop's gain climb past the negative loop's ability to absorb it, and the same system that sat placid for years tips into runaway. Nothing about the parts changed; the ratio crossed a line.

The anchor example

Stand too close to a live microphone with the speaker behind you and you will hear the purest positive feedback loop in ordinary life: the PA squeal. It builds in under a second and it is unmistakable — a rising howl that climbs until someone yanks the mic away or the speaker cone gives out.

Follow the loop around once. The microphone picks up a faint sound and sends it to the amplifier. The amplifier makes it louder and pushes it out through the speaker. The speaker's sound travels back across the room to the same microphone, which picks it up again — now louder than before — and sends it to be amplified again. Each trip around the loop, the sound is bigger than it was on the last trip. That is positive feedback in its naked form: the output of the system is fed straight back into its own input, multiplied. There is no particular note "trying" to sound; the loop simply seizes on whatever frequency the room and the gear amplify best and drives it, in a fraction of a second, from nothing to a scream.

Notice what the squeal teaches. It needs no malice and no plan — just a closed loop with gain above one. It is indifferent to content: it will amplify a cough, a chord, or silence-plus-hiss with equal ferocity. And it has a threshold you can feel with the volume knob. Turn the gain down and the loop is stable; the room absorbs the sound faster than the amplifier can rebuild it, and nothing happens. Turn the gain up past the point where each trip round the loop returns more than it started with, and the system leaps from quiet to howling with almost no in-between. The sound engineer's craft — notch filters, careful placement, a hand on the fader — is nothing but the deliberate injection of negative feedback to hold the positive loop below its threshold.

Now hear the social version underneath it. A bank run is a PA squeal in money: my withdrawal is your reason to withdraw, which is the next person's reason, each trip round the loop louder than the last, the whole thing tipping from calm to collapse in an afternoon once the gain crosses one. The howl in the auditorium and the queue outside the failing bank are the same mechanism wearing different clothes.

The flip

Feedback is not merely a mechanism in this book. It is the mechanism made mechanical — the sign-flipping dial of the whole series rendered as a physical loop. The thesis of these five books is that the wise crowd and the mob are the same people under different conditions, with a nameable condition flipping the sign between wisdom and catastrophe. Positive feedback is that flip, running in hardware.

Because here is what the PA squeal refuses to care about: what it amplifies. Positive feedback is a pure multiplier with no judgement. Feed it a good signal — many independent, well-aimed guesses — and its amplification is the very thing that lets a crowd converge on a truth, information reinforcing information until the answer rings clear. Feed it a bad signal — one frightened withdrawal, one contemptuous rumour — and the identical amplification drives the crowd off a cliff, error reinforcing error until the panic is total. There are not two engines, one under the wise crowd and one under the mob. There is one engine, positive feedback, and the sign of what you feed it decides whether you get Galton's ox or the stampede.

Which means negative feedback is the hero this book keeps quietly naming. Everything that keeps aggregation honest is negative feedback in disguise. Independence (item 21) is negative feedback: when my error does not depend on yours, our errors correct each other instead of compounding. Diversity (item 19) is negative feedback: a different vantage pulls back against a shared mistake. Dissent, correction, the willingness to sell when everyone is buying — all are restoring hands on a system that would otherwise run. Surowiecki's four conditions (item 20) are, read through this chapter, an engineering specification for keeping the negative loops strong enough to govern the positive one. When those safeguards hold, a crowd is a governed engine, humming at a steady truth. When they fail, the positive loop takes the whole system, and you get its pathologies by name: information cascades (item 26), where each copier's choice amplifies the last until a thousand people carry no more independent evidence than the first two; and bank runs (item 28), the squeal in money. And what positive feedback produces when it finally crosses its threshold has its own chapter — the phase transition (item 6), the leap from quiet to howling, the moment the water becomes ice.

The human weight

The most consequential positive feedback loop in human life may be the one that governs who has what. Wealth begets wealth: capital earns a return, the return becomes more capital, which earns a larger return still. Advantage is fed back as further advantage — a better school buys a better network buys a better job buys a better school for the next generation. Economists call one version of this preferential attachment, the rule by which the already-connected node attracts new connections fastest, so that "the rich get richer" is not a proverb but a loop structure (foreshadowing item 46). Left ungoverned, a positive loop over resources drives toward the same place every positive loop drives: concentration, winner-take-all, a few nodes holding almost everything. Reputation runs the same way, in both directions — the esteemed are given the chances that earn more esteem; the stigmatised are denied the chances that would clear the stigma, and the denial becomes the evidence that seems to justify it.

Here the two loops sharpen into something this series will not let go of. An unjust social order — a caste, a hierarchy, a settled inequality — is often a positive feedback loop that has also, deliberately, had its negative feedback cut. The order reproduces itself: privilege buys the means to defend privilege, and the arrangement grows more entrenched with each turn of the wheel. What would correct it is negative feedback — voice, dissent, the honest report from those the order harms that the order is unjust. That signal is precisely what such systems suppress. When people conceal what they truly think because the cost of saying it is too high, they are muting the one loop that could pull the system back toward equilibrium. This series has a name for that muting — preference falsification (item 34) — and read through this chapter it is a suppressed negative-feedback signal: the thermostat wired shut, the governor's weights bolted in place, so the engine can only speed up. An order held by silence is an engine with its governor removed, and the reason it can run so long and so fast is exactly that nothing is fed back to slow it — until, one day, the silence breaks and the correction that was dammed for a generation arrives all at once.

The critique

"Feedback loop" has become one of the most overused phrases in popular science, and its ubiquity is itself a warning. Once you learn the word you see loops everywhere, and the seeing is cheap. Naming a loop is not explaining a system; it is often just relabelling a correlation with a mechanistic-sounding word and declaring the work done.

Three problems deserve more respect than the phrase usually gets. First, measurement. In physical systems you can put a number on the loop gain — turn the knob, measure the squeal. In real social systems the gain is nearly impossible to measure. How strongly does one withdrawal actually raise the probability of the next? How much does one person's clapping recruit another's? We can tell the loop exists after the fact, when the system has run; we can rarely quantify it in advance, which is the only quantification that would let us predict. Second, delays and nonlinearity. Real loops act late and act unevenly, and a loop that is stabilising at one scale can be destabilising at another. A system can look calm for years precisely because a positive loop is being held below threshold by a negative one — and then flip, not because anything visible changed, but because the ratio crossed a line no one was watching. Simple loop-labelling, which treats a system as "a positive feedback loop" or "a negative feedback loop," misses that most systems are both at once and that the interesting behaviour lives in the switching. Third, and most corrosive, the correlation-versus-causation trap. To claim a loop is to claim a specific causal circuit: A raises B and B raises A. Two things rising together is not that. Much confident talk of "vicious cycles" and "virtuous circles" is a story imposed on co-movement that might have a common external cause, or run in only one direction, or not be a loop at all. The concept is indispensable — it is, in this series, the literal machinery of the flip. But indispensable and easy to abuse are the same sentence about feedback, and the discipline is to earn the loop, direction by direction, rather than to name it and move on.
