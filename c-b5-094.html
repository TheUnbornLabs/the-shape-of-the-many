Chapter 94 · The Shape Remade

Agent-based modelling

Write down a few simple rules for a few simple people, set them loose on a grid, and watch a society you never designed climb out of the arithmetic.

The idea

Most of this book has argued that group-level order — wisdom or madness, a smooth flow or a crush — is not built by anyone. It grows out of many people each following local rules while watching only their neighbours. That claim is easy to assert and hard to see. Agent-based modelling is the method that lets you see it. You build a population of simple software agents, each with a handful of rules about how to behave given what is directly around it. You place them in an environment — a grid, a network, a street. You press play. And then you watch what the whole thing does, because the whole thing does something the rules never mentioned.

The move that defines the method is the direction of explanation. Conventional social science tends to reason downward from aggregates: it writes an equation for the average, the market clearing price, the epidemic curve, and treats the population as a single smooth quantity. Agent-based modelling reasons upward. It refuses to write the macro-pattern in by hand and instead insists on growing it from the micro-rules, from the bottom up. If a wealth distribution, a segregated city, or a wave of infection appears on your screen, it appears because thousands of individuals, none of whom can see the pattern, produced it between them. The modeller's discipline is to specify only what an agent could plausibly know and do, and to earn everything above that.

This is emergence (item 1) turned into an instrument, self-organisation (item 2) made repeatable, the flocking of the boids (item 3) generalised from birds to buyers and voters and commuters. It is the computer treated not as a calculator but as a laboratory in which artificial societies can be raised and rerun.

The origin

The lineage runs through several people who each, without quite naming the method, were doing it. It begins with John von Neumann's cellular automata in the 1940s — a grid of cells switching state by local rules, the first proof that complex global behaviour could be generated by trivially simple neighbours (item 97). John Conway's Game of Life made the idea famous in 1970: three or four rules about living and dead cells, and out of them gliders, oscillators, and structures nobody put there. Craig Reynolds' boids (item 3) showed in 1987 that three steering rules produced a convincingly lifelike flock. Robert Axelrod's computer tournaments in the 1980s, gathered in The Complexity of Cooperation (1997), let strategies compete and evolve rather than solving for an equilibrium (item 96).

But the founding social model was earlier and done by hand. In 1971 Thomas Schelling, writing in the Journal of Mathematical Sociology, sat down with a board and coins and ran what we would now call an agent-based model without a computer — the checkerboard segregation model (item 67). He gave each token one mild rule about the neighbours it would tolerate, moved the unhappy ones, and watched a sharply segregated board assemble itself. Schelling had grown a social pattern from the bottom up two decades before the method had a name.

The name, and the manifesto, arrived in 1996 with Joshua Epstein and Robert Axtell's Growing Artificial Societies, which introduced Sugarscape: a landscape of "sugar" and agents who harvest it. Epstein later called the programme generative social science, and gave it a motto that has organised the field ever since — if you didn't grow it, you didn't explain it. The claim was radical: an explanation of a social fact is a set of local rules from which that fact reliably emerges. Three years later, in 1999, Uri Wilensky released NetLogo, a modelling environment simple enough for a schoolchild and serious enough for a journal, and the method spread out of the Santa Fe tradition and into every discipline that studies collectives.

The mechanism

The recipe has three ingredients and one instruction. First, agents: a population of individuals, each carrying its own state — a location, a wealth, a strategy, an opinion, a health status. Crucially they can differ. Heterogeneity is native to the method, not a nuisance to be averaged away. Second, interaction rules: what an agent does as a function of what it perceives locally — of its neighbours, its patch of environment, the others it is linked to. These rules are usually simple and always local; no agent consults the global state, because no real person can. Third, the environment: the space or network the agents inhabit, which may itself change as they act on it.

The instruction is: simulate. Advance the clock, let every agent apply its rules, update the world, repeat. Nobody computes the outcome. The outcome happens. And what emerges is routinely something you could not have deduced by staring at the rules — a wealth distribution with a long tail, a segregated map, a traffic jam that travels backwards up a motorway while every car moves forward. This is the deep pay-off. Equation-based models buy tractability by assuming a representative agent, perfect mixing, smooth aggregates. Agent-based models keep what those assumptions throw out: individual variation, the geometry of space, the topology of who is connected to whom, and adaptation — agents that learn and change their rules over time. When heterogeneity, space, network structure, or feedback matter to the outcome, and in collective behaviour they almost always do, the method earns its cost. You can watch segregation form (item 67), an epidemic spread across a contact network (item 53), a crowd jam at a bottleneck (item 29), a market find or fail to find its price (item 60), traffic congeal from nothing (item 1). You grow the macro-pattern, then you take it apart by changing one rule and running it again.

The anchor example

Set a grid strewn unevenly with sugar — two mountains of it, valleys between. Scatter a few hundred agents across it. Give each agent almost nothing: a range of vision, a metabolism that burns sugar every step, and one rule — look as far as you can see, go to the richest unoccupied cell within range, eat, repeat. That is Sugarscape, and that is very nearly all of it. The agents cannot see the whole board, cannot coordinate, cannot plan. They are near-mindless harvesters.

Press play, and a society climbs out of the grid. The agents cluster on the sugar mountains and thin out in the valleys, tracing the resource by sheer local greed. Then something less obvious appears. Count how much sugar each agent holds and the distribution is not a bell curve clustered around a comfortable middle — it is sharply skewed, a small number of rich agents and a long tail of the poor, an inequality that looks unmistakably like the wealth distributions of real societies and approaches a power law. Nobody built the inequality in. There is no exploiter, no tax, no theft in the base rules — only vision, metabolism, and a landscape that is richer in some places than others, with agents who started in slightly better spots or saw slightly further pulling steadily ahead. Add sexual reproduction, and you get population waves and migration. Add a second resource, spice, and let agents trade what they are short of for what they hold in surplus, and prices emerge from bilateral haggling — a market with no auctioneer. Epstein and Axtell had grown, from a few lines of rules, a moving picture of migration, inequality, trade, and social structure. Whatever one thinks of it as an account of any real society, it is an existence proof of the method's central promise: that macro-structure can be raised from micro-behaviour, and watched while it rises.

The flip

Here the method stops being one item among a hundred and becomes the lens through which the whole series comes into focus. The thesis of these five books is a single sentence: the wise crowd and the mob are the same people, one mechanism under different conditions, with a nameable parameter setting the sign. For most of the book that has been an argument. Agent-based modelling makes it an experiment. Because you can build the agents once and then run them under both conditions, you can see the same local rules produce wisdom in one run and catastrophe in the next, with a single dial moved between them.

Take Schelling's model, the founding agent-based model (item 67). Each agent is content unless the fraction of unlike neighbours exceeds some threshold, at which point it moves. Set that threshold low — agents happy in a mixed neighbourhood — and the board stays integrated. Nudge the threshold up past a modest point and the whole map tips into stark segregation, though no single agent wanted a segregated city. One number, and the collective outcome flips. Granovetter's threshold model of riots and fads (item 7) is the same knife: a crowd in which each person will join once enough others have joined can either fizzle into nothing or cascade into a riot, and the difference between the wise restraint and the mob is a small shift in the distribution of thresholds. Run the boids (item 3) with one set of weights and you get graceful coordinated flocking; shift the alignment rule and you get a stampede that piles into a wall. Every mechanism in Books One through Four — the cascade, the cascade averted, the honeybee democracy and the crowd crush, the governed commons and the ruined one — can be grown as agents and its sign-flip observed by hand. This is why the method opens the toolkit. It is the apparatus in which the book's central claim stops being a rhetorical pairing of "good crowd" and "bad crowd" and becomes a reproducible fact: identical agents, one parameter, two macro-worlds. The Shape you have watched form, break, flow, and hold across four books can, in an agent-based model, be remade at will and watched changing sign.

The human weight

A model that can grow a society can be pointed at a real one, and here the method acquires stakes it cannot shrug off. Agent-based models are no longer academic toys. They inform epidemic response — during the COVID-19 pandemic, agent-based simulations of contact and infection helped shape lockdown and reopening decisions that reordered ordinary life for billions. They plan the evacuation of stadiums and the widths of doorways (item 29). They are used to test economic and financial policy where the heterogeneity of firms and households matters. In each case the model's output arrives clothed in the authority of computation, and a policy affecting the many is nudged one way or another by it.

That authority is where the danger lives, and it deserves stating plainly. A model is an argument written in code. Every rule an agent follows is a claim about how people behave; every rule left out is a claim that it did not matter. When a modeller decides who the agents are, what they can perceive, how they respond, and which frictions to omit, the modeller is embedding a theory of human conduct — and that theory then wears the impartial face of a simulation. Change whose behaviour is represented, and the "optimal" policy changes with it. A model that assumes people comply, or panic, or share the risk equally, will license different interventions than one that does not, and the assumption is usually invisible to everyone downstream of the modeller. This is the frontier note for the whole toolkit: an agent-based model can be authoritative-looking and wrong in the same breath, and its wrongness can drive decisions that fall hardest on the people least represented in its rules. The only defences are transparency about what was assumed and humility about what was left out — the responsibility of the modeller to publish the argument, not just the conclusion. It bridges directly to the epistemics of replication and open science (item 100), where the discipline of showing your work becomes the difference between a tool of understanding and a tool of quiet, deniable power.

The critique

The method's great strength is also the wound at its centre, and honesty about it is the price of using it well. Agent-based models are flexible almost without limit. With enough agents, enough rules, and enough free parameters, you can grow very nearly any pattern you like. That flexibility means that fitting a simulation to real data confirms far less than it appears to. If a model reproduces an observed wealth distribution or epidemic curve, it has shown that one set of rules is sufficient to generate that pattern — Epstein's generative test, that you grew it. But sufficiency is not necessity. A different set of rules might grow the same pattern equally well, and the data alone cannot adjudicate between them. This is the identification problem, and it is the reason "if you didn't grow it, you didn't explain it" states a necessary condition for explanation and not a sufficient one. Growing the pattern is the entry ticket, not the proof.

Around that central problem cluster the practical ones. Agent-based models are hard to validate against reality, hard to calibrate when their parameters have no clean empirical counterpart, and hard to compare with one another because each is its own bespoke world. A striking result can turn out to be an artefact of an arbitrary choice — the shape of the grid, the order in which agents update, a tie-breaking rule — rather than anything about the system being modelled. And reproducibility is a known scandal of the field: many published models are described too incompletely to rebuild, their code unshared, their assumptions buried in prose, so that even a diligent reader cannot check whether the pattern really follows from the rules. None of this makes the method worthless; it makes it demanding. An agent-based model is a powerful instrument that yields knowledge only under discipline — pre-registered questions, sensitivity analysis, shared code, explicit assumptions, humility about what a successful growth does and does not prove. That demand for discipline is exactly the theme the rest of this toolkit will insist on, and it hands off, deliberately, to the epistemic humility that closes the series (item 100).
