Chapter 82 · The Shape Holds Power

Propaganda & manufactured consent

Propaganda is not the art of making people believe lies; it is the art of deciding what they will think about, and of arranging that the answer look like their own.

The idea

Every phenomenon in this series so far has been described as something that happens to a crowd — a contagion that spreads, a cascade that tips, a norm that holds because no one dares test it. Propaganda is what happens when someone reads that description, understands the gears, and reaches in to turn them. It is the deliberate engineering of collective belief: the conscious use of communication to produce, in a large population, a correlated conviction that serves the interests of whoever is doing the producing.

The word "propaganda" now carries the smell of the enemy — it is what the other side does — but its technical meaning is neutral and precise. It is organised persuasion aimed at a mass, using not argument alone but the levers of social influence: repetition until a claim feels self-evident, imagery until a feeling attaches to a symbol, the visible endorsement of others until agreement looks like the natural state of the world. Its most sophisticated form does not tell you what to believe. It decides what is discussed and what is unthinkable, which facts are everywhere and which are nowhere, and lets you supply the conclusion yourself — at which point you will defend it as your own.

Two traditions frame the subject, and they sit in uneasy tension. The first is celebratory: propaganda as a necessary tool of democratic governance, to be wielded by an enlightened elite. The second is critical: the manufacture of consent as the quiet, structural function of a free press in a market society. One tradition teaches the technique; the other diagnoses it. Between them they map most of what it means to engineer a herd.

The origin

The celebratory tradition has a name and a face: Edward Bernays (1891–1995), a nephew of Sigmund Freud, who styled himself the "father of public relations" and was not being modest. In two books — Crystallizing Public Opinion (1923) and Propaganda (1928) — Bernays set out a theory of the mass that he drew directly from his uncle's psychology and from Gustave Le Bon's crowd (item 77). The masses, he held, are irrational, driven by unconscious desire, and therefore governable by anyone who understands the desire. His opening lines in Propaganda remain the most candid description of the project ever written: "The conscious and intelligent manipulation of the organized habits and opinions of the masses is an important element in democratic society." Those who manage this manipulation, he went on, "constitute an invisible government which is the true ruling power of our country."

Bernays did not think this sinister. He thought it inevitable and, handled by the right people, benign — democracy was too unwieldy to run any other way, and consent, like production, had to be manufactured. The phrase he made his own, "the engineering of consent," was a boast.

The critical tradition inherits the phrase and turns it into an indictment. The words "manufacture of consent" are older than Bernays's fame; the journalist Walter Lippmann used them in Public Opinion (1922), arguing that in a modern society too large and complex for any citizen to know directly, the "pictures in our heads" are necessarily supplied by others, and that the deliberate shaping of those pictures had become "a self-conscious art and a regular organ of popular government." Sixty-six years later, Edward Herman and Noam Chomsky borrowed Lippmann's phrase for the title of Manufacturing Consent (1988) and inverted its politics. Where Lippmann and Bernays saw an elite doing a necessary job, Herman and Chomsky saw a market press systematically producing consent for elite interests — not through censorship, but through structure.

The mechanism

Bernays's mechanism is the psychology of this whole series, run as a recipe. To move a mass, do not argue with individuals; alter the environment of cues in which they decide. Attach your product or your position to a pre-existing desire, stage the endorsement of authorities and peers, and let social proof and emotional contagion carry it. Bernays understood, decades before the researchers who named them, the levers this book has been cataloguing: the availability of an idea (item 27), the visible behaviour of others as evidence (social proof, item 39), the transfer of feeling through a crowd (emotional contagion, item 33), and the persuasion principles later systematised by Cialdini (item 38). His signature move was to capture the opinion leaders — the doctors, the society hostesses, the tastemakers — and let influence flow down through them, a strategy that anticipates the two-step flow of communication (item 55) by a generation.

The Herman–Chomsky mechanism is structural rather than psychological, and this is its originality. It does not require any journalist to lie or any editor to take orders. Consent is manufactured, they argue, by five "filters" through which news must pass, so that by the time information reaches the public it has been shaped to elite interests without anyone intending it. First, ownership: mass media are large, profit-seeking corporations owned by wealthy interests, and their output tends not to attack the system that enriches them. Second, advertising: since advertisers, not readers, are the real customers, content that alienates advertisers is filtered out. Third, sourcing: newsrooms depend on a steady, cheap supply of information from official sources — governments, corporations, think tanks — who thereby set the frame. Fourth, flak: organised negative response punishes reporting that strays, disciplining coverage. Fifth, an ideological enemy — in their day, anticommunism — a unifying threat against which dissent looks like disloyalty. The filters operate as a market sieve, not a conspiracy, and their product is a press that is free, competitive, and reliably narrow.

What both mechanisms share is the insight that propaganda works less by controlling belief than by controlling salience and framing: what gets discussed, in what terms, and what never surfaces at all. The most effective propaganda leaves the range of respectable opinion looking like the whole of thinkable opinion.

The anchor example

On Easter Sunday — 31 March 1929 — a group of fashionable young women walked down Fifth Avenue in New York and, at a pre-arranged signal, lit cigarettes in public. To the reporters, who had been tipped off in advance, the women described their cigarettes as "torches of freedom," lit in defiance of a taboo that let men smoke in the street but shamed women who did the same. The next day The New York Times ran the story — "Group of Girls Puff at Cigarettes as a Gesture of 'Freedom'" — and it was picked up across the country. It looked like a spontaneous feminist gesture. It was a public-relations campaign, and Bernays had built it.

The American Tobacco Company had a problem: half the market — women — largely did not smoke in public, held back by social prohibition. Bernays, hired to break the taboo, did not run advertisements arguing that women should smoke. He reframed the cigarette as an instrument of emancipation, consulted the psychoanalyst A. A. Brill — who told him that cigarettes could be read as symbols of male power, "torches of freedom" — and staged a spectacle in which women seized that power for themselves. He recruited the marchers, coached the language, and fed the press the story he wanted told. The women who lit up believed, many of them, that they were making a statement of their own. That was the point. Bernays had not persuaded them of a proposition; he had engineered a piece of social proof so vivid that it made a private industry's interest look like a public movement, and let each woman who joined feel she had arrived at emancipation herself.

(The core facts of this campaign — the 31 March 1929 Easter Sunday parade on Fifth Avenue, the consultation with A. A. Brill, and the "torches of freedom" framing — are documented in contemporary press coverage and later scholarship. The scale of its impact and the tidy claim of a single decisive campaign rest substantially on Bernays's own retelling and should be read with the caution his self-promotion warrants; historians have questioned how spontaneous, how large, and how consequential the march really was.)

The flip

Here is the turn on which this chapter, and much of the book, depends. Propaganda invents nothing. It has no mechanism of its own. Every lever it pulls is one this series has already described as a natural feature of collective life — emotional contagion (item 33), social proof (item 39), information and availability cascades (items 26 and 27), the effervescence of the assembled crowd (item 76), the bond between a leader and the led (item 78). Propaganda is simply these mechanisms taken up as instruments — turned, consciously, by people with power, toward a chosen end.

This is why "engineering the herd" is the right phrase and why it is not a special category of pathology. The mechanisms are neutral tools. The same contagion that spreads a lifesaving rumour spreads an atrocity libel. The same social proof that lets a crowd converge on the true weight of an ox (item 17) lets it converge on a manufactured consensus. The dial that this series keeps returning to — the one that sets the sign between the wise crowd and the mob — turns here on a single question about the communication itself: does it distribute independent information, or does it engineer correlated belief?

That is the whole distinction. Communication that gives people truly independent inputs — diverse sources, private judgment, the raw material to decide for themselves — feeds the machinery of Condorcet's wise crowd (item 18), where independent errors cancel and the aggregate approaches truth. Communication engineered to make everyone believe the same thing for the same supplied reason destroys exactly the independence on which that wisdom rests. It correlates the errors. It manufactures the cascade. The identical medium — a newspaper, a broadcast, a feed — can do either. It can inform a public into wisdom or organise a population into a mob, and the sign is set not by the technology but by whether the flow of communication multiplies independent viewpoints or collapses them into one. Propagandists reached for Le Bon (item 77) precisely because Le Bon had told them how to collapse them; the recommendation algorithm (item 87) and the astroturfing bot (item 89) are only the newest hands on the same dial.

The human weight

The deepest work propaganda does is not to change minds but to manufacture a false picture of what everyone else believes — and this is where it touches the frontier this series keeps circling. Recall the mechanism of imposed silence: an unjust order survives not because a majority privately endorses it, but because each person, reading the apparent consensus around them, conceals their own doubt and performs agreement (preference falsification, item 34). Propaganda is the industrial manufacture of that false consensus. It engineers preference falsification at scale by making dissent look rare, deviant, and dangerous; it fabricates the social proof — "everyone supports the regime, the war, the leader" — that keeps each doubter silent; and it manufactures the availability cascade (item 27) by repeating the official line until it seems too obvious to question. The result is a population that mistakes an engineered consensus for its own considered view, and that polices itself accordingly, because each member believes they are the only one who doubts.

The authoritarian propaganda state does this crudely and visibly, with parades, portraits, and a monopoly on the printed word. But the Herman–Chomsky argument is that the market-media democracy does something subtler and, in one respect, more complete: it produces the same manufactured consensus while leaving the population convinced it arrived at its opinions freely, through a press it experiences as adversarial and open. In both cases the machinery of stratification (item 85) is fed — the correlated silence that lets an order reproduce itself is not merely permitted but actively supplied.

And there is a resistance implied in the diagnosis. If propaganda works by manufacturing correlated belief and hiding the manufacture, then seeing the mechanism is itself a form of counter-power. Media literacy — the habit of asking who benefits from a frame, who is missing from the sources, what is not being discussed — restores a measure of the independence that propaganda exists to destroy. Seeking out truly independent information, from sources with different interests, rebuilds the diversity on which a wise crowd depends. This is not a solved problem; the same tools now operate at machine speed and personalised scale, through the recommendation systems and automated networks of Book Five (items 87 through 89), which can manufacture the appearance of grassroots consensus more cheaply than Bernays ever could. But the principle of resistance is unchanged: protect the independence of the inputs, and the herd cannot be engineered into a single correlated belief without the seams showing.

The critique

Two forms of humility are owed here, one to each tradition, and a warning owed to both.

The first is that the strong theory of propaganda — the "hypodermic needle" or "magic bullet" model, in which a message is injected into a passive audience and produces the intended belief — is empirically weak, and has been since mid-century. Audiences are not the inert mass that both Bernays and his fiercest critics assumed. The two-step flow research (item 55) showed influence flowing through, and being filtered by, opinion leaders who talk back; later work on active audiences found people interpreting, resisting, ignoring, and repurposing messages against their intended grain. Propaganda has effects, but they are usually smaller, slower, and more conditional than either the propagandist's boast or the alarmed critic's fear would have it. Bernays himself is a prime exhibit: a relentless self-promoter whose accounts of his own omnipotence — including, quite possibly, the tidy legend of the Torches of Freedom — should be read as the salesmanship of a man selling the belief that consent can be engineered on demand.

The second humility is owed to Herman and Chomsky. The propaganda model has been enormously influential and captures something real about the structural biases of a commercial press. But critics fault it as functionalist — it explains every media outcome by the interests it is said to serve, which makes it hard to falsify — and as sounding conspiratorial even where its authors insist on structure over conspiracy. It underweights journalistic autonomy, the real if uneven adversarialism of a free press, and the agency of audiences who do not simply absorb what the filters pass. That a model is compatible with the evidence is not the same as its being uniquely required by it.

The warning belongs to both. Propaganda is used by every side, not only the enemy's; the very neutrality of the mechanism guarantees it. And the accusation "it's just propaganda" is itself one of the most effective propaganda weapons available — a way to dismiss inconvenient information without engaging it, to correlate an audience against a source by attaching a smell to it. The reader who has followed this chapter should be able to see the manipulation in the charge as readily as in the thing charged. Recognising the mechanism cuts both ways, or it is not recognition at all.
