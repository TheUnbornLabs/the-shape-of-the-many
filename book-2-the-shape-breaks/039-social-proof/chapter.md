# 39. Social proof

> When we do not know what is true or right, we read the answer off the behaviour of the people around us — and that reading is wisdom or folly depending entirely on whether those people knew anything.

## The idea

Social proof is the habit of treating what other people do as evidence of what is correct. Faced with a question we cannot resolve from our own knowledge — which dish is good, whether the alarm means anything, what a reasonable person believes about a stranger's guilt — we glance sideways and let the crowd's behaviour stand in for the information we lack. If everyone is ordering the fish, the fish is probably good. If nobody is moving toward the exit, there is probably no fire. The move is so automatic that we rarely notice ourselves making it, and so useful that we could not function without it. A person who refused, on principle, to let others' choices inform their own would have to independently verify every restaurant, every rumour, every road sign, and would starve in a week.

What makes social proof more than a synonym for imitation is the word *proof*. We are not merely copying; we are inferring. The other diners' choices are being processed as *data* — as a shortcut to a judgment we would otherwise have to earn the hard way. This is the informational engine that runs beneath most of what the earlier chapters of this book have described. Conformity (item 35) is what social proof looks like from the outside; information cascades (item 26) are what happens when social proof compounds; a good half of the influence techniques catalogued by Cialdini (item 38) are just social proof deliberately staged. Strip the machinery down and it is one inference, repeated everywhere: *other people's behaviour is information about the world.* The trouble is that it is only sometimes true.

## The origin

The phrase "social proof" was made famous by the psychologist Robert Cialdini in his 1984 book *Influence: The Psychology of Persuasion*, where it names one of the six levers of compliance. But Cialdini was giving a memorable label to a mechanism that experimental social psychology had spent three decades assembling. Its deepest root is Leon Festinger's theory of social comparison, set out in 1954 in the journal *Human Relations*: Festinger argued that human beings have a basic drive to evaluate their own opinions and abilities, and that where no objective, physical standard is available, they evaluate themselves by comparison with other people. Other people become the measuring instrument when the world offers no ruler of its own.

Around Festinger sit the two great conformity experiments this book keeps returning to. Muzafer Sherif's autokinetic studies of the 1930s (item 36) showed that when a judgment is truly ambiguous — how far a stationary pinprick of light appears to move in a dark room — people converge on a shared group estimate and then carry it with them, having quietly adopted the crowd as their reality. Solomon Asch's line-judgment experiments of the 1950s (item 35) showed the darker possibility: that people will echo a majority even when their own eyes tell them the majority is wrong. In 1955, in the *Journal of Abnormal and Social Psychology*, Morton Deutsch and Harold Gerard drew the distinction that organises the whole field. People conform for two different reasons: *normative* influence, the wish to be liked and not to stand out, and *informational* influence, the belief that others' behaviour carries genuine information about reality. Social proof is the second of these — conformity's honest, inferential half, the part that is trying to be right rather than merely trying to fit in.

## The mechanism

The gear is an inference under uncertainty, and it is usually a good one. Information is expensive; other people's behaviour is cheap and abundant; and much of the time that behaviour really does encode what those people have learned. When a hundred travellers walk past one food stall to queue at another, their feet are aggregating a hundred private verdicts you did not have to gather yourself. Copying them is not laziness so much as thrift — a way of importing information at almost no cost. On average, across the ordinary run of life, it pays. That is why the disposition is universal and why evolution did not weed it out.

Two conditions sharpen it. The first is *uncertainty*: social proof grips hardest exactly when we are least sure what to do. In a familiar situation with a clear right answer, we consult ourselves; in a novel or ambiguous one, we consult the room. The second is *similarity*: we weight most heavily the behaviour of people we take to be like us. The choices of a crowd that resembles us feel like better evidence about *our* situation than the choices of strangers whose circumstances we cannot read. This is sensible Bayesian housekeeping — a similar person's verdict is more relevant to our own case — but it has a cost we will meet again, because it means social proof and homophily reinforce each other, and the crowd we copy is disproportionately the crowd that already resembles us.

Here is the flaw built into the gear, and it is the whole chapter in one sentence. The inference "if everyone is doing it, it must be sensible" is only valid when the everyones arrived at their behaviour independently. Social proof reads the *number* of people doing a thing as if it were the *amount of evidence* behind it. But if those people are themselves merely copying each other, the number can be enormous and the underlying evidence can be almost nothing. The heuristic cannot tell the difference between a hundred people who each checked and a hundred people who each glanced at their neighbour. It counts heads, and heads are a good proxy for information only when the heads thought for themselves. Social proof is adaptive on average and blind in exactly the case that matters most.

## The anchor example

In 1969, Stanley Milgram, Leonard Bickman, and Lawrence Berkowitz ran a study on a crowded New York City sidewalk and reported it in the *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology* under a title as plain as the effect: "Note on the Drawing Power of Crowds of Different Size." They positioned confederates on the pavement and had them stop and stare up at a sixth-floor window across the street, at nothing in particular, for sixty seconds. Then they filmed what the passing strangers did.

With a single person gazing upward, about four in ten passersby tilted their heads to look at the same empty window. That alone is telling — one stranger's upward glance is enough to make four out of ten people scan the sky for a threat or a wonder that is not there. But the point of the study was what happened as the staring group grew. Five gazers pulled in more onlookers; fifteen pulled in most of the street. When fifteen confederates stared up together, roughly 85 percent of passersby looked up at the same window. The upward gaze propagated through the crowd not because anything was in the window but because each new person was reading the same silent message off the people already looking: *they can see something I can't; I had better look too.* The behaviour of the crowd had become, for each newcomer, evidence about the world — evidence that referred to nothing.

The experiment is the clean skeleton of social proof because it strips away every other motive. There is nothing to buy, no one to impress, no group to join, no punishment for walking on. There is only the bare inference — other people are looking, so there must be something to see — scaling smoothly with the size of the crowd. And it exposes the fatal circularity in a single image: a hundred people can end up staring at an empty window, each one taking the other ninety-nine as proof that the window is worth staring at, while the total quantity of actual information in the scene remains exactly zero.

## The flip

Social proof is the most double-edged idea in this book, and the reason is that it is the individual-level version of the book's entire thesis. The mental move it describes — *do what others are doing* — is not sometimes wisdom and sometimes folly because there are two kinds of crowd. It is wisdom or folly depending on one condition, and it is the same condition that runs under everything: whether the others judged independently or copied one another.

Consider the two poles side by side. When Galton's fairgoers each wrote down a private guess at the ox's weight (item 17), and when Condorcet's jurors each weighed the evidence alone (item 18), the crowd's behaviour was worth reading precisely because each judgment was independent. Their errors pointed in every direction and cancelled (item 21); the aggregate carried real information; deferring to it was rational. This is social proof at its best — the wise-crowd principle, in which the behaviour of many independent minds truly is evidence, and copying it truly makes you smarter.

Now take the identical inference and remove independence. When each person in an information cascade (item 26) decides by watching the person ahead of them rather than by consulting their own knowledge, the crowd's behaviour stops being an aggregate of many judgments and becomes an echo of the first one or two. The heads still multiply, but the evidence behind them does not. Reading social proof off *this* crowd — the crowd of Milgram's sky-gazers, the crowd of Asch's confederates (item 35), the crowd of Sherif's converging estimates (item 36) — imports not a hundred verdicts but a single verdict wearing a hundred faces. The same act that made you wiser at the fair now makes you a link in a chain of error.

This is the whole series stated in one heuristic. *Do what others do* is the mob principle and the wise-crowd principle at once; the sign is set entirely by whether the "proof" is independent evidence or merely an echo of itself. Everything Cialdini teaches marketers about social proof (item 38) is a technique for manufacturing the echo and dressing it as evidence. Everything Surowiecki's conditions try to protect is the independence that keeps the proof honest. The distinction between normative and informational influence (item 41) tells you *why* people copy; the distinction between independent and dependent crowds tells you *whether they should*. And when recommendation algorithms (item 87) show you what is popular and thereby make it more popular, they are automating social proof at a scale that all but guarantees the echo drowns the evidence.

## The human weight

Because visible behaviour reads as proof, a false consensus that *looks* real can become real. This is the frontier this book keeps circling. If a majority appears to believe something — if the reviews are glowing, the crowd is cheering, the slogans are everywhere, "everyone knows" the claim is true — then social proof pulls the undecided toward it, and their compliance thickens the appearance, and the appearance recruits the next wave. The consensus need not have been genuine to begin with. It only needs to be *visible*. Manufactured social proof — the staged crowd, the paid review, the laugh track that makes a mediocre joke play as funny, the bot army amplifying a hashtag (item 89) — works precisely because our inference machinery cannot audit the independence of the heads it counts. Fabricate the appearance of a majority and you can, through ordinary conformity, conjure the real thing.

This is also how orders of imposed silence sustain themselves. A hierarchy or a taboo does not need most people to privately endorse it. It needs the visible behaviour of the crowd to *look* like endorsement, so that each doubter, reading the room, concludes that the doubt is theirs alone. Social proof is the mechanism that converts a few loud or coordinated public performances into an apparently universal norm, and that then makes every private dissenter feel isolated — a minority of one facing a wall of agreement that may be as hollow as Milgram's empty window. This is the informational underside of preference falsification (item 34) and the engine of pluralistic ignorance (item 42): a room in which nearly everyone privately doubts the norm, yet each reads everyone else's outward compliance as proof that the doubt is not shared, and so stays silent, and so supplies one more piece of proof to the next person. The silence is self-certifying. Visible majority behaviour, even when it is false all the way down, makes dissent look like error — and that appearance is often all an unjust order requires to hold.

## The critique

Social proof is real, but the confidence with which it is invoked has outrun the evidence, and three cautions are owed. The first is that it is powerfully context-dependent and often weaker than the popular literature claims. It bites under uncertainty and among the similar, and shrinks when the task is clear, the stakes are high, or the crowd is visibly unlike us. Some of its most-cited demonstrations, moreover, come from the era of social psychology now under the hardest replication scrutiny — small samples, Western and often student subjects, a publication culture that rewarded the striking result. The sky-gazing study is a vivid illustration, not a precisely reproducible constant; treat the exact percentages as of their time and setting, not as a law of nature.

The second caution is that social proof can backfire, and its own leading theorist proved it. When Robert Cialdini and colleagues tested normative signs at Arizona's Petrified Forest National Park in 2006, publishing in the journal *Social Influence*, they found that a sign lamenting how *many* visitors steal petrified wood — meant to shame the practice — actually *increased* theft, because it broadcast the descriptive norm that stealing is common and therefore normal. The parallel energy studies with P. Wesley Schultz (published in *Psychological Science* in 2007) showed the same boomerang: telling households they used less power than their neighbours nudged them to use *more*. Tell people that many others also cheat, litter, or default, and social proof does its work faithfully — it normalises the very behaviour you meant to condemn. The heuristic has no moral compass; it copies whatever it is shown is common.

The third caution is conceptual. "Social proof" is a label elastic enough to explain almost anything after the fact — any time behaviour spreads, one can say the spreaders were reading proof off each other — and a construct that explains everything explains nothing. The hard and rarely-done work is to disentangle social proof from genuine information: to show that the crowd's behaviour moved a person *beyond* what the real underlying evidence should have, rather than simply transmitting evidence that happened to travel through people. That is precisely the independent-versus-dependent distinction this chapter turns on, and it is also why the distinction is not a philosophical nicety but the only thing that tells you whether the crowd you are about to copy is worth copying.
