# 36. Sherif's autokinetic norms

> Put people in the dark with no fact to check against, and they will not wait in silence — they will build a truth together, and it will outlive every one of them.

## The idea

Sit alone in a completely dark room. A single pinpoint of light is switched on some distance away. It is fixed, bolted, motionless — but after a few seconds it will seem to drift. It slides, wanders, jumps back. With no visible frame around it, no wall, no floor, no edge to anchor it against, your eye has nothing to hold the light still, and the tiny involuntary movements of your own eyeballs get read as movement out there in the world. This is the autokinetic effect, a well-documented illusion, and its central feature for our purposes is that there is no correct answer to the question *how far did the light move?* It did not move at all. Whatever you report is a guess pulled out of the dark.

Now the experiment. Ask people, one at a time, how far the light travelled. Their answers scatter all over the place — one says two inches, another says eight, another says a foot. Each person settles into a personal range and stays there. Then bring them together and let them call out their estimates in each other's hearing. Something orderly happens. The wild scatter contracts. Trial after trial, the group's answers pull toward one another and settle on a shared figure — a *group norm* — that none of them held at the start. They have, between them, manufactured a fact about the world where no fact existed.

The deeper finding is what happens next. Test those same people alone again, days later, and they keep using the group's number. The invented norm has moved inside them; it is now simply what they believe the light does. And in the most unsettling version, seed a group with one norm, then swap the members out one by one, replacing the old with the new — and the arbitrary figure passes down the chain of strangers, carried forward by people who never met the ones who made it. A truth spun out of nothing, internalised, and inherited.

## The origin

The work is Muzafer Sherif's, a Turkish-born social psychologist who came to the United States in the late 1920s and completed his doctorate at Columbia. He published the autokinetic studies first in 1935, in a monograph in the *Archives of Psychology* titled *A Study of Some Social Factors in Perception*, and folded them into his landmark 1936 book *The Psychology of Social Norms*. Sherif was after something large. He wanted an experimental account of where social norms — customs, standards, shared expectations, the whole invisible scaffolding of a culture — actually come from. Not where a particular custom came from, but the general fact that groups generate common frames of reference at all.

He needed a task with no built-in answer, so that whatever agreement emerged could only have come from the people themselves. The autokinetic effect was ideal precisely because it is an illusion resting on nothing. Sherif's insight was that if he could watch a norm being born in the laboratory — a shared standard crystallising in real time out of pure ambiguity — he would have a working model of the process that, scaled up and slowed down across generations, produces the norms a whole society lives inside without noticing.

The generational-transmission extension came later, from Robert Jacobs and Donald Campbell, whose 1961 paper in the *Journal of Abnormal and Social Psychology*, "The perpetuation of an arbitrary tradition through several generations of a laboratory microculture," ran the experiment as a relay. They planted an inflated norm using confederates, then removed one real participant and added a fresh one each round, and watched how many "generations" the seeded figure survived after its originators were gone. It is one of the most quietly radical experiments in social psychology: a laboratory image of tradition itself.

## The mechanism

The engine here has a name, and naming it is the whole point of pairing this chapter with the one before it. Sherif's participants are converging by *informational influence* — they treat other people's judgments as evidence about the world. This is not caving to pressure, not fear of standing out, not wanting to be liked. It is something more basic and, in its place, entirely rational. When you truly do not know an answer and have no independent way to check, the estimates of others around you are data. They are, in fact, the only data you have. So you weight them, and they weight yours, and the collective judgment settles toward a common value the way a set of coupled pendulums settles into a shared swing.

Three gears turn in sequence. First, ambiguity: the task must have no external anchor, or people would simply consult the anchor and ignore each other. Second, convergence: with nothing else to go on, each person adjusts toward the others' figures, and because everyone adjusts, the estimates collapse inward to a norm that is a rough average of where they began — a value that belongs to the group and to no individual. Third, and this is the twist that makes it more than a story about pressure, *internalisation*: the norm stops feeling like other people's opinion and becomes the person's own perception. That is why it survives the removal of the group. Nobody is watching when the tested-alone participant still reports the group's number. There is no one left to conform to. The norm has been absorbed as simple fact.

Internalisation is also what lets the norm outlive its makers. Because each member holds the standard as their own belief rather than as deference to present company, they transmit it to a newcomer as *the truth* — this is how far the light moves — and the newcomer, facing the same ambiguity, has every reason to take it as evidence. The norm becomes a self-perpetuating trail: each generation lays it down for the next, long after the surface that first bore it is gone. It is a norm behaving exactly like stigmergy (item 4), where the trace left in the environment, not the individual who left it, coordinates everyone who comes after.

## The anchor example

Hold the two images side by side, because together they are the memory hook. The first is the light itself: a dot in a dark room, dead still, that a lone observer swears is drifting, and that a small group — talking, listening, adjusting — talks into a single agreed drift. Ask them afterward and they will not say they compromised or went along. They will tell you what the light *did*, with the flat confidence of someone reporting a measurement. They have not lied. They have perceived a shared world into being.

The second image is the one that should keep you up. Jacobs and Campbell seed a group's norm high — say, the light appears to move some large distance — using confederates who confidently report the inflated figure. Then the confederates leave, one per round, each replaced by a genuine newcomer who knows nothing. The naive arrivals absorb the inflated norm from the people already there and, on their turn, pass it to the next arrival. Generation follows generation. Every single person who established the norm is eventually gone, and yet the figure persists in a room full of strangers, none of whom chose it, all of whom now believe it, each faithfully handing it to the next. An arbitrary tradition, carried forward entirely by people who inherited rather than made it — which is the plainest possible model of nearly every tradition any of us has ever lived inside.

## The flip

Here is where Sherif turns the wise crowd inside out. Recall the founding optimistic image of this whole series: Galton's crowd at the country fair, whose averaged guesses at the ox's weight landed within a pound of the truth (item 17). That was aggregation delivering wisdom — many independent errors cancelling around a real value. But Galton's crowd had an ox. There was a fact of the matter, a genuine external anchor toward which the errors could cancel. Sherif's crowd has *no ox*. The light does not move. And when there is no truth to track, the very same act of pooling judgments does not converge on reality, because there is no reality out there to converge on. It converges on itself. The agreement is real; the thing agreed upon is manufactured.

This is the thesis in one of its sharpest forms. **The wise crowd and the mob are the same crowd; the sign flips on whether there is an external truth to anchor to.** Give the convergence a real target and you get collective wisdom. Remove the target and the identical mechanism gives you pure social construction — a shared certainty about nothing, indistinguishable from the inside from a shared certainty about something. The crowd cannot tell the difference. Convergence *feels* like truth whether or not any truth is present.

That is exactly why Sherif is the informational twin of Asch (item 35). Asch's line task was unambiguous — one line plainly matched — so when his subjects gave the wrong answer they were yielding to *normative* influence: they knew the truth and buckled to the group anyway. Sherif's task is ambiguous, so his subjects converge by *informational* influence: they truly do not know, and use each other as evidence. Two experiments, two crowds, two mechanisms — and the formal distinction between them, drawn out by Deutsch and Gerard, is the hinge of item 41. Sherif's mechanism reappears wherever people read certainty off one another: in social proof (item 39), where the actions of others become evidence about what to do; in pluralistic ignorance (item 42), where a whole room privately doubts a norm each person believes everyone else accepts; and, at the largest scale, in cultural evolution (item 80), where norms are transmitted down the generations exactly as they are down a Jacobs-Campbell chain. The Shape here is a truth forming out of the dark — and holding, and being handed on.

## The human weight

This is high frontier, and it should be read slowly. Most of the social order any of us inhabits is not a set of independently verified facts. It is a dense inheritance of norms — how close to stand, whom to defer to, what is shameful, what is obvious, what "everyone knows" — absorbed in ambiguity, internalised as perception, and received from people who received them from people now gone. Sherif built the laboratory model of precisely this. His participants did not experience the group norm as a convention they had adopted; they experienced it as how the light moves. That is the mechanism by which arbitrary social arrangements acquire the felt quality of self-evident truth. A caste rule, a taboo, a "that is simply how things are done" — each can begin as one group's contingent settling and, through internalisation and transmission, come to feel like a fact about the world rather than a decision anyone ever made.

The Jacobs-Campbell finding is the darkest and most precise part of the picture, because it isolates *heritability*. An arbitrary norm outliving every person who created it is not a metaphor for tradition; it is tradition, reproduced in miniature. And it explains something the frontier of this series keeps circling — why inherited hierarchies feel natural to those inside them (item 85). Stratification does not persist only because it is enforced. It persists partly because each generation absorbs its ranking as simple perceived reality from a generation that did the same, back and back, until the origin is lost and only the self-evidence remains. The rules feel not like impositions but like the shape of the world. Sherif shows, in a dark room with a still light, how a shared reality with no external warrant can become invisible to the very people living inside it — which is the beginning of understanding how such orders sustain themselves, and, eventually, how they might break.

## The critique

Restraint is owed here, because the temptation to over-read Sherif is strong. Start with the task: the autokinetic effect is a genuine illusion, and a deliberately impoverished one, chosen precisely because it has no anchor. That is its experimental virtue and its interpretive hazard. Almost no real judgment is *that* unmoored. Most of what groups converge on has at least partial external constraint, and where a real anchor exists the informational mechanism can pull toward truth rather than away from it. It is a mistake — one this chapter has tried to guard against — to conclude that *all* norms are as arbitrary as Sherif's light. Many track real coordination problems: which side of the road to drive on has no cosmically correct answer, but it is not arbitrary in the sense that matters, because coordinating on *some* side is really better than none. The frightening cases are the ones with the form of Sherif's task and the stakes of Asch's world.

The transmission finding needs its own caveat, and honesty requires it. The arbitrary norm in the generational chains does not persist forever. Across successive generations of new members it tends to *erode* — drifting back toward the range individuals would produce on their own, the seeded distortion decaying as the confederates' influence recedes. Tradition, in the laboratory, is not immortal; it fades toward baseline judgment. This nuances rather than negates the point: an arbitrary norm can survive well past its makers, but the further it sits from what unaided individuals would independently converge on, the harder it is to hold in place across generations — a hopeful detail for anyone thinking about which false certainties are heritable and which are not. *The exact number of generations of persistence, and the rate of decay, are specifics I have stated only in general terms here — further reading to verify before final publication.*

Finally, the methods are of their period: small samples, participant pools of their time and place, and the informational-versus-normative line that is far cleaner in the textbook than in any actual room, where genuine uncertainty and social pressure almost always arrive tangled together. Sherif's studies replicate well as demonstrations — the effect is robust and easy to reproduce — but their real value is as a clarifying limit case, not a full theory of culture. They show, with unusual purity, one thing: that when the world offers no independent signal, a crowd will not stay silent. It will make one, believe it, and pass it on.
