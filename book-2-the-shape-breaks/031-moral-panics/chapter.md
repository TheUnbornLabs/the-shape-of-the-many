# 31. Moral panics

> A society, frightened, turns and names one of its own the source of the fear — and the naming becomes the crisis it claimed to describe.

## The idea

Every so often a society decides, more or less all at once, that some condition, person, or group has become a threat to its very values and its very survival — and mobilises against it with an intensity wildly out of proportion to anything the threat has actually done. A minor scuffle becomes evidence of a generation gone feral. A handful of unproven allegations becomes proof of a hidden conspiracy of abusers. A style of dress, a genre of film, a category of stranger, is suddenly not a nuisance but a menace, and to say otherwise is to side with the menace. This is a moral panic: a period of collective alarm in which a group is cast as a folk devil — the enemy against whom the frightened community defines its own goodness — and hostility toward that folk devil rises far faster and far higher than the facts can justify.

The shape of the thing is what makes it recognisable across centuries and cultures. It is not simply that people become afraid; fear is ordinary. It is that the fear is disproportionate, that it fixes on a stereotyped and usually vulnerable target, that it is amplified through channels of communication until nearly everyone seems to share it, and that it then subsides almost as suddenly as it rose, often leaving behind new laws and hardened attitudes that outlast the alarm which produced them. A moral panic is a society's fear turned into a weapon and pointed, with remarkable precision, at whichever group is least able to fire back.

## The origin

The phrase entered the social sciences through the sociologist Stanley Cohen, whose 1972 book *Folk Devils and Moral Panics* took as its subject something that looks, from a distance, almost trivial: clashes between two youth subcultures, the Mods and the Rockers, at English seaside towns over the bank-holiday weekends of the mid-1960s. Cohen had watched how a series of scuffles and broken deckchairs at resorts like Clacton and Brighton in 1964 was reported, and he was struck less by what the young people did than by what the newspapers made of it. The disturbances were real but small. The coverage was enormous, lurid, and self-multiplying, and out of it grew a national conviction that British youth had turned violent and that the fabric of society was under assault.

Cohen gave the pattern a name and an anatomy. A condition, he wrote, "emerges to become defined as a threat to societal values and interests"; its nature is "presented in a stylized and stereotypical fashion by the mass media"; moral barricades are manned by editors, bishops, and politicians; experts pronounce; and then the condition "disappears, submerges or deteriorates," though sometimes it leaves lasting marks on law and policy. He drew on the labelling tradition in the sociology of deviance, and on the work of his contemporary Jock Young, whose 1971 study of drug-taking, *The Drugtakers*, had already used the idea of amplification — the way that the reaction to deviance can enlarge the very thing it reacts to.

Two later contributions sharpened the concept. In 1978, Stuart Hall and his collaborators published *Policing the Crisis*, a study of the panic over "mugging" in early-1970s Britain, arguing that the alarm was not a spontaneous public mood but an ideological event — a way of displacing anxieties about a society in economic and political trouble onto the racialised figure of the street robber, and of licensing a harder, more authoritarian style of policing. And in 1994, Erich Goode and Nachman Ben-Yehuda, in *Moral Panics: The Social Construction of Deviance*, offered the criteria that have since become standard. A moral panic, they proposed, is marked by five things: heightened **concern** over the behaviour of a group; **hostility** toward that group, which is cast as an enemy; a broad **consensus** that the threat is real and serious; **disproportionality**, meaning that the concern outstrips the actual danger; and **volatility**, the tendency to erupt suddenly and vanish just as fast.

## The mechanism

The gears of a moral panic turn in a sequence that, once seen, is hard to unsee.

It begins with a triggering incident — usually real, usually minor, sometimes barely more than a rumour. A brawl on a beach; an allegation in a custody dispute; a crime that would ordinarily be one item among many. What matters is not the incident's size but its availability as a symbol. The second gear is amplification: the incident is reported, and the reporting selects, sharpens, and repeats. Coverage feeds on coverage. Editors, sensing an appetite, commission more; each new story cites the accumulating others as evidence that a pattern exists; and the sheer volume of attention becomes, in the public mind, a measure of the threat's size. A thing that is everywhere in the newspaper feels like a thing that is everywhere.

The third gear is the folk devil itself — the compression of a diffuse anxiety into a single stereotyped figure. The Mod, the mugger, the Satanist, the pusher: a caricature detailed enough to be recognised and hated, vague enough to absorb whatever the moment requires it to mean. The fourth gear supplies the moral voltage: moral entrepreneurs and claims-makers, in Howard Becker's phrase — the campaigners, officials, clergy, and pundits who step forward to name the evil, to insist that something be done, and to gain, in the naming, authority and audience. Their claims raise public concern past any level the underlying facts would support; and that concern, in the fifth gear, is answered by control. Laws tighten. Police are deployed. Sentences lengthen. Institutions that live by responding to threats find, in the panic, both a mandate and a budget.

And here the mechanism closes on itself, because the tightening of control produces more of the very deviance it targets — more arrests, more prosecutions, more reported cases — which the media then report as proof that the threat was real and growing all along. The reaction manufactures its own evidence. Young called this the amplification spiral, and it is the engine that lets a small disturbance inflate into a crisis. Then, as suddenly as it began, the panic fades. The appetite is sated, a fresher story arrives, the folk devil is retired — and the laws remain.

## The anchor example

Set the clock back to 1983, in Manhattan Beach, California, where an allegation against a preschool called McMartin swelled into one of the strangest collective frights of the twentieth century: the satanic ritual abuse panic that ran through the United States, Britain, and beyond across the 1980s and into the 1990s.

The trigger was a single mother's report that her son had been abused at the McMartin Preschool — an allegation that investigators pursued through interviews with dozens of children. But the interviewing techniques, later widely criticised, were suggestive: children were asked leading questions, praised for lurid answers, and pressed until the stories grew. And the stories did grow — into accounts of secret tunnels, sacrificed animals, robed figures, and rituals of a kind no physical evidence ever supported. What made this a panic rather than a single misguided prosecution was the amplification. Talk shows, bestselling books, and a circuit of self-appointed experts spread a template of what "ritual abuse" looked like, and once the template existed, it could be found anywhere. Therapists trained to detect it detected it. Police seminars taught its signs. The folk devil here was the most alarming one imaginable — an organised, hidden network of Satan-worshipping abusers of children — and precisely because it was hidden, the absence of evidence could be read as proof of how well it hid.

The McMartin trial became the longest and among the costliest criminal proceedings in American history to that point, and it ended without a single conviction. Later investigations — including a major review commissioned by the FBI's Kenneth Lanning — found no substantiated evidence of the organised satanic networks that thousands of cases had alleged. Yet careers were destroyed, families shattered, and people imprisoned on testimony that would not survive scrutiny. The threat, as described, had essentially not existed. The panic, and its wreckage, had been entirely real. That is the signature of the form: the folk devil is a phantom, and the damage is not.

## The flip

Strip a moral panic to its machinery and it resolves into two mechanisms this series treats elsewhere, welded together and aimed. It is an **availability cascade** (item 27) — a self-reinforcing process in which a belief spreads because its rising public prevalence makes it seem ever more plausible, each repetition raising the next person's estimate of its truth — fused with **emotional contagion** (item 33), the involuntary spread of an affective state, here fear and moral outrage, from person to person through a crowd. The cascade supplies the belief; the contagion supplies the heat; and the two together are pointed at a scapegoat. What distinguishes a panic from ordinary opinion is that target: the energy does not disperse but concentrates on a folk devil, in a way that draws on the loss of individual restraint this book calls **deindividuation** (item 32) — the anonymity of the aroused crowd, which lowers the threshold for hostility that no single member would voice alone.

This is the thesis of the whole series in one of its sharpest forms. The wise crowd and the mob are the same people. The very faculty that lets a society mobilise its collective moral attention against a genuine harm — the movement that at last names a real injustice and refuses to look away — is the same faculty that, under different conditions, produces a panic. There is one mechanism of shared moral arousal and one dial that sets its sign. The dial is **proportionality**, and behind proportionality stands a second reading: whether dissent is possible. A justified collective reckoning tracks the size of the harm and can survive being questioned; its claims invite evidence. A panic outruns the harm and punishes the question; to ask for proof is treated as complicity with the devil. When those two conditions fail — when concern floats free of evidence and doubt becomes dangerous to voice — the same moral energy that could have corrected an injustice curdles into one.

The condition that seals a panic is therefore the one this book keeps returning to. Once the folk devil is established and the hostility is high, the fear of being named next silences the doubters. This is **preference falsification** (item 34) operating in real time: people who privately suspect the alarm is overblown say nothing, because to dissent is to invite suspicion of sympathy with the enemy — and their silence is read by everyone else as agreement, which is the false **consensus** Goode and Ben-Yehuda listed among the defining marks. A panic is thus close cousin to a **mania or delusion** (item 30), its financial and epidemic twins; where a mania fixes the crowd's shared conviction on a bubble, a panic fixes it on a scapegoat.

One caution belongs in this beat, because the concept contains its own trap. The label "moral panic" is contested, and it can be used to dismiss legitimate concern as readily as to diagnose a false one. The same episode looks like a panic to those who think the fear disproportionate and like an overdue reckoning to those who think it earned. The sign is not always obvious from inside. That symmetry is not a flaw to be argued away; it is the whole difficulty, and it returns in the critique.

## The human weight

Moral panics sit squarely on the frontier this series was built to map: they are among the most reliable tools by which a collective is mobilised against its own, and by which an existing order reproduces itself. This is the high-stakes reading, and it is not conspiratorial — it is structural. A society under strain generates anxiety it cannot easily name or resolve: economic fear, status insecurity, the vertigo of rapid change. That anxiety is diffuse and unbearable, and a folk devil gives it a face. To fear the Satanist, the mugger, the deviant, is far more tolerable than to fear an economy or a future, because the folk devil can be arrested, banned, punished — the anxiety can be *acted on*. This is why panics so often erupt in periods of dislocation, and why *Policing the Crisis* read the mugging scare not as a lie invented by cynics but as a displacement: a real social crisis discharged onto a manageable, and racialised, target.

Two consequences follow, and both reproduce the order that produced the panic. First, the target is almost never chosen at random. The folk devil is typically drawn from a group already marginal — the young, the poor, a religious or ethnic minority, a sexual out-group — a group with little power to answer the charge and much to lose from it. The panic redirects the majority's fear downward and outward, onto those least able to resist, which is exactly how caste and communal panics have always worked: a rumour, a lurid claim, a demand that *something be done*, and a vulnerable community bears the collective anxiety of a society that will not look at its own foundations. Second, the panic licenses expanded control. New laws, new police powers, new surveillance are demanded in the emergency and kept after it, and they rarely bear only on the folk devil. The machinery of control, once built, remains available for other uses. A panic is thus a mechanism by which a **stratified** order (item 85) renews the boundary between the respectable and the dangerous, and by which fear is converted into conformity: deviance is made not merely disreputable but dangerous, so that stepping out of line risks being cast as the next devil. In this the panic shades into **propaganda** (item 82) — not always top-down and orchestrated, but often bottom-up and then seized, amplified, and steered by those it serves.

The even-handedness the series demands must be held here most carefully, because the label cuts both ways, and the same instinct that exposes a scapegoating panic can, misused, protect a genuine predator. Some alarms are proportionate. Some folk-devil framings are simply true — the abuser, the demagogue, the polluter can be real, and the collective attention that brings them down is the wise crowd doing its work. The frontier reading is a warning about a mechanism, not a verdict on any particular fear. What it insists on is this: whenever a society mobilises against a group with a fervour that punishes doubt, the burden of proof rises rather than falls, precisely because that fervour is the sign under which orders have always devoured their own.

## The critique

The concept's deepest problem is the one its own success created. "Moral panic" has escaped the seminar room and become a rhetorical weapon, deployed to wave away any public alarm the speaker happens to disagree with. To call a concern a panic is now, too often, to end the argument rather than to begin an inquiry — a way of asserting that the worried are irrational without having to show that they are wrong. Cohen himself, in later editions of his book, reflected on how the term had been stretched and cheapened, and worried that it had become a lazy label. The trouble is symmetrical and it is real: sometimes the concern is proportionate, the threat is genuine, and it is the "panic" framing that is the error, a bit of sociological condescension used to dismiss ordinary people's defence of things they rightly value.

That points to the concept's hardest technical weakness: disproportionality, the criterion that does most of the work, requires knowing the true size of the risk — and the true size of a risk is exactly what is usually unknown, especially early, when the alarm is loudest and a genuine response would matter most. To declare a reaction disproportionate is to claim a knowledge of the real danger that the analyst rarely possesses any more securely than the frightened public does. Some of the most consequential threats looked, at first, like panics to sober observers; some genuine panics looked, at first, like prudence. The label is easiest to apply in hindsight, when the outcome is known — which is precisely when it is least useful as a guide to action.

There is a further charge, pressed from within sociology: that the classic concept can be condescending, treating the moral concerns of ordinary people as false consciousness to be explained away rather than positions to be argued with, and quietly assuming that the analyst's own values are the neutral baseline against which others' "panic" is measured. And the model's origins are narrow — a particular British press, a particular period, a particular set of media institutions — so that its account of amplification, built for the age of national newspapers, transfers only imperfectly to a fragmented, networked information landscape where panics can now form and dissolve without any central gatekeeper at all.

None of this dissolves the concept; the pattern Cohen named is real, recurrent, and sometimes lethal to the innocent. But it demands the discipline the series asks of every idea in it. The label diagnoses a mechanism, not a verdict. The presence of fear, even loud and spreading fear, does not settle whether the fear is earned. Only proportionality and the survival of dissent can do that — and both are harder to read from inside the crowd than anyone caught in one would like to believe.
