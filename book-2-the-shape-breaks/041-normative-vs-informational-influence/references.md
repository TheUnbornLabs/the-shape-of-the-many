# References — 41. Normative versus informational influence

## Confirmed sources

- Deutsch, Morton, and Harold B. Gerard. "A study of normative and informational social influences upon individual judgment." *Journal of Abnormal and Social Psychology*, vol. 51, no. 3, 1955, pp. 629–636. — The founding paper; names and experimentally separates the two influences, and introduces the anonymity/private-response manipulation that removes normative but not informational influence.
- Asch, Solomon E. "Studies of independence and conformity: I. A minority of one against a unanimous majority." *Psychological Monographs: General and Applied*, vol. 70, no. 9, 1956, pp. 1–70. — The line-judgment experiments; the post-experiment interviews distinguishing conformers who came to perceive the lines the majority's way (informational) from those who knew the majority was wrong and complied to avoid standing out (normative). Cross-referenced as item 35.
- Sherif, Muzafer. *The Psychology of Social Norms*. Harper & Brothers, 1936. — The autokinetic-effect studies; convergence on a shared norm under genuine perceptual ambiguity, the paradigm case of informational influence. Cross-referenced as item 36.
- Turner, John C. *Social Influence*. Open University Press / Brooks/Cole, 1991. — The self-categorization / social-identity critique; argues the normative–informational dichotomy is too clean and introduces referent informational influence, in which conformity to in-group norms blurs the two. Basis of the critique beat.

## Further reading to verify before final publication

- Deutsch & Gerard 1955: confirmed — *Journal of Abnormal and Social Psychology*, vol. 51, no. 3, pp. 629–636 (DOI 10.1037/h0046408).
- The specific proportions and precise wording of Asch's post-experiment interview categories (subjects who "came to see" the lines the majority's way vs. those who knowingly complied) — verify against Asch 1956 monograph; the two-camp characterisation is well attested but the exact distribution should be checked before quoting figures.
- The precise design and magnitude of the conformity reduction under Deutsch & Gerard's anonymous/private-response condition — verify the reported effect sizes and experimental conditions against the original paper before citing any number.
- Turner *Social Influence* (1991): confirmed — Open University Press (Milton Keynes); US edition Brooks/Cole (Mapping Social Psychology series). "Referent informational influence" and the self-categorization reinterpretation of the normative/informational dichotomy are correctly attributed.
- The claim that most (rather than merely many) Asch conformers were normatively rather than informationally driven — well supported by the literature but stated here as a general characterisation; verify no overstatement before final pass.
