# References — 33. Emotional contagion

## Confirmed sources

- Hatfield, Elaine; Cacioppo, John T.; Rapson, Richard L. "Emotional Contagion." *Current Directions in Psychological Science*, Vol. 2, No. 3, 1993, pp. 96–100. — The article that named and framed the modern concept as a primitive, automatic mimicry-feedback process distinct from deliberate empathy.
- Hatfield, Elaine; Cacioppo, John T.; Rapson, Richard L. *Emotional Contagion*. Cambridge University Press / Éditions de la Maison des Sciences de l'Homme, 1994. — The book-length statement of the mechanism; source for the mimicry → feedback → convergence account and the primitive-vs-reflective-empathy distinction.
- Kramer, Adam D. I.; Guillory, Jamie E.; Hancock, Jeffrey T. "Experimental evidence of massive-scale emotional contagion through social networks." *Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences (PNAS)*, Vol. 111, No. 24, 2014, pp. 8788–8790. — The Facebook News Feed experiment on 689,003 users; the anchor example. Note the *Editorial Expression of Concern* subsequently published by PNAS regarding informed consent.
- Strack, Fritz; Martin, Leonard L.; Stepper, Sabine. "Inhibiting and facilitating conditions of the human smile: A nonobtrusive test of the facial feedback hypothesis." *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*, Vol. 54, No. 5, 1988, pp. 768–777. — The classic pen-in-mouth study underpinning the facial-feedback hypothesis; cited in origin and critique.
- Wagenmakers, E.-J.; et al. "Registered Replication Report: Strack, Martin, & Stepper (1988)." *Perspectives on Psychological Science*, Vol. 11, No. 6, 2016, pp. 917–928. — The seventeen-laboratory replication that failed to reproduce the 1988 facial-feedback effect; the load-bearing replication-humility source for the critique.
- Smith, Adam. *The Theory of Moral Sentiments*. 1759. — Opening observation on involuntary fellow-feeling (flinching at another's coming blow); cited as the historical antecedent.
- Durkheim, Émile. *The Elementary Forms of Religious Life*. 1912. — Source for "collective effervescence," cross-referenced (item 76) as the good-pole reading of the same convergence-of-feeling mechanism.
