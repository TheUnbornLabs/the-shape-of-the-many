# References — 32. Deindividuation

## Confirmed sources

- Le Bon, Gustave. *Psychologie des foules* (*The Crowd: A Study of the Popular Mind*). Félix Alcan, 1895. — Origin of the "mental unity of crowds" and the notion that the crowd submerges the conscious individual; the pre-scientific template for deindividuation, cited here as the discredited "beast within" pole.
- Festinger, Leon; Pepitone, Albert; Newcomb, Theodore. "Some consequences of de-individuation in a group." *Journal of Abnormal and Social Psychology*, 47(2), 1952, 382–389. — Coins the term "deindividuation"; the reduced-identifiability / freer hostile-expression finding.
- Zimbardo, Philip G. "The human choice: Individuation, reason, and order versus deindividuation, impulse, and chaos." *Nebraska Symposium on Motivation*, vol. 17, University of Nebraska Press, 1969, 237–307. — The anonymity / arousal / diffused-responsibility input model and the reduced-self-observation account.
- Diener, Edward; Fraser, Scott C.; Beaman, Arthur L.; Kelem, Roger T. "Effects of deindividuation variables on stealing among Halloween trick-or-treaters." *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*, 33(2), 1976, 178–183. — The anchor example: anonymous and grouped children stole more candy; being asked one's name restored restraint.
- Reicher, Stephen D.; Spears, Russell; Postmes, Tom. "A social identity model of deindividuation phenomena." *European Review of Social Psychology*, 6(1), 1995, 161–198. — The SIDE model: anonymity shifts the self from personal to social identity and heightens conformity to the salient group norm rather than releasing antisocial impulse. The central revision of beat 7.
- Le Texier, Thibault. "Debunking the Stanford Prison Experiment." *American Psychologist*, 74(7), 2019, 823–839. — Archival evidence of coaching, demand characteristics, and lack of experimental control in the 1971 Stanford Prison Experiment.
- Reicher, Stephen D.; Haslam, S. Alexander. "Rethinking the psychology of tyranny: The BBC Prison Study." *British Journal of Social Psychology*, 45(1), 2006, 1–40. — The restaged prison study reaching conclusions opposed to Zimbardo's; tyranny as contingent on identification with role norms, not passive role-drift.
