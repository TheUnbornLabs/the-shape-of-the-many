# 37. Milgram's obedience studies

> The distance between an ordinary person and an instrument of harm is often just one voice in a lab coat saying *the experiment requires that you continue.*

## The idea

Put an ordinary person in a room and give them a task: teach another person a list of word pairs, and every time the learner gets one wrong, punish the mistake with an electric shock. The shocks start small — 15 volts, a faint tingle — but each error moves the dial one notch higher, and the notches run all the way to 450 volts, past a bank of switches labelled *Danger: Severe Shock* and, at the very end, a mute and ominous *XXX*. The learner, strapped in another room, begins to protest. He grunts, then complains, then cries out, then screams, then bangs on the wall, then falls silent. And beside the teacher sits a calm man in a grey coat who, whenever the teacher hesitates, says only: *Please continue. The experiment requires that you continue. It is absolutely essential that you continue. You have no other choice, you must go on.*

The question the setup asks is brutally simple. How far up the dial will an ordinary person go, not because they are cruel, not because they hate the learner, not because they gain anything, but because a person they have accepted as the legitimate authority in the room keeps telling them to? The finding that made Stanley Milgram famous, and infamous, is that a large fraction of ordinary people go all the way to the end. Under the baseline conditions of his experiment, roughly two-thirds of participants pushed every switch to the final 450 volts. They did not do it happily. They sweated, trembled, argued, laughed in horror, begged to stop — and then, when told to continue, continued. Milgram had built a machine for measuring the point at which conscience yields to command, and found the point far higher than anyone predicted.

## The origin

Stanley Milgram was a young social psychologist at Yale when he ran the studies, between 1961 and 1963, in a laboratory in Linsly-Chittenden Hall. He published the first and most cited account, "Behavioral Study of Obedience," in the *Journal of Abnormal and Social Psychology* in 1963, and the full programme — twenty-four variations on the basic design — eventually in the 1974 book *Obedience to Authority: An Experimental View*. Participants were recruited by newspaper advertisement from the ordinary population of New Haven, paid a small fee, and told they were taking part in a study of memory and learning. The rest was theatre. The "learner" was a confederate, a mild-mannered accountant named in Milgram's accounts, who received no shocks at all; his cries were recorded on tape, played the same way each time so that every teacher heard the same escalation. The shock generator was a real and convincing prop, thirty switches in a row, and each teacher was given a genuine sample 45-volt jolt at the start so that they would not doubt the machine.

The question behind the theatre was not academic. Milgram, the son of Jewish immigrants, ran the studies in the shadow of the Holocaust, and the trial of Adolf Eichmann was unfolding in Jerusalem as he designed them. Eichmann, the bureaucrat who had organised the transport of millions to the death camps, had defended himself as a mere functionary following orders — and Hannah Arendt, reporting the trial for *The New Yorker*, coined the phrase that would haunt the century in her 1963 book *Eichmann in Jerusalem: A Report on the Banality of Evil*. Milgram wanted to know whether the banality was general — whether the capacity to inflict grave harm on command was a peculiarity of a broken regime or a latent feature of ordinary human beings anywhere. He built the New Haven laboratory to find out how much of Germany was already present in Connecticut.

## The mechanism

Milgram's own explanation was that people enter what he called the *agentic state*. In everyday life we act as autonomous agents, judging our own conduct against our own conscience. But under legitimate authority, Milgram argued, a person can shift into a different mode: they stop seeing themselves as the author of their actions and start seeing themselves as an instrument carrying out another's will. Responsibility is handed upward. The teacher pressing the switch does not feel that *he* is hurting the learner; he feels that he is doing his job, and that the consequences belong to the experimenter who gave the order. Guilt does not disappear — the trembling proves it does not — but it is relocated. The moral question shifts from *is this right?* to *am I performing my assigned role well?*

Two structural features push people into that state and keep them there. The first is gradual escalation, the foot-in-the-door: no one is ever asked to deliver 450 volts to a screaming man cold. They are asked for 15 volts, which is nothing, then 30, then 45, each step only fractionally worse than the one already taken and already justified. By the time the shocks are dangerous, refusing means repudiating everything you have already done, and the psychological cost of that reversal climbs with every switch. The second is the architecture of authority and distance. Milgram's variations showed that obedience was not a fixed human constant but a dial responsive to physical arrangement. When the experimenter gave orders by telephone from another room rather than sitting beside the teacher, obedience fell sharply. When the learner was moved into the same room, or when the teacher had to press the learner's hand onto a shock plate, obedience fell again. Proximity to the authority raised compliance; proximity to the victim lowered it. The moral gears turned on how close the harm felt and how close the commander stood.

## The anchor example

The thing to hold in memory is the apparatus itself and the four sentences. Picture the shock generator: a long grey panel, thirty toggle switches in a horizontal row, each with a voltage printed beneath it rising in fifteen-volt steps from 15 to 450. Above the switches, verbal designations march from *Slight Shock* through *Moderate*, *Strong*, *Very Strong*, *Intense*, *Extreme Intensity*, *Danger: Severe Shock*, and finally two switches marked only *XXX*. A red light flashes and a buzzer sounds with each throw of a switch. The teacher reads a word, the learner gives a wrong answer through the wall, and the teacher is required to announce the voltage aloud before administering it — *three hundred volts* — so that there is no hiding from the number.

And when the teacher stops, as nearly all of them did at some point, the experimenter does not argue, does not threaten, does not raise his voice. He delivers, in sequence, exactly four scripted prods. *Please continue.* If the teacher still balks: *The experiment requires that you continue.* Then: *It is absolutely essential that you continue.* And finally: *You have no other choice, you must go on.* There is no penalty attached to refusal — the experimenter cannot fine, arrest, or fail the participant, and everyone knows it. The authority is naked, backed by nothing but a grey coat and the setting of a famous university. And yet, prod after prod, a majority of ordinary New Haven adults picked the switch back up. The horror of the studies is not that a monster obeyed. It is that a schoolteacher, an engineer, a salesman obeyed, sweating and protesting, over a barrier made of four calm sentences.

## The flip

If Asch (item 35) mapped the horizontal axis of collective behaviour — the sideways pressure of *peers*, of equals whose unanimous agreement bends what we are willing to say — then Milgram mapped the vertical one. This is deference not to the many beside us but to the one above us: obedience to *authority*. The two axes are the coordinate system of social influence, and this book needs both. Asch shows that we will deny the evidence of our eyes to stay in step with a group; Milgram shows that we will override the protest of our conscience to stay in step with a command. Together they describe how a person can be moved off their own judgment from two directions at once.

And here is the thesis in its starkest human form: **the deference that lets societies function and the deference that produces atrocity are the same deference.** We obey legitimate authority constantly, and we must — a society in which no one followed instructions from doctors, pilots, engineers, or elected officials would not be a free society but a rubble heap. The agentic state that let Milgram's teachers hand responsibility upward is the same faculty that lets a nurse administer a drug she did not prescribe or a soldier hold fire on command. The wise crowd and the mob are one crowd; the obedient citizen and the obedient functionary of an atrocity are one person. The sign is set entirely by what the authority commands and by whether conscience retains the power to interrupt. Point the same obedience toward a safe landing and it is civilisation; point it toward a gas chamber and it is the machinery of genocide, running on ordinary people.

But — and this is the finding that Milgram's popular legend usually amputates — the obedience was never blind. It was a variable, not a constant, and the most important variable of all was the presence of a fellow rebel. In the condition where the teacher worked alongside two confederate "peers" who themselves refused and walked out, obedience collapsed: the overwhelming majority of real participants followed the rebels and stopped. One person breaking ranks broke the spell for everyone watching. This is precisely Asch's disobedient ally (item 35), where a single dissenting voice restored people's freedom to trust their own eyes, and precisely the first follower (item 58), whose visible defection converts a lone refusenik into a movement others can join. The escape route out of destructive obedience is the same escape route out of destructive conformity: someone has to go first, and be seen.

## The human weight

The frontier this series keeps circling — how orders of imposed harm operate through ordinary people — runs directly through this laboratory. Oppressive systems do not run on monsters, because monsters are rare. They run on functionaries: the clerk who stamps the deportation order, the guard who follows the rota, the official who enforces the rule he privately doubts. Milgram's darkest contribution is the demonstration that the supply of such functionaries is not limited to the cruel. It is drawn from the general population, and it is large. This connects to the machinery of silence that this book keeps naming. Preference falsification (item 34) is obeying an authority *publicly* against one's private conscience — saying the loyal words, giving the salute, while believing otherwise — and deindividuation (item 32) strips away the individual identity that might have resisted; both lower the cost of doing what one is told and raise the cost of refusing. Diffusion of responsibility (item 40) is the agentic state distributed across many hands, so that no single person feels themselves to be the author of the outcome. And when authority controls the informational field itself — propaganda and manufactured consent (item 82) — it does not merely command obedience but manufactures the belief that the command is legitimate, closing off the very conscience that might have interrupted.

Yet the same laboratory that reveals the danger also reveals the exit, and this is the hopeful edge of a grim finding. Because obedience was a dial and not a switch, it could be turned down. Move the authority away, bring the victim close, and above all let one visible ally refuse — and the majority who would have gone to 450 volts stopped instead. The corrective to a society of functionaries is not a better class of human being. It is a change in conditions, chief among them the presence of the first person willing to say *no* out loud where others can see. That is why refusal spreads the way it does, and why regimes built on obedience fear the single public defector far more than the private dissenter: one is a fact others can act on, the other is only a feeling. The escape from the agentic state is social before it is heroic.

## The critique

No classic study in social psychology has been more thoroughly reopened, and the reopening matters. The clean headline — *65% of people will obey to the point of killing a stranger* — is not what the archive supports, and this chapter should not pretend otherwise. That 65% figure comes from one condition, the "voice-feedback" baseline, out of twenty-four variations whose obedience rates ranged from nearly zero to over ninety per cent. There is no single Milgram number; there is a family of numbers that depend entirely on the arrangement, which is itself the study's deepest point and the thing the legend erases.

Gina Perry's 2012 book *Behind the Shock Machine*, built on Milgram's own archived tapes and papers at Yale, complicated the picture further. She documented that the experimenter frequently improvised well beyond the four scripted prods, cajoling and pressuring subjects in ways the published method conceals; that a substantial number of participants reported suspecting the shocks were not real, which would inflate apparent obedience; and that the debriefing and duty of care to distressed subjects fell far short of what Milgram's published accounts implied. The ethics alone would prevent the study today: participants were deceived, exposed to acute distress, and in some cases not promptly told the truth. That ethical wall is also why the study can barely be replicated — Jerry Burger's carefully constrained 2009 partial replication, published in *American Psychologist*, stopped at 150 volts (the point of the learner's first strong protest) for exactly this reason, and found obedience rates broadly comparable to Milgram's, which tells us the effect is real but leaves the full arc forever untested.

Even the mechanism is contested. Milgram's *agentic state* — the passive instrument surrendering its will — has been challenged by Stephen Reicher and Alexander Haslam's "engaged followership" account, which argues that participants did not become mindless tools at all. On this reading they continued because they actively identified with the experiment's scientific purpose, were persuaded that the work mattered, and chose to help a cause they had come to see as good — obedience driven by identification and belief, not by the evaporation of the self. If they are right, the lesson darkens rather than lifts: the functionaries of atrocity may not be sleepwalkers who lost their agency but willing believers who kept it and pointed it at the wrong goal. Either way, the sober conclusion holds and the crude one does not. Milgram did not prove that people are blindly obedient. He proved that obedience is disturbingly available, powerfully shaped by conditions, and — the hopeful half — reliably broken when someone else refuses first.
