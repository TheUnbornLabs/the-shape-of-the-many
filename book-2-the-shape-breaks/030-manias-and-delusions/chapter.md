# 30. Manias & delusions

> Point the same crowd at the truth and it finds an ox's weight to the pound; let it price the future by watching itself and it will pay any sum for a bulb, a share, or a scapegoat.

## The idea

There is a recurring pattern in human affairs in which a whole society, or a large enough part of one to matter, becomes gripped by a shared certainty that later looks like collective insanity. The tulip is worth a townhouse. The company will make everyone rich. The relic heals. The woman down the lane is a witch. For a season — sometimes a few years, sometimes a few months — the belief is not merely held but *acted on*, at scale, with money and reputation and occasionally lives staked on it, and then it breaks, and the survivors look back on their recent selves with something between embarrassment and horror.

A mania is a self-reinforcing belief that has detached from the thing it is supposedly about. What sustains it is not evidence but other people. The price of the tulip rises because it has been rising; the more it rises, the more plausible it becomes that it will keep rising, and the more people buy, which makes it rise. The witch is real because the neighbours believe she is real, and each fresh accusation confirms the danger that made accusation seem reasonable in the first place. The belief feeds on its own acceptance. It runs until the fuel of new believers runs out, and then it collapses, usually faster than it rose.

The thing to hold onto is that this is not a different *kind* of crowd from the wise one. It is the same crowd — the same faculty for pooling many judgments into one — running under a condition that has inverted its output. Where the wise crowd cancels its errors, the mad crowd correlates them.

## The origin

The founding popular text is Charles Mackay's *Extraordinary Popular Delusions and the Madness of Crowds*, published in London in 1841. Mackay was a Scottish journalist and songwriter, and the book reads like it: three volumes of gloriously told stories, sweeping across the Mississippi Scheme of John Law, the South Sea Bubble, Dutch tulipomania, the witch trials, the Crusades, alchemists, fortune-tellers, hauntings, and the slow-magnetism quacks. Its thesis is announced in one of the most quoted sentences in the literature of crowds: "Men, it has been well said, think in herds; it will be seen that they go mad in herds, while they only recover their senses slowly, and one by one." It is the ancestor of every later book on bubbles and panics, and it has never been out of print.

Its most important modern descendant is an economist's book: Charles Kindleberger's *Manias, Panics, and Crashes: A History of Financial Crises*, first published in 1978. Kindleberger, an economic historian at MIT, took the anecdotal sprawl of Mackay and gave it a spine — building on the work of Hyman Minsky (item 62) to lay out a recurring sequence: a *displacement* (some genuine new opportunity — a canal, a railway, a technology, a newly opened trade), followed by credit-fuelled *euphoria* and overtrading, a phase of *distress* as insiders quietly exit, and finally *revulsion* and panic as the crowd turns and the whole structure unwinds. Between them, Mackay and Kindleberger frame the field: the storyteller who named the phenomenon and the economist who gave it a mechanism.

## The mechanism

Strip a mania down and the same gears are turning underneath, whether the object is a bulb or a heretic.

The first gear is **social proof**. When we are uncertain — and the value of a novel asset or the guilt of an accused stranger is deeply uncertain — we treat other people's behaviour as information about what is true. If everyone is buying, buying must be sensible; if everyone is accusing, the danger must be real. This is entirely reasonable as a heuristic, which is why intelligent people fall into manias. The trouble is that when everyone is inferring the truth from everyone else, the crowd's belief loses its anchor in any independent fact. Each person is reading a signal that is itself composed of other people reading the signal.

The second gear is **reflexivity** — the belief changing the thing it is a belief about. A conviction that a stock's price will rise, acted on, *makes* the price rise, which appears to confirm the conviction. Ordinary observation assumes the world holds still while we measure it. In a mania the measurement moves the world. Rising prices are read as evidence of value, when they are only evidence of rising prices. This loop, named later by George Soros (item 65), is what lets a belief bootstrap itself into apparent reality.

The third gear is **emotional contagion** (item 33): fear and greed spread person to person like a physical infection, faster than argument, bypassing the slow machinery of independent judgment. A trading floor and a witch-panicked village are both rooms in which affect is passed around and amplified until it swamps whatever private doubts individuals brought in.

The whole assembly runs because these gears reinforce one another and, crucially, because they *destroy independence*. Each person, instead of forming a view and contributing it, forms their view by copying the aggregate — so the aggregate is copying itself. The system has positive feedback and no ballast, and positive feedback with no ballast has exactly two states: runaway and collapse.

## The anchor example

Take the South Sea Bubble of 1720, because it is the case Mackay tells and the sources actually support, and because it caught one of the most careful minds who ever lived.

The South Sea Company had been founded in 1711 and granted a monopoly on British trade with Spanish South America. The trade itself was thin and mostly notional. What the company really dealt in was a scheme to take over a large slice of the British national debt in exchange for shares — a financial engineering play dressed in the romance of distant gold. Through 1720 the share price left the ground entirely: from roughly £128 in January to around £1,000 by summer, on a wave of subscriptions, easy credit, and a swarm of imitator companies (one, legendarily, "for carrying on an undertaking of great advantage, but nobody to know what it is"). Then, in the autumn, it fell as fast as it had risen. Fortunes built on paper and margin evaporated. Ruin was widespread and, this time, real — the collapse triggered parliamentary investigation and a lasting suspicion of joint-stock companies.

Among those caught was Isaac Newton. He is reported to have sold early at a tidy profit, watched the price keep climbing, bought back in near the top, and lost a great deal — a sum often given as around £20,000. And he is supposed to have remarked that he "could calculate the motions of the heavenly bodies, but not the madness of people." That line is too good to trust uncritically: it is widely attributed to Newton but its earliest sourcing is late and shaky, and it should be flagged as apocryphal rather than quoted as fact. The loss itself is better attested than the epigram. Either way the image is exact — the man who had weighed the planets could not weigh the crowd, because the crowd's price was not a fact about the world but a fact about the crowd, and it was changing while he watched.

## The flip

This chapter is the direct dark twin of item 17, **Galton's ox**, and through it of item 18, **Condorcet's jury**. Those are the light poles of the whole series — the country-fair crowd whose averaged guess landed within a pound of a live ox's weight, and the mathematics showing that many independent, better-than-chance judgments converge on truth as they multiply. A mania is that identical machine with one wire cut.

Galton's crowd was wise because of two conditions. Its members guessed *independently* — each wrote down a number without seeing the others — so their individual errors pointed in every direction and cancelled in the average. And because the errors were uncorrelated, more guesses meant more cancellation, and the aggregate converged on the truth. A mania keeps the aggregation and destroys the independence. Nobody prices the tulip alone; everyone prices it by watching what others will pay. The errors, instead of scattering and cancelling, all lean the same way — because they are all copies of one another — and now more participants means more *correlation*, not more cancellation. The crowd converges, with the full statistical force of large numbers, on a shared delusion instead of on the truth. Same aggregation, errors correlated by social proof, sign inverted. That is the thesis of this book in a single mechanism: the wise crowd and the mob are the same people, and the flip condition is whether they judge independently or by watching each other.

The manias chapter is thus the hub of a whole cluster of the way independence dies. It is the macro-scale outcome of **information cascades** (item 26), where each rational actor copies the last and the chain carries almost no independent information; it is fed by **availability cascades** (item 27), where a belief becomes self-reinforcing through sheer repetition, and by emotional contagion (item 33). In markets it becomes the machinery of **bubbles and crashes** (item 64), sits on the credit dynamics of **Minsky** (item 62) and the **reflexivity** of Soros (item 65), and is anticipated by Keynes's **beauty contest** (item 61) — the observation that a speculator does not buy what he thinks is valuable but what he thinks others will think others will value, judgment folded back on itself until the underlying facts drop out entirely.

## The human weight

Bubbles cost money, and lost money is real enough. But the darker manias in Mackay's catalogue cost people. This is the frontier where the phenomenon stops being a financial curiosity and becomes a moral one.

A witch panic is a mania with a body count. The same self-reinforcing certainty that inflates a share price can inflate a fear, and the object of that fear is a human being. In the European and colonial witch trials, a shared conviction that maleficent witches walked among the community made every misfortune — a dead cow, a sick child, a failed harvest — evidence of witchcraft, and every accusation evidence of danger, so that fear and accusation fed each other exactly as price and belief feed each other in a bubble. The correlation of belief across a frightened community converged not on the truth but on the destruction of real, specific, innocent people. A society can be gripped by a collective certainty and use it to kill. Moral panics (item 31) are the general form of this, and it is a short bridge to them from here.

But there is a hard complication, and the honest analyst has to hold it. The phrase "the madness of crowds" is not neutral. It is a weapon, and the powerful reach for it. When a mass of people rises — demanding rights, refusing an order, naming an injustice their rulers would rather not name — it is very convenient to dismiss them as a hysterical mob, a delusion, a contagion of feeling rather than a movement with a case. Sometimes the crowd truly is deluded. Sometimes the crowd is *right* and is being called deluded precisely because it is inconvenient. The framing that lets us see a witch panic clearly is the same framing that lets a regime slander a legitimate uprising as mass hysteria. This is the identical problem that haunts availability cascades (item 27): a belief that spreads by social reinforcement might be a false alarm or an under-recognised truth finally breaking through, and the spread alone does not tell you which. Distinguishing a true delusion from a dismissed truth is the whole difficulty, and it cannot be done from the *shape* of the crowd's behaviour — which looks the same either way — but only by going back to the evidence the crowd claims to be responding to. This connects, too, to preference falsification (item 34): sometimes what looks like a sudden mania is a long-hidden belief that was always there and has only now become safe to voice.

## The critique

The founding text of this field is half myth, and that fact has to lead the critique.

Charles Mackay is a wonderful storyteller and an unreliable historian. The clearest case is his account of tulipomania, which for a century and a half was passed down as the archetypal bubble: a whole nation, from nobles to chimney-sweeps, trading bulbs at insane prices until the crash beggared the Dutch economy. The historian Anne Goldgar, in *Tulipmania: Money, Honor, and Knowledge in the Dutch Golden Age* (2007), went to the archives and found that most of it does not hold. The trade involved a fairly small circle of merchants and skilled artisans, not the whole society; the wildest prices attached to a handful of rare bulbs; there is little evidence of mass bankruptcies, suicides, or macroeconomic ruin; and much of the lurid detail traces back to moralising pamphlets written to make a point, which Mackay then repeated as reportage. The archetypal mania, in other words, was substantially exaggerated by the very book that made it famous. That is exactly the series' replication-humility rule applied to its own founding text: the story got better each time it was told, and better meant less true.

The consequences run deeper than one bad chapter. "Madness of crowds" is a rhetorically loaded frame, and it can obscure the rational, incentive-driven components of a bubble. A trader who buys an asset he privately believes is overvalued, intending to sell it to a greater fool before the top, is not mad — he is playing a rational, if dangerous, game, and cascade theory (item 26) and the rational-bubble literature show how far a bubble can inflate without anyone being deluded at all. Calling the whole thing "madness" flatters our hindsight and lets us off the analytic hook. And hindsight is the deepest trap: every past enthusiasm that did not pan out looks like folly from the far side of the crash, while the enthusiasms that *did* pan out — the railways that were overbuilt but transformed the world, the internet shares that were absurd but sat on top of a real revolution — are quietly reclassified as foresight. The category "delusion" is applied selectively, after the fact, and often politically. Naming a mania is easy in the archive and nearly impossible in the moment — which is precisely why the crowd, unable to tell from the inside whether it is converging on truth or on a shared error, keeps making the same mistake, and why the analyst who claims to see it clearly should hold that claim loosely.
