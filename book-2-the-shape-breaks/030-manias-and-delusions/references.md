# References — 30. Manias & delusions

## Confirmed sources

- Mackay, Charles. *Extraordinary Popular Delusions and the Madness of Crowds*. London: Richard Bentley, 1841. — The founding popular text of the field; source of the Mississippi Scheme, South Sea Bubble, tulipomania, witch-trial, and Crusade chapters, and of the "think in herds... go mad in herds" line. Cited in the chapter explicitly as a wonderful but unreliable historical source.
- Kindleberger, Charles P. *Manias, Panics, and Crashes: A History of Financial Crises*. New York: Basic Books, 1978. — The modern economic descendant; supplies the displacement / euphoria / distress / revulsion sequence and the Minsky-derived model of financial crisis. (Later editions co-authored/revised by Robert Aliber.)
- Goldgar, Anne. *Tulipmania: Money, Honor, and Knowledge in the Dutch Golden Age*. Chicago: University of Chicago Press, 2007. — The archival corrective showing that tulipomania was far smaller in scope and far less economically ruinous than Mackay and the moralising-pamphlet tradition implied. Headline critique of the chapter.
- Minsky, Hyman P. Financial-instability hypothesis (developed across his work, e.g. *Stabilizing an Unstable Economy*, 1986). — Underpins Kindleberger's credit-cycle mechanism; cross-referenced as item 62.

## Further reading to verify before final publication

- The Newton quotation — "I can calculate the motions of the heavenly bodies, but not the madness of people" (and variants). Web-verified (2026): the earliest version traces to Joseph Spence's anecdotes (Spence reports Lord Radnor recounting that Newton, asked about the continued rise of South Sea stock, replied "that he could not calculate the madness of the people"; published 1820 in Singer's edition of Spence's *Anecdotes*, from Spence's Second Memorandum Book, c. 1756). Crucially, Spence's original does NOT contain the "motions of the heavenly bodies" flourish — that was a later embellishment. The chapter correctly presents the full quotation as apocryphal / attributed-but-not-verified. KEEP FLAGGED as apocryphal.
- Newton's South Sea loss figure (~£20,000): web-verified (2026) against Odlyzko, "Newton's financial misadventures in the South Sea Bubble," *Notes and Records: the Royal Society Journal of the History of Science* vol. 73, no. 1 (2019), pp. 29–59, which finds losses conservatively exceeding £10,000 and that "plausible methods easily produce values that exceed the £20,000 figure that family lore claimed." The chapter's hedged "a sum often given as around £20,000" is appropriate.
- South Sea Company share-price figures web-verified (2026): stock rose from ~£128 in January 1720 to roughly £1,000 by summer (August), then collapsed in September/autumn — confirmed. South Sea Company founded 1711 (chartered January 1711) with a monopoly on British trade to Spanish South America — confirmed.
- The "carrying on an undertaking of great advantage, but nobody to know what it is" bubble-company anecdote — commonly repeated (originating with Mackay); verify its documentary basis before final publication.
- Book citations web-verified (2026): Mackay, *Extraordinary Popular Delusions and the Madness of Crowds* (orig. *Memoirs of Extraordinary Popular Delusions*), London: Richard Bentley, 1841, three volumes — confirmed.
