# References — 42. Pluralistic ignorance

## Confirmed sources

- Allport, Floyd H. *Social Psychology*. Houghton Mifflin, 1924. — The textbook in which Allport developed the early social-psychological thinking behind the concept; the *term* "pluralistic ignorance" does not appear here but was coined in the 1931 Katz & Allport study (confirmed: the 1924 book does not use the exact term).
- Katz, Daniel, and Floyd H. Allport. *Students' Attitudes: A Report of the Syracuse University Reaction Study*. Craftsman Press, 1931. — The Syracuse survey in which the term "pluralistic ignorance" was first coined and empirically used; documents systematic gaps between students' private attitudes (e.g., toward racial integration in fraternities) and their perceptions of peers.
- O'Gorman, Hubert J. "Pluralistic ignorance and white estimates of white support for racial segregation." *Public Opinion Quarterly*, vol. 39, no. 3, 1975, pp. 313–330. — The revival and landmark application: white Americans who did not personally favour strict segregation frequently overestimated other whites' support for it.
- Prentice, Deborah A., and Dale T. Miller. "Pluralistic ignorance and alcohol use on campus: Some consequences of misperceiving the social norm." *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*, vol. 64, no. 2, 1993, pp. 243–256. — The canonical modern study; students privately less comfortable with campus drinking than they believed the average peer to be, with behavioural shifts (notably among men) toward the misperceived norm over time.
- Miller, Dale T., and Cathy McFarland. "Pluralistic ignorance: When similarity is interpreted as dissimilarity." *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*, vol. 53, no. 2, 1987, pp. 298–305. — Foundational demonstration that people fail to seek help or ask questions because they misread others' identical inaction as evidence of understanding or comfort; the actor–observer asymmetry underlying the mechanism.
