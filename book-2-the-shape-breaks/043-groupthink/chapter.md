# 43. Groupthink

> The most cohesive, harmonious, confident group in the room is often the one about to make the worst decision — because comfort has quietly killed the dissent that error-correction requires.

## The idea

Take a small group of capable people who like and respect one another, who share a common purpose and a certain esprit de corps, and put them under pressure to reach a decision that matters. Something strange begins to happen to their thinking. The very warmth that makes the group pleasant to belong to starts to bend it. Doubts go unspoken because no one wants to be the sour note. Objections that would have been raised in a room of strangers die in the throat. A sense settles over the table that everyone agrees — a sense that is only partly true, because several people privately do not agree and have simply chosen not to say so. And the more the group congratulates itself on its unity, the more confident and the more wrong it becomes.

Groupthink is the name Irving Janis gave to this failure: a mode of thinking that people fall into when concurrence-seeking becomes so dominant in a cohesive in-group that it overrides their motivation to appraise alternative courses of action realistically. The drive is not toward the best decision. It is toward agreement. Members suppress their own reservations, discount warnings, and share an illusion of unanimity and of the group's own invulnerability — and out of that shared illusion come decisions that any one of them, thinking alone, might have seen through.

The unsettling part is where it strikes. Groupthink is not a disease of stupid or dysfunctional committees. Janis found it in exactly the groups that ought to have been best equipped to decide well: expert, experienced, high-morale teams at the summit of power. The cohesion that makes a group effective is the same cohesion that, past a certain point, makes it foolish. This is why the phenomenon closes Book Two. It is the pure case — the wise crowd's conditions destroyed not by an outside shock but from within, by the group's own good feeling about itself.

## The origin

The idea belongs to Irving L. Janis (1918–1990), a research psychologist at Yale who had spent decades studying stress, persuasion, and decision-making. In the early 1970s he turned to a puzzle that the study of individual cognition could not touch: how had small circles of intelligent, well-informed American policymakers arrived at decisions that were, in retrospect, spectacular failures of judgment? He set out his answer in *Victims of Groupthink* in 1972, revised and expanded a decade later as *Groupthink: Psychological Studies of Policy Decisions and Fiascoes* (1982).

Janis borrowed the word's shape deliberately from George Orwell — the clipped, Newspeak sound of *doublethink* and *crimethink* — to signal a deterioration of mental efficiency and moral judgment that results from group pressure. His method was the comparative case study. He read the documentary record of a set of American foreign-policy episodes and asked what the decision process had actually looked like inside the room. The fiascoes gave him his pattern: the Bay of Pigs invasion of 1961, the failure to anticipate the attack on Pearl Harbor, the escalation of the Vietnam War, the crossing of the 38th parallel in Korea. Against these he set two well-run decisions from the same institutions — the Marshall Plan, and above all the handling of the Cuban Missile Crisis in 1962 — to show that the same government, even the same men, could decide well when the process was right and disastrously when it was not.

From the wreckage Janis drew a catalogue of eight symptoms, grouped in three families. First, illusions: an illusion of invulnerability that breeds excessive optimism and risk-taking, and collective rationalisation that discounts warnings and reassembles the facts to fit the preferred course. Second, closed-mindedness: an unquestioned belief in the group's inherent morality, and a stereotyped view of adversaries as too weak or too evil to negotiate with. Third, and most corrosive, the pressures toward uniformity: direct pressure on any member who voices doubts; self-censorship, in which members keep their own deviations from the apparent consensus to themselves; a shared illusion of unanimity, in which silence is read as consent; and the emergence of self-appointed *mindguards* — members who shield the group from information that might disturb its confidence, the way a bodyguard shields a body.

## The mechanism

The gears of groupthink are causal, not merely descriptive, and Janis was careful to specify the conditions that turn them. Three feed in at the front. First, high cohesiveness — a group whose members value their membership and their mutual regard, so that the cost of breaking ranks feels personal. Second, structural insulation — a group sealed off from outside experts, dissenting voices, and the discipline of having to justify itself to anyone else. Third, situational provocation: high-stakes decisions taken under stress, often with a directive leader who signals a preferred option early and unmistakably.

Feed those three together and a single dynamic dominates: the pursuit of concurrence. The pleader's early preference becomes the anchor everyone reads the room against. Cohesion supplies the motive to protect the group's warmth by not disturbing it. Insulation removes the outside voice that might have said the emperor has no plan. Stress makes the relief of a settled decision feel like clarity. And so members begin to self-censor — to swallow the objection, discount the private doubt, assume that the others' silence means they have thought it through and are satisfied. Each swallowed doubt makes the next person's doubt lonelier, because the visible surface of the group grows ever smoother. The mindguard finishes the job, intercepting the memo or the outside expert who might reopen what has comfortably closed.

Note what has been destroyed by the end of this sequence. A group's capacity for good judgment rests, as this whole book has argued, on independent minds bringing diverse and privately-held information to bear, and on some mechanism that lets that information actually surface and combine. Groupthink dismantles exactly these. It converts independent judgment into concurrence. It suppresses the diversity of view that was the group's whole reason for being more than one person. And it replaces genuine aggregation with premature, illusory consensus. The wise-crowd conditions do not fail here because they were never present; they fail because a cohesive group, left to its own good feeling, tears them down from inside.

## The anchor example

Janis's paradigm case — the one he returned to first and treated most fully — was the Bay of Pigs.

In April 1961, three months into his presidency, John F. Kennedy approved a plan, inherited from the Eisenhower administration and pressed by the CIA, to land a brigade of some 1,400 Cuban exiles at the Bay of Pigs on the southern coast of Cuba, in the expectation that they would spark a popular uprising against Fidel Castro. The plan was, on almost any sober examination, a fantasy. The landing site was hundreds of miles from the mountains the fighters were supposed to melt into if things went wrong; the assumption of a spontaneous uprising rested on no evidence; the cover story of American non-involvement was transparent. The invasion collapsed within three days. More than a hundred exiles were killed and over a thousand captured. Kennedy was left humiliated, and the episode helped set the stage for the far more dangerous confrontation to come.

What haunted Janis was not that the plan was bad but that Kennedy's advisers — a group of exceptional intelligence, several of them world-class minds — let it through with scarcely a word of the objection many of them privately held. Arthur Schlesinger, the historian in the room, had grave doubts and wrote a memo expressing some of them, but stayed silent at the decisive meetings, later reproaching himself for it. Others harboured misgivings they never voiced. The group had reached, in Janis's reading, a state of assumed consensus that no one wanted to be the one to break — a smooth surface under which private dissent lay hidden and unpooled. Kennedy himself asked afterward the question that is the whole subject of this chapter: *How could we have been so stupid?* They were not stupid. They were cohesive.

The instructive half of the story is what Kennedy did next. Eighteen months later, facing Soviet missiles in Cuba, he deliberately re-engineered the process. He broke the group into subgroups, invited outside experts, and at times absented himself from the meetings so that his own preference would not anchor the discussion — for he had seen how the room read him. He is reported to have assigned members, including his brother Robert, to play devil's advocate, to attack every emerging plan on principle. The Executive Committee argued, dissented, and cycled through options rather than converging on the first. The same president, the same institution, many of the same men — and, because dissent was now protected rather than punished, one of the best-managed crises of the century instead of one of the worst-managed operations. The two Cubas, side by side, are Janis's entire argument in miniature.

## The flip

Here is the load-bearing turn of this chapter, and of Book Two. Groupthink is the exact anti-twin of the wise crowd. It is, precisely, what Surowiecki's four conditions look like when they are destroyed — not by scale, not by a cascade rippling through strangers, but from inside a small, warm, cohesive group.

Recall the conditions (item 20): diversity of opinion, independence, decentralisation, and a means of aggregation. Groupthink attacks the first three in turn. Diversity of view, the property the diversity prediction theorem (item 19) proves is what actually subtracts error from a group's estimate, is suppressed — members converge on the leader's frame and prune away the very differences that were their collective value. Independence, the condition on which Condorcet's whole guarantee rests, is voluntarily surrendered to cohesion — people stop consulting their private judgment and start reading the room, so their "votes" cease to be independent draws and become echoes. And genuine aggregation is replaced by a premature, illusory consensus, a smooth surface mistaken for a settled truth. Where the wise crowd pools independent errors so they cancel, groupthink pools nothing, because the errors have all been quietly aligned to the same face.

This is the same-people, two-conditions thesis in its most concentrated form. The wise crowd and the groupthink fiasco are not different kinds of group. They are the same capable people, separated only by whether dissent is protected or punished. Protect it — engineer independence and diversity, as Kennedy did in the second Cuba — and the group's collective intelligence appears. Punish it, or merely let cohesion make it socially costly, and the identical group produces confident catastrophe. The comfort that makes a group feel wisest is often the very thing that makes it most foolish, because that comfort is purchased by silencing the independent dissent on which error-correction depends.

The connections run out in every direction. Collective intelligence *c* (item 25) is, in effect, the measured version of what groupthink kills: the studies that found a general factor of group performance found it rising with equal turn-taking and social sensitivity and falling where one voice dominated — and domination, the throttling of turn-taking, is anti-*c* made visible. The self-censorship at groupthink's core is preference falsification (item 34) at committee scale: private dissent hidden behind a public face of agreement. The direct pressure on doubters is Asch's group (item 35) with the stakes raised from line-lengths to wars. And the Delphi method (item 22) is the engineered antidote — anonymised, iterated, de-personalised judgment, designed precisely to give a group the benefit of its members' knowledge without the social pressure that makes them hide it.

## The human weight

There is a reason this closes Book Two rather than sitting quietly among the conformity studies. Groupthink shows the frontier operating at the *top*.

Most of this book's failures happen from below — panicked crowds, cascading shoppers, silent conformers in a room full of strangers. It is tempting to imagine that the powerful are immune, that cabinets and boards and general staffs, being expert and few and unafraid, escape the pressures that bend ordinary crowds. Janis's finding is the opposite. The powerful fall silent too. In the sealed room where the invasion is planned or the merger approved or the coup ordered, the same mechanism runs: the ambitious adviser swallows the doubt that might mark him as disloyal; the board member reads the chairman's face and lets the acquisition through; the officer does not contradict the general. Cohesion is mistaken for correctness at the summit exactly as conformity is mistaken for truth on the factory floor. And because these groups are insulated by their own power from the outside voices that might correct them, their errors are catastrophic in proportion to their reach — a bad committee decision that ends careers, invades countries, or sinks institutions.

Which is why the antidotes Janis proposed are the same institutional fixes this whole series keeps pointing toward, and why they belong at Book Two's close. Appoint a devil's advocate whose job is to attack the emerging consensus. Stand up a red team tasked with making the enemy's case. Have the leader withhold his preference until others have spoken, so the room is not anchored. Bring in outside experts to puncture the insulation. Solicit input anonymously, so the doubter need not weigh his objection against his standing. Every one of these is a technology for protecting the dissenter — for making it safe, or at least survivable, to break the smooth surface and say the thing others are privately thinking. This is the bridge to preference falsification (item 34) and the value of the protected dissenter that runs under the entire frontier: orders of imposed silence, whether in a junta or a caste, sustain themselves by making the first honest word too costly to speak, and they break when that word becomes speakable. Groupthink is that dynamic caught inside a decision, on a timescale of days rather than generations. It is the common root that Book Two has been circling all along: suppressed dissent, whether by cascade, panic, or cohesion, is the shared mechanism by which good collective process fails.

## The critique

Groupthink is one of the most influential ideas in the social sciences and one of the empirically shakiest, and intellectual honesty requires holding both facts at once — a fitting note on which to end a book about how the shape breaks, because the humility applies to the theory as much as to its subjects.

The first problem is in the foundations. Janis built the model from a handful of hand-picked historical cases, selected on their outcomes. He chose famous fiascoes, read the process backward from the disaster, and found the symptoms he was looking for. This is selection on the dependent variable — the cardinal sin of case-study inference. If you start with known catastrophes and go looking for cohesion and suppressed dissent, you will tend to find them; the method cannot tell you how often the same antecedents were present in decisions that turned out well, or how often good decisions were made by cohesive, insulated groups. The retrospective, hand-selected character of the evidence means the theory was, from birth, more compelling narrative than tested hypothesis.

The experimental record has not rescued it. Decades of laboratory work attempting to induce groupthink by manipulating Janis's antecedents — cohesion, insulation, directive leadership, stress — have produced weak and inconsistent support for the full model. The specific antecedents do not reliably generate the specific symptoms, and the symptoms do not reliably produce poor decisions. Cohesion, the linchpin of the whole account, turns out to be the most contested variable of all: some highly cohesive groups decide superbly, and several studies find cohesion improving rather than degrading decision quality, which is precisely backward from the theory's central claim. Sara Esser's 1998 review of the experimental literature and Robert Baron's 2005 chapter — pointedly titled, on the strong model, "So Right It's Wrong" — both reach a similar verdict: the tight, causal, eight-symptom model as Janis specified it is not supported, even as a looser observation survives, that concurrence-seeking and premature consensus are real and common failure modes of groups. Paul 't Hart's *Groupthink in Government* (1994) took the framework seriously and applied it carefully to political decision-making, but even sympathetic development has had to loosen the original's causal claims.

There is a further, sociological cost to the concept's fame: it has become a lazy label. "Groupthink" is now reached for to explain almost any bad group decision after the fact, which drains it of content — a phenomenon that explains everything explains nothing, and post-hoc labelling is exactly the retrospective trap that produced the theory in the first place, now operating in the wider culture. The honest position is the one Baron gestures at: groupthink is ubiquitous as a description and unproven as a mechanism. The strong model, with its ordered symptoms and its confident causal arrows, does not hold. But the loose version does — cohesive groups really do sometimes strangle their own dissent and mistake the resulting silence for wisdom — and it survives because it names, in the register of a single decision, the same thing this book has named a hundred ways: that the crowd's wisdom and its folly are the same people, and dissent, protected or punished, is the dial between them.
