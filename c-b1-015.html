Kin selection & inclusive fitness

A gene for self-sacrifice can conquer a species, provided it spends its host on behalf of the copies of itself riding in the host's relatives.

The idea

For a century after Darwin, one behaviour sat in evolutionary theory like a splinter that would not work its way out: the animal that helps at its own expense. Natural selection is a bookkeeping of survival and reproduction; whatever leaves more descendants spreads, whatever leaves fewer disappears. So how does a creature that gives away food, raises another's young, or dies raising an alarm ever leave enough descendants of its own to keep the giving-away trait alive? On its face, altruism should be self-erasing. Every generation should scrub it out and leave the pure takers behind.

Kin selection is the resolution, and it is deceptively simple once seen. A gene does not care about the body it happens to sit in. It cares — to speak loosely, as biologists do — about copies of itself, wherever they are. And copies of a gene are not confined to one body. They are scattered, in predictable proportions, through that body's relatives. Your sibling carries, on average, half of your genes; your niece a quarter; your first cousin an eighth. So a gene that inclines its bearer to help relatives is, in a real accounting sense, helping itself — helping the other copies of itself housed next door. If the help delivered to those copies outweighs what it costs the copy doing the helping, the gene comes out ahead, and it spreads. Altruism toward kin is not a violation of selfishness at the level of the gene. It is selfishness at the level of the gene, wearing the face of generosity at the level of the body.

That single move — shifting the ledger from the individual to the gene and its copies — is the deepest answer evolutionary biology has to the oldest question in this book: why cooperate at all.

The origin

The intuition was in the air before the mathematics arrived. J.B.S. Haldane, the great and cantankerous British geneticist, is supposed to have said, when asked whether he would lay down his life for his brother, that he would do it for two brothers or eight cousins — the arithmetic being that two brothers or eight cousins carry, between them, about as many of his genes as he does himself. The line is almost certainly apocryphal in its exact form; it survives as pub anecdote and second-hand report rather than a sourced quotation, and it is worth flagging as such. But it is a perfect miniature of the idea, and Haldane had gestured at the underlying logic in print. What it lacked was rigour.

The rigour came from William Donald Hamilton, a shy, half-neglected graduate student who did much of the work in the reading room of the British Museum and on park benches, because he had no proper office and few colleagues who took the problem seriously. In 1964 he published, in the Journal of Theoretical Biology, a two-part paper titled "The Genetical Evolution of Social Behaviour, I and II" (volume 7, pages 1–52). It is one of the most consequential papers in twentieth-century biology, and at the time it was nearly unreadable — dense, algebra-choked, and easy to overlook. Buried in it was a condition of startling economy, since christened Hamilton's rule:

rb > c

An act of altruism is favoured by selection when the relatedness r between actor and recipient, multiplied by the reproductive benefit b the recipient gains, exceeds the reproductive cost c to the actor. The term "kin selection" itself was not Hamilton's; John Maynard Smith coined it in a 1964 note in Nature, giving the phenomenon the name it has carried ever since. And a few years later George Price — an eccentric, tragic American chemist who had wandered into evolutionary theory — handed Hamilton a cleaner way to derive the whole thing, the covariance equation now called the Price equation, which recast selection as a statistical relationship between a trait and reproductive success and let Hamilton's rule fall out as a special case.

The mechanism

The gear that makes it all turn is a redefinition of fitness. Classical fitness counts an organism's own offspring. Hamilton's inclusive fitness counts more: your own reproduction, plus your effect on the reproduction of your relatives, each relative's contribution discounted by how related to you they are. Help your sister raise three extra offspring and you have, in inclusive-fitness terms, banked the equivalent of one and a half of your own (three, halved by the relatedness of one-half). If producing those extra nieces and nephews cost you fewer than one and a half of your own offspring, you have come out ahead in the only currency selection reads.

That discount factor, r, is the heart of it. It measures the probability that a gene in the actor is also present, by shared descent, in the recipient. Full siblings share on average one-half; parent and child one-half; grandparent and grandchild one-quarter; first cousins one-eighth. Multiply the benefit to the recipient by that number and you get the benefit as it registers on the actor's genes. Weigh it against the cost. If rb beats c, help; if not, don't. The rule prices altruism.

Hamilton's rule also explains a case that had baffled Darwin himself: the sterile worker. In a beehive or an ant colony, most females never reproduce at all. They labour their whole lives to raise the queen's offspring, not their own — the extreme of self-sacrifice, an entire caste that has surrendered reproduction. Darwin called such sterile castes a "special difficulty" that at first seemed "insuperable, and actually fatal to my whole theory." Hamilton saw why it was not fatal. In bees, ants, and wasps, sex is determined by a curious system called haplodiploidy: males develop from unfertilised eggs and so carry a single set of chromosomes, while females develop from fertilised eggs and carry two. A quirk falls out of this arithmetic. Full sisters in such a species share, on average, three-quarters of their genes with one another — more than they would share with their own daughters, which is the usual one-half. Under those numbers, a female can propagate her genes more efficiently by raising fertile sisters than by raising daughters. Sterility stops being a paradox and becomes a strategy. The worker is not sacrificing her genetic interests; she is pursuing them by another route.

This was the great reorientation. The older biology had explained cooperation loosely, by appeal to "the good of the species" — animals restrain themselves, the story went, so the group will not exhaust its resources. Hamilton's mathematics made that story unnecessary and, in most forms, wrong. Selection does not work for the good of the species; it works for the propagation of genes, and cooperation emerges wherever the gene's-eye arithmetic favours it. Richard Dawkins would carry this reframing to a wide public in The Selfish Gene in 1976, a book that did more than any other to install the gene's-eye view in the general imagination — and, as we will see, to muddle it.

The anchor example

Go to a high meadow in the Sierra Nevada in early summer and watch a colony of Belding's ground squirrels. They live in burrow systems, feeding above ground where hawks quarter the sky and weasels move through the grass. When a predator appears, a squirrel will sometimes rise on its hindlegs and give a shrill, repeated alarm call — a sound that sends its neighbours diving for their burrows, and that, unmistakably, tells the predator exactly where the caller is standing. To call is to volunteer as the most conspicuous animal in the field. It is, on its face, the self-erasing behaviour that should not exist.

In 1977 the biologist Paul Sherman published, in Science, the results of years spent watching these squirrels and recording, animal by animal, who called and who stayed silent. The pattern was not random and it was not about who was in danger. It was about kinship. Females called far more than males — and the reason, Sherman found, lay in how the two sexes are distributed. Female Belding's ground squirrels stay near where they were born, so a female is typically surrounded by mothers, sisters, daughters, and aunts. Males disperse away from their birthplace, ending up among strangers. When a female called, she was warning her relatives — the carriers of copies of her own alarm-calling genes. When a male stood in a crowd of the unrelated, he mostly kept quiet. Sherman even found that a female's tendency to call tracked whether living kin were actually present: with relatives nearby, she called; without them, less so. The animals were behaving, with eerie precision, as if they were running Hamilton's rule. The cost was real — callers were stalked and killed more often than the silent. But the benefit, discounted by relatedness, was paid to the genes that made the calling happen. The squirrel that draws the hawk to save its sisters is not defying natural selection. It is natural selection, doing its bookkeeping in a mountain meadow.

The flip

Kin selection is not one theory among the many in this book. It is the bedrock beneath the whole aggregation story — the deepest layer of the answer to why any collective holds together at all. Every later chapter about crowds pooling wisdom, about herds moving as one, about hives choosing a home, sits on top of a prior fact: that evolution built creatures capable of acting against their own narrow interest for the sake of others, and it built them by way of shared genes. The tightest, most self-sacrificing collectives on Earth — the beehive, the ant colony, the human family — are exactly the ones where relatedness runs highest. The honeybee democracy we will marvel at later (item 68), in which a leaderless swarm chooses a new nest with something close to optimality, runs on a colony of near-clones, thousands of sisters whose genetic interests very nearly coincide. That coincidence is what lets the collective behave as if it had a single will. Kin selection is why collective "wisdom" and collective self-sacrifice require no group-level designer, no wise architect of the hive — only gene-level bookkeeping, run out to its conclusion over deep time.

But the rule has a second face, and it is set entirely by where the boundary of "kin" is drawn. Hamilton's arithmetic is a machine for sorting the world into those who carry copies of your genes and those who do not — into us and them — and for lavishing help on the first while withholding it from the second. Turn that machine toward your children and it produces devotion. Turn it toward your tribe and it can produce nepotism, clannishness, and the hard bright line between the people who count and the people who do not. The same evolved psychology that makes a mother die for her child can make a mob murder a stranger. This is the seam this book keeps returning to: not two mechanisms, one for love and one for hatred, but a single mechanism whose sign flips with a boundary condition. Kin altruism is the evolutionary ground under the warm collectives — the family, the mutual-aid band, reciprocal altruism among familiars (item 14), the tight cooperative herd whose members shield one another (the selfish herd, item 9). And it is the evolutionary ground under the coldest ones too, because the machinery that binds an in-group is the same machinery that can be pointed at an out-group. The condition that tips it is simply: who gets counted as kin.

The human weight

Here the frontier of this series comes into view, and it should be drawn clearly but without melodrama. Human beings do not restrict their kin psychology to actual genetic relatives. We extend it — by symbol, by ritual, by language — to people who share no more genes with us than a random stranger does. We call comrades "brothers," we call the nation a "motherland" or "fatherland," we speak of blood and soil, of the family of the faithful, of a band of brothers. This is fictive kinship, and it is one of the most powerful coordinating technologies our species has ever stumbled into. It takes an evolved circuitry built to bind small groups of relatives and hijacks it to bind millions of unrelated strangers into armies, congregations, nations, and movements. The relatedness cues — shared names, shared descent myths, shared blood, the honorific of "brother" or "sister" — are the levers, and manipulating them is how large-scale human cooperation, and large-scale human violence, get built.

The cooperation is real and often admirable; so is the danger. The tighter a fictive-kin group is bound, the sharper the line around it, and the machinery that says these are my kin, I will die for them comes bundled with its shadow: those are not, and their deaths do not weigh the same. Soldiers fight and die less for the abstraction of a cause than for the men beside them, whom they have come to feel as brothers — the useful edge of the mechanism. But the same fusion of self and group, when it fuses to an ethnic or sectarian or ideological "family," is what turns a grievance into a pogrom and a doctrine into a holy war. This bridges directly to identity fusion and sacred values (item 84), where the felt oneness of a person with a group can license violence that no ordinary cost-benefit calculation would permit. And it bridges to the systems that carve human populations into ranked kin-boundaries in the first place — caste and clan, the great machinery of stratification (item 85), which take the raw material of relatedness and freeze it into hierarchy, deciding by birth who is inside the circle of obligation and who is permanently outside it. The lesson is sober and it is double-edged: the same rule that underwrites our capacity for love underwrites our capacity for tribal slaughter, and the difference between them is nothing but the radius of the word kin.

The critique

For all its reach, kin selection has been the site of one of the fiercest controversies in modern biology, and honesty demands laying the disputes out rather than reciting the theory as gospel.

The loudest eruption came in 2010, when Martin Nowak, Corina Tarnita, and E.O. Wilson — Wilson being one of the founders of the whole field of social evolution — published a paper in Nature arguing that inclusive-fitness theory was mathematically limited, less general than its proponents claimed, and dispensable in favour of standard natural-selection models. The reaction was extraordinary: in 2011, again in Nature, 137 biologists signed a rebuttal (Abbot et al.) defending inclusive fitness as robust, well-supported, and empirically fertile. The exchange did not fully resolve, but it sharpened a distinction worth keeping. There is kin selection as a real biological process — the fact that relatedness shapes the evolution of social behaviour, which almost no one disputes — and there is inclusive fitness as one particular accounting method for tracking that process. One can accept the first while arguing about whether the second is the best or only bookkeeping. Much of the heat came from conflating them.

The specific haplodiploidy story has also lost ground. Hamilton's elegant three-quarters-relatedness argument for why eusociality arose in bees, ants, and wasps looked, for decades, like the theory's crown jewel. But eusociality has since turned up in plenty of species that are not haplodiploid — termites, some beetles, snapping shrimp, even naked mole-rats among mammals — where the three-quarters arithmetic simply does not apply. The current view emphasises something else Hamilton's rule predicts anyway: lifetime monogamy. When a mother mates with a single male, her offspring are full siblings related by one-half, which is already enough, under the right ecology, to make helping at the nest pay. High and reliable relatedness, however it is achieved, seems to be the precondition for eusociality — not haplodiploidy specifically. The rule survives; the favourite illustration of it does not.

Finally, a caution about the popular afterlife of the idea. "The selfish gene" became a catchphrase and, in becoming one, was widely misread — as if it licensed selfishness in people, or claimed that organisms consciously calculate relatedness, or reduced human beings to gene-puppets. It does none of these. The "selfishness" is a metaphor for a statistical tendency of genes to spread; it says nothing about the motives, kindness, or freedom of the animals that carry them. Kin-selected altruism is, at the level of experience, entirely genuine altruism — the squirrel feels no arithmetic, the mother no ledger. The bookkeeping is done by selection across generations, not by the creature in the moment. To read Hamilton's rule as a counsel of coldness is to make exactly the error the mathematics was built to dissolve. The rule explains where warmth comes from. It does not tell anyone to stop feeling it.
