Chapter 89 · The Shape Remade

Bots, astroturfing & manufactured consensus

If you cannot win the crowd, you can forge one — and a forged crowd, believed, recruits the real one.

The idea

There is a trick older than the internet and now industrialised by it. You cannot easily change a million minds directly. But you can change what those minds believe other minds are thinking, and the mind does the rest of the work itself. Human beings are exquisitely tuned to social proof: when it looks as though everyone around us has already settled a question, we take that apparent consensus as evidence and quietly move toward it. Manufactured consensus is the deliberate exploitation of that reflex. You fabricate the appearance of a crowd — many voices, saying the same thing, seemingly independently — so that real people, unable to tell the forged crowd from a real one, update toward the position the forgery was built to sell.

Two overlapping techniques do the fabricating. Astroturfing is the older, human word for it: a campaign engineered by an interested party — a corporation, a party, a state — but costumed as spontaneous popular sentiment welling up from below. The name is a small joke at grassroots' expense. If real popular feeling is grassroots, then this manufactured version is AstroTurf: fake grass, laid down to look like a lawn that grew on its own. Bots and troll farms are the newer, automated engine. A bot is a software-run social-media account; a troll farm is a room of paid humans running many accounts each. Between them they can produce, cheaply and at scale, the one thing manufactured consensus needs above all — volume. Many mouths, one script.

The target is never the direct persuasion of the undecided by argument. The target is the early social proof — the first, fragile impression that a position is already popular, already winning, already the sensible default. Get that impression to take hold in a susceptible network and the network's own dynamics — virality, herding, the pull of the visible majority — carry it the rest of the way. You fake the crowd in order to move the crowd.

The origin

The word astroturfing is usually traced to United States politics in 1985. Senator Lloyd Bentsen of Texas is credited with the coinage, describing the flood of suspiciously uniform mail his office received from an industry lobbying campaign — letters engineered to look like a groundswell of ordinary constituents. "A fellow from Texas can tell the difference between grass roots and AstroTurf," he is reported to have said; the quip named a practice that long predated it and would soon outgrow it.

The digital study of the phenomenon has a clearer lineage. In the 2010s a research programme took shape around what came to be called computational propaganda — the use of automation and algorithms to manipulate public opinion across social platforms. Philip N. Howard and Samuel C. Woolley, working through the Oxford Internet Institute's Computational Propaganda Project, documented coordinated bot and troll operations across dozens of countries and gathered the findings in their 2018 volume Computational Propaganda. In parallel, a strand of computer science set out to measure the machines themselves: Emilio Ferrara and colleagues' 2016 survey "The Rise of Social Bots," in Communications of the ACM, became a standard reference, laying out how automated accounts imitate human behaviour, why they are hard to detect, and how they can be marshalled to distort online discourse. And running underneath the academic literature was the case that made the term a household one: the Russian Internet Research Agency, a St Petersburg troll farm whose operatives ran networks of fake American personas across the mid-2010s, later the subject of United States indictments and congressional investigation. (Specific IRA claims — account counts, reach figures, the indictment details — should be verified before final publication.)

The mechanism

The gears are borrowed, every one of them, from the machinery of the wise crowd — and run in reverse.

Start with social proof (item 39). We treat the visible behaviour of others as information about what is true or wise, because usually it is: if a crowd has gathered, there is probably something worth seeing. Manufactured consensus supplies a crowd where there is nothing to see. Fabricated accounts post, repost, and reply in coordinated bursts, so that a message arrives already wrapped in the signal many people already believe this. The reader need not be argued into agreement; the apparent agreement of others is itself the argument.

Next, manufactured availability (item 27). We judge how common or important something is by how easily examples come to mind. Flood a feed with a thousand near-identical posts and the position they carry becomes cognitively available — it is the thing you keep seeing, therefore the thing that must be everywhere. Repetition masquerades as prevalence.

Then the tipping machinery. A network of real users has thresholds: many people will not adopt a view, share a hashtag, or voice an opinion until they see enough others do so first (complex contagion, item 54; virality and network thresholds, item 57). Manufactured consensus exists to supply that first push — the seeded early momentum that carries a message over the threshold where real, unpaid humans begin to spread it on their own. The bots do not need to convince everyone. They need only to fake the first wave convincingly enough that a real second wave forms behind it, at which point the operation can go quiet and let the genuine crowd do the amplifying.

And note where the whole apparatus plugs in. It exploits the same trace-following that the platforms are built on — the stigmergy (item 4) by which each of us leaves marks (likes, shares, trends) that guide the next person, and by which recommendation systems (item 87) decide what to show. A manufactured trend is a false trace, deliberately laid, that both the recommendation algorithm and the human eye read as a real one. The forger is not fighting the platform's logic. He is feeding it exactly the signals it was designed to trust.

The anchor example

The clearest way to see the mechanism is the manufactured trend. A hashtag begins with no natural constituency — a slogan, an accusation, a product. A coordinated network of accounts, some automated, some human-run, posts it in a tight, synchronised burst: hundreds or thousands of near-simultaneous mentions. The platform's trending algorithm, watching for exactly this kind of spike, registers a surge of interest and promotes the hashtag onto its "trending" list. Now the false trace becomes a real signal. Ordinary users, scrolling, see the tag trending and read that placement as what it is normally supposed to mean — lots of real people are talking about this — and some of them click, some post, some argue. The manufactured wave has recruited a real one. By the time analysts notice the burst's suspicious geometry, the crowd carrying the hashtag is no longer mostly fake. The forgery has done its one job: it faked the early consensus long enough for the real consensus to assemble around it.

The Internet Research Agency operations of the mid-2010s are the case most people reach for, and they illustrate the human-run end of the same logic — networks of invented American personas, on both sides of contested issues, posting as ordinary citizens to inflame, divide, and manufacture the impression of grassroots feeling that did not exist. What makes the case instructive is precisely its even-handedness of method: the operation seeded content across the political spectrum, because the aim was less any single position than the general manufacture of apparent popular passion. (The specific scope, account numbers, and demonstrated effects of IRA activity are contested and should be verified before final publication — see the critique.)

Keep the structure in view rather than the villain. The tactic is not the property of any one state or faction. Corporations astroturf reviews and "citizen" campaigns; parties of every stripe run amplification networks; activists buy followers and coordinate posting. The mechanism is neutral; it is a way of faking the crowd, available to anyone who wants to move one.

The flip

Here is the turn on which this chapter rests, and it is the darkest inversion in the whole series. Astroturfing is not the opposite of the wise crowd. It is the wise crowd's own logic, captured and turned against it.

Recall what makes a crowd wise. Galton's averaged ox (item 17), Condorcet's converging jury (item 18): both work only because the many voices are independent. Independence is what lets errors cancel (item 21); independence and diversity are the load-bearing conditions of the whole edifice (item 20). When a hundred people agree independently, that agreement is powerful evidence, because it is a hundred separate draws on reality all landing the same way. That is the signal we are built to trust, and rightly.

Manufactured consensus attacks exactly there. It produces many voices that are secretly one voice — a single operator behind a thousand accounts, or a single script run by a room of trolls. The voices are not independent; they are perfectly correlated, their independence precisely zero. But — and this is the whole cruelty of it — from the outside a hundred correlated voices look identical to a hundred independent ones. We cannot easily tell manufactured agreement from real agreement, because the surface signal is the same: many mouths, one message. The forger exploits the fact that the very thing the wise crowd depends on — independent convergence — is the thing we have no reliable way to verify at a glance. Astroturfing is the counterfeiting of independence.

Which places it in the exact seam this book keeps returning to. The wise crowd and the mob are the same crowd under different conditions. Here the condition — independence — is not lost by accident, as in an information cascade (item 26) where real people stop consulting themselves and start copying. Here it is destroyed on purpose, faked from the outset, so that a cascade forms around a consensus that never existed. This is the mob manufactured deliberately: engineered dependence dressed as spontaneous agreement. It is digital propaganda (item 82) refined into its purest form — not an argument aimed at minds, but a forged crowd aimed at the reflex that reads crowds. And it feeds on the same substrate as preference falsification (item 34): where people already tune their public voice to the apparent majority, a faked majority is all the more easily obeyed.

The defence, when you see it this way, is nothing exotic. It is simply the wise-crowd condition itself, stated as a demand: verify independence. A consensus is only worth what its independence is worth. If the many voices trace back to one source, they are not a crowd. They are one voice wearing a crowd's clothes, and the evidentiary weight of the whole chorus collapses to the weight of a single speaker.

The human weight

The stakes are not confined to any one election or campaign; they reach the epistemic commons itself — our shared, working sense of what other people actually think. That common knowledge is already fragile. Pluralistic ignorance (item 42) and preference falsification (item 34) mean that even without any manipulation we routinely misread the true distribution of opinion, silently overestimating a norm nobody privately holds. Manufactured consensus is a deliberate assault on that same, already-clouded instrument. It lets an actor fabricate the impression that the people support us — or that dissent is fringe, marginal, held only by cranks — and lets the same actor manufacture fake dissent, or drown a real movement in noise. A regime that can flood the channel can make its support look total and its opposition look tiny, whichever it needs, and the citizen has no clean way to see through the flood to the real numbers underneath.

The deepest damage is not any single forged consensus but what the mere possibility of forgery does once it becomes common knowledge. If everyone knows that crowds can be faked, then every real crowd can be dismissed as fake. A regime accused by a real popular movement can wave it away as "just bots." An inconvenient groundswell can be discredited as astroturf without a shred of evidence, because the accusation is now always plausible. This is the manufactured-consensus version of what has been called the liar's dividend: once fabrication is known to be possible, the liar profits, because the true and the false become equally deniable. The forgery of consensus corrupts not only the forged instances but our trust in real grassroots expression as such — and that trust, once gone, is not cheaply rebuilt.

This bridges directly to the frontier this series tracks: the maintenance of unjust orders by silence and the misreading of the crowd. Stratification (item 85) holds in part because people cannot see how many others privately dissent. Manufactured consensus is a technology for keeping that veil in place and thickening it — for making an imposed order look like a popular one, and for making the real cracks in it look like foreign forgeries. It is even-handed in menace: the same tools serve the incumbent silencing dissent and the challenger faking a groundswell. That even-handedness is the point. The threat is not a particular liar. It is the general degradation of our ability to know what the many actually think.

The critique

The threat is real, and the field studying it has real problems — problems large enough that anyone who takes the danger seriously must also take the uncertainty seriously.

Begin with measurement. Much of what we "know" about bot prevalence rests on automated bot-detection tools, and those tools are error-prone and contested. Botometer, the most widely used, has been shown to misclassify heavily: Adrian Rauchfleisch and Jonas Kaiser's 2020 analysis found its accuracy varying sharply with language and time and its thresholds arbitrary enough that headline estimates of "how many accounts are bots" can swing wildly depending on where the line is drawn. Many accounts flagged as bots are real, if unusual, humans; many sophisticated bots pass as human. Estimates of bot prevalence in any given conversation should therefore be read as rough and often inflated, not as measurements.

Then the harder question: even where coordinated inauthentic activity is real and documented, what does it actually do? The persuasive impact of bots and trolls is truly uncertain and may be smaller than the alarm suggests. Alessandro Bessi and Emilio Ferrara's 2016 work established that bots were present and active around the 2016 United States election — but presence is not persuasion. Much manufactured content reaches users who are already committed to the position it pushes; it may deepen or excite existing partisans far more than it converts anyone. The Internet Research Agency's reach, measured in impressions, was large; its measurable effect on votes or beliefs remains unproven and contested. It is entirely possible for an operation to be vast, well-funded, and real, and yet to have moved very little.

And the dismissal cuts both ways. Just as "it's all real grassroots" can be a lie, "it's all bots" is often a lazy evasion — a way to wave off genuine opposition without engaging it, the liar's dividend claimed by whoever finds a real crowd inconvenient. The honest position holds two things at once: manufactured consensus is a real technique with real deployment, and our ability to measure its scale and prove its effect is weak and improving only slowly, in an ongoing arms race between the manufacturers and the detectors that neither side is winning. The forged crowd is a real weapon. How often it hits, and how hard, we do not yet reliably know — and pretending otherwise would be its own small forgery of consensus.
