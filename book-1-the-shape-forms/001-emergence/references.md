# References — 1. Emergence

## Confirmed sources

- Aristotle. *Metaphysics*, Book VIII (Zeta/Eta), c. 350 BCE. — The proto-idea that a whole compounded of parts is something over and above the parts, and that a syllable is not identical to its letters; the ancient root of emergence as the causal work of structure.
- Mill, John Stuart. *A System of Logic*, 1843 (Book III, on the "Composition of Causes" and heteropathic laws in chemistry). — Mill's distinction between mechanical/homopathic effects and chemical/heteropathic effects; the direct antecedent Lewes built his terminology on.
- Lewes, George Henry. *Problems of Life and Mind*, First Series, Vol. 2, 1875. — Coins the term "emergent" and its contrast with "resultant"; the origin of the word as used throughout the chapter.
- Anderson, Philip W. "More Is Different." *Science*, Vol. 177, No. 4047 (1972), pp. 393–396. — The modern manifesto of emergence from condensed-matter physics; the arguments about reductionism, symmetry-breaking, and new properties at each level of complexity, quoted and paraphrased in the origin beat.
- Holland, John H. *Emergence: From Chaos to Order*. Addison-Wesley, 1998. — Standard complexity-science treatment of emergence as generated by simple rules and local interaction; underpins the mechanism beat's account of local interaction plus nonlinearity.
- Bedau, Mark A. "Weak Emergence." *Philosophical Perspectives*, Vol. 11 (1997), pp. 375–399. — The canonical formulation of weak emergence (derivable only by simulation) versus stronger claims; underpins the weak/strong distinction in the mechanism and critique beats.
- Sugiyama, Yuki; Fukui, Minoru; Kikuchi, Macoto; Hasebe, Katsuya; Nakayama, Akihiro; Nishinari, Katsuhiro; Tadaki, Shin-ichi; Yukawa, Satoshi. "Traffic jams without bottlenecks — experimental evidence for the physical mechanism of the formation of a jam." *New Journal of Physics*, Vol. 10, No. 3, 033001 (2008). DOI 10.1088/1367-2630/10/3/033001. — The ring-road experiment in which a backward-travelling "phantom" jam emerges spontaneously from steady driving; the empirical spine of the anchor example. VERIFIED.
- Santa Fe Institute, founded 1984 (initially as the "Rio Grande Institute"; first defining workshop 5–6 October 1984) by George Cowan, Murray Gell-Mann, David Pines and others largely associated with Los Alamos. — The institutional home of the complexity sciences referenced in the origin beat. VERIFIED against the Institute's official history.
- British Emergentism: Samuel Alexander, *Space, Time, and Deity* (Macmillan, 1920, from the 1916–18 Gifford Lectures); C. Lloyd Morgan, *Emergent Evolution* (Williams & Norgate, 1923, from the 1921–22 Gifford Lectures); C. D. Broad, *The Mind and Its Place in Nature* (Kegan Paul, 1925). — The "matter → life → mind" hierarchy of truly novel emergent levels; each title/date VERIFIED. Secondary survey: Brian McLaughlin, "The Rise and Fall of British Emergentism," in *Emergence or Reduction?*, eds. Beckermann, Flohr & Kim (de Gruyter, 1992).
- Backward propagation speed of phantom jams (~20 km/h). — VERIFIED as the common measured value on real highways (typically ~15 ± 5 km/h depending on safe headway and vehicle length); the ~20 km/h figure in the anchor example is well within the reported range.
- Flynn, M. R.; Kasimov, A. R.; Nave, J.-C.; Rosales, R. R.; Seibold, B. "Self-sustained nonlinear waves in traffic flow." *Physical Review E*, Vol. 79, No. 5, 056113 (2009). — Coins "jamiton" (a play on "soliton") for the traffic-wave analogy used in the anchor beat. VERIFIED. (Preprint arXiv:0810.2820, 2008.)
