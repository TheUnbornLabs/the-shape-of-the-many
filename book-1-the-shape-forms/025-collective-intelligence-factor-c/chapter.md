# 25. The collective intelligence factor "c"

> A group has an IQ, it is measurable, and it is set less by how clever its members are than by how they take turns.

## The idea

Psychologists have spent a century chasing a single number. Give one person a wide enough battery of mental tasks — vocabulary, arithmetic, spatial rotation, pattern-finding — and their scores across all of them tend to rise and fall together. Someone strong at one is, on average, a little strong at the rest. That shared tendency, the statistical shadow cast across every kind of thinking, is the general intelligence factor, Spearman's *g*. It is what an IQ test is trying, however imperfectly, to catch.

The idea of this chapter is that groups have one too. Assemble a handful of people, hand them a varied set of problems to solve together — a visual puzzle, a brainstorming task, a moral judgment, a negotiation, a typing job — and their performances across those tasks also rise and fall together. A team that does well on one tends to do well on the others. Behind that consistency sits a single measurable quantity, a general *collective* intelligence factor, labelled *c* to rhyme with *g*. It is not a metaphor. It is the group-level analogue of individual intelligence, extracted by the same statistics, and it predicts how the group will fare on tasks it has not yet seen.

The finding that makes *c* worth a chapter, and worth closing a book on, is what does *not* explain it. You might assume a smart group is just a bag of smart people — that *c* is the sum, or the average, or the peak of the members' individual IQs. It is barely any of those. The intelligence of the members turns out to be a weak predictor of the intelligence of the group. What predicts it is how the group behaves: whether people can read one another, whether everyone gets to speak, who is in the room. A group, in other words, can be much smarter or much dumber than the people composing it, and the deciding factor is process.

## The origin

The claim comes from a 2010 paper in *Science* with a title that leaves nothing to inference: "Evidence for a Collective Intelligence Factor in the Performance of Human Groups." Its authors — Anita Williams Woolley, Christopher F. Chabris, Alex Pentland, Nada Hashmi, and Thomas W. Malone — were working across Carnegie Mellon, Union College, and MIT's Center for Collective Intelligence, and they set themselves a deliberately old-fashioned question in a deliberately modern way (*Science* vol. 330, pp. 686–688, published 29 October 2010; DOI 10.1126/science.1193147).

The design borrowed its logic wholesale from the study of individual intelligence. To show that a person has a general mental ability, you show that their scores on many unrelated tasks correlate and that a single underlying factor accounts for the shared variance. So the team put people into small groups, gave each group a battery of short, varied tasks spanning the kinds of things groups actually do — generating ideas, making collective judgments, planning, solving problems, coordinating — and asked the same statistical question of the groups that Spearman had asked of individuals in 1904. Did performance across the tasks hang together? Was there a single factor?

There was. A first general factor explained a substantial share of a group's performance across the whole battery, and — the crucial test — a group's score on the tasks used to measure *c* predicted its performance on a later, harder, criterion task it had not been scored on. That is exactly the move that licenses talk of intelligence rather than task-specific luck. Woolley and colleagues had done for the group what a century of psychometrics had done for the individual: pulled a general factor out of a spread of specific performances, and named it. The analogy to *g* was not incidental. It was the whole argument.

## The mechanism

The engine of the finding is not in the tasks but in what turned out to correlate with *c* — and, more tellingly, what did not.

Start with the null result, because it is the memorable one. The average intelligence of a group's members was only weakly related to *c*. So was the maximum — the single cleverest person in the room. A team stocked with high-IQ individuals was not, on that basis, a high-*c* team. The brilliance of the parts did not assemble itself into the brilliance of the whole. Whatever collective intelligence is, it is not individual intelligence poured into a group and stirred.

Three things predicted it instead, and all three are about interaction rather than intellect.

The first was the average *social sensitivity* of the members — their ability to read the mental and emotional states of other people. The researchers measured it with the "Reading the Mind in the Eyes" test, in which you infer a person's state from a photograph cropped to just their eyes. Groups whose members scored higher on that reading-of-others were, on average, more collectively intelligent. The capacity that lets you tell that a colleague is unconvinced, or hesitating, or holding something back is, it seems, a component of group cognition.

The second was the *evenness of conversational turn-taking*. In groups where a few people dominated the discussion, collective intelligence was lower. In groups where speaking was distributed more equally around the table, it was higher. Note that this is not about total talk, or enthusiasm, or even about the quality of any single contribution. It is about distribution — whether the channel is shared or captured. A conversation monopolised by two loud voices is a conversation that has muted whatever the quiet members knew.

The third was the *proportion of women* in the group: more women, higher *c*, an effect that ran up to a point and was, in the data, largely explained by the first factor — women in the sample scored higher on average on the social-sensitivity test, and it was the sensitivity that carried the effect. Composition mattered because of what it changed about process.

Put the three together and a mechanism emerges. A group is a system for pooling what its members separately know and can do. That pooling only works if information can flow between them: if people can tell what others are thinking (sensitivity), if everyone can actually put their piece on the table (turn-taking), and if the mix of the group supports both. A room of geniuses who cannot read each other and will not take turns is a room in which most of the genius never reaches the shared problem. A room of ordinary people who listen well and share the floor is a room in which nearly everything each of them knows gets used. Process is the pipe through which member ability has to pass to become group ability, and a narrow pipe wastes whatever it is fed.

## The anchor example

The anchor here is the study itself, because its central result is more startling than any illustration could be, and worth stating in the bluntest form the data allow.

Imagine two teams facing the same battery of problems. Team A has been selected for raw individual horsepower: high test scores, the confident and the quick, several people used to being the smartest in the room. Team B is unremarkable on paper — average scores, no stars — but its members happen to read one another well and, when they talk, the airtime spreads evenly around the table. On the strength of *c*, Team B is the better bet. The individual intelligence that would have let you pick the winner of a solo contest barely helps you pick the winner of a group one. Turn-taking and eye-reading do.

Sit with how counter-intuitive that is against every instinct organisations run on. We recruit for individual brilliance, promote the loudest and most certain, and assemble teams as if collective performance were an addition problem: gather enough smart people and the group must be smart. Woolley's result says the addition does not go through. The group's intelligence lives in the space *between* the members — in the pattern of their exchange — far more than in the members themselves. You could swap in cleverer people and lower the group's IQ, if the cleverer people talked over each other. You could change nothing about who is in the room and raise the group's IQ by changing only how they take turns.

That is the empirical shape the whole of Book One has been circling. Galton's crowd averaging an ox's weight (item 17), Condorcet's jurors converging on truth (item 18), the diversity prediction theorem splitting error into ability and spread (item 19) — each argued, from a different direction, that a collective's competence is a property of the collective, not a sum of individual competences. *c* is that argument made flesh and measured with a number. The wisdom was never simply in the people. It was in the arrangement.

## The flip

This chapter closes the movement because it measures the thing the movement was built to describe. Everything from Galton onward asserted that a group can be wiser than its members; *c* shows that group intelligence is real, general, and quantifiable, and then does the thing the whole series is about — it names the condition that sets the sign.

The thesis of these books is that the wise crowd and the mob are the same people under different conditions, and that a nameable condition flips between them. *c* states that condition in human-process terms, cleaner than anywhere else in Book One. **A group is not automatically wiser or dumber than its members. Process decides.** Even turn-taking and social sensitivity turn a crowd wise; domination by a few — low turn-taking evenness — turns the same people foolish. Not different rooms. The same room, run two ways.

And that low-evenness setting is the seed of nearly every pathology in Book Two. When a few voices capture the conversation, the group stops pooling what its members separately know and starts amplifying what the dominant few already believe. That is the mechanism, told from the inside, of groupthink (item 43), the anti-*c* pathology in which a cohesive group's process closes down until it can no longer see its own errors — *c*'s dark twin, the same variables set to their destructive values. It is the near end of the information cascade (item 26), where each person, deferring to what the confident have already said, stops adding their private information to the pool. It is the loud, certain voice that starts a herd. Turn-taking evenness is, in the vocabulary of this series, a measure of how much independence survives contact — the human-scale echo of the ensemble's decorrelation (item 24), where a model's accuracy comes not from any one predictor's strength but from keeping the predictors from collapsing into agreement. A group with even turn-taking keeps its members' contributions decorrelated; a dominated group correlates them onto a single voice and loses the cancellation that made the crowd wise in the first place.

Read this way, *c* is the process reading of machinery the movement has been building in parts. Surowiecki's four conditions (item 20) — independence, diversity, decentralisation, aggregation — are the engineering specification; even turn-taking and social sensitivity are what two of those conditions, independence and honest aggregation, look like when you watch a real group in a real room. The diversity prediction theorem (item 19) proves that spread subtracts error; *c* shows that spread only helps if the group's process lets the diverse voices actually reach the table. And it points forward, past Book One, to the institutions that try to engineer high *c* on purpose: deliberative democracy (item 72) and the disciplined machinery of jury deliberation (item 74), both of which are, at bottom, attempts to force even turn-taking and mutual attention onto groups that would otherwise let a few voices decide.

## The human weight

The frontier reading of *c* is sharp, and it should be stated soberly, because it turns a fairness argument into an intelligence argument.

Most human groups do not take turns evenly. They take turns by rank. The senior speak and the junior listen; men speak and women are interrupted; the high-caste pronounce and the low-caste defer; the credentialed hold the floor and the rest wait to be asked. These are not accidents of personality. They are the ordinary operating settings of hierarchy — arrangements that, by design, concentrate the conversation in a few authorised voices and quiet everyone else.

Woolley's finding says something uncomfortable about all of it. A group organised so that a few dominate the talking is, measurably, a *less intelligent* group. The domination does not merely offend against fairness; it lowers collective IQ, because it shrinks the pool of what the group can use to whatever the dominant few already think. Enforced silence is not only an injury to the silenced. It is a lobotomy performed on the group. When a hierarchy suppresses its juniors, its women, its low-status members, it is not just being unjust; it is throwing away information and paying for it in worse decisions. The turn-taking and social-sensitivity results are, read plainly, a scientific case against domination as a mode of organising — not on moral grounds, though those hold, but on the grounds that domination makes groups stupid.

This is the exact seam where Book One hands off to the darkness of Book Two. A group can look unanimous and confident while being, in fact, an amputated version of itself — because the members who disagree have read the room and gone quiet. That gap between what people privately think and what the room appears to believe is pluralistic ignorance (item 42) and preference falsification (item 34), the twin engines by which silence sustains a norm no one has actually endorsed. Stratification (item 85) is what those engines build over time: an order held in place not by agreement but by the suppression of the very turn-taking that would reveal the disagreement — and, in *c*'s terms, held in place by keeping the group's collective intelligence low enough that it never notices what it has silenced. The chapter that measures the wise crowd's engine turns out, in its last reading, to point straight at the mechanism of the unwise one.

## The critique

The influence of *c* has outrun its evidence, and the series' replication-humility rule bites hard here. The 2010 result is truly important and truly contested, and both halves of that sentence belong in the record.

Begin with the interpretation. A 2011 comment in *Intelligence* by Michael Woodley and Edward Bell pushed back on the *Science* paper, arguing that what Woolley and colleagues had extracted might be read differently — that the collective-intelligence factor might be, mostly, a group-level manifestation of the "general factor of personality," and that a general factor pulled from correlated performances need not be the group-level intelligence it was billed as. The debate rhymes with a long-running one about *g* itself and about the general factor of personality: whenever you extract a first factor from correlated measures, you have to ask whether you have found a thing or merely a shadow of your correlations.

Then the components. The "Reading the Mind in the Eyes" test, on which the social-sensitivity finding rests, is itself much debated — over what it actually measures, how stable it is, and whether it captures social sensitivity or something narrower like emotion vocabulary or test-taking skill. If the instrument is shaky, the headline mechanism built on it is shaky by inheritance. The gender effect, mediated through that same test, is only as robust as the mediation, and later work has found the social-sensitivity and gender links less consistent than the original framing suggested.

Then replication. The picture is mixed rather than damning. Some studies do recover a general collective-intelligence factor — Engel and colleagues, in 2014, found evidence for *c* in both face-to-face and online groups, suggesting the factor is not merely an artifact of people being in the same room, and later work by Riedl, Woolley, and others has continued to develop and, in places, support the construct. But other replications and meta-analytic looks have found the *c* factor weaker than the headline, the effect sizes modest, and the specific predictors — especially the social-sensitivity and gender links — less reliable than the clean 2010 story implied. The original samples were small, drawn from the usual WEIRD pool of Western students and convenience participants, and the effect sizes throughout are moderate, not the towering correlations that fund a paradigm.

So the honest position is this. That group performance shows some general consistency across tasks is a reasonable and partly replicated claim. That this consistency is best called a *collective intelligence factor* strictly parallel to *g* is a strong claim still under argument. That even turn-taking and social sensitivity matter to group performance is supported from several directions and coheres with everything else in this movement — which is a reason to take it seriously and a reason not to lean on the single 2010 study as if it were settled. The capstone of Book One is not a proof. It is the best current measurement of a thing the whole movement has argued must exist, offered with its error bars showing, and pointed at the darkness the next book will name.
