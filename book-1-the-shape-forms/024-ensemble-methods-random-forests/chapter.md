# 24. Ensemble methods & random forests

> Machine learning rediscovered the wisdom of crowds — and then had to prove, in code, that the crowd is only wise if you force its members to disagree.

## The idea

Suppose you want to predict something — whether a loan will default, what a tumour is, which film a stranger will love — and you have a learning algorithm that builds a model from data. You could train the best single model you can and trust it. Or you could do something that sounds, at first, like a waste of effort: train many models, each a little different, each individually mediocre, and let them vote. Average their answers on a number; take the majority on a category. The remarkable finding, proven both in theory and across three decades of practice, is that the committee reliably beats its best member. A crowd of weak models is stronger than any strong model you could have built alone.

This is the family of techniques called *ensemble methods*, and they are the machine-learning mirror of everything this movement has argued about human crowds. The name for the workhorse version is the *random forest*: a committee of hundreds or thousands of decision trees, each grown on a random slice of the data and — this is the crucial twist — allowed to look at only a random subset of the available clues at each branching point. No single tree is trusted. Each is a rough, overconfident, easily-fooled little expert. But the forest, taking a vote, is one of the most accurate general-purpose predictors ever devised.

The reason the trick works turns out to be the same reason Galton's crowd guessed the ox's weight, and it comes with the same fatal condition attached. The models must be *diverse* — more precisely, their errors must be *decorrelated*. Build a thousand nearly-identical models and you have not built a wise crowd. You have built a thousand-voiced echo of one opinion, and it will be wrong in unison exactly when its single ancestor would have been wrong. Ensemble learning is the wise crowd rendered in silicon, and its central discovery is that diversity is not a nicety but a load-bearing requirement you must engineer on purpose.

## The origin

The intellectual line runs through one figure more than any other: Leo Breiman (1928–2005), a statistician at Berkeley who had spent years as a consultant before returning to academia, and who came back with a working practitioner's contempt for elegant models that lost to crude ones on real data. In 1996, in the journal *Machine Learning*, Breiman published "Bagging predictors" — *bagging* being his contraction of *bootstrap aggregating*. The idea was almost embarrassingly simple. Take your dataset. Draw from it, with replacement, a new dataset of the same size — a *bootstrap sample*, in which some rows appear twice and others not at all. Train a model on it. Repeat many times, each on a fresh resample, then average the models' predictions. The averaged predictor, Breiman showed, was more accurate and more stable than any single model trained on the original data, especially for "unstable" learners like decision trees whose structure lurches wildly when the data shifts.

Five years later, in 2001, again in *Machine Learning*, Breiman published "Random Forests," which added the second and more important dose of randomness. Bagging alone gave you trees grown on different data — but the trees still tended to resemble one another, because a few strong predictive features would dominate the top of nearly every tree. So Breiman crippled them deliberately: at each split, he let a tree consider only a random handful of the features, not all of them. This forced different trees to build themselves around different clues, driving down the correlation *between* the trees. The forest became truly diverse, and its accuracy jumped.

Running alongside Breiman's averaging approach was a second tradition, *boosting*, which combined weak models not in parallel but in sequence. Its theoretical origin lies in a question Michael Kearns and Leslie Valiant posed in the late 1980s — could a merely "weak" learner, one only slightly better than chance, be *boosted* into a strong one? Robert Schapire proved in 1990 that the answer was yes, and with Yoav Freund he developed the practical algorithm AdaBoost, set out in their 1997 paper in the *Journal of Computer and System Sciences*. Boosting trains models one after another, and each new model is made to focus on the examples its predecessors got wrong. Gradient boosting later generalised the idea into one of the most dominant predictive tools in modern practice. Two lineages, then — Breiman's parallel averaging and Schapire and Freund's sequential correction — but a single underlying discovery: that many imperfect learners, properly combined, beat one polished one. By 2000, Thomas Dietterich's survey of ensemble methods could treat this not as a curiosity but as a settled pillar of the field.

## The mechanism

The mathematics is the mathematics of item 17 and item 21 wearing new notation, and it is worth stating plainly because it is the whole argument.

Every model's error can be decomposed into three parts: *bias*, how wrong the model is on average; *variance*, how much its answer jitters as the training data changes; and irreducible noise. A single deep decision tree is a low-bias, high-variance beast — it can fit almost anything, which means it also chases the accidents in whatever particular data it was shown. Averaging attacks the variance term directly. If you average the predictions of many models, and their errors are *independent*, the variance of the average shrinks in proportion to the number of models. Ten decorrelated predictors cut the error variance to roughly a tenth. This is nothing more than the fact that independent errors cancel — the same law that let a hundred fairground guesses converge on the true weight of an ox.

But read the condition again, because it is where everything turns. The variance falls by a factor of the number of models *only if their errors are independent*. If the models are correlated — if they tend to be wrong on the same cases, in the same direction — then averaging them barely helps. In the limit, a thousand perfectly-correlated models carry exactly as much information as one, and average to exactly its error. The formula for the variance of an average of correlated predictors has two terms: one that shrinks as you add models, and one, proportional to the average correlation between them, that does not shrink at all. That second term is the floor. It is why "just add more models" hits a wall, and it is the mathematical statement of a claim this book has been making about people since its opening pages.

This is precisely why Breiman's feature randomness was the decisive move rather than a refinement. Bagging reduces correlation somewhat; random feature selection reduces it a great deal, by preventing every tree from leaning on the same dominant clues. The forest's accuracy is governed, Breiman showed, by two quantities in tension: the *strength* of the individual trees and the *correlation* between them. You want strong trees that nonetheless disagree. Push diversity too far and the trees become useless individually; allow too little and their errors stack. The random forest is an engine for sitting at the sweet spot — and the engineer's job is to hold it there by manufacturing disagreement.

## The anchor example

Picture the forest literally as a committee, because that is what it is. A hospital wants to predict, from a battery of measurements, whether a patient's tumour is malignant. It could hire one brilliant diagnostician — a single decision tree, grown to full depth, that has memorised every quirk of the cases it trained on. That expert is dazzling on familiar patients and brittle on new ones; shift the data slightly and its confident rules reshuffle.

Instead the hospital convenes five hundred lesser diagnosticians. Each is handed a random resample of the patient records — some cases duplicated, some missing — so no two see quite the same history. And each labours under an odd restriction: at every decision it must make, it is allowed to consult only a random few of the available measurements. One tree, forbidden at a key juncture from looking at tumour size, is forced to reason from cell texture instead. Another, blind to texture, leans on the pattern of the cell nuclei. Each individual diagnostician is, by design, somewhat handicapped and easily fooled. But because they have been forced to build their judgments around *different evidence*, they are fooled by different things. When a new patient arrives, all five hundred vote, and the majority verdict is taken. The cases where one tree's blind spot would have produced a confident error are precisely the cases where the other four hundred and ninety-nine, not sharing that blind spot, outvote it. The committee of handicapped experts routinely outperforms the single brilliant one — not despite the handicaps, but because of them. The deliberate crippling is what keeps their errors from lining up.

## The flip

Here is the sentence this chapter exists to deliver: ensemble learning is the wisdom of crowds proven in silicon, and in proving it, it makes the series' central condition *unmissable* — because the engineer who forgets to decorrelate the models watches the ensemble fail, every time, in a way no amount of scale will rescue.

When a human crowd's errors correlate, the failure is slow and deniable; you can tell yourself the group was simply unlucky. When a random forest's trees correlate, the failure is instant and quantified — the accuracy stalls, the held-out error refuses to drop, and the diagnosis is written in the correlation term of a variance formula. Machine learning thus does something the social sciences never quite could: it converts the thesis of this movement from an argument into an engineering constraint. Galton's ox (item 17) suggested that averaging diverse guesses cancels error. Scott Page's diversity prediction theorem (item 19) made it an identity — collective error equals average individual error minus diversity, so diversity is *subtracted* from error as a matter of arithmetic. Independent-error cancellation (item 21) named the condition. Condorcet (item 18) supplied the underlying law. Random forests take all four and *build a machine on them* — and the machine only runs if you enforce, by hand, the independence that human crowds enjoy only by luck. Breiman's random feature selection is the algorithmic version of item 21's whole demand: it is independence, manufactured on purpose because the mathematics will not forgive its absence.

And the dark twin is right there in the same code. It is *model monoculture*: the condition in which everyone trains on the same data with the same method and arrives at the same model — a thousand-voiced echo of one opinion, wrong in unison. This is the algorithmic face of institutional herding (item 63), the mechanism behind the correlated risk models that let the whole financial system misprice the same mortgages at once before 2008 (the bubbles of item 64), and the shape of the algorithmic monoculture that emerges when every platform runs a near-identical recommender (item 87). Correlated models are as useless as a room of yes-men — the ensemble and the boardroom fail for the identical reason. The wise crowd and the mob are the same crowd; the decorrelated forest and the monoculture are the same algorithm, run under two conditions.

## The human weight

The reason this matters beyond the leaderboard is that ensembles turn a social hope into a design principle, and design principles travel. When Breiman writes that a forest's error is bounded by the correlation between its trees, he has handed every institution a template it can copy: if you want a group to be reliably right, do not merely gather many judges — gather judges who will be wrong in *different* ways, and protect their differences. This is the case for truly independent teams, for red teams whose job is to fail in the directions the main team cannot see, for combining methods rather than perfecting one. "Diversity is subtracted from error" stopped being a slogan the moment it became the load-bearing equation of a tool that runs on every phone and in every hospital. Enforced homogeneity is now a *measurable* loss of accuracy, not a matter of taste — which is exactly the reinforcement the frontier argument of this series needs. Where an order imposes sameness of judgment, it is not merely being unjust; it is degrading the collective's capacity to be right, and the degradation can be written down.

The danger runs the other way with equal force, and it is the more urgent one. The world is increasingly run by models, and the models increasingly rhyme. When every bank uses similar risk models fed similar data, when every trading desk hedges against the same scenarios, when every platform's recommender learns from the same behavioural signals, the global system quietly becomes a monoculture — an ensemble that has forgotten to decorrelate itself. Such a system is efficient and accurate right up to the moment its shared blind spot is tested, at which point every model fails together, because they were never truly independent to begin with. The lesson of the random forest, addressed to a civilisation building its institutions out of correlated algorithms, is stark: an ensemble that has stopped disagreeing has stopped being wise, however many members it has. That warning connects directly to this series' later arguments about diversity destroyed (item 85) and about the recommender monocultures that flatten a plural culture into one feedback loop (item 87).

## The critique

Ensembles are not magic, and honesty about their limits is part of the point.

The first cost is interpretability. A single decision tree is a thing a human can read — a chain of yes-or-no questions ending in a verdict, auditable and explainable to the person it judges. A random forest of five hundred such trees is a black box; it may be highly accurate and yet unable to tell you *why*, in terms a loan applicant or a patient could challenge. Ensembles buy accuracy with legibility, and in domains where the reason for a decision has legal or moral weight, that is not a free trade.

The second and deeper limit is that decorrelation *within* the ensemble does nothing about bias *in the data*. The whole variance-reduction argument assumes the models' errors point in different directions and cancel. But if every base learner is trained on the same skewed data — a facial-recognition forest trained mostly on light-skinned faces, a hiring model trained on a firm's biased history — then every tree shares the same blind spot, their errors do *not* cancel because they are not independent errors at all but a common bias, and the forest confidently amplifies it. Engineered diversity among the trees is no defence against a defect they all inherit from the data. The crowd of models is only as unbiased as the world it was shown.

Boosting adds its own failure mode: because each round chases the examples the last got wrong, it can end up fitting the *noise* in the data, hunting mislabelled or freak cases as if they were signal, and overfitting in ways bagging resists. And the whole enterprise obeys diminishing returns — the correlation floor in the variance formula guarantees that beyond some point, adding models buys almost nothing but compute. "Just add more learners" is not a strategy; past the sweet spot it is waste. The random forest earns its place in this book not because it is invincible but because its failures are so legible: it succeeds exactly when its members are diverse and its data is fair, and it fails exactly when they are not — which is the thesis of the whole movement, compiled and run.
