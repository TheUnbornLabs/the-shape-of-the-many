# References — 11. The predator confusion effect

## Confirmed sources

- Neill, S. R. St. J. & Cullen, J. M. "Experiments on whether schooling by their prey affects the hunting behaviour of cephalopods and fish predators." *Journal of Zoology*, 172(4), 549–569, 1974. — The foundational quantitative result that predators' capture success per strike declines as prey school size increases; core empirical support for beats 2 and 3.

- Krakauer, D. C. "Groups confuse predators by exploiting perceptual bottlenecks: a connectionist model of the confusion effect." *Behavioral Ecology and Sociobiology*, 36(6), 421–429, 1995. — Neural-network model showing why numerous, identical prey overload a predator's target-tracking system; source of the perceptual-overload mechanism and the oddity-effect corollary in beats 3, 4, and 7.

- Landeau, L. & Terborgh, J. "Oddity and the 'confusion effect' in predation." *Animal Behaviour*, 34(5), 1372–1380, 1986. — Experiments with largemouth bass (*Micropterus salmoides*) preying on silvery minnows (*Hybognathus nuchalis*); a single visually odd individual suffered disproportionate predation, demonstrating the oddity effect. Support for beats 3, 5, and the critique in beat 7.

- Miller, Robert C. "The significance of the gregarious habit." *Ecology*, 3(2), 122–126, 1922. — Early naturalist argument that grouping carries anti-predator advantages, including the difficulty a predator has singling out one individual from an aggregation; the historical "first noticing" cited in beat 2.
