# 21. Independent-error cancellation

> Average enough honest mistakes and they annihilate each other; average enough shared mistakes and they add up. Same arithmetic, opposite fate — and only one word decides which.

## The idea

Here is the plainest possible statement of why a crowd can be wiser than any of its members. Suppose there is a true quantity — the weight of an ox, the mass of an electron, the number of beans in a jar — and suppose that when a person estimates it they land not on the truth but somewhere near it, sometimes high, sometimes low. Call the gap between their guess and the truth their error. Now collect many such guesses and take the average. Provided the errors are scattered independently on either side of the truth, the ones that ran high will be cancelled by the ones that ran low, and the average will sit closer to the true value than a typical single guess. Add more guesses and the cancellation tightens further. The average converges on the truth, and it does so at a rate that can be written down exactly: the spread of the averaged estimate shrinks in proportion to one over the square root of the number of guesses. Four times as many people, half the residual error. A hundred times as many, a tenth.

That is the whole engine of crowd wisdom, stripped of ceremony. It is not that crowds contain hidden genius. It is that random error is a coin that comes up heads and tails in roughly equal measure, and if you flip it enough times the surplus of heads over tails, as a fraction of the total, drifts toward nothing.

But read the setup again and notice the load-bearing word. *Independently.* The cancellation is a fact about errors that are unrelated to one another — one person's overestimate has nothing to do with the next person's. Remove that condition and the machine does not merely weaken; it reverses its promise. If every guesser is wrong in the same direction — because they all inherited the same faulty assumption, or all heard the same wrong rumour, or all copied one another — then their errors do not cancel. They stack. Averaging a thousand people who are all biased high gives you, with great precision, a number that is biased high. The crowd's confidence grows while its accuracy does not move. This chapter is about that single hinge, because it is the mechanical heart of everything Movement III has been circling, and the exact seam along which the wise crowd tears into the mob.

## The origin

The mathematics arrived in pieces across two centuries, each piece answering a practical fear.

The oldest piece is the law of large numbers, proved by Jacob Bernoulli and published posthumously in his *Ars Conjectandi* (1713). Bernoulli asked the question a gambler or an insurer asks: if a thing happens with some fixed underlying probability, does watching many trials let me pin that probability down? His answer, which he called his "golden theorem," was yes — the observed frequency converges on the true probability as trials accumulate. That is error-cancellation in its first dress: the fluctuations of individual outcomes wash out in the long-run average.

The second piece is the central limit theorem, whose modern form was assembled by Pierre-Simon Laplace in the early nineteenth century and refined for another hundred years after. It says something sharper than Bernoulli: not merely that the average converges, but *how* the sum of many small independent influences distributes itself — into the bell-shaped normal curve, with a spread that narrows in that characteristic one-over-root-N way. This is the theorem that turns a vague promise of cancellation into a rate.

The third piece is the statistical theory of errors, built for astronomers and geodesists who were drowning in slightly discordant measurements of the same star or the same meridian. Carl Friedrich Gauss (his *Theoria Motus* of 1809 and later work) and Laplace developed the method of least squares and the treatment of observational error that let a scientist combine many imperfect readings into one best estimate — and, crucially, taught the discipline to distinguish two species of error that behave under averaging in opposite ways. Random errors, scattering symmetrically, obey the cancellation law. Systematic errors — a mis-calibrated instrument, a consistent parallax, a warped ruler — do not scatter symmetrically; they push every reading the same way, and no amount of averaging removes them. That distinction, drawn two hundred years ago in observatories, is the entire moral of this chapter.

## The mechanism

Model any judgment as a sum of two parts: **signal plus noise.** The signal is the truth the person is trying to recover; the noise is their individual error. Write each estimate as the true value plus an error term. When you average N estimates, the true value — being identical in every one — passes through the averaging untouched. It is the error terms that get averaged, and their fate is the whole story.

If the errors are independent and centred on zero — as many above the truth as below, unrelated across people — then averaging them is like averaging a swarm of random pushes in every direction. They partially cancel. The mathematics of variance makes this precise: when you average N independent quantities each with spread σ, the average has spread σ divided by the square root of N. That is the famous standard error of the mean. The residual error does not vanish, but it decays, predictably, as the square root of the crowd. This is the sunny face of the machine, and it is truly remarkable: mediocre, uncoordinated judges, none of them expert, collectively home in on a truth none of them individually knew.

Now introduce correlation. Suppose the errors are not independent but share a common component — everyone tugged the same way by a shared assumption. The correlation coefficient ρ measures how much of each person's error is this shared push versus their own private scatter. The variance of the average no longer collapses cleanly. Work through the arithmetic and a second term survives: as N grows large, the spread of the average does not fall toward zero but toward a floor set by ρ — roughly, the square root of ρ times the original spread. When ρ is zero, the floor is zero and you recover the full one-over-root-N gain. When ρ is one — everyone's error identical — the floor is the entire original error, and averaging a million people buys you exactly what averaging one person did. The **effective sample size** collapses. A crowd of a million with ρ near one has the error-cancelling power of a crowd of, roughly, one.

And note what averaging never touches: the mean of the errors. If the errors are not centred on zero — if there is a shared bias, a systematic offset that every judge carries — that offset is the one part of the error that survives averaging completely intact, no matter how large N grows or how independent the private scatter is. Averaging suppresses variance. It is helpless against bias. This is the ghost that haunts every claim about the wisdom of crowds: the machine only cleans up the random part, and only when the errors are uncorrelated.

## The anchor example

Picture two jars.

The first is a physical constant. In the decades of measuring the speed of light or the charge on the electron, dozens of laboratories, using different apparatus in different countries, produced readings that disagreed at the far decimal places. Plot them and they scatter — some high, some low — around a value none of them hit exactly. Average them, weighting by their precision, and the composite lands closer to the truth than any single lab's reading, because the idiosyncratic quirks of each apparatus, being unrelated to one another, cancel. This is why science pools measurements at all; the pooled figure is better than its parts precisely because the parts erred independently.

Now the failure case, and it is not hypothetical. The history of measured constants contains a notorious pattern in which successive measurements cluster tightly around a *wrong* value for years before lurching to the correct one — because later experimenters, knowing the accepted figure, unconsciously discarded readings that strayed too far from it and trusted those that agreed. Their errors were no longer independent; each was correlated with the published consensus. The tight clustering that looked like precision was manufactured agreement, and averaging it only sharpened confidence in a number that was off. The instruments differed, but the minds behind them shared a bias, and the bias survived every average. *(The specific numerical drift in particular constants — Millikan's electron charge and later "bandwagon" episodes — is Further reading to verify before final publication.)*

The jellybean jar tells the same fable in a fairground. Let a hundred people guess independently, each seeing only the jar, and the average of their guesses lands eerily close to the true count — Galton's ox in miniature (item 17). But let one loud person announce "I reckon it's about four hundred" before the others write down their answers, or let a rumour circulate that "the label said around four hundred," and watch the guesses pull toward four hundred. If the true count is six hundred, you have just injected a shared error into every judgment, and the average — once a near-oracle — now reports four hundred with a straight face. Nothing about the arithmetic of averaging changed. Only the independence did.

## The flip

This is the exact hinge on which the entire series turns, and it can be stated in a single sentence: **the wise crowd and the mob run the same averaging machine, and the only difference between them is whether the errors are independent.** There are not two mechanisms, one benign and one malign. There is one mechanism — signal plus noise, aggregated — and one dial, the correlation of the errors. Turn ρ toward zero and the noise cancels: wisdom. Turn ρ toward one and the noise stacks: folly. The crowd does not change species when it goes wrong. Its errors change from independent to correlated, and the same machine that was subtracting error starts preserving it.

Every optimistic result in this book is a special case of holding ρ near zero, and every catastrophe is a case of driving it toward one. Condorcet's jury theorem (item 18) is this chapter's binary twin: its guarantee that majorities converge on truth is precisely a guarantee that independent errors cancel across voters, and it collapses the instant votes correlate. Galton's ox (item 17) is the continuous demonstration of the sunny face. The diversity prediction theorem (item 19) is this same law read as a decomposition — crowd error equals average individual error minus diversity — where "diversity" is nothing but the extent to which errors point in different directions and cancel. Surowiecki's four conditions (item 20) put *independence* first among the four because it is the condition this chapter shows to be load-bearing. And the dark twins are all machines for manufacturing correlation: information cascades (item 26), where people copy predecessors and stop contributing independent signal; emotional contagion (item 33), where a shared feeling sweeps identical error into every mind at once; echo chambers (item 50), where the diversity of sources is engineered away; institutional herding (item 63), where analysts and firms converge on the same forecast for fear of standing alone. Each of them works by the identical sabotage: raise ρ, collapse the effective sample size, and let the crowd's confidence outrun its accuracy. The Shape that forms so beautifully when errors are independent is the same Shape that breaks when they are made to align.

## The human weight

The practical corollary is that collective accuracy is an achievement of institutional design, not a gift of numbers, and the thing being designed is independence. A free press of many truly separate outlets cancels the errors of any one newsroom; a press consolidated into a handful of owners, or captured by a state and made to repeat one line, has ρ near one, and its thousand voices carry no more independent evidence than a single mouth. Independent scientific replication — different teams, different labs, different assumptions — cancels the idiosyncratic errors of the original study; a monoculture in which everyone uses the same method and cites the same authorities correlates the errors and can lock a field onto a shared mistake for a generation. The secret ballot cancels the errors of individual voters by letting each decide alone; open voting under the eyes of a patron or a mob correlates every vote with the visible expectation, and the tally, however lopsided, measures pressure rather than judgment.

So the destruction of collective accuracy rarely requires changing anyone's mind. It requires only making the errors point the same way — capturing the outlets, enforcing the monoculture, coordinating the message, exposing the ballot. Each is a technology for raising ρ. And this is the bridge to the frontier this series keeps returning to. Preference falsification (item 34) is the deliberate correlation of everyone's *stated* errors: an order that compels public agreement is not persuading its subjects but forcing their expressed judgments into lockstep, driving the effective independence of the whole society toward zero. A caste order sustains itself not by winning an honest average but by destroying the conditions under which an honest average could form — by making sure that what each person says is a copy of what everyone else appears to say. Read against this chapter, imposed public unanimity is exactly ρ engineered to one: a crowd made to look certain by the surgical removal of the independence that was its only route to truth.

## The critique

The machinery is necessary, but it flatters. Perfect independence is a fiction: real human judgments are never uncorrelated, because people share a language, a culture, an education, a common stock of half-true information. Some shared error is always present, so the honest ρ is never zero, and the clean one-over-root-N gain is an upper bound the world does not reach. Real crowds only approximate the machine; the standard error of the mean is the best case, not the expected one.

Worse, the whole result assumes errors are centred on zero — no shared bias — and that assumption is frequently false in exactly the cases that matter. A crowd that is collectively mis-informed does not have zero-mean errors; it has a systematic offset that averaging cannot see, let alone remove. The machine is silent about bias and loud about noise, which means it is most confident precisely where a shared prejudice would leave no trace in the spread of the guesses. A tight, unanimous crowd can be tightly, unanimously wrong, and the arithmetic will report high precision either way.

There is a subtler failure too. Averaging assumes the truth is a single number the guesses surround. When a group is truly split — half convinced of one thing, half of its opposite — the average lands in an empty middle that no one believes and that may correspond to nothing real. The mean of a bimodal disagreement is a statistical artefact, and reporting it as "the crowd's answer" hides the very fact that matters: that there was no crowd, only two crowds. Independent-error cancellation is the clean floor beneath collective wisdom, the theory that says why it can work at all. But it is a theory of an idealised crowd, and the distance between that crowd and any real one — always somewhat correlated, sometimes biased, occasionally split — is the space in which every chapter of this book, wise and mad alike, actually lives.
