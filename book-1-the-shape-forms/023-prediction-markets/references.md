# References — 23. Prediction markets

## Confirmed sources

- Hayek, Friedrich A. "The Use of Knowledge in Society." *American Economic Review*, vol. 35, no. 4, 1945, pp. 519–530. — The foundational argument that dispersed private knowledge is aggregated by the price system; the intellectual seed of prediction markets and the source of the "communicating information" framing.
- Forsythe, Robert, Forrest Nelson, George R. Neumann, and Jack Wright. "Anatomy of an Experimental Political Stock Market." *American Economic Review*, vol. 82, no. 5, 1992, pp. 1142–1161. — First formal report of the Iowa Electronic Markets (launched 1988); documents that market prices often forecast election outcomes more accurately than polls, and analyses trader behaviour and the marginal-trader mechanism.
- Wolfers, Justin, and Eric Zitzewitz. "Prediction Markets." *Journal of Economic Perspectives*, vol. 18, no. 2, 2004, pp. 107–126. — Survey defining the field: how contract prices map to probabilities, evidence on accuracy across domains, favourite-longshot bias, manipulation, thinness, and the DARPA episode.
- Hanson, Robin. "Could Gambling Save Science? Encouraging an Honest Consensus." *Social Epistemology*, vol. 9, no. 1, 1995, pp. 3–33. — Hanson's "idea futures" proposal; his later work develops decision/decision-conditional markets. Supports the origin beat's account of Hanson.
- Keynes, John Maynard. *The General Theory of Employment, Interest and Money*, Macmillan, 1936, ch. 12. — The "beauty contest" analogy for markets pricing other participants' expectations rather than fundamentals; underpins beat 5's account of the bubble mode (cross-ref item 61).

## Further reading to verify before final publication

- Iowa Electronic Markets trader demographics ("young, male, educated, disproportionately Iowan") — directionally supported by the IEM research literature (e.g., Berg, Forsythe, Nelson, Rietz analyses), but the specific demographic characterisation should be checked against a primary IEM study before final publication.
- The claim that IEM final prices "matched or beat" contemporaneous polls across a run of presidential elections — broadly supported by Berg, Nelson & Rietz and related IEM accuracy studies, but the exact scope and any counterexamples should be confirmed and dated before publication.
- DARPA Policy Analysis Market / FutureMAP (2003): the program existed and was cancelled within days after public outcry led by U.S. senators (notably framed as "terrorism futures"). The precise contract scope, the accuracy of the "betting on assassinations" characterisation (widely reported as exaggerated relative to the actual proposal), the exact dates, and the roles of specific officials should be verified against primary sources (e.g., the Wolfers–Zitzewitz survey, contemporaneous reporting, and DARPA/Congressional records) before publication.
- The IEM operating under a CFTC no-action letter as a limited academic exercise — accurate in broad outline; confirm the exact regulatory instrument, date, and conditions before final publication.
- Robin Hanson's coinage and dating of "decision markets" as distinct from "idea futures" — confirm terminology and first-use dates against Hanson's primary papers.
