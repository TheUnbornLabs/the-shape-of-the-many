# 17. Galton's ox

> Eight hundred people who each guessed wrong guessed the ox's weight together to within a pound — and the man who ran the experiment had expected them to prove the opposite.

## The idea

Ask a large number of people to estimate some quantity that has a true value — the weight of an animal, the number of jelly beans in a jar — and throw away every individual answer, keeping only the aggregate: the average, or the middlemost value. That single pooled figure will often sit startlingly close to the truth, closer than the great majority of the guesses that went into it, and sometimes closer than any expert in the room. No one had to be right. The crowd was right anyway.

This is the founding demonstration of what a later century would name the wisdom of crowds, and it is the continuous-variable cousin of Condorcet's binary jury (item 18). Where Condorcet asks a yes-or-no question and takes the majority, Galton's ox asks *how much* and takes the average. The claim is the same underneath: a collection of independent, imperfect judgments carries, buried in its scatter, a signal that aggregation can extract and error cannot survive. Each estimate is the truth plus a personal mistake; pool enough of them and the mistakes, pointing every which way, tend to cancel, leaving the thing they were all circling.

It seems to get something for nothing. It does not: what it requires — the whole argument that follows — is that the guesses be *independent*. The moment the crowd starts guessing together, watching each other, the mistakes stop cancelling and begin to compound, and the same crowd that found the ox to within a pound can lose it entirely.

## The origin

The demonstration belongs to Francis Galton (1822–1911) and to a single short paper, "Vox Populi," which appeared in *Nature* in March 1907. Galton was then in his mid-eighties, a Victorian polymath of uncomfortable range — a pioneer of statistics who gave us correlation and regression, and, less forgivably, the man who coined the word *eugenics* and spent his last decades arguing that human quality could and should be bred. He was, by temperament and conviction, a believer in expertise and no democrat; he did not go looking for a hymn to the common man.

The occasion was a fat-stock and poultry exhibition in Plymouth, England. Among the sideshows was a weight-judging competition: for a small fee, entrants guessed the weight of an ox as it would be *after it had been slaughtered and dressed*, and the closest won prizes. About 800 people paid to enter. Galton obtained the used tickets — 787 of them legible enough to analyse — and treated the competition as an unplanned experiment in collective judgment, expecting, he as much as admitted, to find the folly of the crowd laid out in numbers.

What he found surprised him. He arranged the 787 estimates in order and took the median — the "middlemost" value, which he regarded as the democratic figure because it gives every voter, sensible or foolish, exactly one equal vote. The median guess was 1,207 lb. The dressed ox actually weighed 1,198 lb — the crowd's collective verdict was wrong by nine pounds, less than one per cent. Galton, whose priors ran entirely the other way, wrote it up honestly: the result, he concluded, was "more creditable to the trustworthiness of a democratic judgment than might have been expected" — as close to a recantation as data could compel from a lifelong sceptic. The paper then slept, a curiosity, until James Surowiecki opened his 2004 book *The Wisdom of Crowds* with it and turned Galton's ox into the founding parable of an entire popular field.

## The mechanism

The gears are the same ones that drive Condorcet, on a continuous dial rather than a binary switch. Think of each estimate as two parts: the true weight of the ox, the same for everyone, plus an error term, different for everyone. That error is where the individuality lives — one person overweights because the beast looked huge, another underweights from caution, a butcher shades it by trade knowledge.

Now aggregate. If the errors are *independent* — each person's mistake their own — something clean happens. The true-weight part, identical across everyone, survives the averaging untouched. The error part, a scatter of pluses and minuses drawn independently, partly cancels: for every guess that ran too high, there tends to be one that ran too low, and their excesses subtract. The more independent estimates you pool, the more completely the errors annihilate each other, and the more nakedly the shared signal — the ox itself — stands out. This is the law of large numbers in everyday clothes: the same independent-error cancellation that item 21 states in general form, and the same logic that made Condorcet's wrong majorities improbable, since independent mistakes stacking the same way is a coincidence, and coincidences of independent events get rare, fast.

A subtler ingredient, which item 19 — the diversity prediction theorem — makes exact: a crowd's collective error is provably its average individual error *minus* the diversity of its guesses. Spread the guesses out and you subtract more error; make everyone think alike and the subtraction shrinks toward nothing.

Which is why the whole mechanism hangs on independence: every term in that cancellation assumed the errors were drawn separately. Correlate them — a common rumour, a loud early guess, a shared bias — and the pluses stop matching the minuses. The errors no longer scatter around the truth; they pile up on one side of it, and the averaging meant to be the crowd's salvation now faithfully returns a shared mistake as wisdom.

## The anchor example

So picture the thing itself. Plymouth, a raw exhibition hall smelling of straw and livestock, the ox standing in its pen, indifferent, enormous. A stream of fairgoers files past — perhaps 800 across the day — and each writes down a number: what this animal will weigh once slaughtered and dressed. Some are experts of a kind: butchers who have broken down a thousand carcasses, farmers who have sold cattle by the hundredweight. Most are not — shopkeepers, labourers, children with a penny to spend, whose entire acquaintance with beef is the joint on Sunday. They do not confer. Each guesses alone, drops the ticket in the box, and moves on. That is the crucial fact, hiding in plain sight: nobody hears anybody else's answer.

The individual guesses are all over the map — a smear of human fallibility; read one by one, they look like noise. But Galton did not read them one by one. He ranked all 787 and put his finger on the middle. The middlemost guess: 1,207 lb. The truth: 1,198 lb. Nine pounds apart.

And there is a coda that made the parable famous — and, as we will see, slightly misleading. The *mean* of the same 787 estimates comes out at 1,197 lb: within a single pound of the true 1,198. That is the near-magical "within one pound" Surowiecki's readers remember. It is real — but it is not quite the number Galton himself leaned on, and the gap is where this chapter's final beat begins. For now, hold the image: a crowd of amateurs, guessing in ignorance and isolation, that between them knew the weight of an ox better than almost any single one of them did.

## The flip

This is the optimistic pole's founding image — the picture the entire wise-crowd movement is built to explain — and one half of the pairing this whole series turns on. State the thesis in its plainest form, with Galton's own crowd as the specimen: **the wise crowd and the mob are the same people, and independence is the switch between them.** Not different crowds — the same crowd, under two conditions.

Run the counterfactual. Take those exact 787 people, and before they guess, let them *hear each other* — let a confident voice near the front of the queue call out "fourteen hundred!" and pass it down the line, and let the fairgoers, uncertain and social as people are, shade their private estimates toward what everyone around them seems to be saying. Now the errors are no longer independent. They no longer scatter above and below the truth and cancel; they correlate — they lean, together, toward that anchoring shout — and the median and the mean lean with them. The crowd converges not on the ox but on a shared error, and the averaging still runs, faithfully delivering the wrong answer with the same mechanical confidence it delivered the right one. The ox has become, in miniature, a mania.

That names the exact dark twin. Galton's ox pairs with **manias and delusions** (item 30), where a crowd's estimates of a thing's value — a tulip bulb, a share, a house — are no longer drawn independently but copied down a chain of people watching each other, until the collective figure races away from truth. The mechanism that builds the mania is **information cascades** (item 26): rational people, seeing others' choices, stop consulting their private information and simply follow, so that a thousand estimates carry no more independent evidence than the first loud one. Galton's fair is a market with the copying switched *off*; a bubble is Galton's fair with the copying switched *on*. Same people, same arithmetic, one condition inverted.

Everything optimistic in this movement is a way of protecting that condition. Condorcet (item 18) is this same result in binary form — one theorem in two costumes. The diversity prediction theorem (item 19) says why spreading the guesses out subtracts error; independent-error cancellation (item 21) states the engine in general; Surowiecki's four conditions (item 20) are the checklist for keeping the switch on. And the machinery that deliberately *manufactures* independence to harvest this wisdom runs forward into the book: the ensemble methods of machine learning (item 24), which average many decorrelated models for exactly Galton's reason, and prediction markets (item 23), which pay people to reveal private information independently and price it into a single number. Galton's ox is the seed of all of them.

## The human weight

The result carries a democratic promise that Galton, of all people, was forced to certify against his will: the many can out-know the expert. Not always, but on a wide class of estimation problems a diverse crowd of amateurs pooling independent judgments beats the trained specialist working alone. That is a levelling idea, with real institutional descendants — juries, markets, the aggregation of dispersed knowledge no central expert could hold in one head. It is the strongest quantitative case anyone has made that authority is not the only route to truth.

But the promise is fragile in a specific and politically loaded way, and the fragility is the point. Aggregation liberates *only if the guesses are truly independent*. A crowd made to conform does not average toward truth; it averages toward whatever it has been made to say. This is where the ox opens onto the frontier this series keeps circling: the maintenance of imposed silence. In a population that reads the room before it speaks — where people voice not what they privately estimate but what they judge it safe to be seen estimating — the aggregate reveals not the truth but the regime's preferred answer, delivered with all the mechanical confidence of Galton's median and mistaken for the voice of the people. A show of unanimity is not evidence; it may be the fingerprint of destroyed independence. This is the mechanism preference falsification (item 34) will name and dissect — a technology for correlating a population's expressed guesses so thoroughly that the crowd can be enormous, unanimous, and confidently wrong, without anyone changing their private mind.

And there is a shadow over the demonstrator himself. The man who discovered, to his surprise, that ordinary people pooling their ignorance could find the truth was the same man who founded eugenics, arguing all his life that human "quality" was hereditary and ought to be engineered. Galton trusted the aggregate ox and distrusted the aggregate human being. There is no tidy lesson in the contradiction, but there is a caution: the finder of crowd wisdom was no friend of the crowd, and the tool he handed us says nothing about whether we deserve it. It only says what pooling does to independent error; what we point it at, and whether we protect the independence it needs, is on us.

## The critique

Honesty requires starting with the number itself, because the famous version of this story is subtly rigged in wisdom's favour. The line everyone quotes — "the crowd was right to within one pound" — uses the *mean* of the 787 estimates, 1,197 lb against a true 1,198. But that is not the statistic Galton emphasised. He stressed the *median*, 1,207 lb, which he considered the properly democratic figure, and which missed by nine pounds — still excellent, under one per cent, but an order of magnitude worse than the legend. The discrepancy is not a footnote; it is the difference between "uncanny" and "impressive," and the parable quietly upgrades itself by choosing the flattering number after the fact. A 2014 re-examination by the statistician Kenneth Wallis, published in *Statistical Science* as "Revisiting Francis Galton's Forecasting Competition," worked through exactly these subtleties, confirming the results while cautioning against the mythology grown around them.

The deeper limits are conditions the parable hides. The effect requires **independence** and a **bounded, well-anchored quantity**. Galton's crowd did well partly because everyone knew, roughly, what an ox weighs; the guesses were already clustered in the right neighbourhood, so error cancellation had a real signal to sharpen rather than manufacture. Ask a crowd something with no shared anchor, or something where the whole population is biased the same way — where the errors are *correlated by construction* — and averaging cancels nothing, because there is nothing symmetric to cancel. It faithfully returns the collective bias. Crowds are wise about the weight of an ox; they are not wise about facts they have no purchase on, and they are actively *anti*-wise about anything on which they have all been misled together.

Finally, the effect is real but oversold. It replicates reliably for the narrow task Galton studied — estimating a bounded quantity from independent judgments — and has been extended into serious tools, from Delphi panels to prediction markets. But "the wisdom of crowds" as a slogan promises far more than the evidence supports, as though any crowd were wise about anything provided it was large. It is not. Galton ran one demonstration, on one favourable problem, and it became a myth larger than the data — a single dead ox in a Plymouth show-ring pressed into service as proof of a general law it never claimed to be. The law it does support is narrower, harder, and more interesting: pool independent, diverse errors about a well-anchored quantity, and they cancel. Break any of those clauses, and the same crowd that found the ox will lose it — which is not a footnote to the wisdom of crowds but its other half, and the hinge on which the rest of this book turns.
