# References — 17. Galton's ox

## Confirmed sources

- Galton, Francis. "Vox Populi." *Nature* 75, no. 1949 (7 March 1907): 450–451. — The founding paper. Reports the Plymouth fat-stock and poultry exhibition weight-judging competition: ~800 entrants, 787 usable tickets, median ("middlemost") estimate 1,207 lb against a dressed-ox true weight of 1,198 lb; source of the "trustworthiness of a democratic judgment" remark and Galton's emphasis on the median as the democratic figure.
- The mean of the 787 estimates (1,197 lb, against the true dressed weight of 1,198 lb) — derivable from the data Galton published in "Vox Populi" and independently confirmed by Wallis 2014 (below). Galton emphasised the median (1,207 lb) in the paper; the mean's near-exact accuracy is the number the popular "within one pound" legend rests on. Supports the mean-vs-median distinction central to beats 4 and 7. (Galton's related methodological note "One Vote, One Value," *Nature* 75, no. 1948, 28 Feb 1907, p. 414, precedes "Vox Populi" and argues for the median as the democratic statistic; it does not itself report the 1,197 lb mean.)
- Wallis, Kenneth F. "Revisiting Francis Galton's Forecasting Competition." *Statistical Science* 29, no. 3 (2014): 420–424. — Re-examines Galton's original data and the mean/median statistics; grounds the beat-7 caution about the mythology. Confirms the headline results while clarifying the arithmetic.
- Surowiecki, James. *The Wisdom of Crowds*. New York: Doubleday, 2004. — Opens with the Galton ox as founding parable and revived it for a general audience; source for the popular "within one pound" framing (which uses the mean).
