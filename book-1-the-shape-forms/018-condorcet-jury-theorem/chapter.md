# 18. Condorcet's Jury Theorem

> A crowd of the barely-competent can be nearly infallible — or reliably, catastrophically wrong. The same equation says both, and the sign is set by a single number.

## The idea

Take a group facing a yes-or-no question that has a right answer: guilty or innocent, safe or unsafe, buy or sell. Suppose each person is a little better than a coin — each is right, on their own, slightly more than half the time. Now let them vote and take the majority verdict. Condorcet's Jury Theorem says something that feels like sleight of hand the first time you meet it: as you add more voters, the probability that the *majority* is correct climbs toward certainty. Not toward "pretty good." Toward one.

The theorem has a second face, and it is the more important one for this book. Flip the assumption. Suppose each person is a little *worse* than a coin — right slightly less than half the time. Now the same mathematics runs in reverse: as the group grows, the probability that the majority is correct collapses toward zero. A large enough crowd of slightly-biased people is not merely unreliable. It is a machine for manufacturing the wrong answer, and it gets more reliable at it the bigger it grows.

So the theorem is not a hymn to democracy, though it is often quoted as one. It is a threshold law. It tells you that scale is an amplifier with no opinion of its own. Point the crowd's average competence above one-half and scale delivers wisdom; point it below and scale delivers confident error. The number that sets the sign is whether the average member clears the coin-flip bar. Everything in this book about when crowds are wise and when they are mad is, in one form or another, an argument about which side of that line a group is standing on — and about the hidden assumption, independence, that the whole result quietly rests on.

## The origin

The theorem comes from Marie Jean Antoine Nicolas de Caritat, the Marquis de Condorcet (1743–1794): mathematician, philosophe, one of the last figures of the French Enlightenment to believe, without embarrassment, that reason could be turned on human affairs and made to yield laws as clean as those of mechanics. He set it out in 1785, in a book with a title that announces the whole programme: *Essai sur l'application de l'analyse à la probabilité des décisions rendues à la pluralité des voix* — an essay on applying analysis to the probability of decisions reached by a plurality of votes. He dedicated it to his mentor Turgot, the reformer whose fall from power Condorcet had watched at close range.

The setting Condorcet had in mind was literal: a court, a panel of judges or jurors, each weighing the same evidence, each capable of error, deciding by majority whether an accused person should be condemned. His question was moral before it was mathematical. If a verdict can be wrong, how many judges, and what quality of judgment, does it take before we are entitled to trust the collective decision enough to hang someone on it? The answer he derived — that competence above one-half, aggregated across enough independent voters, drives error toward vanishing — became one of the founding results of social choice theory, though it slept for a century and a half before twentieth-century political scientists and statisticians rediscovered how much it could carry.

Condorcet's own end is part of the theorem's weight. A decade after the *Essai*, during the Terror, the man who had tried to put a probability on the reliability of collective verdicts was condemned by a collective verdict of his own revolution, and died in a prison cell in 1794. The catalogue of what large groups of the confident can decide was one he entered himself.

## The mechanism

The engine is nothing more than the mathematics of repeated independent trials — the same machinery that governs coin flips — but pointed at a question with a correct answer.

Give each voter an identical, independent probability *p* of voting for the correct option on a binary question. Ask what a majority of *n* voters will do. The number voting correctly follows a binomial distribution, and the majority is right whenever more than half the voters land on the correct side. Two things then follow, and they are the whole theorem.

First, if *p* > 1/2, the probability that the majority is correct is larger than *p* itself, and it increases as *n* grows, approaching 1 as *n* goes to infinity. The reason is the law of large numbers: with each voter independently biased toward truth, the *fraction* voting correctly clusters ever more tightly around *p*, which sits above the one-half line the majority needs to cross. Wrong majorities require an improbable coincidence — a large minority of independent errors all falling the same way — and coincidences of independent events get rarer, fast, as you add more of them.

Second, and symmetrically, if *p* < 1/2, the identical logic drives the probability of a correct majority *down* toward zero. The fraction voting correctly now clusters below one-half, so the majority is almost surely wrong, and more voters only tighten the near-certainty of error. Exactly at *p* = 1/2, the crowd is a coin: adding voters does nothing, and the majority is right half the time no matter how large it grows.

That is the two-edged floor. Scale does not create competence; it magnifies whatever competence is already there, including negative competence. And the magnification depends utterly on one word in the setup: *independent*. The clustering that makes wrong majorities improbable is a fact about independent errors cancelling. Let the voters copy one another, share a source, or take their cue from a common rumour, and their errors stop being independent, stop cancelling, and start *stacking*. The mathematics that promised certainty quietly withdraws the promise. Hold that thought; it is where the theorem hands off to its dark twin.

## The anchor example

The cleanest demonstration of the sunny edge is not a jury at all but an ox — and it belongs to the next chapter's neighbour, Francis Galton's 1907 country-fair weight-guessing crowd, whose averaged estimate landed within a pound of the animal's true weight (item 17). Galton's crowd was Condorcet's theorem wearing a continuous rather than a binary face: many independent, individually-mediocre judgments, aggregated, converging on truth.

But the theorem's darker edge deserves its own picture, because it is the one people forget. Imagine a jury pool in a town gripped by a particular certainty — that a certain kind of person is the kind who commits a certain kind of crime. Each juror, taken alone, is now worse than a coin on the question of this defendant's guilt: their prejudice makes them right less than half the time. Condorcet's mathematics is merciless here. Do not empanel more of them hoping the numbers will wash the bias out. A larger jury drawn from the same poisoned pool does not dilute the error; it concentrates it. Twelve biased jurors will convict an innocent person more reliably than three will. The size of the crowd, so often invoked as a guarantor of fairness — *surely twelve people couldn't all be wrong* — is, when *p* has slipped below one-half, precisely the thing that makes the wrong verdict a near-certainty rather than a gamble. The history of collective condemnation, from witch trials to lynch mobs to show trials, is in one sense a series of demonstrations of Condorcet's second edge.

## The flip

This chapter is one of the three nails the whole series hangs on, because it states the thesis in its most naked, mathematical form: **the wise crowd and the mob are the same crowd, and a single parameter sets which one you get.** Above one-half, aggregation is the finest truth-finding instrument we have. Below one-half, the identical aggregation is a truth-destroying instrument of equal power. There are not two kinds of crowd. There is one mechanism and one dial.

But the dial has a second setting that matters even more than *p*, and it is the assumption the theorem smuggles in: independence. Condorcet's guarantee is a guarantee about independent voters. The moment voters stop deciding for themselves and start deciding by watching each other, the errors that were supposed to cancel begin to align, and the theorem's promise inverts. This is the exact seam along which Book One (the wise crowd) tears into Book Two (the mob). Its dark twin is item 26, **information cascades**, where perfectly rational people, each observing the choices of those ahead of them, stop consulting their own private information and simply copy — at which point a thousand voters can carry no more independent evidence than the first two, and a confident majority can be built on almost nothing. Condorcet tells you the crowd is wise *if the voters are independent and better than chance*. Surowiecki's four conditions (item 20) are, in effect, the engineering checklist for keeping both halves of that "if" true. Everything optimistic in this book is a way of protecting independence; everything catastrophic in the next is a way of losing it.

## The human weight

The theorem is a quiet argument for a particular kind of humility about crowds, in both directions. It refutes the aristocrat's reflex — that the many are a rabble and only the trained few should decide — by showing mathematically that a large body of modestly-competent independent judges can out-perform a lone expert. That is the democratic case, and it is real. But in the same breath it refutes the populist's reflex — that the voice of the many is the voice of truth — by showing that a large body of *biased* judges is not a corrective but an amplifier. Numbers sanctify nothing. A majority is only as trustworthy as the average competence and the independence of the people composing it, and it can be enormous and unanimous and still be a machine for producing exactly the wrong answer.

This is where the theorem touches the frontier this series keeps returning to: the maintenance of unjust social orders. An order of caste or hierarchy does not usually survive because a majority independently judges it to be right. It survives, in part, because it drives the effective independence of judgment toward zero — because people vote, speak, and act by reading what everyone around them appears to believe rather than by consulting what they privately think. Condorcet's theorem, read against the grain, says that a society can be made to converge with tremendous confidence on a proposition that is false, provided you can arrange for its members to stop judging independently and start echoing one another. The mechanism that does the arranging has a name in this series — preference falsification (item 34) — and it is, in Condorcetian terms, a technology for destroying the independence on which the crowd's wisdom depends, and thereby flipping the sign of the collective verdict without changing a single mind.

## The critique

The theorem's fame rests on a set of assumptions its critics have spent two centuries dismantling, and honesty requires laying them out. Three matter most.

The first and gravest is **independence**. Condorcet assumes each voter decides alone, uninfluenced by the others. Real groups are the opposite: people confer, defer, follow leaders, and read the room. Once votes are correlated, the tidy convergence toward certainty weakens or fails outright, and a whole literature since the 1980s has explored just how fragile the result is once you let voters share information or influence — for many realistic patterns of correlation, adding voters stops helping well short of certainty, and can hurt. The theorem is best read not as a description of how crowds behave but as a statement of the *ideal conditions* under which they could be wise — a benchmark that shows, by contrast, exactly what real crowds are missing.

The second is **homogeneous competence**. Condorcet gives every voter the same *p*. Drop that, and the picture gets subtler: a crowd's wisdom then depends on the distribution of competence, and in some configurations a few highly-competent voters outperform a large mediocre majority, which complicates any simple "more voters is better" reading. Later results generalise the theorem to unequal and even individually-sub-threshold voters under certain conditions, but the clean guarantee softens.

The third is the binary, single-shot, one-right-answer frame itself. Many real collective decisions are not binary, have no verifiable correct answer, or involve strategic voting where people misrepresent their views to influence the outcome — and Condorcet's own broader work exposed a related problem, the voting paradox in which majority preferences over three or more options can cycle incoherently (A beats B beats C beats A), a discovery that runs straight into Arrow's impossibility theorem (item 71) in Book Four. The Jury Theorem is thus both foundational and narrow: a beautiful, exact result about an idealised case, whose chief value is the sharpness with which its assumptions, when violated, explain the failures. It is the clean floor beneath a very messy building — which is precisely why it is one of the three nails.
