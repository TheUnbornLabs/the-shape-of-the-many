# 13. The costs of group living

> Every benefit of the crowd arrives with an invoice, and the same density that buys safety also concentrates hunger, disease, and betrayal.

## The idea

The preceding chapters of this movement have made group living sound like a bargain too good to refuse. Bunch together and each animal buys itself a smaller share of the predator's attention (the selfish herd, item 9); dilute the risk across many bodies and any single death becomes improbable (item 10); flicker and swirl in a coordinated mass and you overload the hunter's aim (the confusion effect, item 11); pool a thousand pairs of eyes and no approach goes unseen (many-eyes vigilance, item 12). If aggregation were pure gift, the world would be one continuous flock, and every solitary animal a fool.

It is not, and they are not, because grouping is never free. The bill runs to several lines. Animals packed together compete more fiercely for the same food and the same mates — jostling directly, and racing to strip a shared resource before a neighbour does. Pathogens and parasites, which travel from body to body, move faster and farther through a dense crowd than through a scattered population: a group is a transmission network as much as a fortress. A large aggregation is also simply easier to detect — more conspicuous, more smell, more noise, a bigger silhouette on the horizon — so some of the safety that numbers buy is spent back on attracting notice. And living cheek by jowl opens the door to social theft: eggs slipped into a neighbour's nest, paternity stolen, subordinate reproduction suppressed by dominants. The central claim of this chapter is therefore a correction, not a denial. Group size is not something to be maximised. It is something to be balanced — a figure set where the rising costs of crowding meet the falling returns of one more companion.

## The origin

The person who assembled these scattered debits into a single ledger was the American evolutionary biologist Richard D. Alexander, in a 1974 review titled simply "The evolution of social behavior," published in the *Annual Review of Ecology and Systematics*. Alexander's move was quietly radical. Where much earlier writing on sociality had reached for the benefits — cooperation, defence, the warm rhetoric of the group — he asked the harder question first: given that grouping carries obvious automatic costs, what benefit could possibly be large enough to pay for them? He listed the costs plainly. Living in groups increases competition for resources; it raises the likelihood of disease and parasite transmission; it can increase conspicuousness to predators; it exposes individuals to interference and to the reproductive interests of others. For a trait to spread despite all this, some countervailing advantage — predator defence, or the exploitation of a food source no lone animal could reach — had to be doing heavy lifting. Sociality, in Alexander's frame, is a bargain struck against a standing headwind of costs, and the costs are the thing to explain first.

A decade later, Richard M. Sibly sharpened one implication into a paradox. In a 1983 note in *Animal Behaviour*, Sibly pointed out that the group size which is best for the individuals already inside it is not, in general, the group size that will actually form — because a lone outsider will keep choosing to join a group as long as joining benefits *that outsider*, even once its arrival has pushed the group past the size that was optimal for the existing members. The optimum, in other words, tends to be unstable: left to individual choice, groups swell beyond it. The costs of group living thus carry a second, structural sting, one this chapter will return to.

## The mechanism

Picture two curves drawn against group size, *N*, on the horizontal axis. The first is a benefit curve: the per-individual payoff from safety and foraging, which in most models rises steeply at first — going from one animal to a handful transforms your survival odds — and then flattens, because the tenth pair of watching eyes adds far less than the second. The second is a cost curve, and it does the opposite: it starts low and climbs, often accelerating, as crowding intensifies competition and greases the paths of contagion. The individual's net benefit is the gap between the two curves, and it is largest not at the smallest group nor the largest, but at the intermediate size where the vertical distance between them is greatest. Past that point every additional member still adds something, but adds cost faster than benefit. That single geometric fact — a rising cost curve crossed against a saturating benefit curve — is the whole logic of optimal group size, and it is why real groups have characteristic sizes rather than growing without limit.

Two of the cost lines deserve their gears shown. Competition splits into two textures. **Scramble** competition is impersonal: everyone races to consume a shared, depletable resource, and the more mouths, the less each gets, with no direct aggression required — a field grazed bare feeds no one well. **Interference** competition is personal: animals actively obstruct one another, fighting over a mate or a feeding site, so that the mere presence of rivals imposes a cost through jostling, threat, and time lost to defence. Disease is the subtler and more remorseless line, because it is *density-dependent*. A pathogen's spread depends on the rate of contact between hosts, and contact rises with crowding; pack animals closer and each infection finds more susceptible neighbours before it burns out. This is not a fixed toll but an accelerating one — the reason the disease cost curve bends upward rather than rising in a straight line, and the reason it can, in a large enough aggregation, come to dominate the whole ledger.

And then Sibly's instability sits on top of all this. Because an individual decides whether to join by weighing its *own* net benefit, not the group's, groups accrete past the collectively optimal size. The stable outcome is the point where the *marginal joiner* is indifferent — where the last animal to arrive gains nothing more from being in than out — which lies well to the right of the size that would have been best for those already there. The optimum is real; it is just not what free entry delivers.

## The anchor example

The cleanest measurement of the bill comes from a bird that nests in dense mud-cup colonies under bridges and cliff overhangs across the American West: the cliff swallow, *Petrochelidon pyrrhonota*. Beginning in the early 1980s in the Nebraska panhandle, the biologists Charles R. Brown and Mary Bomberger Brown ran what became a decades-long study of thousands of these colonies, which range in size from a lone pair to several thousand nests packed wall to wall. Colonial nesting brings the swallows real benefits — shared information about where the day's insect swarms are, extra eyes against hawks. But the Browns were able to price the cost with unusual precision, because it has a body you can count: the swallow bug, a blood-feeding ectoparasite that overwinters in the nests and swarms the nestlings.

The larger the colony, the worse the infestation. In their work reported in *Ecology* in 1986, the Browns found that the number of bugs per nest climbed sharply with colony size, and the parasites exacted a measurable fitness toll — nestlings in heavily infested nests were lighter and less likely to survive than those in fumigated nests from which the bugs had been experimentally removed. Here the abstract cost curve becomes literal and countable: a swelling colony is a swelling parasite load, drawn in the mass of the nestlings and the number that fledge. The swallows pay for their crowd in blood, and the size of the payment rises with the size of the crowd — the disease-and-parasite line of Alexander's ledger, made visible in a single species. Some birds, tellingly, respond exactly as the theory predicts they should: they abandon bug-ridden nests and even parasitise their neighbours' nests instead of building anew, trading one cost of coloniality against another.

## The flip

This chapter is the honest counterweight of Movement II, and it earns its place by refusing the romance of the group. Everything the preceding four chapters celebrated — the selfish herd, dilution, confusion, many eyes — is here re-priced. The thesis of this whole series is that the wise crowd and the mob are one crowd under two conditions; this chapter adds the flatter, older truth beneath it: *even the wise crowd is a trade-off, never a pure good.* The same aggregation that pools vigilance and cancels individual error also pools pathogens and stacks conflict. Density is the single variable that buys the benefits and levies the costs at once. You cannot take the safety of numbers without also taking the contagion of numbers, because they are the same fact of proximity viewed from two sides.

And that is precisely why this ecological chapter reaches forward into the dark book. A crowd is a transmission network, and what a network transmits is not fussy about its cargo. The density dependence that makes disease spread faster through a large group is the identical mathematics that will make *panics and rumours* spread faster through a large gathering — the SIR models that epidemiologists use for pathogens are borrowed, almost unchanged, to model the spread of ideas (item 53). Literal contagion and informational contagion share the same engine. So the cliff swallow's parasite load prefigures **emotional contagion** (item 33), where an aroused crowd transmits fear or fury body to body the way a colony transmits bugs nest to nest, and it prefigures **information cascades** (item 26), where proximity lets one animal's choice propagate through the group faster than private judgement can correct it. Conspicuousness and density, benign here as the cost of being noticed, turn lethal in the **crowd crush** (item 29), where the same packing that once meant safety becomes the mechanism of suffocation. The costs this chapter prices in blood and hunger are the ecological seed of every catastrophe in Book Two.

## The human weight

Human beings pay the same bill, and we have chosen, over the last ten thousand years, to pay ever more of it. The city is the largest voluntary aggregation our species builds, and it runs the same ledger as the swallow colony, only larger. Density is the engine of everything we prize about urban life — the pooling of talent and labour, the collision of ideas, the collective intelligence that no scattered population could generate. It is also the engine of the epidemic. Cholera needed the crowded, water-sharing city; measles and smallpox needed dense human populations to sustain their chains of transmission at all. The same streets that concentrate opportunity concentrate contact, and contact is what a pathogen eats. Crime, anonymity, the fraying of the informal watchfulness that binds a village — these are the human forms of interference competition and lost vigilance, the social costs of packing strangers close.

The sober note is that "more connected" is not a costless upgrade, and the density dependence does not care whether what spreads is a virus or a rumour. A hyperconnected society transmits verified knowledge and coordinated relief with unprecedented speed; it transmits panic, false alarm, and manufactured certainty at exactly the same speed, through the same channels, for the same structural reason. This is where the frontier of this series stirs faintly. Dense human groups do not only concentrate contagion of germs and moods; they concentrate the mutual surveillance that lets a social order read itself — the constant checking of what everyone around us appears to believe. That watchfulness is a benefit when it coordinates and a cost when it silences, and the machinery by which proximity converts private doubt into public conformity has a name later in this series (preference falsification, item 34). For now the ecological point stands on its own and stands sober: the crowd that makes us smart is the same crowd that makes us sick, in body and in belief, and the price rises with the density that delivers the good.

## The critique

The costs are real, but the ledger is far harder to close than its elegant geometry suggests, and honesty requires saying so. The first problem is measurement. Benefits and costs are denominated in different currencies — a marginal gain in vigilance against a marginal rise in parasite load — and converting both into a common unit of fitness, so the two curves can actually be crossed, is fiendishly difficult in the field. The cliff swallow work is celebrated precisely because it is the exception: a cost you can count in bugs and grams. Most systems yield nothing so clean, and the crossing point is inferred rather than seen.

The second problem is that optimal-group-size theory is more beautiful than it is testable. Sibly's instability result is airtight as logic, but demonstrating in nature that a particular group has swollen past its collective optimum, and by how much, requires knowing the true shape of both curves — which we almost never do. Field tests are messy, confounded, and often consistent with several models at once. There is, too, a quiet publication asymmetry: a study that finds a striking cost of grouping, or a striking benefit, is more tellable than one that finds a wash, and the literature may tilt toward whichever sign the field's current fashion favours. Above all, there is no universal answer, and it would be a mistake to want one. The balance between the benefits and the costs shifts with the ecology — with predation pressure, food distribution, parasite prevalence, the local currency of mates — so that the optimal group for a species in one valley may be wrong for the same species in the next. This chapter prices the crowd; it does not name a single fair price. The most it can honestly claim is that the price is always there, that it rises with density, and that any account of collective life which forgets to pay it is telling only half the story.
