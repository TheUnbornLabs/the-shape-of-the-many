# References — 13. The costs of group living

## Confirmed sources

- Alexander, Richard D. "The evolution of social behavior." *Annual Review of Ecology and Systematics* 5:325–383, 1974. — The landmark review that assembled the costs of group living (increased competition, disease and parasite transmission, conspicuousness to predators, interference and reproductive conflict) into a single ledger and argued that a benefit must offset them for sociality to evolve.
- Sibly, R. M. "Optimal group size is unstable." *Animal Behaviour* 31(3):947–948, 1983. — Shows that the group size best for existing members is not the size that forms, because outsiders keep joining as long as joining benefits themselves; the optimum is therefore unstable (the free-entry problem).
- Brown, Charles R., and Mary Bomberger Brown. "Ectoparasitism as a cost of coloniality in cliff swallows (*Hirundo pyrrhonota*)." *Ecology* 67(5):1206–1218, 1986. — Documents that swallow-bug ectoparasite load rises with colony size and imposes a measurable fitness cost (reduced nestling mass and survival), demonstrated via experimental fumigation; the chapter's anchor cost data.
- Hoogland, John L. *The Black-Tailed Prairie Dog: Social Life of a Burrowing Mammal*. University of Chicago Press, 1995. — Long-term colonial-rodent study documenting costs of coloniality including disease/parasitism and infanticide; cited as a parallel body of cost evidence.
