# References — 16. Costly signalling & the handicap principle

## Confirmed sources

- Zahavi, Amotz. "Mate selection—a selection for a handicap." *Journal of Theoretical Biology*, vol. 53, no. 1, 1975, pp. 205–214. — The original statement of the handicap principle; the source for the claim that signals are honest because differentially costly, and for its initially hostile reception.
- Grafen, Alan. "Biological signals as handicaps." *Journal of Theoretical Biology*, vol. 144, no. 4, 1990, pp. 517–546. — The formal evolutionary-game-theory model showing handicap signalling can be an evolutionarily stable strategy; the source for the vindication of Zahavi's idea.
- Spence, Michael. "Job Market Signaling." *Quarterly Journal of Economics*, vol. 87, no. 3, 1973, pp. 355–374. — The independent economic parallel: education as a costly signal honest because differentially costly to lower-productivity workers. Spence shared the 2001 Nobel Memorial Prize in Economic Sciences.
- Maynard Smith, John, and David Harper. *Animal Signals*. Oxford University Press, 2003. — The standard treatment distinguishing handicaps (costly signals) from indices (un-fakeable signals constrained by physiology); source for the correction that not all honest signals are costly, and the red-deer-roar index example.
- Zahavi, Amotz, and Avishag Zahavi. *The Handicap Principle: A Missing Piece of Darwin's Puzzle*. Oxford University Press, 1997. — Book-length popular and theoretical exposition of the principle across many species; general background for the chapter's framing.
- Caro, T. M. "The functions of stotting in Thomson's gazelles: some tests of the predictions." *Animal Behaviour*, vol. 34, no. 3, 1986, pp. 663–684. — Field tests of eleven hypotheses for stotting; the best-supported functions are that stotting signals to the predator that it has been detected. Note: Caro's data indicate stotting communicates detection rather than reliably deterring cheetah pursuit — hence the chapter's explicit hedge that the strength of the cheetah field data is debated.
- FitzGibbon, C. D., and J. H. Fanshawe. "Stotting in Thomson's gazelles: an honest signal of condition." *Behavioral Ecology and Sociobiology*, vol. 23, no. 2, 1988, pp. 69–74. — The honest-signal-of-condition finding: gazelles that stot at higher rates are less likely to be selected and killed. Concerns coursing predators (African wild dogs) rather than stalking cheetahs; supports the differential-cost mechanism while sharpening which predator the pursuit/abandonment data actually track.
