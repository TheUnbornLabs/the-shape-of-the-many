# 7. Granovetter's threshold model

> A riot and a quiet afternoon can be made of the exact same people; what differs is not who they are but the order in which they would move.

## The idea

Picture a decision each of us makes constantly without naming it: whether to join something already underway. Whether to stand for the ovation, walk out of the strike meeting a striker, throw the first stone or the fortieth, laugh at the joke, leave the party. In almost none of these do we decide in a vacuum. We decide by counting — half-consciously, in the corner of the eye — how many others have gone before us. Some of us need only a nudge; a single person rising is enough to lift us to our feet. Others hold back until the room is nearly unanimous. And a stubborn few will never move at all.

Mark Granovetter's threshold model gives this its clean form. Assign to each person a **threshold**: the number, or proportion, of others who must already be acting before that person joins. A threshold of zero is the instigator, who acts alone and needs no one. A threshold of one hundred, in a crowd of a hundred, is the person who will never move because the required crowd can never assemble without them. Between the extremes sits everyone else, each waiting for their own private quorum.

The idea sounds almost too simple to carry weight. Its power is in what happens when you line the thresholds up and let them run. Whether the group erupts or stays still turns out to depend not on how radical or peaceable the people are on average, but on the precise arrangement of those thresholds — on whether each person's trigger point is met by the number of people who acted just before them. And here is the result that makes the model one of the load-bearing ideas of this book: **two crowds with identical average sentiment, identical distributions of feeling, can produce a full-blown riot or a dead-calm nothing.** The difference is not in the hearts. It is in the sequence.

## The origin

The model belongs to Mark Granovetter, the sociologist better known for "The Strength of Weak Ties," and it appeared in 1978 in the *American Journal of Sociology* under the plain title "Threshold Models of Collective Behavior" (volume 83, issue 6, pages 1420–1443). Granovetter was writing against a tradition that explained crowds by explaining crowd members — that reached for a riot and concluded the rioters must have been an unusually angry, irrational, or morally deficient kind of person. The contagion theorists of the early twentieth century, following Gustave Le Bon, had treated the mob as a lowering of the individual, a dissolution of reason in the mass.

Granovetter's move was to strip all of that away and ask a colder question. Suppose the people are entirely rational, each with fixed, reasonable preferences, each simply weighing the costs and benefits of joining given how many others already have. What collective outcomes does that produce? The answer, worked out in a handful of arithmetic examples, was that you did not need irrationality, or a special crowd psychology, or morally defective people to get a riot. You needed only ordinary individuals with an ordinary spread of thresholds — and the outcome could swing wildly on differences between crowds that were, in every measurable respect, the same.

Granovetter shared the year, and the intellectual air, with Thomas Schelling, whose *Micromotives and Macrobehavior* also appeared in 1978 and made the companion argument in a different key: that the aggregate pattern a society settles into can bear no resemblance to what any of its members intended, and cannot be read back off their intentions. The two books belong together on the same shelf. Between them they founded the modern study of how small individual rules produce large collective shapes nobody chose.

## The mechanism

Take the group and lay its members out by threshold. Person A has threshold 0, person B has threshold 1, C has threshold 2, and so on. Now let the process run in rounds. At the start, only the zero-threshold person is acting — one instigator. But their acting means one person is now in motion, which satisfies the threshold-one person, who joins. Now two are acting, which satisfies the threshold-two person, who joins. Two becomes three, three becomes four, and the wave rolls forward, each new participant unlocking the next, until it reaches the far end of the distribution.

This is the causal gear, and it is worth naming precisely: the model is a **feedback loop running through a queue of trigger points**. Each act of joining changes the count, and the changed count is what the next person is reading. The system does not care about the total reservoir of discontent. It cares only about whether, at each step, the running tally has climbed high enough to trip the next-lowest threshold that has not yet fired. The cascade continues exactly as long as there is no gap — no missing rung in the ladder — between the number currently acting and the next person's threshold. Where the tally meets or exceeds an unfired threshold, the process advances. Where a gap opens, it stops, wherever it happens to be.

That is the whole engine, and its cruelty lies in how local the failure point is. The cascade does not need a large deficit of will to stall. It needs one gap of size one. If the ladder runs 0, 1, 2, 3 and then jumps to 5 — with no one holding a threshold of 4 — the wave climbs to four participants and dies, because the next person in line was waiting for a fifth and the fifth never came. Everyone downstream of that gap, however eager, however low their own threshold relative to a full crowd, is now stranded on the far side of a bridge that was never built. Their willingness is real and entirely inert. They would have joined a riot of five. There will be no riot of five.

## The anchor example

Granovetter's own illustration is a square filled with a hundred people, and it repays being made exact, because the exactness is the point.

Give the hundred a perfectly even spread of thresholds: one person with threshold 0, one with threshold 1, one with 2, all the way up to one with threshold 99. Now watch it go. The zero-threshold radical breaks a window; that is one person acting. The threshold-one person sees a single actor, which is precisely their trigger, and joins: two. The threshold-two person now sees two, and joins: three. Each person in the line has a threshold one below the number now assembled, so each falls in turn like a tipped domino. The wave runs cleanly up the whole distribution and every last one of the hundred ends up rioting. A full-scale riot, from a standing start, with no gap to stop it.

Now change one person. Take the individual who held threshold 3 and nudge them up to threshold 4. Nothing else about the crowd is different. The average threshold has barely moved. Every person still holds essentially the same feelings they held a moment ago. But the ladder now reads 0, 1, 2, and then — where a 3 used to sit — nothing, because that rung has been carried up to 4, leaving *two* people demanding a crowd of four before either will move. Run it again. The radical breaks the window: one. Threshold-one joins: two. Threshold-two joins: three. And there the wave hits open air. The next unfired thresholds are both 4, and only three people are acting. Neither will move without a fourth; there is no one left willing to be that fourth. The riot that a moment ago consumed a hundred people now dies at three, and the square goes quiet.

That is the demonstration in its starkest form. One person's threshold shifting from 3 to 4 — a change no observer could see, in a single individual, in a crowd of a hundred — is the entire difference between a riot that runs to completion and one that never gets past its first three participants. Same people. Same sentiments, almost exactly. Opposite worlds.

## The flip

This is the micro-engine of tipping, and it states the thesis of this whole series in its most disquieting form. The wise crowd and the mob, the argument runs, are the same people under different conditions. Granovetter tells us what one of those conditions is, and how little of it there needs to be. **It is not the people who set the outcome. It is the arrangement of their thresholds.** Rearrange nothing about who anyone is, shift a single trigger point by one, and the identical population delivers eruption or stillness. You cannot read the collective result off the individual hearts, because the hearts were the same in both worlds.

The radical upshot follows without mercy, and it is one of the deepest points the series makes. **You cannot infer the crowd from its outcome.** A riot is not proof that everyone, or even most people, wanted a riot; it may be proof only that the thresholds happened to line up without a gap. Calm is not proof that no one wanted to act; it may be proof only that a single gap, somewhere in the distribution, stranded a square full of would-be participants. The presence of an uprising tells you almost nothing about the depth of feeling behind it, and neither does its absence. The outcome is a fact about arrangement, not about aggregate desire — and to reason backward from "there was a riot" to "they were an angry people," or from "there was calm" to "they were content," is to commit exactly the error Granovetter built the model to expose.

This is the sociological cousin of the physics in item 6, phase transitions — the same abrupt, all-or-nothing flip from one macro-state to another driven by a slow change in a single parameter, water holding liquid until the last degree and then, at no dramatic threshold visible from inside, becoming ice. It is the micro-mechanism beneath the tipping points of item 59 and the first-follower dynamic of item 58, where the second person to act — the one who converts a lone crank into the front of a movement — is doing nothing more than being the low-threshold individual whose joining trips the next rung. It is a near-relative of the information cascades of item 26, though the logic differs: in a cascade people copy because others' actions carry information they lack, while in a threshold model people join because others' actions change the payoff of joining. And it hands directly to its darkest twin, item 34, preference falsification, where the thresholds are not merely arranged but hidden — where nobody, not even the participants, can see how close the whole system is to flipping, because everyone is concealing the very number the model runs on.

## The human weight

The hidden threshold is why oppressive orders can look so stable right up until the hour they don't. Consider a society in which a great many people privately reject the ruling arrangement but each holds a high threshold for saying so — high not because they lack conviction but because the cost of being the first, or the fifth, or the visible dissenter is severe, and because each of them systematically underestimates how many others feel the same. Everyone is waiting for a crowd that everyone else is also waiting for. The thresholds are met by no one, because no one can see them. The square stays quiet. From outside — and from inside — the order appears to rest on consent.

Then the perceived count drops. One dissenter becomes visible and survives; a single credible act lowers everyone's estimate of what it costs and raises their estimate of how many others are ready. In threshold terms, one public act has tripped the next rung, and if the distribution behind it has no gap, the cascade that could not begin for years completes in days. This is the mechanism under the sudden collapse of orders that seemed eternal — the abrupt emptying of a regime's legitimacy that surprises even the people doing the emptying. What looked like contentment was a stalled cascade the whole time, a hundred people in a square each waiting for a fourth.

Here the model locks directly into the frontier this series keeps returning to. Preference falsification, item 34, is precisely the machinery that keeps the thresholds hidden and the perceived count low, holding a structure of caste or hierarchy or imposed silence frozen not because people endorse it but because each has been arranged into ignorance of how many share their dissent. The first follower, item 58, is the intervention: the visible act that lowers the perceived threshold for everyone downstream and lets the stranded willingness on the far side of the gap finally connect. Granovetter's arithmetic is, read this way, a theory of both how the silence holds and how it breaks — and its lesson cuts against the reflex to read a quiet society as a contented one. The quiet may be one visible dissenter away from a flood.

## The critique

The model's honesty about its own limits should be at least as loud as its ambition. Four objections matter.

The first is measurement. Thresholds are close to impossible to observe before the fact; we mostly infer them from the outcome we were trying to explain, which makes the model far stronger at explaining after the event than at predicting before it. Told that a cascade completed, we say the thresholds had no gap; told that it stalled, we say there was one. That flirts with unfalsifiability, and Granovetter's framework does more to organise our hindsight than to sharpen our foresight.

The second is the informational assumption. The model supposes each person can read, reasonably accurately, how many others are already acting — and in real crowds that count is noisy, delayed, rumoured, and often badly wrong. People act on their estimate of the number, not the number, and the gap between the two can dominate everything the clean arithmetic predicts.

The third, and heaviest, is that the basic model ignores network structure. It treats the crowd as a single pool in which everyone responds to the same global tally, when in fact people watch their neighbours, their contacts, their particular corner of the room, not the aggregate. Whether a cascade spreads depends enormously on who is connected to whom — the work taken up by the small-world and network-contagion ideas of items 44 and 57, which put the missing structure back and show that the same distribution of thresholds can spread or fizzle depending purely on the wiring.

The fourth is the deepest in spirit. A single number is a caricature of a human motive. Real people do not carry one fixed threshold; they carry moods, loyalties, fears, second thoughts, and reasons that shift with who is acting rather than merely how many. The threshold is a deliberate simplification, and like all the best ones it earns its keep by being wrong in a clarifying way — showing, with a starkness no richer model could match, that collective outcomes need not be read off individual desires, and that a riot and a still afternoon can be cut from the very same cloth.
