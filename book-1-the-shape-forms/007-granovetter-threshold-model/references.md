# References — 7. Granovetter's threshold model

## Confirmed sources

- Granovetter, Mark. "Threshold Models of Collective Behavior." *American Journal of Sociology*, vol. 83, no. 6, 1978, pp. 1420–1443. — The founding paper. Source of the threshold definition, the rational-actor framing against contagion theory, and the 100-person riot thought-experiment including the 3-to-4 shift that turns a full cascade into a stalled one.
- Schelling, Thomas C. *Micromotives and Macrobehavior*. W. W. Norton, 1978. — Companion volume of the same year; the argument that aggregate social patterns cannot be inferred from, and need not resemble, the intentions of the individuals composing them.
- Le Bon, Gustave. *The Crowd: A Study of the Popular Mind (Psychologie des foules)*. 1895. — The contagion / crowd-psychology tradition Granovetter was writing against; cited here only as the target of his critique.
- Granovetter, Mark. "The Strength of Weak Ties." *American Journal of Sociology*, vol. 78, no. 6, 1973, pp. 1360–1380. — Granovetter's better-known paper, referenced only to identify the author.

## Verified during citation pass

- Granovetter, "Threshold Models of Collective Behavior," *American Journal of Sociology* 83(6) (1978), pp. 1420–1443 (DOI 10.1086/226707) — citation confirmed exactly.
- The 100-person uniform-threshold thought experiment (thresholds 0 through 99 producing a full cascade) is Granovetter's own illustration; his canonical demonstration of the fragility of a single gap removes/shifts one person's threshold so the cascade stalls at the gap. The chapter's "3-to-4" version is a faithful instance of that same one-person-gap logic (the paper's own worked case shifts the threshold-1 individual); it illustrates rather than quotes the paper, so the prose is kept as a demonstration in the model's spirit rather than a verbatim citation.
