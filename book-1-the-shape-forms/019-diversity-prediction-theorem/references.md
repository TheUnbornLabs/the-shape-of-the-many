# References — 19. The diversity prediction theorem

## Confirmed sources

- Page, Scott E. *The Difference: How the Power of Diversity Creates Better Groups, Firms, Schools, and Societies*. Princeton University Press, 2007. — The book that names and popularises the "diversity prediction theorem" and the "diversity trumps ability" framing; primary source for the identity and its social interpretation.
- Hong, Lu, and Scott E. Page. "Groups of diverse problem solvers can outperform groups of high-ability problem solvers." *Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences*, vol. 101, no. 46, 2004, pp. 16385–16389. — The formal problem-solving result underlying Page's broader claim; source for the "diversity trumps ability" argument.
- Krogh, Anders, and Jesper Vedelsby. "Neural Network Ensembles, Cross Validation, and Active Learning." *Advances in Neural Information Processing Systems 7 (NIPS 1994)*, MIT Press, 1995, pp. 231–238. — The ambiguity decomposition for ensembles: ensemble error equals average member error minus the variance (ambiguity) of member outputs; the direct mathematical ancestor of the prediction theorem and the machine-learning mirror (ensemble methods, item 24). Presented at NIPS 1994, published in the 1995 proceedings volume.
- Thompson, Abigail. "Does Diversity Trump Ability? An Example of the Misuse of Mathematics in the Social Sciences." *Notices of the American Mathematical Society*, vol. 61, no. 9, 2014, pp. 1024–1030. — The central published critique: argues the Hong–Page/Page results are mathematically trivial (a near-tautology once corrected) and the social conclusions overreach. Source for the critique beat. Volume, issue, and pagination confirmed against the AMS Notices record.
