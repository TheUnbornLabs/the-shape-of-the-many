# 19. The diversity prediction theorem

> A group's error is its members' average error minus their diversity — so difference is not decoration on the crowd's wisdom; it is a term that gets subtracted, exactly, from the crowd's mistake.

## The idea

Here is the closest thing this book has to an equation for its own thesis. Take a group of people, each trying to predict the same number — a company's earnings, a patient's survival odds, the weight of an ox. Let each person guess, and let the group's answer be the average of those guesses. Now ask how wrong the group is. The diversity prediction theorem answers with a sentence you can carry for life:

**Collective error equals average individual error minus prediction diversity.**

Read it slowly, because every word is load-bearing. *Collective error* is how far the group's averaged guess sits from the truth. *Average individual error* is how far the typical member sits from the truth, taken one at a time. *Prediction diversity* is how spread out the guesses are from each other — how much the members disagree. And the operator between the last two terms is a minus sign. Diversity is not added to the crowd's wisdom as a bonus, nor invoked as a vague virtue. It is *subtracted* from the crowd's mistake, term for term, unit for unit.

The consequence is startling and exact. Two things make a group accurate: members who are individually good, and members who are usefully different from one another. And because diversity enters with a minus sign, a group of merely mediocre predictors who disagree in the right ways can beat a group of individually excellent predictors who all think alike. Low average ability can be more than paid off by high diversity. This is not a metaphor or a management slogan. It is an algebraic identity, true by the definition of an average, every time.

## The origin

The theorem was made famous by Scott E. Page, an economist and political scientist at the University of Michigan, in his 2007 book *The Difference: How the Power of Diversity Creates Better Groups, Firms, Schools, and Societies*. Page named it the "diversity prediction theorem" and put it at the centre of a larger argument: that under the right conditions, cognitive diversity contributes to collective performance in a way that is not soft or aspirational but formal and provable.

Page's work grew from a 2004 paper written with Lu Hong in the *Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences*, "Groups of diverse problem solvers can outperform groups of high-ability problem solvers." That paper concerned problem-solving rather than prediction, and it carried the more provocative slogan that came to be attached to Page's name — *diversity trumps ability* — but its spirit is the same: a group's collective capability is not just the sum of individual talents; the *differences* between members do real work, and sometimes decisive work.

The prediction theorem itself, though, is older than its name. Its mathematical bones are the bias–variance decomposition familiar to statisticians, and its immediate ancestor is a 1995 result from the machine-learning literature: Anders Krogh and Jesper Vedelsby's "ambiguity decomposition," presented at the NIPS conference, which showed that the error of an ensemble of neural networks equals the average error of the individual networks minus the "ambiguity" — the variance of their outputs. Page's contribution was to see that this same identity, dressed in the language of people rather than networks, was a mathematical statement of why crowds are wise. What the machine-learning world called ambiguity, the social world could call diversity, and the minus sign meant the same thing in both.

## The mechanism

The theorem is, at bottom, a fact about how squared errors behave under averaging, and it is worth building it once with your own hands.

Fix a true value — call it the target *θ*. Each of *n* people offers a prediction. Measure each person's error the standard way, by squaring the gap between their guess and the truth, so that overshooting and undershooting both count as error and large misses count heavily. Now form three quantities. The *average individual error* is the mean of those squared gaps across all members. The group's prediction is the average of the individual guesses, and the *collective error* is the squared gap between that average guess and the truth. And the *prediction diversity* is the variance of the guesses around their own average — the mean squared distance of each guess from the group's guess, ignoring the truth entirely.

The identity is then exact:

**Collective error = Average individual error − Prediction diversity.**

Why does the minus sign appear, and why is it inevitable? Because when you average guesses, their errors partly cancel. If one person overshoots the truth and another undershoots it, their errors point in opposite directions, and the average lands closer to the target than either. That cancellation is precisely what the diversity term measures. The more the guesses spread out from one another — the more diverse they are — the more room there is for over- and under-shoots to offset, and the further the collective error falls below the average individual error. If every member guessed identically, diversity would be zero, nothing would cancel, and the group would be exactly as wrong as its typical member: collective error would equal average individual error, full stop. Diversity is the size of the discount aggregation earns you, and it is never negative — variance cannot be negative — so the crowd can never be worse than its average member, and is strictly better whenever its members disagree at all.

That last point deserves a beat of its own. The theorem guarantees, for squared error, that *the group beats its average member by exactly the amount its members differ.* Difference is not a hedge against being wrong. It is the mechanism of being right.

## The anchor example

Put numbers on it, because the arithmetic is where the surprise lives.

Three people are asked to guess the number of jellybeans in a jar. The true count is **100**. Ada guesses **80**, Ben guesses **90**, and Cara guesses **130**.

First, the group's answer: the average of 80, 90, and 130 is **100**. The crowd is exactly right. Its collective error is zero.

Now look at how badly each individual did. Ada was off by 20, so her squared error is 400. Ben was off by 10, squared error 100. Cara was off by 30, squared error 900. The *average individual error* is (400 + 100 + 900) ÷ 3 = **466.7**. Taken one at a time, these are not impressive guesses. The typical member is off by more than twenty jellybeans.

Where did all that individual error go? Into the diversity term. The group's guess was 100, so measure how far each guess sat from *that*: Ada was 20 below, Ben 10 below, Cara 30 above — squared, those are 400, 100, and 900, averaging to **466.7**. That is the prediction diversity.

And now the identity closes with a click: collective error (0) = average individual error (466.7) − prediction diversity (466.7). Every unit of the members' average wrongness was cancelled by an exactly equal unit of their disagreement. The crowd was perfect not despite the fact that its members were individually mediocre and pulling in different directions, but *because* of it. Had all three guessed 90 — a better individual guess than Ada's or Cara's, closer to the truth on average — the group would have answered 90 and been off by 10, worse than the diverse trio of poorer guessers. Three sharper minds in agreement lose to three duller minds who disagree. That is the theorem's whole scandal, done in jellybeans.

## The flip

This chapter is the series' thesis written as an equation, so read it as the hinge on which everything turns. The wise crowd and the mob are separated by a single subtracted term.

Look again at the identity. Collective error can only be small if the diversity term is large enough to eat into the average individual error. Diversity is the discount — and a discount you can lose. Every mechanism this book will spend two volumes anatomising as a destroyer of crowd wisdom is, in the language of this theorem, a mechanism for driving the diversity term toward zero. When diversity vanishes, the subtraction vanishes, and the crowd's error rises to meet the average individual error — or worse, because the same forces that erase diversity often degrade individual judgment too. Kill the difference between people and you have not merely blunted the crowd; you have algebraically converted it back into an echo of one confused person, amplified.

Name the killers, because they are the dark twins of this chapter. **Homophily** (item 48), the tendency to associate with the similar, quietly shrinks the spread of predictions by assembling groups that were never diverse to begin with. **Echo chambers** (item 50) take a diverse population and, by feeding everyone the same inputs, collapse their guesses toward a common point — manufacturing agreement, which is to say manufacturing zero diversity, which is to say manufacturing error. **Groupthink** (item 43) does it socially, by making disagreement costly, so that private differences never reach the average at all. Each is a different instrument aimed at the same term in the same equation.

And the sunny twins are here too. Galton's ox (item 17) was this theorem wearing a country-fair coat: hundreds of independent, diverse guesses whose errors cancelled into an averaged estimate within a pound of the truth. Condorcet (item 18) is its binary sibling, where independence rather than diversity does the cancelling — the two theorems are the continuous and the discrete faces of one fact about aggregation. Surowiecki's four conditions (item 20) name diversity explicitly as one of the four requirements for a wise crowd, and this theorem is *why* it made the list. Independent-error cancellation (item 21) is the deeper current running beneath both Condorcet and this identity: errors that point in different directions annihilate; errors that align survive and compound. And item 24, the ensemble methods of machine learning, is the theorem's mirror in silicon — random forests and bagging and boosting are engineering disciplines built entirely around deliberately manufacturing diverse predictors so that their variance can be subtracted away. The Shape of the crowd is wise exactly to the degree that its members differ, and mad exactly to the degree they have been made the same.

## The human weight

If diversity is a term you can subtract from error, then homogeneity is a term you can add to it — and this reframes some very old questions in a cold new light.

The ordinary case for cognitive diversity in teams, juries, and institutions is usually made on grounds of fairness or representation, and those grounds are real. But the theorem makes a colder and in some ways more powerful case: a group that assembles a genuine range of perspectives will, mechanically, make smaller errors on prediction and judgment tasks than a group of like-minded experts, because the range itself is a term working in the group's favour. A jury drawn from twelve different lives, a research team spanning several disciplines, a central bank whose committee argues from different models — these are not merely more legitimate. On the mathematics, they are more accurate, provided their differences bear on the question.

The frontier this series keeps returning to comes into focus here with unusual sharpness. Systems of *enforced homogeneity* — caste that assigns thought by birth, ideological conformity that punishes deviation, censorship that removes disagreeing inputs from circulation — do something worse than offend our sense of justice. They mathematically degrade collective intelligence. They drive the diversity term toward zero, and by the identity, that pushes collective error up toward the average error of the enforced consensus. A society that compels its members to think alike has not thereby become more reliably right; it has stripped out precisely the disagreement that was cancelling its mistakes, and has kept only the average of its confusion. This is stratification (item 85) read as an error term: an order that reproduces itself by suppressing difference is an order that has traded accuracy for uniformity and will not know it, because the very dissent that would reveal the error has been silenced. And the machinery of that silencing is preference falsification (item 34) — people misrepresenting their true predictions to match the visible consensus, which does not merely hide diversity but *deletes it from the average*, since a falsified guess contributes the crowd's error to the crowd and none of its own difference. To make people lie about what they think is, in the exact terms of this theorem, to set their diversity to zero while keeping their vote.

## The critique

Honesty about this theorem starts with its greatest strength, which is also the root of its greatest weakness: it is a mathematical identity. For squared error it is *always* true, by the definition of a variance, in every case, with no empirical content of its own. You cannot test it; you can only apply it. That means the theorem cannot, by itself, tell you that any real group is wise. It tells you only that *if* a group is diverse in its predictions, its error is lower by exactly that amount — and it says nothing about whether real, observable diversity produces the useful kind of prediction diversity the identity requires. The whole bite of the result is empirical and sits precisely where the algebra falls silent.

That gap is where the theorem's most serious critic struck. In 2014, the mathematician Abigail Thompson published a critique in the *Notices of the American Mathematical Society* arguing that Page's mathematical results are, as mathematics, essentially trivial — true almost by definition — and that the sweeping social claims built atop them, especially the *diversity trumps ability* slogan of the Hong–Page work, dramatically overreach what the math can support. Her charge is not that the equation is wrong; it is that an identity cannot carry the weight of a social program, and that dressing a tautology in the language of policy lends borrowed rigour to conclusions the theorem does not actually establish. The exchange is worth reading in full before invoking Page in any high-stakes argument.

Two further cautions follow. First, the diversity that the theorem rewards is diversity of *prediction* — of perspective, model, and information — not diversity of identity as such. The two can correlate, sometimes strongly, because people with different lives often bring different information and different priors; but the correlation is contingent and must be argued, not assumed. A demographically diverse committee that has all read the same brief and adopted the same model has high identity diversity and low prediction diversity, and the theorem rewards only the latter. Second, and finally, the subtraction only helps when the diverse predictors are actually *informative*. Diversity that is pure noise — guesses scattered at random with no signal — inflates the variance term, yes, but it also inflates the average individual error by at least as much, and the group gains nothing real. The theorem does not say that any difference improves a crowd. It says that difference among people who each know something cancels the parts of their errors that were pulling in opposite directions. Diversity is a discount on error only when there was some competence there to discount. Empty heads pointing in different directions still average to an empty answer.
