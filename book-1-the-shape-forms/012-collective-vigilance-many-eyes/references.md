# References — 12. Collective vigilance & the many-eyes effect

## Confirmed sources

- Pulliam, H. R. "On the advantages of flocking." *Journal of Theoretical Biology*, vol. 38, no. 2, 1973, pp. 419–422. — The foundational formal model of the many-eyes effect: shows that as flock size grows, the probability that at least one bird is scanning at any instant rises, so each individual can reduce its own vigilance while collective alertness stays high.
- Elgar, M. A. "Predator vigilance and group size in mammals and birds: a critical review of the empirical evidence." *Biological Reviews*, vol. 64, no. 1, 1989, pp. 13–33. — Influential review synthesising field studies; documents the robust empirical pattern that individual vigilance declines as group size increases, while critically assessing confounds.
- Lima, S. L. Work on the economics of vigilance and the feeding/scanning trade-off (1980s–1990s), much in collaboration with L. M. Dill and others. — Established vigilance as an individual cost-benefit optimisation problem embedded in a group context. *Exact paper title/year to be pinned in verification pass — see below.*
- Roberts, G. "Why individual vigilance declines as group size increases." *Animal Behaviour*, vol. 51, no. 5, 1996, pp. 1077–1086. — Examines competing explanations for the group-size/vigilance relationship, including detection vs. scramble-competition accounts; central to the critique beat. (Verified: volume 51, pages 1077–1086.)
