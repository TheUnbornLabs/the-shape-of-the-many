Chapter 61 · The Shape Holds Power

Keynes' beauty contest

When the prize goes to whoever guesses the crowd's taste, nobody is left looking at the faces — only at each other.

The idea

Picture a competition run by a newspaper. A hundred photographs of faces are printed, and readers are asked to choose the six prettiest. But here is the twist that makes it a puzzle rather than a matter of taste: the prize does not go to the reader whose choices are, by some objective standard, the loveliest. It goes to the reader whose six faces come closest to the six that the whole readership, on average, selects.

Think for a moment about what that instruction does to your mind. You are no longer being asked which faces you find beautiful. You are being asked which faces you think most other people will find beautiful. And the moment you notice that everyone else is under the same instruction, the ground shifts again. The other readers are not picking the faces they like either; they are picking the faces they think the average reader will pick. So you are now trying to guess what they are guessing about what the crowd will guess. There is no bottom to this. Each layer of anticipation sits on another. You have been detached from the faces entirely and set to reasoning about reasoning about reasoning.

This is Keynes' beauty contest, and its point is not really about beauty. It is about what happens to judgment when value is decided not by any fixed quality out in the world but by the collective expectation of the group itself. When the thing everyone is chasing is the group's own opinion, private judgment becomes irrelevant. What matters is not what is true, or good, or beautiful, but what everyone thinks everyone thinks. The crowd stops looking outward and starts looking at itself, and the object of attention — the faces, the fundamentals, the reality — quietly drops out of the picture.

The origin

The analogy belongs to John Maynard Keynes, and it appears in one of the most famous chapters of twentieth-century economics: chapter 12 of The General Theory of Employment, Interest and Money, published in 1936. The chapter is titled "The State of Long-Term Expectation," and in it Keynes is trying to explain why financial markets behave the way they do — why the prices of shares can swing on rumour and mood, why they seem so loosely tethered to the underlying earnings of the companies they represent.

His answer is that professional investment is, in large part, not a matter of estimating what an asset is worth. It is a matter of estimating what other people will shortly believe it is worth. "It is not a case of choosing those which, to the best of one's judgment, are really the prettiest," Keynes wrote, "nor even those which average opinion genuinely thinks the prettiest." The clever player, he observed, devotes his intelligence "to anticipating what average opinion expects the average opinion to be." And he pressed the recursion one crucial step further, noting that there are those who "practise the fourth, fifth and higher degrees" — anticipating what average opinion expects average opinion to expect it to be, and onward.

Keynes wrote from the inside. He was not only the century's most influential economist but an active speculator, managing his own fortune and the endowment of King's College, Cambridge, through the booms and crashes of the interwar years. He had felt, in his own account books, the vertiginous quality of trying to be one jump ahead of a crowd that was itself trying to be one jump ahead of him. The beauty contest was his attempt to name that sensation precisely — the sensation of a game in which the fundamentals matter only insofar as you can predict when other people will start to care about them.

The mechanism

The gear that turns here is self-reference. In an ordinary estimation problem, there is an answer sitting outside the crowd: the ox weighs what it weighs, the horse finishes when it finishes, the earnings arrive or they don't. Each guesser is tethered, however loosely, to that external fact. Their errors scatter around it, and averaging pulls the scatter toward the truth. The reality anchors the crowd.

In a beauty contest, that anchor is gone. Value is defined as the crowd's average opinion. There is no fact outside the group against which a guess can be right or wrong; the target is the group's own behaviour. And because everyone knows this, each person's best move is to model everyone else's move — which requires modelling their models of everyone else, which requires modelling those. Rational analysis of the object is replaced by recursive anticipation of the crowd. The system folds back on itself, and reasoning about it becomes reasoning about expectations of expectations, with no floor.

Economists gave the mechanism a laboratory form clean enough to measure. It is called the p-beauty contest, or the "guess two-thirds of the average" game. Everyone in a group picks a number between 0 and 100. The winner is whoever chose closest to two-thirds of the average of all the numbers picked. Work out the logic and it drives you downward relentlessly. If you imagine everyone else picking at random, the average is 50, so you should guess about 33. But if everyone reasons that far, the average will be 33, so you should guess two-thirds of that, about 22. If everyone reasons that far, you should go lower still. Carried to its conclusion, the only self-consistent choice — the point at which no one wishes they had guessed differently — is zero. That is the Nash equilibrium, the answer flawless recursive reasoners would all converge on.

The game was formalised in the game-theory literature by Hervé Moulin in 1986 and turned into a landmark experiment by the German economist Rosemarie Nagel, whose results appeared in the American Economic Review in 1995. And what she found is the whole point. Real people do not pick zero. Winning guesses in typical runs cluster somewhere in the low twenties to mid-thirties — the exact band tends to vary with the group. People stop after one or two rounds of the reasoning. Some pick 50 and stop. Many take two-thirds of 50 and stop at 33. A smaller group iterates once more to around 22. Very few descend all the way to zero. Nagel called these levels of reasoning "steps," and the pattern she uncovered became known as level-k thinking: level-0 players guess without strategising, level-1 players best-respond to level-0, level-2 to level-1, and the population thins out fast as the levels climb. Most human beings, it turns out, reason about two steps deep into the crowd's mind, then stop.

The anchor example

Run the two-thirds game yourself, in a room of clever people who have never seen it, and you will watch the theory come alive. Ask everyone to write a number from 0 to 100. Collect them, take the average, multiply by two-thirds, and announce the winner.

The equilibrium says zero. Reality says otherwise, almost every time. A cluster of numbers lands near 50 — the people who did not think about the "two-thirds" at all, or chose not to. A larger cluster lands near 33 — the people who imagined an average of 50 and took two-thirds. A thinner cluster lands near 22 — the people who went one step further. And a scattering of zeroes and ones come from those who worked out the full logic and assumed, wrongly, that everyone else would too. The winning number typically falls somewhere in the twenties or low thirties, precisely because the crowd is shallow. The people who guessed zero lose. They were too rational for the room. They correctly solved the game as if everyone were a perfect reasoner, in a world where almost no one is.

What makes this such a durable memory hook is the lesson buried in the loss. To win, you must not out-think the crowd. You must out-guess how far the crowd will think — and stop exactly one step ahead of where it actually stops. Being more logical is a losing strategy. The prize goes to whoever best models the crowd's limited depth, not to whoever reasons most deeply. That is Keynes' insight in miniature: in a self-referential game, the correct answer is not the true answer but the popular one, and the skill is anticipation, not analysis.

The flip

Here is the reversal on which this whole book turns. Go back to Francis Galton's ox (item 17). Eight hundred fairgoers at Plymouth guessed the weight of a slaughtered animal, and the average of their guesses came within a pound of the truth. That is the wise crowd in its purest form. And the reason it worked is that every guesser was looking at the same real ox. Their estimates were independent shots at an external fact. The errors scattered on both sides and cancelled, and what survived the averaging was the signal — the truth the fair-goers were collectively, unknowingly, triangulating.

Keynes' beauty contest is that mechanism with the anchor cut. Take away the ox — take away any external fact that the guesses are aimed at — and set the crowd to guessing about the crowd, and aggregation stops being wise and becomes a hall of mirrors. The estimates no longer scatter around a truth; they chase each other. Independence, the condition that made Galton's average trustworthy, is precisely what a beauty contest destroys, because your best guess is defined by everyone else's guess. This is the self-referential flip, and it is one of the sharpest statements of this series' thesis: the same crowd is wise or mad depending on whether its members are anchored to reality or only to each other. Galton's fairgoers and Keynes' speculators are not different species. They are one crowd under two conditions. Point the guesses at a real ox and you get wisdom. Point them at one another and you get a bubble.

This is the deep structure beneath the market items that surround this chapter. The efficient-market view (item 60) holds that prices track fundamentals; the behavioural-finance reply is, in essence, that markets are shot through with beauty-contest dynamics. Bubbles and crashes (item 64) are what beauty contests look like when they run — everyone buying not what they value but what they expect others to value, until the last greater fool runs out. It is George Soros's reflexivity (item 65) named a different way: prices shaped by expectations that are in turn shaped by prices, self-reference all the way down. It is the continuous-value cousin of information cascades (item 26), where each actor copies the visible choices of those ahead rather than consulting their own knowledge. And it is exactly what prediction markets (item 23) fight against by paying people for being right about reality rather than right about the crowd — an attempt to bolt the anchor back on.

The human weight

The beauty contest is not confined to trading floors. It is the hidden logic of a great deal of social life. Fashion is a beauty contest: you wear not what you most like but what you expect to read as current, which is what everyone else is also expecting. Fame and viral popularity run on it: things become popular in part because people believe they will be popular, and share them to be early to what everyone will be sharing. Political bandwagons are built from it: voters peel toward the candidate they think will win rather than the one they privately prefer, and the expectation of victory helps manufacture the victory. In each case, the object of the game is the group's own anticipated behaviour, and private preference is subordinated to the forecast of consensus.

The sober version of this thought reaches the frontier this series keeps circling. When legitimacy itself becomes a beauty contest — when what counts as respectable opinion is whatever everyone believes everyone else believes — then "public opinion" can detach entirely from any actual majority view. Everyone conforms to a phantom consensus, each person reading the room and adjusting to what the room seems to hold, and the room holds nothing but everyone's reading of everyone else. This is the direct link to preference falsification (item 34) and pluralistic ignorance (item 42). In those chapters, the "public opinion" that individuals track and defer to is shown to be, sometimes, a mirage — a belief that the majority holds a view when in truth almost no one privately holds it. The beauty contest supplies the missing mechanism: it explains why a crowd would track a mirage rather than the truth. Once value or legitimacy is defined by expected consensus, people rationally attend to the expected consensus and ignore reality, and a false norm can hold a whole society in its grip while scarcely anyone believes it. An order can be sustained not because people judge it right but because each expects the others to comply.

The critique

The metaphor is powerful, which is exactly why it needs handling with care. Markets are not purely self-referential, and Keynes never quite said they were. Fundamentals do reassert themselves. A company that earns nothing cannot indefinitely command a price built on the expectation that others will pay more; when reality intrudes, the beauty contest ends, often violently, in a crash. The anchor is not permanently severed — it is stretched, sometimes for years, and then it snaps back. A theory that treated all valuation as nothing but convention would miss the moment the fundamentals return to collect, and would be unable to say why bubbles ever burst rather than inflating forever.

The laboratory model has its own limits. Level-k thinking is a wonderfully clean description of how people reason in the two-thirds game, but it is more descriptive than predictive: it fits the distribution of guesses after the fact better than it forecasts them before. The parameter "k" is often estimated from the very data it is meant to explain. And the two-thirds game is artificial — a one-shot puzzle with a known structure and a cash prize, played mostly by students and economists, a long way from the messy, high-stakes, information-rich environment of a real market. Its charm is its cleanliness, and cleanliness is bought at the price of realism.

There are structural checks on beauty-contest dynamics too. Arbitrage lets traders who do look at fundamentals profit at the expense of those merely chasing the crowd, which should, in theory, pull prices back toward value; long investment horizons reward patience over the anticipation of next week's mood. How strong these correctives are is contested — the behavioural school argues that arbitrage is limited and risky, that the market can stay irrational longer than a rational trader can stay solvent — but they are real, and a fair account cannot pretend the mirror-hall has no exits.

The deepest risk is overextension. It is tempting, once you have the beauty-contest lens, to dismiss all social valuation as mere convention with nothing real underneath — to treat every price, every reputation, every norm as pure crowd-guessing. That is a cynicism the analogy does not license. Most of the world's estimation problems still have an ox in them somewhere: a fact that eventually asserts itself, a reality the crowd is, however clumsily, trying to track. The beauty contest is what happens when the ox is removed, or forgotten, or drowned out by the noise of everyone watching everyone else. Naming that condition precisely is the achievement. Mistaking it for the whole of human judgment is the error.

References & sources

Confirmed sources

  • Keynes, John Maynard. The General Theory of Employment, Interest and Money. Macmillan, 1936. — Chapter 12, "The State of Long-Term Expectation," contains the newspaper beauty-contest analogy and the passage on anticipating what average opinion expects average opinion to be, including the "third degree, fourth, fifth and higher degrees" of anticipation. The foundational source for the chapter's central idea and Keynes' framing of investment as crowd-anticipation.
  • Nagel, Rosemarie. "Unraveling in Guessing Games: An Experimental Study." American Economic Review, vol. 85, no. 5, 1995, pp. 1313–1326. — The landmark experiment introducing the "guess two-thirds of the average" game (p-beauty contest) as an empirical instrument, reporting that real guesses cluster well above the Nash equilibrium of zero and introducing the levels-of-reasoning ("steps") interpretation that became level-k thinking. Supports the mechanism and anchor-example beats.
  • Moulin, Hervé. Game Theory for the Social Sciences. 2nd ed., New York University Press, 1986. — Source in the game-theory literature for the p-beauty contest / guessing-game structure and the dominance-solvability argument driving the equilibrium to zero. Supports the formal statement of the game.
  • Camerer, Colin F. Behavioral Game Theory: Experiments in Strategic Interaction. Princeton University Press, 2003. — Synthesis of the beauty-contest and level-k literature; develops cognitive-hierarchy and level-k models of limited strategic reasoning and reviews the experimental evidence that most players reason only one or two steps deep. Supports the mechanism, critique, and level-k discussion.
  • Galton, Francis. "Vox Populi." Nature, vol. 75, 1907, pp. 450–451. — The ox-weight-guessing account used as the anchored counter-case in beat 5 (cross-referenced as item 17).
