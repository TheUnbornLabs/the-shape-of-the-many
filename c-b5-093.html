Chapter 93 · The Shape Remade

Crowdsourcing & citizen science

Give a problem too large for any expert to too many strangers, and the answer walks in through a million doors at once — but so does the question of who is paying whom.

The idea

Some problems are not hard in the way a proof is hard. They are hard in the way a beach is hard to count grain by grain: not deep, but vast. No single mind and no single institution has enough eyes, enough hands, or enough hours to work through them. Crowdsourcing is the discovery that you do not need one. If you can break such a problem into many small pieces that ordinary people can each handle, hand those pieces out to a large, loosely defined group of volunteers or workers, and then stitch the answers back together, you can accomplish what no individual and no laboratory could — often faster, sometimes better, and at a scale that would bankrupt any payroll.

Citizen science is crowdsourcing pointed at research: it enlists non-professionals to gather or interpret real scientific data. Birdwatchers logging what they see, volunteers sorting telescope images, gamers folding proteins on their laptops — each is one node in a distributed instrument. Both ideas rest on the same wager the whole series has been circling. A great many modest contributions, aggregated with care, can converge on something an elite could not produce alone. The crowd here is not deliberating or panicking. It is sensing and labouring — a planet-spanning array of eyes and hands. And like every crowd in this book, it can be pointed toward wisdom or toward exploitation, and the same infrastructure serves both.

The origin

The word is young. The journalist Jeff Howe coined "crowdsourcing" in a June 2006 article in Wired, "The Rise of Crowdsourcing," describing how companies were beginning to take work once done in-house and put it out, over the internet, to a large undefined public — stock photography shot by amateurs, product ideas submitted by customers, tasks parcelled out to whoever would take them. Howe's portmanteau — crowd plus outsourcing — named a shift that the web had quietly made possible: the transaction cost of coordinating strangers had collapsed nearly to nothing.

But the intellectual roots run deeper than the coinage. Crowdsourcing is James Surowiecki's wisdom of crowds (item 20) put to work — the four conditions of diversity, independence, decentralisation, and aggregation, operationalised as a task pipeline. It descends, further back, from Francis Galton's ox at the 1906 country fair (item 17), where averaged guesses from a fairground crowd landed within a pound of the true weight. And it has a parallel origin in computer science, in Luis von Ahn's idea of human computation: deliberately routing to human brains the small perceptual and interpretive tasks that machines still fail at, and harvesting the results. Von Ahn's reCAPTCHA, described in a 2008 Science paper, is the purest instance — the security check that asks you to transcribe a warped word was, for years, quietly using those keystrokes to digitise words that optical scanners had failed to read in old books and newspapers. Every human proving they were not a robot was, without knowing it, a labourer in the largest transcription project in history.

Citizen science as a named practice is older still. The Audubon Society's Christmas Bird Count has enlisted amateur ornithologists to tally birds across North America every winter since 1900 — more than a century of distributed biodiversity data gathered long before anyone thought to name the method.

The mechanism

The engine has three gears, and a problem must fit all three or the machine seizes.

The first is decomposition. The large task must break cleanly into many small tasks, each doable by a non-expert in minutes without special training: is this galaxy spiral or elliptical? what bird is this? does this fold lower the energy? If a piece cannot be made small and self-contained, it cannot be handed to a stranger.

The second is aggregation. The small answers must recombine into something meaningful — a catalogue, a map, a distribution, a solution. This is the same aggregation step that runs under Galton's ox and Condorcet's theorem (item 18): individual contributions are noisy and often wrong, but pooled correctly, the noise cancels and the signal survives. Averaging works for continuous guesses; majority voting works for categories; a leaderboard works when you only need the single best answer anyone found.

The third, and the one amateurs of crowdsourcing forget, is quality control. Volunteers vary, effort is uneven, and some contributors are careless or malicious. So every serious system builds in defences: redundancy (show the same galaxy to forty people and keep the consensus), voting (discard the outliers), reputation (weight contributors by their track record), and calibration against known answers (seed the stream with items whose truth you already know, and measure who gets them right). Quality is manufactured after the fact, out of overlap and cross-checking, not assumed up front.

When all three gears mesh, something remarkable happens: the crowd becomes a distributed instrument. Its many eyes see more of the world than any single observer could — this is the evolutionary many-eyes principle (item 12), the vigilance of the herd, rebuilt in silicon at planetary scale. And its many hands perform more labour than any institution could afford. The crowd senses, and the crowd works.

The anchor example

In July 2007, an astronomer named Kevin Schawinski faced a wall. He needed to classify the shapes of galaxies — spiral or elliptical, and which way the spirals turned — across the roughly one million images in the Sloan Digital Sky Survey. Human eyes still beat computers at this pattern-recognition task, but a single graduate student sorting a million galaxies by hand would need years. So Schawinski and his colleague Chris Lintott built a website called Galaxy Zoo and invited the public to help. They expected, perhaps, a modest turnout.

Within twenty-four hours the site was receiving classifications at a rate of around seventy thousand an hour. In its first year, more than a hundred and fifty thousand volunteers delivered on the order of fifty million classifications — every galaxy seen not once but many times over, so that agreement among strangers stood in for the judgment of an expert. Lintott and colleagues laid out the method and early results in a 2008 paper in Monthly Notices of the Royal Astronomical Society. The redundancy was the point: with dozens of independent eyes on each image, a reliable consensus classification emerged from unreliable individuals — Condorcet's arithmetic, running on a distributed workforce of hobbyists.

Then came the part no algorithm would have produced. A Dutch schoolteacher named Hanny van Arkel, browsing the images as a volunteer, noticed a strange glowing blob beneath one galaxy — a bright ghostly cloud unlike anything in the classification scheme. She flagged it. It turned out to be a truly novel astrophysical object, a vast cloud of ionised gas lit by the echo of a quasar that had since faded, and it entered the literature as Hanny's Voorwerp — Dutch for "Hanny's object." A million eyes did not merely sort the known catalogue faster than a machine. They found something in it that no one had thought to look for. That is the distinctive gift of the crowd-as-sensor: not just speed, but serendipity — the many-eyes principle catching the anomaly that no query would have specified.

The flip

Crowdsourcing is the wise crowd wearing its most constructive face. This is not the crowd deliberating toward a verdict or stampeding toward a cliff. It is the crowd as distributed sensing and distributed labour — the evolutionary many-eyes of the vigilant herd (item 12) rebuilt by the internet at the scale of the whole species. Where Surowiecki's four conditions (item 20) are the engineering and Galton's ox (item 17) the demonstration, crowdsourcing is the wise crowd put to productive work: producing knowledge, maps, and discoveries no elite could produce alone. It sits alongside Wikipedia and open source (item 91) as the constructive twin of the digital swarm — stigmergy that builds — and draws its power from the same well as superforecasting (item 92) and diversity prediction (item 19): independent, diverse contributions aggregated so that error cancels and coverage compounds.

And here is the seam this whole series traces. The very infrastructure that lets a crowd build — the internet's ability to decompose any task into micro-pieces and route them to a million strangers — is identical to the infrastructure that lets a crowd be exploited. Point the pipeline at galaxies and volunteers discover Hanny's Voorwerp. Point the same pipeline at underpaid piecework and you get "ghost work": invisible, precarious micro-labourers cleaning data, labelling images, and moderating content behind systems the public believes are automatic. Point it at a target and you get crowdsourced harassment — the online mob (item 90) using the same distributed coordination to swarm a person instead of a problem. Point it at fabrication and you get manufactured content (item 89), the crowd's distributed labour weaponised to counterfeit consensus. The mechanism does not change. Only the sign changes, and the sign is set by a single question: is the crowd empowered and fairly treated, or is it invisibly exploited? The wise crowd and the mob are, once again, the same people — here, the same distributed workforce — under two conditions.

The human weight

Follow the empowering edge first, because it is real and it is large. Citizen science has turned amateurs into a genuine scientific instrument. eBird, run by the Cornell Lab of Ornithology, aggregates hundreds of millions of bird observations from ordinary watchers into maps of species distribution and migration fine enough to inform conservation policy — distributed biodiversity sensing that no funded survey could match for coverage. Foldit, a puzzle game released in 2008, turned protein folding into a competition; in 2011, its players collectively solved the three-dimensional structure of a retroviral protease that had defeated computational methods, a result its designers published in Nature Structural & Molecular Biology with the gamers credited as co-authors. In crises, crowds map disaster zones from satellite imagery faster than any agency can survey them. This is the hopeful frontier: participation in knowledge-making prised open, gatekept institutions bypassed, citizens promoted from spectators to sensors and solvers.

But the same architecture builds a hidden underclass. The anthropologist Mary Gray and the computer scientist Siddharth Suri gave it a name in their 2019 book Ghost Work: the millions of people worldwide doing on-demand micro-tasks — tagging images, transcribing audio, judging whether a photo is obscene, rating whether an answer is good — the invisible piecework that makes "artificial intelligence" and "automated" platforms function. This labour is deliberately effaced. The interface says the algorithm did it; a person did it, for cents, with no benefits, no security, and no name. The AI economy that presents itself as the triumph of machines is, in a large and unadmitted part, a triumph of cheaply aggregated human effort — the crowd labouring behind the curtain. This is stratification (item 85) reborn inside the "automated" economy: a new hierarchy in which some people are seen and credited and others are structurally invisible. The frontier double-edge could not be sharper. The same decomposition-and-aggregation that lets a schoolteacher discover a Voorwerp lets a platform extract a data-labeller's day without ever letting the public see her. Whether crowdsourcing democratises or exploits turns entirely on who is credited, who is paid, and who is permitted to be seen — the same question of visible and invisible participation that runs beneath Wikipedia (item 91) and beneath every order held together by what is kept out of sight.

The critique

The method's reach is narrower than its enthusiasts admit, and the limits are structural, not incidental.

First, it works only for problems that fit the three gears. A task must be decomposable into small, non-expert pieces, its answers aggregable into something coherent, and its quality checkable. Problems needing deep expertise, sustained context, or global coherence resist decomposition — you cannot crowdsource a legal argument, a diagnosis, or a novel by handing out sentences to strangers. Where a task cannot be sliced without losing its meaning, the crowd has nothing to grip.

Second, quality control is a permanent battle, not a solved problem. Spam, low-effort submissions, and deliberate gaming degrade every open system; the defences — redundancy, reputation, calibration — cost effort and never fully close the gap. The Netflix Prize, the famous 2006–2009 recommendation contest, showed both faces: it drew brilliant crowd-sourced solutions, but the winning approaches were so baroque that Netflix reportedly never deployed the full model, and a related privacy failure in the released data ended the sequel. Crowds optimise the metric you give them, not the outcome you wanted.

Third, and most corrosive to citizen science specifically, is participation bias. The crowd is not a random sample of anyone. Volunteers are self-selected — wealthier, more educated, more online, more concentrated in certain regions and demographics — so the data they produce is skewed by where the observers are, not only by where the phenomenon is. eBird records more birds where more birdwatchers live, not where more birds live; galaxy classifications inherit the biases of the people who volunteer. Serious citizen-science programs now spend real effort calibrating these distortions, and the calibration is never perfect. The "wisdom" of these crowds rests, as ever, on Surowiecki's conditions (item 20) — diversity, independence, aggregation — and where the volunteers are homogeneous, correlated, or unrepresentative, the wisdom quietly drains out and biased data takes its place.

Finally, the honest word about the labour. Much of what is filed under "the crowd" is not volunteers at all but paid micro-workers, and the ethics travel with the wage. A method celebrated for democratising participation is, in one of its largest deployments, a method for buying human judgment at piece rates from people the system is designed to keep invisible. Any full account of crowdsourcing that mentions Hanny's Voorwerp but not ghost work has told the sunny half and called it the whole.
