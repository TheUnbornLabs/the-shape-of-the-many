# References — 81. Ritual & behavioural synchrony

## Confirmed sources

- McNeill, William H. *Keeping Together in Time: Dance and Drill in Human History*. Harvard University Press, 1995. — Origin of the "muscular bonding" thesis; the army-drill memoir and the historical survey of synchronous movement (war dance, folk dance, religious procession, military drill) as a motor of human solidarity.
- Wiltermuth, Scott S., and Chip Heath. "Synchrony and Cooperation." *Psychological Science* 20, no. 1 (2009): 1–5. — The anchor experiments: synchronised walking, singing, and moving increased cooperation and self-sacrifice in subsequent economic (public-goods) games. Journal/volume/issue/pages CONFIRMED (three experiments; the authors note positive affect is not required for synchrony to foster cooperation).
- Hove, Michael J., and Jane L. Risen. "It's All in the Timing: Interpersonal Synchrony Increases Affiliation." *Social Cognition* 27, no. 6 (2009): 949–961. — Tapping in synchrony with another person raised liking and felt affiliation, with degree of synchrony predicting degree of affiliation. (Journal/volume/issue confirmed; page range given variously as 949–960 or 949–961.)
- Durkheim, Émile. *Les formes élémentaires de la vie religieuse*. Félix Alcan, 1912. (Eng. trans. *The Elementary Forms of Religious Life*.) — *Effervescence collective*: the self-transcending emotion generated by concerted assembly, treated here as the phenomenon for which ritual synchrony supplies a proximate mechanism (cross-referenced as item 76).
- Whitehouse, Harvey. *Modes of Religiosity: A Cognitive Theory of Religious Transmission*. AltaMira Press, 2004. — The "doctrinal" (high-frequency, low-arousal, routinised) versus "imagistic" (low-frequency, high-arousal) modes distinction, referenced as the two ritual routes and cross-referenced to identity fusion (item 84).
