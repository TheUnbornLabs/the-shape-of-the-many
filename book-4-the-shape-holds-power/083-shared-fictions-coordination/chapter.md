# 83. Shared fictions as coordination devices

> A banknote is a worthless scrap of cotton that moves armies, feeds cities, and topples governments — because enough of us agree, all at once, to act as if it were not.

## The idea

Hold a fifty-euro note up to the light. It is a rectangle of cotton-linen paper, a few grams of pigment, a hologram and a metallic thread. Nothing about its physics gives it worth. You cannot eat it, burn it usefully, or build with it. And yet it will buy a week of groceries or settle a debt. Its power lives nowhere in the object. It lives in a fact about other people: the near-certain expectation that the next person will take it too.

This is the strange class of thing at the centre of this chapter. Money, nations, corporations, gods, human rights, laws, weekends, kingship — none of these exists in the way a mountain or an electron exists. Physics and chemistry do not know about them. They are not delusions either; a delusion is something one person believes and the world contradicts. These become real precisely because enough people *act as if* they are real, together, at the same time. Call them shared fictions, or intersubjective realities: facts that exist in the space between minds rather than in any single mind or in the material world. Their reality is not that of the note in your hand; it is the reality of an agreement so widely and reflexively held that no individual can wish it away.

The claim of this chapter is that such fictions are not a curiosity at the edge of human life but the load-bearing structure of it. They are how millions of strangers who will never meet coordinate their behaviour without a word passing between them — the largest coordination technology our species ever invented, older than writing and more consequential than the wheel.

## The origin

The idea that our largest structures are shared fictions has two intellectual lineages, one philosophical and old, one popular and recent.

The old line runs through Émile Durkheim, who at the close of the nineteenth century insisted that "social facts" — the currency, the law, the moral rules of a society — are external to any individual and coercive upon them, to be studied as objectively as a biologist studies a cell. A social fact was real even though it existed only in the collective; it constrained you whether or not you assented.

The rigorous modern statement belongs to the philosopher John Searle. In *The Construction of Social Reality* (1995) and later *Making the Social World* (2010), Searle distinguished *brute facts* — the mountain is 8,000 metres high — from *institutional facts*, which are true only relative to human agreement. His formula for how the second kind is built is deceptively plain: *X counts as Y in context C*. This piece of paper (X) counts as legal tender (Y) in the eurozone (C); this person counts as president in this republic. The Y-term Searle called a *status function*: a function a thing performs not because of its physics but because we collectively assign it and collectively accept the assignment. Money, he wrote, is money because we count it as money — and the acceptance is not a private opinion but a shared, self-referring one, held together by what he named *collective intentionality*: not many separate beliefs that happen to coincide, but a truly shared "we accept."

The popular line is Yuval Noah Harari's *Sapiens* (2011), which took Searle's institutional facts and Durkheim's social facts, fused them with cognitive evolution, and gave them a memorable name: shared fictions, or imagined orders. Harari's argument was that *Homo sapiens* conquered the planet not because we are stronger or smarter one-on-one, but because we can cooperate flexibly in vast numbers — and we can do that because we believe in things that exist only in our collective imagination. Chimpanzees cannot organise a crusade or float a bond issue; they lack the shared fiction. Benedict Anderson had made a version of the point in 1983 for one such fiction — the nation, he wrote, is an *imagined community*, imagined because most of its members will never meet yet each carries "the image of their communion." Harari's move was to see the nation as one case of a general and very old trick.

## The mechanism

Why does a shared fiction hold? The engine is not faith in the religious sense. It is a coordination equilibrium — a self-fulfilling expectation about what everyone else will do.

Return to the banknote. I accept it not because I value the paper but because I expect you to accept it from me tomorrow. You accept it for the same reason about the next person. Each of us is rationally right to take the note *so long as everyone else takes it too*. The belief that money has value is, at bottom, a belief about other people's beliefs, and it makes itself true by being widely held. This is reflexivity in its purest form (item 65): the expectation validates itself. There is no bedrock underneath the tower of expectations — no final person whose acceptance is grounded in the paper's intrinsic worth. It is expectation resting on expectation, all the way down, and it stands *because* it is circular.

This is why a shared fiction can solve a problem that direct human relationship cannot. Personal trust runs out at the edge of the group you can know — Dunbar's number (item 51), the roughly one hundred and fifty relationships a human can actively hold. Beyond that ceiling, cooperation with strangers cannot rest on knowing them. The shared fiction is the workaround. I do not need to trust *you* to trade with you; I need only trust the note — that is, trust that the fiction we both inhabit will hold. The fiction substitutes a shared belief for a personal bond, and shared beliefs scale to millions where bonds cannot.

Two further properties keep such fictions standing. They are *reflexively self-validating*: because everyone acts as if the border or the currency is real, the world fills with the consequences — checkpoints, prices, contracts — which are then read as proof that the thing was real all along (item 65). And they are *culturally transmitted* (item 80): no one reasons their way to the value of money each generation; children are raised inside the fiction, absorb it before they can question it, and pass it on. A shared fiction is a piece of software that runs on a whole population and installs itself on the next one.

## The anchor example

Keep to money, because money shows both the power and the fragility with unusual clarity.

Consider what a currency does. It lets a stranger's hour of work in one city be exchanged, days later, for a stranger's loaf of bread in another, through a chain of intermediaries none of whom trust one another or ever meet. It prices a barrel of oil against a bushel of wheat against an hour of a surgeon's time. No committee arranges this; the price system does, and it is denominated in a fiction. The whole choreography of a modern economy — the wisest crowd our species runs, a computation no planner could match — is conducted in a currency whose value is nothing but the shared expectation that it will be accepted.

Now watch the fiction fail. In Weimar Germany in 1923, or Zimbabwe in 2008, the expectation cracked. Once people suspected that the next person would *not* accept the note tomorrow at today's value, the rational move was to spend it now, before it fell further — which pushed prices up, which confirmed the suspicion, which accelerated the spending. The reflexive loop that had held the currency up now drove it down with the same self-fulfilling logic. Wheelbarrows of banknotes bought a loaf of bread. The paper had not changed; its chemistry was identical on the last day and the first. What collapsed was the shared act of acceptance — and when that went, so did the coordination it had carried. A bank run (item 28) is the same collapse in miniature: a bank is solvent as long as we all believe it is and act accordingly, and insolvent the instant enough of us stop.

That is the whole lesson in one object. The note works because we act as if it works. Its reality is our coordinated pretence — and the pretence is not weakness but the mechanism itself.

## The flip

Here is the turn on which this chapter, and the book, depends.

The shared fiction is humanity's supreme *coordination* technology. It lets the wise crowd operate at continental scale, far past the Dunbar ceiling (item 51) where personal bonds give out — the mechanism behind money, contract law, the limited-liability corporation, and the audacious modern fiction of universal human rights, in which we all agree to act as if every person carries an inviolable worth that no microscope will ever find. All of these are imagined orders, and all let strangers cooperate. This is the constructive pole, and it is magnificent.

But the very same capacity builds the opposite. The mind that can imagine "all humans have equal rights" is the mind that can imagine "some humans are born to rule and others to serve." Caste, racial hierarchy, the divine right of kings, the natural superiority of one bloodline or skin or sex over another — these too are shared fictions. They too coordinate the behaviour of millions, and they too are real in their consequences: the untouchable kept from the well, the serf tied to the land, the enslaved person bought and sold, all because enough people act as if the imagined order were a fact of nature. The fiction that liberates and the fiction that subordinates are made of the same material and run on the same engine.

So the thesis of this whole series arrives here in a new key. There is not a species of good fiction and a species of bad fiction, any more than there is a wise crowd and a mob of different kinds. There is one capacity — the human gift for collective make-believe — and a condition that sets its sign. A shared fiction is neither true nor false in the ordinary way; as the sociologist W. I. Thomas put it, if people define situations as real, they are real in their consequences. What sends a fiction to the bright or the dark pole is not its truth but its *function*: whether it lets strangers cooperate as equals, or enforces the subordination of some to others. Money and human rights are the constructive pole; caste and divine right are the destructive twin — identity fusion (item 84) and stratification (item 85) are where that dark work gets done.

## The human weight

Now the frontier of this series, stated as plainly as it can be. Caste, race, nobility, the "natural" superiority of some people over others — these are shared fictions. There is no gene for the Brahmin, no biology of the aristocrat, no natural fact that makes one skin worthier than another. They are intersubjective realities with no ground in nature, which coordinate behaviour and become real in their consequences precisely because, and only because, enough people act as if they were true.

Say why that matters. If an unjust order were a fact of nature — like gravity, like the freezing point of water — then to resist it would be to rage at physics. But an order that is a shared fiction is a thing we are collectively *doing*, an agreement we are all, right now, enacting — and what is enacted can, in principle, be un-enacted. To recognise a stratifying order as a shared fiction rather than a natural fact is the first move toward changing it, which is exactly why every such order works so hard to disguise itself. Caste presents itself as cosmic law. Racial hierarchy dressed itself in false biology. Kings claimed to rule by God. The one thing a dominating fiction cannot afford to admit is that it is a fiction — a collective agreement that could be revised — because the admission hands its subjects the knowledge that the order lives only in their continued performance of it.

And the performance is held in place by a further mechanism this series has named. The fiction persists not because everyone privately believes it, but because everyone acts as if everyone else believes it — preference falsification (item 34), the hidden dissent that keeps a silent order looking unanimous. Each person doubts, sees no one else doubting, and so keeps performing, and the performance is read by all as consent. The order is reflexively self-validating (item 65): the acting-as-if manufactures the appearance of a fact, and the appearance commands the next round of acting-as-if. This is where the series' frontier and its deepest apparatus meet. An unjust order is a shared fiction, sustained by preference falsification, reflexively self-validating, culturally transmitted from one generation into the next before it can be examined — and therefore, however permanent it feels, revisable. Not easily, and not without the silence breaking. But revisable, because it was never nature. It was only us, agreeing, and able to stop.

## The critique

The framing is powerful, which is exactly why it needs guarding against its own excess.

Begin with the popular source. Harari is a synthesiser of great reach, and specialist historians and anthropologists have criticised *Sapiens* for sweeping claims that outrun the evidence and for a confidence the underlying research does not always warrant. The idea of shared fictions is sound and old; the popular packaging can be glib. A careful reader takes the concept and leaves the swagger.

The graver hazard is the word *fiction* itself. To call money or the law a fiction can slide into the suggestion that it is arbitrary or unreal — "it's all just made up." This is the error Searle spent his career forestalling. Institutional facts are real facts. They are *observer-relative* — they exist only because of human agreement — but within that agreement they are as hard and consequential as any brute fact. Your mortgage is a shared fiction and it will also put you on the street. The right word is not *false* but *constructed*, and construction is a way of being real, not a way of being fake. Lazy relativism, the shrug that says nothing social is really real, is a misreading, not an implication.

Nor is everything social equally fictional. Some institutions are near-arbitrary conventions — which side of the road we drive on could as easily have been the other. But others track real functional needs: a system of promising, some form of property, a way to settle disputes recur across cultures because the coordination problems are real, and calling them "just fictions" flattens a distinction that matters.

And the last caution guards the frontier itself. The move from "X is a social construction" to "X can and should be changed" does not follow automatically. A fiction can be constructed and still be extraordinarily stable, held by equilibria, incentives, and generations of transmission that no act of recognition dissolves overnight. Realising that money is a shared belief does not let you print your own; realising that a caste order is a fiction does not by itself lift the untouchable from the well. Construction establishes possibility, not ease, and not obligation — the normative case has to be made on its own merits. What the shared-fiction insight offers is precise and limited, and it is enough: not a lever, but the knowledge of where the lever might go. The order is not nature. It is us. That is the beginning of the argument, not its end.
