# References — 83. Shared fictions as coordination devices

## Confirmed sources

- Harari, Yuval Noah. *Sapiens: A Brief History of Humankind*. Harvill Secker / Harper, 2011 (English edition 2014). — The popular source for "shared fictions" / "imagined orders"; the argument that large-scale flexible cooperation among strangers rests on collective belief in things that do not exist in nature (money, gods, nations, corporations, human rights).
- Searle, John R. *The Construction of Social Reality*. Free Press, 1995. — The rigorous philosophical account: brute vs. institutional facts, status functions, collective intentionality, and the "X counts as Y in context C" formula; money as money by collective acceptance.
- Searle, John R. *Making the Social World: The Structure of Human Civilization*. Oxford University Press, 2010. — Searle's later, systematic development of status functions and the ontology of institutional/observer-relative facts; the point that social facts are real though observer-relative.
- Durkheim, Émile. *The Rules of Sociological Method* (*Les Règles de la méthode sociologique*). 1895. — The concept of "social facts" as external to and coercive upon the individual, to be studied objectively; the deep ancestor of the intersubjective-reality idea.
- Anderson, Benedict. *Imagined Communities: Reflections on the Origin and Spread of Nationalism*. Verso, 1983 (rev. 1991). — The nation as an "imagined community," imagined because most members never meet yet hold "the image of their communion"; a canonical case of one shared fiction.
- Thomas, W. I., and Dorothy Swaine Thomas. *The Child in America: Behavior Problems and Programs*. Alfred A. Knopf, 1928. — Source of the Thomas theorem: "If men define situations as real, they are real in their consequences." CONFIRMED (the chapter paraphrases "if people define situations…"; the original reads "if men define situations…"). Used in beat 5 for the "real in its consequences" claim.
