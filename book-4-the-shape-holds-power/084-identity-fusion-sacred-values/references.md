# References — 84. Identity fusion & sacred values

## Confirmed sources

- Swann, W. B. Jr., Gómez, Á., Seyle, D. C., Morales, J. F., & Huici, C. "Identity fusion: The interplay of personal and social identities in extreme group behavior." *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*, 96(5), 2009, pp. 995–1011. — Introduces identity fusion as distinct from social identification; the visceral oneness of personal and social self.
- Swann, W. B. Jr., Jetten, J., Gómez, Á., Whitehouse, H., & Bastian, B. "When group membership gets personal: A theory of identity fusion." *Psychological Review*, 119(3), 2012, pp. 441–456. — The theoretical synthesis; fusion keeps the personal self active and pooled with the collective, predicting extreme pro-group action. Journal/volume/issue/pages CONFIRMED.
- Gómez, Á., Brooks, M. L., Buhrmester, M. D., Vázquez, A., Jetten, J., & Swann, W. B. Jr. "On the nature of identity fusion: Insights into the construct and a new measure." *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*, 100(5), 2011, pp. 918–933. — The pictorial (circles) measure of fusion and its validation. Journal/volume/issue/pages CONFIRMED.
- Tetlock, P. E. "Thinking the unthinkable: Sacred values and taboo cognitions." *Trends in Cognitive Sciences*, 7(7), 2003, pp. 320–324. — Taboo trade-offs; moral outrage and moral cleansing when protected values are priced against secular ones. Journal/volume/issue/pages CONFIRMED.
- Atran, S. *Talking to the Enemy: Faith, Brotherhood, and the (Un)Making of Terrorists.* Ecco/HarperCollins, 2010. — Fieldwork with combatants and networks; sacred values, fictive kinship ("brotherhood"), and the failure of material incentives.
- Atran, S., & Ginges, J. "Religious and sacred imperatives in human conflict." *Science*, 336(6083), 18 May 2012, pp. 855–857. — Sacred values in intractable conflict; material incentives to compromise can backfire. Journal/volume/issue/pages/date CONFIRMED.
- Whitehouse, H. "Modes of religiosity: Towards a cognitive explanation of the sociopolitical dynamics of religion." *Method & Theory in the Study of Religion* / *Modes of Religiosity* (AltaMira Press), 2004. — Imagistic vs. doctrinal modes; rare high-arousal (dysphoric) rituals and their binding of tightly cohesive groups.
- Whitehouse, H., McQuinn, B., Buhrmester, M., & Swann, W. B. Jr. "Brothers in arms: Libyan revolutionaries bond like family." *Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences*, 111(50), 2014. — Shared dysphoric experience in combat producing fusion; fusion among frontline fighters.
- Whitehouse, H., et al. "The evolution of extreme cooperation via shared dysphoric experiences." *Scientific Reports*, 7, 2017. — Shared dysphoria as a pathway to fusion and self-sacrifice.
- Durkheim, É. *The Elementary Forms of the Religious Life.* 1912. — Background for the sacralisation of collective objects and values (cross-referenced to item 76).

## Further reading to verify before final publication

- Atran's post-2014 fieldwork specifically with Kurdish/Peshmerga and frontline anti-ISIS combatants ("devoted actors" who fight against insurgents) — the claim that his teams documented fusion and sacred values symmetrically on both sides is drawn from Atran's reported work (e.g., Atran, Sheikh & Gómez, *PNAS* 2014, "Devoted actors sacrifice for close comrades and sacred cause," and related field reports); confirm the exact studies, populations, and dates before final publication.
- The specific survey finding that offering material compensation to compromise on a sacred value *increases* support for violence — attributed to Ginges & Atran experimental work (e.g., Ginges, Atran, Medin & Shikaki, *PNAS* 2007, on the Israeli–Palestinian context); verify the exact citation, populations, and effect direction.
- The framing of Ambedkar's movement as drawing on sacred, non-negotiable commitments to dignity/equality and the mass conversion as forging a new fused identity — interpreted here through the fusion/sacred-values lens (an analytic framing, not a claim Ambedkar made in these terms). The underlying event is CONFIRMED: the mass conversion to (Navayana) Buddhism took place on 14 October 1956 at Deekshabhoomi, Nagpur, with roughly 365,000–400,000 followers converting alongside him. The interpretive characterisation should still be attributed to a specific work of Ambedkar scholarship before final publication.
- Neuroimaging evidence that reasoning about sacred values recruits duty/rule-based rather than utilitarian cost-benefit processing — attributed to Atran/Berns and colleagues (e.g., Berns et al., *Philosophical Transactions of the Royal Society B*, 2012); confirm exact study and findings.
- Critiques of the fusion pictorial scale as capturing intense identification rather than a distinct construct, and evidence that "sacred" values are sometimes traded under reframing — real strands in the literature; pin specific critical citations before final publication.
