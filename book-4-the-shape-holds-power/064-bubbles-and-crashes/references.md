# References — 64. Bubbles & crashes

## Confirmed sources

- Kindleberger, Charles P. *Manias, Panics, and Crashes: A History of Financial Crises*. Basic Books, 1978 (and later editions, several revised with Robert Aliber). — The originating synthesis; the stage model of the bubble (displacement, boom, euphoria, crisis/distress, panic/revulsion) that structures this chapter.
- Minsky, Hyman P. *Stabilizing an Unstable Economy*. Yale University Press, 1986; and his "financial instability hypothesis" papers. — The debt-cycle and stability-breeds-instability engine Kindleberger built on; supports the leverage mechanism and the "Minsky moment." Cross-referenced as item 62.
- Shiller, Robert J. *Irrational Exuberance*. Princeton University Press, 2000 (2nd ed. 2005; 3rd ed. 2015). — Feedback-loop account of bubbles; title from Alan Greenspan's 1996 phrase; published near the dot-com peak. Supports the positive-feedback and psychology-of-manias mechanism.
- Reinhart, Carmen M., and Kenneth S. Rogoff. *This Time Is Different: Eight Centuries of Financial Folly*. Princeton University Press, 2009. — Long-run cross-country data showing the recurrence of financial crises and the "this time is different" delusion.
- Galbraith, John Kenneth. *The Great Crash, 1929*. Houghton Mifflin, 1954. — Classic narrative of the 1929 mania and crash; general support for the recurring anatomy and the historical sweep.
- Mackay, Charles. *Extraordinary Popular Delusions and the Madness of Crowds*. 1841. — The historical catalogue of manias (tulips, South Sea); cross-referenced as item 30 and used in the human-weight and critique beats.
- Fama, Eugene F. — Efficient Market Hypothesis and his public scepticism that "bubbles" are a well-defined, identifiable phenomenon. Supports the critique beat. (See Fama's Nobel lecture and interviews, e.g. 2013–2016.)
- Keynes, John Maynard. *The General Theory of Employment, Interest and Money*. 1936, ch. 12. — The "beauty contest" metaphor; cross-referenced as item 61.

## Further reading to verify before final publication

- **Nasdaq Composite peak:** confirmed — the index closed at its all-time high of 5,048.62 on 10 March 2000, with an intraday high of 5,132.52 the same day (5,048.62 is the closing peak, not the intraday figure). Chapter corrected accordingly.
- **Fivefold rise:** the run-up from 1995 to the March 2000 peak is commonly given as roughly 600% (about sixfold); "roughly quintupled" is a conservative rounding and remains safe.
- **~78% peak-to-trough decline:** confirmed — the Nasdaq Composite fell about 78% from its March 2000 peak, closing at its bear-market low of 1,114.11 on 9 October 2002.
- **Netscape IPO (1995):** confirmed — 9 August 1995, a landmark high-visibility internet IPO widely read as the opening marker of the dot-com era.
- **"Eyeballs over earnings" / "Old Economy" framing:** confirm as representative period language rather than attributing to a single named source.
- **2008 housing bust distributional effects:** the claim that foreclosure and negative equity concentrated among lower-margin households — support with a specific source (e.g. Mian & Sufi, *House of Debt*, 2014) before publication.
- **"Minsky moment":** confirmed — coined by Paul McCulley of PIMCO in 1998, in the context of the Russian financial crisis.
- **Greenspan "irrational exuberance":** confirmed — from his speech "The Challenge of Central Banking in a Democratic Society," 5 December 1996, American Enterprise Institute.
