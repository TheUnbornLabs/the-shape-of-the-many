# References — 78. Freud on the group and the leader

## Confirmed sources

- Sigmund Freud. *Massenpsychologie und Ich-Analyse*. Internationaler Psychoanalytischer Verlag, Vienna, 1921. Translated as *Group Psychology and the Analysis of the Ego* (Standard Edition, Vol. XVIII, trans. James Strachey, Hogarth Press, 1955). — The primary text: the libidinal-tie theory of the group, the leader installed in the place of the ego ideal, identification among members, and the Church-and-Army analysis of the two "artificial groups" held by the illusion that the leader loves all members equally.
- Gustave Le Bon. *Psychologie des foules*. Félix Alcan, Paris, 1895. Translated as *The Crowd: A Study of the Popular Mind* (1896). — Freud's principal interlocutor; the account of suggestion and contagion that Freud quotes at length and seeks to explain (item 77).
- William McDougall. *The Group Mind*. Cambridge University Press, 1920. — Freud's second interlocutor; the distinction between unorganised crowd and structured group that Freud takes up and reworks.
- Theodor W. Adorno, Else Frenkel-Brunswik, Daniel J. Levinson, and R. Nevitt Sanford. *The Authoritarian Personality*. Harper & Brothers, New York, 1950. — A major psychoanalytically informed descendant of Freud's collective psychology, applying related ideas to the followership of authoritarian and fascist movements.
- Henri Tajfel and John C. Turner. "An Integrative Theory of Intergroup Conflict." In *The Social Psychology of Intergroup Relations* (eds. Austin & Worchel), 1979. — Foundational statement of social identity theory, the evidenced modern framework invoked in the critique.
- S. Alexander Haslam, Stephen D. Reicher, and Michael J. Platow. *The New Psychology of Leadership: Identity, Influence and Power*. Psychology Press, 2011 (2nd ed. Routledge, 2020). — The identity-leadership account (leader as prototype/embodiment of a shared "we") offered as the more evidenced alternative to Freud's displaced-libido model.
