# 78. Freud on the group and the leader

> A crowd is not held together by contagion alone; it is held together by a love it has all placed in the same man, and having loved him together, its members become as one.

## The idea

Gustave Le Bon had described what a crowd does: how the individual, submerged in the mass, sheds his reason, his restraint, his sense of himself, and behaves in ways he never would alone. What Le Bon had not explained is *why*. Why should mere proximity to others dissolve a person's independence? What is the force that reaches into a member of a crowd and switches off the private judgment he carried in with him?

Freud's answer, set out in 1921, is that the force is love. Not love in its familiar sense, but libido — the emotional energy that binds people to one another — redirected and put to work. A group, Freud argues, is a number of individuals who have taken one and the same object and put it in the place of their own inner standard, their conscience, the internal voice that measures and approves and forbids. That object is the leader. Each member idealises him, loves him, hands him the authority that used to belong to their own self-judgment. And here is the decisive second move: because they have all done the *same* thing with the *same* man, they now recognise one another as fellows. They are bound to each other sideways because they are all bound upward to him. The crowd is not a heap of people accidentally infected by the same mood. It is a structure of shared devotion, a web of emotional ties radiating from a single loved figure and, through him, connecting every follower to every other.

Surrender the inner judge to the leader, and you have surrendered the thing that would have made you hesitate. That is Freud's account of why the individual in the group can do what the individual alone cannot.

## The origin

The book is *Massenpsychologie und Ich-Analyse*, published in Vienna in 1921 and translated as *Group Psychology and the Analysis of the Ego*. Freud, then in his mid-sixties, was extending psychoanalysis outward from the consulting room toward the collective — a movement that would culminate a decade later in *Civilization and Its Discontents*.

He begins not by asserting his own view but by taking Le Bon's *The Crowd* (1895, item 77) as his text, quoting it at length and paying it real respect. Le Bon, Freud allows, had brilliantly described the crowd's suggestibility, its emotional volatility, its slide into a kind of primitive mind. But description is not explanation. Le Bon leaned on "suggestion" and "contagion" as though naming them settled the matter, and Freud presses exactly there: suggestion is not a cause but a thing to be accounted for. What *is* this susceptibility, and where does it come from?

He also engages the English psychologist William McDougall, whose *The Group Mind* (1920) had appeared the year before and had distinguished the raw, unorganised crowd from the stable, structured group. Freud takes McDougall's structured group as the more interesting case and asks what supplies the structure. His answer draws on machinery he had already built — the theory of the libido, of identification, and of the ego ideal, that internal authority he had recently described. The essay is thus a hinge: Le Bon and McDougall supply the phenomena, and Freud supplies the depth-psychological engine he claims runs beneath them.

## The mechanism

The gears are three, and they turn in sequence.

The first is **idealisation**. Freud had proposed that within each of us sits an *ego ideal* — an internal standard we measure ourselves against, an inherited authority, the seat of conscience and self-approval, the voice that says *you are not yet what you ought to be*. In ordinary life this authority is our own, lodged inside us. In the group, the member takes an external object — the leader — and installs him in the place of this ego ideal. The follower no longer measures himself against his own internal standard; he measures himself against the leader, defers to him, seeks his approval as he once sought his own conscience's. The leader is loved as an idealised father-figure, credited with a strength and rightness the follower has ceased to demand of himself.

The second gear follows automatically. Once the internal judge has been handed over, its functions lapse. Conscience, which forbade; reality-testing, which checked belief against the world — these were offices of the ego ideal, and with the ego ideal externalised, they fall silent. The follower can now believe what the leader believes and do what the leader wills, unrestrained by the private scruple that would otherwise have intervened. This is Freud's translation of Le Bon's contagion into mechanism: the individual's judgment is not overwhelmed from outside so much as *abdicated* from within.

The third gear is **identification among the members**. Because every follower has placed the same object in the same inner position, they have this profound thing in common, and Freud holds that people who share the same ego ideal identify with one another. The vertical tie to the leader generates a horizontal tie among the led. They are brothers because they share a father. This lateral identification is what converts a scatter of separately devoted individuals into a cohesive mass that feels itself a unity.

The energy powering all of this is libido — but libido *aim-inhibited*, diverted from any directly sexual end into tenderness, admiration, and loyalty: emotional glue, displaced from its origin and set to hold a crowd.

## The anchor example

Freud's own demonstration is a pairing that would not, at first, seem to belong together: the Church and the Army. These are his two "artificial groups" — highly organised, externally sustained, resistant to dissolution — and he uses them to make the abstract structure concrete.

In the Catholic Church, Freud observes, there is a head — Christ — who is held to love all the members of the faithful with an equal love. Every believer stands in the same relation to him; no one is preferred; the leader's love falls evenly on all. And precisely *because* each believer is loved equally by Christ, believers are bound to love one another as fellow Christians. The vertical tie to the head generates the horizontal tie of the congregation. In the Army the structure is identical in form: the Commander-in-Chief stands where Christ stands, a father who loves each soldier equally, and from that shared, equal love descends the fierce lateral bond of comrades, the willingness to die beside and for men one has never met.

The crucial word is *illusion*. Freud is unsparing here: the belief that the leader loves each member equally is a necessary fiction, and the group depends on it. Puncture it — let it be seen that the Commander does not in fact love his soldiers, that he spends them without care — and the ties give way and the army panics. The panic of a routed army, in Freud's reading, is not simply fear of the enemy; it is the sudden collapse of the emotional structure, the moment the illusion of the loving leader fails and, with it, the mutual bonds it was holding up. Take the loved leader out of the centre, and the web that hung from him falls, and the members, no longer brothers, become a stampede of separate frightened people (item 29, crowd crushes).

## The flip

Here the series' argument declares itself. Durkheim had named the *horizontal* force — collective effervescence (item 76), the heat generated among assembled bodies acting in rhythm, an energy that rises from the group itself. Le Bon had named contagion, the sideways spread of feeling through a crowd (item 77). Freud names the *vertical* bond, the tie that runs upward from every follower to the single idealised leader — and shows that it is this vertical tie that *generates* and *organises* the horizontal ones. He completes the geometry. Effervescence and contagion tell you the crowd is hot and its feeling spreads; Freud tells you what gives the heat a direction and the mass a spine.

And the bond, like every mechanism in this book, is sign-neutral. The libidinal tie to a leader is the same emotional structure whether it fuses a people for creation or for atrocity. A beloved leader who points his followers toward justice can draw from them a courage and a solidarity no argument could have produced — the movement that marches because it loves the one who calls it, and finds in that love the nerve to face dogs and fire hoses. The identical structure, the identical idealisation, the identical surrender of private conscience to a loved figure at the centre, produces the personality cult and the fascist *Führerprinzip* when the leader points the other way: the adored demagogue whose followers hand him their judgment and then do, in his name and with clear hearts, what none of them alone could have brought themselves to do. **The wise crowd and the mob are the same crowd; here they are the same crowd loving the same kind of leader — and the sign is set entirely by where the leader points.** Freud is not describing two species of follower. He is describing one bond, and one dial.

Writing in 1921, before the movements that would prove him right had taken their final shape, Freud had in effect diagrammed the psychological architecture of the totalitarian cult a decade before it arrived. The mechanism he traced — a population converting a man into its ego ideal and, through him, into a mass — is the mechanism authority would later exploit deliberately, through propaganda (item 82) engineered to sustain the idealisation, and that Milgram would isolate in the laboratory as the bare obedience-bond to authority (item 37). The same surrender that lets a crowd find its courage is the surrender that lets it lose its conscience.

## The human weight

Freud's essay is, read closely, one of the most penetrating accounts we have of how an authoritarian order holds a population. Its power is that it locates the binding force not in fear alone but in *love* — and love is far harder to resist and far more durable than coercion, because the follower is not merely obeying but adoring, not merely complying but *identifying*, both upward with the leader and sideways with every fellow-follower. This is why the personality cult is not decoration on top of a dictatorship but structural to it. The demagogue who can install himself in the place of a people's ego ideal has done something a mere tyrant cannot: he has converted subjects into devotees, and devotees police themselves and one another. The lateral identification Freud described is precisely the mechanism by which a mass becomes a body that will surrender its own conscience and defend the surrender.

This is the series' frontier, seen from a new angle. An order held by a leader-cult is held by an emotional bond, and emotional bonds can be broken. Freud's own analysis names the fracture point: the *illusion* that the leader loves each member equally. De-idealise the leader — puncture the illusion, show the father to be neither loving nor strong, expose the object of devotion as small, venal, ridiculous — and the vertical tie can fail, and when it fails the horizontal ties that hung from it fail with it, and the devoted mass reverts to a collection of separate people who no longer see one another as brothers. This is why authoritarian regimes police mockery of the leader with a ferocity that seems out of all proportion to its content. A joke that makes the leader ridiculous is not a nuisance; it is an attack on the load-bearing beam. To de-sacralise the leader is to threaten the idealisation on which the whole structure rests.

The bond does not hold by love alone, and the frontier deepens here. Alongside the genuine devotion runs fear — and the two are hard to tell apart from inside. Preference falsification (item 34) holds many a follower in the outward posture of devotion long after the inner idealisation has cooled, because each follower sees every other still performing love and dares not be the first to stop. The cult and the climate of fear reinforce each other: the love that is real in some sustains the performance that is coerced in others, and the performance sustains the illusion of universal love that the love itself requires. This is the psychology beneath stratification and authoritarian rule (item 85) — an order in which the leader-bond, whether felt or feigned, converts a population into the instrument of the one at the centre. It connects, too, to identity fusion (item 84), the modern account of how the boundary between self and group can dissolve until a person will act for the group as for himself — the mechanism Freud was reaching toward with his talk of identification, now given sharper form.

## The critique

Freud's account is profound and it is largely untestable, and honesty requires holding both at once.

The specifics are not measurable. Libido, the ego ideal, aim-inhibited desire displaced onto a leader — these are interpretive constructs, not quantities that can be observed, operationalised, or falsified. Psychoanalysis's standing as science has been contested for a century, from Popper onward, precisely because claims of this kind are difficult to state in any form a study could disconfirm. The essay is a work of speculative theory built on the method Freud had developed in the clinic, extended by analogy to the crowd — and the analogy carries no guarantee. It should be read as a searchlight, not a proof.

It also over-centres the leader. Freud's structure requires a single loved figure at the middle, and many groups cohere with no such figure at all. The honeybee swarm reaches a collective decision with no leader whatever (item 68); leaderless movements have held together and acted with force in the modern era, their cohesion generated laterally rather than descending from a head. If the vertical tie were truly the source of the horizontal ones, these groups should not exist as they do. The Church-and-Army model may describe one important *kind* of group — the hierarchically led kind — while mistaking it for the group as such.

And there is a more evidenced rival. Modern social identity theory — developed from Henri Tajfel and John Turner, and applied to leadership by Reicher, Haslam, and colleagues — explains the leader-follower bond without displaced libido at all. On this account, followers do not love a leader who has replaced their conscience; they identify with a shared social *category*, a "we," and a leader gains influence by being seen as a prototypical member of that "we," an embodiment of the group's identity rather than an idealised father. The binding force is shared identity, and the account comes with decades of experimental support Freud's does not have. It captures much of what Freud saw — the fusion, the surrender of the individual to the collective, the leader's peculiar power — while grounding it in evidence and dispensing with the unfalsifiable machinery.

Freud remains indispensable as an interpretive lens. He asked the right question, the one Le Bon dodged — *why* does the individual surrender in the crowd — and he answered it with an image, the loved leader installed where the conscience used to be, that has never quite lost its grip because it so often *feels* true. But feeling true is not the same as being established, and the honest verdict is that Freud gave us a great and enduring metaphor for the leader-cult, not a demonstrated theory of it.
