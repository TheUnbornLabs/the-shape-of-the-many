# References — 77. Le Bon and The Crowd

## Confirmed sources

- Le Bon, Gustave. *Psychologie des foules*. Félix Alcan, 1895. (English: *The Crowd: A Study of the Popular Mind*, 1896.) — The founding text; source of all attributed claims about the "collective mind," contagion, suggestibility, prestige, and the crowd's supposed intellectual inferiority.
- Nye, Robert A. *The Origins of Crowd Psychology: Gustave Le Bon and the Crisis of Mass Democracy in the Third Republic*. Sage, 1975. — Situates Le Bon in the reactionary, elitist milieu of the Third Republic; supports the account of his conservative, anti-democratic standpoint and the ideological character of his "science."
- Moscovici, Serge. *L'Âge des foules* / *The Age of the Crowd: A Historical Treatise on Mass Psychology*. Cambridge University Press, 1985 (French orig. 1981). — Reconstructs Le Bon's influence and its reach beyond its evidential base; links Le Bon, Freud, and the propaganda tradition.
- Bernays, Edward. *Propaganda*. Horace Liveright, 1928. — Documents the operationalisation of crowd/mass-manipulation ideas for public relations; supports the Le Bon–Bernays lineage (cross-ref item 82).
- Reicher, Stephen D. "The Psychology of Crowd Dynamics." In *Blackwell Handbook of Social Psychology: Group Processes* (M. A. Hogg & R. S. Tindale, eds.), Blackwell, 2001. — Social-identity account of crowd behaviour refuting the "mindless mob"; basis for the critique beat.
- Reicher, S., Spears, R., & Postmes, T. "A Social Identity Model of Deindividuation Phenomena." *European Review of Social Psychology*, 6(1), 1995. — The SIDE model; crowds shift to a shared social identity rather than losing self and reason (cross-ref item 32).
- Drury, John, & Stott, Clifford (and colleagues). Research on collective resilience and crowd behaviour in emergencies and protest policing (e.g., Drury et al., work on mutual aid in disasters; Stott & Reicher on crowd policing and disorder). — Supports claims that crowds cooperate in emergencies and that protest crowds are organised, with disorder often traceable to policing.

## Further reading to verify before final publication

- Mussolini's admiration for Le Bon's *The Crowd* — VERIFIED as well documented: Mussolini is directly quoted stating "I don't know how many times I have re-read his *Psychologie des foules*," calling it "an excellent work to which I frequently refer." This is the firmest of the dictator-influence claims and is safe to state.
- Hitler's use of Le Bon — asserted by numerous scholars (Le Bon's crowd/propaganda ideas are widely held to have influenced *Mein Kampf*), but this remains scholarly inference rather than a documented direct citation by Hitler; keep attributed as "said/argued," not stated as fact. Goebbels — no specific documentary evidence of direct reliance on Le Bon was found; treat any Goebbels link as diffuse absorption through interwar propaganda culture, not established fact. The chapter's hedged phrasing is correct and should be retained.
- First English translation VERIFIED: *The Crowd: A Study of the Popular Mind*, London: T. Fisher Unwin, 1896 (translation of *Psychologie des foules*, Paris: Félix Alcan, 1895).
- Galton's ox-weighing observation VERIFIED: Francis Galton, "Vox Populi," *Nature*, Vol. 75 (1907), pp. 450–451 (Plymouth fair; median guess 1207 lb vs. actual dressed weight 1198 lb). Cross-referenced from item 17; date and journal consistent.
