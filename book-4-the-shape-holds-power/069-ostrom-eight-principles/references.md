# References — 69. Ostrom's eight principles

## Confirmed sources

- Ostrom, Elinor. *Governing the Commons: The Evolution of Institutions for Collective Action*. Cambridge University Press, 1990. — The founding work; sets out the eight design principles derived from long-enduring common-pool resource institutions, and presents the Törbel Swiss alpine commons among its detailed cases.
- Ostrom, Elinor. "Beyond Markets and States: Polycentric Governance of Complex Economic Systems." Nobel Prize (Sveriges Riksbank Prize in Economic Sciences in Memory of Alfred Nobel) lecture, 8 December 2009; published in *American Economic Review*, 100(3), 2010, pp. 641–672. — The mature statement of polycentric, nested self-governance; supports the "neither market nor state" framing and the human-weight beat.
- Cox, Michael; Arnold, Gwen; and Villamayor-Tomas, Sergio. "A Review of Design Principles for Community-based Natural Resource Management." *Ecology and Society*, 15(4): 38, 2010. — Systematic empirical review testing Ostrom's eight principles across 91 case studies; finds broad empirical support and recommends reformulating/subdividing several of the principles rather than treating them as eight fixed rules. Supports the critique beat. (Citation verified: Ecology and Society vol. 15, iss. 4, art. 38.)
- Ostrom, Elinor; Burger, Joanna; Field, Christopher B.; Norgaard, Richard B.; and Policansky, David. "Revisiting the Commons: Local Lessons, Global Challenges." *Science*, 284(5412), 1999, pp. 278–282. — On the difficulty of scaling local commons solutions to global commons; supports the critique of scope and the frontier discussion.
- Hardin, Garrett. "The Tragedy of the Commons." *Science*, 162(3859), 1968, pp. 1243–1248. — The orthodoxy Ostrom answered; referenced as the paired pole (item 66).

## Further reading to verify before final publication

- Törbel specifics: the founding dates of surviving communal charters (commonly cited as 1224 and 1483), the 1517 regulation restricting alp rights to recognised local citizens, and the rule tying summer grazing to winter feeding capacity. These derive from Ostrom's account in *Governing the Commons* (Ch. 3), drawing on the ethnographic/historical work of Robert McC. Netting (*Balancing on an Alp*, Cambridge University Press, 1981). Confirm exact dates and the precise wording of the 1517 and earlier regulations against Ostrom and Netting before publication.
- The claim that governance arrangements at Törbel ran "roughly five hundred years" in recognisable form — verify the span and continuity against Netting's and Ostrom's records.
- Ostrom as the first woman to receive the economics Nobel (2009), shared with Oliver E. Williamson, "for her analysis of economic governance, especially the commons" — verified against the official Nobel record (nobelprize.org).
