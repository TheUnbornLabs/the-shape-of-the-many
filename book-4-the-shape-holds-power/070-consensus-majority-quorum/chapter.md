# 70. Consensus, majority, and quorum

> The rule you use to count the room decides who the room becomes; there is no neutral way to turn many minds into one decision.

## The idea

Every group that decides anything faces a hidden prior question, one it usually answers without noticing it has been asked. Before it can settle *what* to do, it must settle *how it will settle* — by what rule will the scattered opinions in the room be compressed into a single collective act? Raise a hand and count to a bare majority? Talk until no one objects? Require two-thirds, or nine of twelve, or everyone? Refuse to decide at all unless enough members have shown up to make the count mean something? These are not procedural trivia. They are the load-bearing wall of collective decision, and the choice among them is one of the deepest political choices a group ever makes.

The candidate rules are few, and each buys one virtue at the price of another. **Majority rule** is fast and decisive: whoever gets more than half prevails, and the group can act. But a bare majority can steamroll the minority on the losing side, and can flip its verdict on the movement of a single vote — the "tyranny of the majority" is majority rule's own shadow. **Consensus** requires that everyone consent; nothing passes over a live objection. It confers enormous legitimacy and shields the minority absolutely, but it is slow, and it hands every member a potential veto — one holdout can freeze the whole body. **Supermajority** — two-thirds, three-fifths — splits the difference, demanding more than half but less than all. And **quorum** governs a different axis entirely: the minimum number who must be present for any decision to count at all, a floor that stops a tiny unrepresentative faction from acting in the group's name — unless the floor is set so high that staying home becomes a weapon.

There is no rule that is best in general. Each protects against one failure by exposing you to another. To pick a decision rule is to pick which disaster you are willing to risk.

## The origin

The problem is as old as any assembly. The Athenian *ekklesia* decided by show of hands and simple majority, and set attendance floors for its gravest acts — a quorum of six thousand citizens was required to grant certain privileges. The Roman Senate could not transact business without enough members present, and the very word *quorum* is Latin, lifted from the opening of a medieval English commission — *quorum vos... unum esse volumus*, "of whom we wish you to be one" — that named the members whose presence was indispensable.

The codification of these rules into a portable technology of meetings belongs to the nineteenth century. In 1876 an American army engineer, Henry Martyn Robert, published a slim manual after a Baptist meeting he was asked to chair dissolved into chaos he had no rules to govern. *Robert's Rules of Order* fixed majority as the ordinary default, reserved the two-thirds vote for actions that curtail members' rights — closing debate, suspending the rules — and made quorum a precondition of any valid business. It became the parliamentary common law of countless clubs, boards, and legislatures.

The formal economic analysis of the trade-off came later still, in 1962, when James Buchanan and Gordon Tullock published *The Calculus of Consent*. They asked a question no one had posed so cleanly: what is the *right* majority threshold? Their answer, examined below, reframed the whole subject as an optimisation problem rather than a matter of democratic faith. And running alongside this codifying tradition was an older, quieter one — the consensus practice of Quaker meetings, feeling for the "sense of the meeting" rather than counting votes, and the deliberative councils of many Indigenous polities, where a decision was not made until objection had exhausted itself.

## The mechanism

Four things are in tension whenever a group chooses a rule: **decisiveness** (can the group act, and quickly?), **inclusiveness** (whose consent must be gathered?), **legitimacy** (will the losers accept the outcome as binding?), and **speed**. No rule maximises all four. The genius of Buchanan and Tullock was to reduce the muddle to two costs that move in opposite directions as you slide the required threshold from bare majority up toward unanimity.

The first is the **external cost**: the expected harm inflicted on those who are outvoted and must live under a decision they opposed. Under bare majority, a coalition of just over half can impose real losses on just under half, so the external cost is high. As you raise the threshold — two-thirds, nine-tenths, all — fewer and fewer people can be bound against their will, and at unanimity the external cost falls to zero, because nothing passes that anyone rejects.

The second is the **decision cost**: the time, effort, and bargaining needed to assemble the required agreement. Under bare majority this is modest; you need only persuade half the room. But every step you take toward unanimity multiplies the number of people who must be brought on board, and each additional required assent hands another member the power to hold out, to bargain, to extract a price for consent. At unanimity the decision cost can approach the prohibitive: a single stubborn member can block indefinitely.

The optimal rule, Buchanan and Tullock argued, is the threshold that **minimises the sum** of these two costs — and it is almost never bare majority, and almost never unanimity. It sits somewhere in between, and *where* depends on the stakes. For decisions where the harm of being outvoted is small — what colour to paint the clubhouse — the external-cost curve is low and flat, so you tolerate a low threshold and decide fast. For decisions that can inflict grave, irreversible loss on the losers — amending a constitution, expelling a member, going to war — the external-cost curve rears up, and it pays to demand a supermajority, accepting slower decisions to buy protection for the minority. This is why serious institutions do not use one rule but a graded ladder of them, matching the threshold to the weight of the act.

Quorum is a separate gear. It does not change *how many* of the present must agree; it changes *how many must be present* before agreement counts. Set too low, and a handful can act in the name of thousands. Set too high, and absence becomes obstruction: those who oppose a measure need not vote it down — they need only stay away and deny the meeting the bodies it needs to be quorate at all.

## The anchor example

Watch the same question decided two ways. A Quaker meeting for business does not vote. It gathers, and the clerk listens for what the Friends call the "sense of the meeting" — the emerging shared discernment of the gathered body. Members speak; a member who feels the meeting moving wrongly may "stand in the way" of the decision, and if that objection is grounded in conscience rather than mere preference, the matter waits. It may be laid over for months. Nothing is imposed on the dissenter. When a decision finally comes, it comes with something a vote can never confer: no one was outvoted, so no one has grounds to resent the outcome as something done *to* them. The external cost is near zero. The legitimacy is total. And the decision cost is enormous — a single Friend, sincerely convinced, can hold the meeting for a season.

Now hand the same question to a body that decides by simple majority. The vote is called; fifty-one percent prevails; the matter is settled by lunch. The decision cost is trivial. But the forty-nine percent have lost outright, and if they are a *permanent* minority — outvoted not on this question but on every question, structurally, because they will always be the smaller number — then majority rule has handed them a lifetime of losing with a democratic face. They consented to nothing.

The starkest version of the consensus problem lives in bodies that require unanimity among sovereign members: the United Nations Security Council, where any of five permanent members can veto, or the European Union on matters still requiring unanimous assent, where one national government can freeze a measure the other twenty-six want. Here the holdout is not a scrupulous Friend but a state pursuing its interest, and the veto meant to protect the minority becomes a tool to extract concessions or simply to obstruct. Consensus, at scale, can be indistinguishable from paralysis wearing the mask of respect.

## The flip

The decision rule is the fourth of Surowiecki's four conditions — **aggregation** (item 20) — made concrete and consequential. Surowiecki argued that a crowd is wise only when there exists some mechanism to turn private judgments into a collective one; the decision rule *is* that mechanism, and this chapter is the discovery that the mechanism is not neutral plumbing. It has a shape, and the shape bends the outcome. The identical group of people, holding the identical private views, will reach *different collective decisions* under majority, supermajority, and consensus. Aggregation does not merely reveal what the group thinks. It partly determines it.

This is the thesis of the whole series in its governance register: the same crowd is wise or foolish, legitimate or captured, depending on the conditions imposed on it — and the decision rule is one of the sharpest conditions of all. Each rule has its own failure mode, and choosing the rule is choosing which failure you will court. Majority rule, run on independent competent voters, is the engine of Condorcet's theorem (item 18) — the crowd converging on truth. But run the same rule on *correlated* voters, and it becomes the vehicle of information cascades (item 26) and the tyranny of a bare, transient, steamrolling majority. Consensus protects every minority absolutely, and in doing so hands every member a veto — the holdout's tyranny, paralysis by scruple or by strategy. Quorum guards against unrepresentative fragments and thereby arms the boycott, obstruction by orchestrated absence.

The honeybee swarm (item 68) shows aggregation done beautifully: bees reach a *quorum* at a candidate nest site — enough scouts committed — and only then commit the whole swarm, a threshold rule tuned by evolution to be fast *and* accurate. Ostrom's design principles for the commons (item 69) insist that those bound by a rule must share in the collective-choice arrangements that make it — the decision rule as the hinge of durable self-governance. And Arrow's impossibility theorem (item 71), which follows immediately, will prove the deep reason no rule can be perfect: the search for a flawless aggregation mechanism is not merely hard but formally hopeless. Aggregation is never neutral. Someone chose the rule, and the choice was political before the first vote was cast.

## The human weight

Decision rules are how a group encodes power. Who holds a veto; whose consent must be sought; what, exactly, counts as "the group" whose presence makes a quorum — these are the questions that decide, beneath the visible politics, who can be overridden and who cannot. A rule that requires unanimity among the powerful and mere majority among the rest is a machine for entrenching the powerful, and it will look, on paper, perfectly democratic.

The moral ambiguity runs both ways, which is why romanticism about any single rule is dangerous. Consensus can be the shield of the vulnerable — the practice that ensures the smallest voice cannot be simply outnumbered and forgotten. It can also be the stubborn minority's instrument for holding the many hostage, the one holdout who will not be moved and cannot be overruled. Majority rule can empower the many against an entrenched elite. It can also crush a permanent minority, grinding them under a verdict they can never win, election after election, with the full legitimacy of the count.

And here the frontier this series keeps returning to comes into view, at its most unsettling. Oppressive orders love the *appearance* of consensus. The unanimous vote, the acclamation with no dissent, the show of hands where every hand is raised — these are the theatre of manufactured agreement, and they are produced not by real concord but by fear. This is preference falsification (item 34): people voting for what they believe the room demands rather than what they privately think, because the cost of visible dissent is ruin. The chilling fact is that real consensus and coerced consensus look *identical from the outside*. A meeting where every objection has been honestly resolved and a meeting where every objection has been silenced both produce the same unanimous minute. The decision rule cannot tell them apart; only the freedom to dissent without cost can, and that freedom is exactly what an oppressive order removes. Which is why the design of decision rules cannot be separated from the deliberation that precedes them (item 72), or from Ostrom's insistence (item 69) that the governed must truly help make the rule that binds them.

## The critique

The hardest truth about decision rules is that there is no optimal one in general, and Arrow's impossibility theorem (item 71) will shortly make this a theorem rather than an observation: no aggregation rule can satisfy a handful of minimal fairness conditions at once. What Buchanan and Tullock offer is not a universal answer but a framework for a *contextual* one — the best trade-off depends on the stakes, the reversibility, the size of the group, and how much the losers stand to lose. Anyone who tells you a single rule is right for all decisions has not understood the problem.

Both romances fail on inspection. Consensus romanticism, common in movements that prize inclusion, ignores holdout power and hidden coercion — it treats the veto as pure protection and forgets that a veto is also a weapon, and that a "consensus" reached under social pressure may be no consensus at all. Majority romanticism, common in appeals to the popular will, ignores the persistent minority — the group that loses every vote by construction and for whom "let the majority decide" is a sentence, not a settlement. Quorum rules, meant to guarantee representativeness, are gameable in a direction their designers rarely intend: raise the bar high enough and the way to defeat a measure is not to argue against it but to boycott the meeting and deny it a quorum, obstruction disguised as absence.

There is a further limit worth stating plainly: the analysis above treats the decision costs and external costs as if they could be weighed on one scale, and often they cannot. What is the external cost of binding a minority on a matter of conscience, in the same units as the hours lost to bargaining? The optimisation is real but its inputs are frequently incommensurable, which is why the choice of rule remains, in the end, a political and moral judgment and not a calculation. Real institutions know this, which is why almost none of them use a single rule. They mix them — majority for routine business, supermajority for grave acts, quorum floors, consensus norms layered over formal votes — building a portfolio of failure modes rather than betting everything on one. That is not indecision. It is the only honest response to a problem with no clean solution, and the reason it has no clean solution is the subject of the chapter that follows.
