# References — 70. Consensus, majority, and quorum

## Confirmed sources

- Buchanan, James M., and Gordon Tullock. *The Calculus of Consent: Logical Foundations of Constitutional Democracy*. University of Michigan Press, 1962. — The core analytical frame of the chapter: the trade-off between external costs (harm to the outvoted) and decision-making costs (time, bargaining, holdouts), and the argument that the optimal majority threshold minimises their sum and is rarely simple majority or unanimity.
- Robert, Henry M. *Pocket Manual of Rules of Order for Deliberative Assemblies* (commonly *Robert's Rules of Order*). First edition, 1876. — Source for the nineteenth-century codification of majority as default, the two-thirds vote for actions curtailing members' rights, and quorum as a precondition of valid business.
- May, Kenneth O. "A Set of Independent Necessary and Sufficient Conditions for Simple Majority Decision." *Econometrica*, Vol. 20, No. 4, 1952, pp. 680–684. — Formal characterisation of simple majority rule by the conditions (decisiveness, anonymity, neutrality, positive responsiveness) that uniquely single it out; underpins the claim that majority rule has a precise mathematical identity rather than being merely conventional.
- Condorcet, Marquis de (Marie Jean Antoine Nicolas de Caritat). *Essai sur l'application de l'analyse à la probabilité des décisions rendues à la pluralité des voix*. Paris, 1785. — Foundational source for majority rule's truth-tracking property under voter independence and competence above one-half; cross-referenced via item 18.
- Arrow, Kenneth J. *Social Choice and Individual Values*. Wiley, 1951 (2nd ed. 1963). — Basis for the chapter's closing claim (developed fully in item 71) that no aggregation rule can satisfy a small set of minimal fairness conditions simultaneously; hence no decision rule is optimal in general.

- The Athenian quorum of six thousand citizens for certain acts (e.g. grants of citizenship / *adeia* and ostracism-related procedures). — The figure of 6,000 is widely cited but the specific decisions to which it applied vary across sources; verify the exact procedures against a standard reference on Athenian institutions (e.g. Hansen, *The Athenian Democracy in the Age of Demosthenes*) before final publication.
- Etymology of "quorum" from the Latin wording of medieval English commissions of the peace (*quorum vos... unum esse volumus*). — The general etymology is well attested; the exact Latin phrasing and the specific commission of origin should be confirmed against an authoritative dictionary/legal-history source.
- The account of Henry Martyn Robert being prompted to write his manual by a disorderly church meeting he was asked to chair. — This origin story is commonly repeated and broadly accepted; confirm the specific circumstances and date against a reliable biography or the official Robert's Rules Association history.
- Quaker "sense of the meeting," the practice of a member "standing in the way," and matters being "laid over." — The general description reflects standard Quaker business practice; verify terminology and the precise mechanics against a Friends' book of discipline / faith and practice before final publication.
- Characterisation of Indigenous council consensus traditions as a broad category. — Kept general in the text deliberately; any specific tribal or national example added later must be sourced to that nation's own documented practice rather than generalised.
- UN Security Council permanent-member veto (five permanent members) and EU unanimity requirements on specified matters. — Institutional facts are well established; confirm current scope of EU unanimity (which policy areas still require it) against an up-to-date source, as this has shifted over successive treaties.
