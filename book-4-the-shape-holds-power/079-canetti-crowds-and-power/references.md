# References — 79. Canetti's Crowds and Power

## Confirmed sources

- Canetti, Elias. *Masse und Macht*. Claassen Verlag, Hamburg, 1960. — The primary text; the original German edition of the crowd typology, the discharge, the four attributes (growth, equality, density, direction), the emotional-content classification of crowds, and the analysis of power, the survivor, and command/sting.
- Canetti, Elias. *Crowds and Power*. Translated by Carol Stewart. Victor Gollancz, London, 1962 (English translation of *Masse und Macht*). — The standard English text used for terminology throughout the chapter (baiting crowd, flight crowd, prohibition crowd, reversal crowd, feast crowd; the "discharge"; the "sting" of command).
- Canetti, Elias. *Die Blendung*. 1935; translated as *Auto-da-Fé* by C. V. Wedgwood, 1946. — Canetti's novel; cited to place his literary reputation.
- The Nobel Foundation. Nobel Prize in Literature 1981, awarded to Elias Canetti "for writings marked by a broad outlook, a wealth of ideas and artistic power." — Source for the 1981 Nobel; official citation wording verified against the Nobel Foundation record.
- Schreber, Daniel Paul. *Denkwürdigkeiten eines Nervenkranken* (*Memoirs of My Nervous Illness*), 1903. — The paranoia memoir Canetti draws on for his portrait of the paranoid ruler / survivor; also the subject of Freud's 1911 case study ("Psycho-Analytic Notes on an Autobiographical Account of a Case of Paranoia").
- Le Bon, Gustave. *Psychologie des foules*. 1895. — The verdict tradition ("the crowd is always intellectually inferior to the isolated individual") that Canetti complicates; cross-referenced as item 77.
- Durkheim, Émile. *Les formes élémentaires de la vie religieuse*. 1912. — Collective effervescence, cross-referenced as item 76 against Canetti's discharge.
