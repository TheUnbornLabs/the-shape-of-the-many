# References — 82. Propaganda & manufactured consent

## Confirmed sources

- Bernays, Edward L. *Propaganda*. Horace Liveright, 1928. — Source for the "conscious and intelligent manipulation of the organized habits and opinions of the masses" and the "invisible government" formulations; the celebratory theory of engineered consent.
- Bernays, Edward L. *Crystallizing Public Opinion*. Boni and Liveright, 1923. — Bernays's earlier systematic statement of public-relations method drawing on crowd psychology and Freudian theory.
- Lippmann, Walter. *Public Opinion*. Harcourt, Brace and Company, 1922. — Origin of the phrase "the manufacture of consent" and the "pictures in our heads" argument that citizens' knowledge of a complex world is necessarily mediated.
- Herman, Edward S., and Noam Chomsky. *Manufacturing Consent: The Political Economy of the Mass Media*. Pantheon Books, 1988. — The propaganda model and its five structural filters (ownership, advertising, sourcing, flak, and the ideological enemy / anticommunism). Filters verified against the text and standard summaries.
- Le Bon, Gustave. *Psychologie des foules (The Crowd: A Study of the Popular Mind)*. Félix Alcan, 1895. — The crowd psychology on which Bernays and later propagandists drew (cross-referenced as item 77).
- Bernays, Edward L. "The Engineering of Consent." *Annals of the American Academy of Political and Social Science* 250, no. 1 (March 1947): 113–120. — Source and date confirmed for the "engineering of consent" phrase; later expanded into the 1955 edited volume of the same title.
- "Torches of Freedom" campaign, contemporary record: *The New York Times*, 1 April 1929, "Group of Girls Puff at Cigarettes as a Gesture of 'Freedom.'" — Confirms the 31 March 1929 (Easter Sunday) Fifth Avenue parade and the public "torches of freedom" framing. Bernays's consultation with psychoanalyst A. A. Brill is corroborated in secondary scholarship. See also Larry Tye, *The Father of Spin* (1998), and the critical reassessment in *American Journalism* 32, no. 3 (2015), "Edward Bernays's 1929 'Torches of Freedom' March: Myths and Historical Significance," which cautions against over-crediting the march's scale and spontaneity.
