# References — 60. Efficient markets versus behavioural finance

## Confirmed sources

- Fama, Eugene F. "Efficient Capital Markets: A Review of Theory and Empirical Work." *Journal of Finance* 25, no. 2 (1970): 383–417. — The canonical statement of the efficient market hypothesis and the weak / semi-strong / strong-form taxonomy; supports the chapter's account of the efficient view and the random-walk claim.
- Fama, Eugene F. "The Behavior of Stock-Market Prices." *Journal of Business* 38, no. 1 (1965): 34–105. — Fama's earlier empirical work establishing the random-walk character of prices; supports the "1965" dating of the hypothesis's modern origin.
- Shiller, Robert J. "Do Stock Prices Move Too Much to be Justified by Subsequent Changes in Dividends?" *American Economic Review* 71, no. 3 (1981): 421–436. — The excess-volatility finding that anchors the chapter; supports the claim that prices swing far more than dividend fundamentals justify.
- Shiller, Robert J. *Irrational Exuberance*. Princeton University Press, 2000. — The popular statement of the behavioural case, published at the dot-com peak; supports the framing of markets as mood-driven and prone to bubbles.
- Kahneman, Daniel, and Amos Tversky. "Prospect Theory: An Analysis of Decision under Risk." *Econometrica* 47, no. 2 (1979): 263–291. — Foundational behavioural-economics paper; supports the account of loss aversion and systematic, patterned departures from rational choice.
- Shleifer, Andrei, and Robert W. Vishny. "The Limits of Arbitrage." *Journal of Finance* 52, no. 1 (1997): 35–55. — Supports the mechanism claim that arbitrage is neither riskless nor unlimited and that smart money can be forced out precisely when mispricing is widest.
- The Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences. Sveriges Riksbank Prize in Economic Sciences in Memory of Alfred Nobel 2013, awarded jointly to Eugene F. Fama, Lars Peter Hansen, and Robert J. Shiller "for their empirical analysis of asset prices." — Supports the shared-Nobel emblem of the unresolved debate.
- Malkiel, Burton G. *A Random Walk Down Wall Street*. W. W. Norton, first edition 1973 (and subsequent editions). — Supports the well-established practical finding that most active managers underperform a passive index and the case for index investing.
