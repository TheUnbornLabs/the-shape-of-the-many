# References — 85. Stratification as collective behaviour

## Confirmed sources

- B. R. Ambedkar. *Annihilation of Caste*. Self-published, Bombay, 1936. — CONFIRMED: the undelivered presidential address written for the 1936 annual conference of the Lahore-based Jat-Pat Todak Mandal, which withdrew the invitation after Ambedkar refused to delete passages renouncing the authority of the Vedas and Shastras; he then published the text at his own expense (1,500 copies) on 15 May 1936. Source for the argument that caste is sustained by internalised belief and the sanctity granted to scripture, that endogamy and the commensality prohibition are the load-bearing reproductive mechanism, and that inter-dining/inter-marriage reforms are palliatives while the real remedy is destroying belief in the sanctity of the Shastras ("annihilate the idea, not merely the practice").
- B. R. Ambedkar. "Castes in India: Their Mechanism, Genesis and Development." Paper read 9 May 1916 at A. A. Goldenweiser's anthropology seminar, Columbia University; published in *Indian Antiquary* 46 (May 1917). — CONFIRMED. The foundational analytic claim that the essence of caste is endogamy "superimposed" on a naturally exogamous population — the mechanism the chapter treats as caste's load-bearing reproductive act. Anchors the chapter's endogamy argument in Ambedkar's own earliest formulation.
- Charles Tilly. *Durable Inequality*. University of California Press, 1998. — Categorical inequality reproduced through the paired organisational mechanisms of exploitation and opportunity-hoarding; inequality as boundary between categories rather than gradient between individuals; reproduction without requiring malicious intent.
- Max Weber. *Economy and Society* (*Wirtschaft und Gesellschaft*). Posthumously published, 1922; standard English edition ed. Roth & Wittich, University of California Press, 1978. — The class / status (*Stand*) / party distinction; caste analysed as the extreme, ritually closed form of a status group, bounded by honour, commensality, and endogamy.
- Timur Kuran. *Private Truths, Public Lies: The Social Consequences of Preference Falsification*. Harvard University Press, 1995. — The mechanism of preference falsification underpinning beats on hidden dissent, self-sustaining silent equilibria, and sudden collapse (cross-referenced to item 34).
- Antonio Gramsci. *Selections from the Prison Notebooks* (*Quaderni del carcere*, written 1929–1935; English selection ed. Hoare & Nowell-Smith, International Publishers, 1971). — The concept of hegemony: domination sustained by the internalised consent of the subordinated, used in the critique beat as the counter to preference-falsification's assumption of suppressed private dissent.
- Douglas S. Massey and Nancy A. Denton. *American Apartheid: Segregation and the Making of the Underclass*. Harvard University Press, 1993. — Residential racial segregation in the United States produced and maintained by concrete institutional machinery (discriminatory lending, restrictive covenants, violence), used in the critique beat to insist on the material/coercive scaffolding.
- Damon Centola, Joshua Becker, Devon Brackbill, Andrea Baronchelli. "Experimental evidence for tipping points in social convention." *Science* 360 (6393), 2018, pp. 1116–1119. — The ~25% committed-minority threshold for overturning an established social convention; cited for the tipping-point figure (cross-referenced to items 58, 59). Journal/volume/issue/pages and the ~25% finding CONFIRMED. As the critique beat notes, the 25% figure is specific to the linguistic-convention experimental paradigm and is used here as analogy, not as a measured constant of real stratified orders.
