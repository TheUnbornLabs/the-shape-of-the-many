# References — 76. Durkheim's collective effervescence

## Confirmed sources

- Durkheim, Émile. *The Elementary Forms of Religious Life* (*Les formes élémentaires de la vie religieuse*). Paris: Félix Alcan, 1912. (Widely read in the Karen E. Fields translation, Free Press, 1995.) — The founding text; introduces collective effervescence, the totemic case, and the thesis that the sacred is society experiencing itself. Source of the alternation between profane dispersal and sacred assembly, and of Durkheim's claim that congregating is itself a powerful stimulant.
- Spencer, Baldwin, and Frank J. Gillen. *The Native Tribes of Central Australia*. London: Macmillan, 1899. — Primary ethnographic source Durkheim relied upon for his account of Aboriginal totemism and ceremonial gatherings; Durkheim did no fieldwork himself. Basis for the colonial-anthropology and second-hand-evidence caveats in the critique.
- Collins, Randall. *Interaction Ritual Chains*. Princeton: Princeton University Press, 2004. — Modern reconstruction of Durkheim's insight into a testable micro-sociology: co-presence, mutual focus, and emotional entrainment generating solidarity and "emotional energy." Supports the partial-vindication argument in the critique.
- Páez, Dario; Rimé, Bernard; Basabe, Nekane; Wlodarczyk, Anna; Zumeta, Larraitz. "Psychosocial Effects of Perceived Emotional Synchrony in Collective Gatherings." *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*, 108(5), 2015, pp. 711–729. DOI: 10.1037/pspi0000014. — Empirical study operationalising participation in collective gatherings and linking perceived emotional synchrony to social identification, shared beliefs, and meaning. Supports the "recent work gives the mechanism teeth" claim. (Full citation verified.)
