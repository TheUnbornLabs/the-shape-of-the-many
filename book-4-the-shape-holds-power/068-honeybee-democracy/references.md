# References — 68. Honeybee democracy

## Confirmed sources

- Seeley, Thomas D. *Honeybee Democracy*. Princeton University Press, 2010. — The definitive account; source for the whole decision process, the Appledore Island nest-box experiments, the quorum-sensing mechanism, and the explicit lessons drawn for human group decision-making (beats 1–7).
- Seeley, Thomas D.; Visscher, P. Kirk; Schlegel, Thomas; Hogan, Patrick M.; Franks, Nigel R.; Marshall, James A. R. "Stop Signals Provide Cross Inhibition in Collective Decision-Making by Honeybee Swarms." *Science*, vol. 335, no. 6064, 2012, pp. 108–111. — Source for cross-inhibition via head-butting "stop signals," directed preferentially at rival-site dancers, and its role in breaking deadlock (mechanism beat, flip beat).
- Seeley, Thomas D.; Visscher, P. Kirk. "Quorum sensing during nest-site selection by honeybee swarms." *Behavioral Ecology and Sociobiology*, vol. 56, 2004, pp. 594–601. — Source for the quorum threshold (~15–20 scouts at a site) triggering the swarm's commitment and lift-off.
- Lindauer, Martin. "Schwarmbienen auf Wohnungssuche." *Zeitschrift für vergleichende Physiologie*, vol. 37, 1955, pp. 263–324. — Foundational discovery that swarm scouts advertise nest sites via dance and the swarm concentrates its dancing on one site before flying; the skeleton of the house-hunting decision.
- von Frisch, Karl. *The Dance Language and Orientation of Bees* (Tanzsprache und Orientierung der Bienen, 1965; English trans. Harvard University Press, 1967). — Decoding of the waggle dance (direction and distance encoding). Von Frisch shared the 1973 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine (with Lorenz and Tinbergen) for this work.
- Seeley, Thomas D.; Buhrman, Susannah C. "Nest-site selection in honey bees: how well do swarms implement the 'best-of-N' decision rule?" *Behavioral Ecology and Sociobiology*, vol. 49, 2001, pp. 416–427. — Support for the claim that swarms reliably choose the best available site, not merely an adequate one (anchor example).
- Surowiecki, James. *The Wisdom of Crowds*. Doubleday, 2004. — The four conditions (diversity, independence, decentralisation, aggregation) the bees are argued to satisfy by design (item 20 cross-reference).
