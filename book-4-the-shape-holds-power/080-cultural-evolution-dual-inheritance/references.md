# References — 80. Cultural evolution and dual inheritance

## Confirmed sources

- Cavalli-Sforza, L. L., & Feldman, M. W. *Cultural Transmission and Evolution: A Quantitative Approach*. Princeton University Press, 1981. — The founding formal framework importing population-genetic mathematics into the study of cultural transmission.
- Boyd, R., & Richerson, P. J. *Culture and the Evolutionary Process*. University of Chicago Press, 1985. — The book that established dual inheritance theory; models transmission biases (content, conformist, prestige/model-based) as formal terms.
- Richerson, P. J., & Boyd, R. *Not by Genes Alone: How Culture Transformed Human Evolution*. University of Chicago Press, 2005. — Accessible statement of gene-culture coevolution for a wider readership.
- Henrich, J. *The Secret of Our Success: How Culture Is Driving Human Evolution, Domesticating Our Species, and Making Us Smarter*. Princeton University Press, 2016. — Argues cumulative culture (the ratchet), not individual intelligence, underlies human ecological dominance; source for the cassava-detoxification and cumulative-know-how examples.
- Henrich, J. *The WEIRDest People in the World: How the West Became Psychologically Peculiar and Particularly Prosperous*. Farrar, Straus and Giroux, 2020. — Argues that WEIRD psychology is a cultural product, not a human universal.
- Henrich, J., Heine, S. J., & Norenzayan, A. "The weirdest people in the world?" *Behavioral and Brain Sciences*, 33(2–3), 2010, pp. 61–83. — The paper documenting the narrowness of the samples underpinning behavioural science; the replication-humility anchor.
- Sperber, D. *Explaining Culture: A Naturalistic Approach*. Blackwell, 1996. — Source for the "cultural attraction" / reconstructive-transmission critique used in the critique beat.

## Further reading to verify before final publication

- Lactase persistence as gene-culture coevolution — causal ordering (dairying preceding and driving selection for lactase persistence) is well established. Primary sources VERIFIED: Tishkoff, S. A., et al. "Convergent adaptation of human lactase persistence in Africa and Europe." *Nature Genetics*, 39(1), 2007, pp. 31–40 — identifies three African persistence-associated SNPs (G/C-14010, T/G-13915, C/G-13907) arising on distinct haplotype backgrounds from the European C/T-13910 variant, a case of convergent evolution driven by dairying. Bersaglieri, T., et al. "Genetic Signatures of Strong Recent Positive Selection at the Lactase Gene." *American Journal of Human Genetics*, 74(6), 2004, pp. 1111–1120 — the European variant. The chapter's general statement (high frequency in northern Europeans and several African/Middle Eastern pastoralist groups; rare where dairying was absent) is consistent with these sources. Exact onset dates remain an active area; the chapter keeps them general ("some thousands of years ago"), which is correct.
- Cassava / bitter-manioc detoxification example — VERIFIED against Henrich (2016): the multi-step processing (scraping/grating, prolonged soaking, fermenting, drying) and the delayed cyanide toxicity of bitter manioc's cyanogenic glucosides trace to Henrich's account of the Tukanoan of the Colombian Amazon, where no individual can reconstruct the full causal rationale yet everyone follows the protocol. The chapter's general phrasing ("Indigenous peoples of the Americas") is accurate.
- The claim that conformist transmission (frequency-dependent, disproportionate copying of the common variant) is empirically demonstrated in humans — supported by Boyd & Richerson's models and subsequent experimental work (e.g., Efferson et al.), but the strength and universality of the empirical evidence for conformist bias specifically is debated; verify the current state of the evidence.
- Sperber's cultural-attraction critique and the "reconstruction vs. copying" debate — accurately attributed, but the balance of the current scholarly debate (Sperber and collaborators vs. the Boyd-Richerson-Henrich program) should be checked to ensure the critique beat represents the present state fairly.
