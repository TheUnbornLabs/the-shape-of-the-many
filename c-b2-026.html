Chapter 26 · The Shape Breaks

Information cascades

Watch enough people choose before you, and it becomes rational to stop thinking — at which point a thousand followers carry no more knowledge than the first two.

The idea

Here is where the shape breaks. For three chapters we have watched crowds converge on truth: Galton's fairgoers averaging their way to the weight of an ox, Condorcet's jurors driving error toward zero, Surowiecki naming the conditions under which the many out-reason the one. All of it rested on a single quiet assumption — that each person judges for themselves, that the errors are independent and so cancel. Now remove that assumption. Let people decide not all at once but in sequence, and let each one see what the others chose before them. Watch what happens to wisdom when the shape is allowed to fold back on itself.

An information cascade is what forms when people make a decision in order, can observe the choices of those who went first, but cannot see the private reasons behind those choices — and, being sensible, begin to weigh the visible tally of the crowd against their own hidden hunch. At first this is harmless. The early choosers act on what they privately know, and their actions broadcast that knowledge to everyone downstream. But there comes a point — and it comes astonishingly early, often after just two agreeing predecessors — when the accumulated weight of other people's choices so outweighs any single person's private signal that the rational move is to ignore your own information entirely and copy the herd. You do it not because you are weak or stupid but because it is, given what you can see, the correct calculation.

And here is the trap sprung. From the moment you stop consulting your private signal, you also stop transmitting it. Your choice tells the next person nothing new — it only echoes the choices you were copying. So does theirs, and theirs. The crowd goes on growing, each new member adding a body to the tally and not one atom of new evidence to it. A cascade is a column of people all pointing the same direction, and the direction was set by the first one or two, on the strength of almost nothing, and then frozen. The majority can be enormous, unanimous, and confident — and be built on a foundation of two coin-flips. That is the mob's mathematics, and it is not a failure of reason. It is reason, working perfectly, on information that has been quietly starved.

The origin

The idea arrived, fully formed and twice over, in 1992 — a rare case of two independent teams reaching the same summit in the same year. Sushil Bikhchandani, David Hirshleifer, and Ivo Welch published "A Theory of Fads, Fashion, Custom, and Cultural Change as Informational Cascades" in the Journal of Political Economy (volume 100, pages 992–1026). In the same season, Abhijit Banerjee published "A Simple Model of Herd Behavior" in the Quarterly Journal of Economics (volume 107, pages 797–817). The two papers set out to solve the same puzzle from different doors and met in the middle.

The puzzle was an old embarrassment for economics. Standard theory prized the individual weighing evidence and choosing rationally, yet the visible world was full of behaviour that looked like sheep: fashions that swept in and out for no reason anyone could name, technologies that triumphed over better rivals because they got a head start, restaurants that filled while identical ones next door sat empty, whole professions lurching between conventions. The lazy explanation was that people are irrational conformists. The 1992 papers refused that explanation. They asked instead: what if herd behaviour is exactly what fully rational, self-interested, information-hungry people would produce, under one specific arrangement — deciding in sequence, in public?

Their answer was mathematically clean and, once seen, hard to un-see. Rational Bayesian agents, each holding a private clue and each able to watch their predecessors, will predictably abandon their own clues and pile onto whatever the early movers happened to do. The herd is not a lapse from rationality. It is a product of it. Bikhchandani, Hirshleifer, and Welch returned six years later, in the Journal of Economic Perspectives (1998), with a review essay that carried the framework out of the seminar room and into fashion, finance, politics, and law, and it is largely from that essay that the concept entered the wider vocabulary.

The mechanism

Three ingredients, and no more, are needed to build a cascade: decisions made in sequence, actions that are observable while the reasons behind them are hidden, and each person holding a private signal that is informative but not decisive. Take them together and the gears turn on their own.

The canonical way to see it is with an urn. Imagine there are two urns. One holds mostly red balls; the other holds mostly blue. A researcher has secretly chosen one urn, and a line of people must each guess which. Each person, in turn, steps behind a screen, draws one ball, looks at its colour privately, returns it — and then announces, out loud, a guess. Everyone hears every guess; nobody sees anyone else's ball. The single ball you draw is your private signal: it tips you, but only slightly, toward one urn.

Person one draws — say, red — and has nothing to go on but that ball, so she announces "red." Her guess now transmits her signal perfectly: everyone downstream knows she drew red. Person two draws red too, and announces "red." Now person three steps up and draws blue. Watch closely, because this is the hinge of the whole chapter. Person three holds a blue ball in his hand. His own eyes, his own private evidence, say blue. But he has also heard two reds ahead of him, and he can reason: two reds and my one blue — the balance of evidence is two-to-one for the red urn. The rational announcement is "red." He sets his own ball down and speaks against it.

That is the step people find hard to believe until they feel it themselves: your private signal has just been discarded, and correctly so. And now the damage compounds. Person four sees three reds and knows that person three's "red" might have been spoken over a blue ball — it carries no reliable information at all. So person four, whatever she draws, is in the same position as person three, and also says "red." So does person five, and everyone after. The cascade has locked. From person three onward, no one's announcement reveals their draw; the public tally simply reprints the verdict of the first two draws, over and over, in a longer and longer column. If those first two draws happened to come from the blue urn by chance — and two unlucky draws from a mostly-blue urn is nothing exotic — then the entire crowd, however large, will confidently guess red, and be wrong, forever.

Two features of this fall out of the model and matter enormously later. First, cascades are information-starved: after they begin, group size stops meaning anything, because new members add no new evidence. Second, and paradoxically, cascades are fragile. Precisely because they rest on so little — two draws, no more — a single credible piece of public information can shatter them. Let one person's private signal become visible to all, or let a trusted source announce the truth, and the whole column can reverse in an instant. A cascade is a tower built on two stones; it stands rigid until someone kicks a stone, and then it comes down all at once.

The anchor example

Forget the urn for a moment and stand on a real street on a Friday night, hungry, in front of two restaurants you have never tried. They are side by side, similar menus, similar prices. One is packed, a warm hum spilling out the door and a small queue on the pavement. The other is empty, a waiter folding napkins alone. Which do you walk into?

You walk into the full one, of course — and you do it for a reason you would call good if asked. All those people, you think, must know something I don't. Their presence is evidence. The crowd is a review you can read from the street. This is not foolishness; it is exactly the Bayesian move the model describes. You are treating other diners' choices as informative, and folding them into your own decision.

But now inhabit the people who filled that restaurant, one by one, earlier in the evening. The first couple picked it on a whim, or a half-remembered recommendation, or because it was two steps closer — some private signal, faint and personal. The third party to arrive saw two tables taken and reasoned as you are reasoning: they must know something. They sat down, and now there were three. The fourth saw three and joined. Every single person after the first two chose that restaurant not because they had any independent evidence about the food, but because they were reading the choices of people who were themselves just reading the choices of the couple who arrived first. The "wisdom of the crowd" you thought you were consuming from the pavement does not exist. There is no accumulated judgement inside that warm room — only a copy of a copy of a whim, wearing the costume of consensus. The empty restaurant next door may well have the better kitchen. It simply lost the first coin-flip, and in a cascade the first coin-flip is the whole game.

The unbearable part is that your reasoning and theirs were identical, and correct at every step, and it produced a crowd that knows nothing pretending to be a crowd that knows something. Anderson and Holt reproduced exactly this in the laboratory in 1997, running the urn game with real people and real payoffs in the American Economic Review, and found that subjects fell into cascades — including wrong ones — at close to the rates the Bayesian model predicts. The herd on the pavement is not a metaphor for the theory. It is the theory, made of people.

The flip

This is the dark twin, and it is the most exact inversion in the entire series. Galton's ox (item 17) worked because his 787 fairgoers never heard one another. Each sealed a private guess on a ticket, in ignorance of every other guess, and only afterward were the tickets gathered and averaged — and the average landed within a pound of the truth. Independence was the whole engine. Condorcet (item 18) proved the same thing in binary form: independent voters, each a little better than chance, drive the majority toward certainty because their errors, being independent, cancel.

Now take those very same 787 people — not different people, not a rabble instead of a crowd, the same 787 — and change one thing. Let them guess in sequence, and let each see the guesses made before. Independence is gone. Their errors no longer cancel; they align. After the first few guesses, everyone downstream is anchoring on the running tally rather than on the ox in front of them, and the crowd converges not on the animal's true weight but on whatever number the first two guessers happened to write. The wisest crowd in the book and a herd that knows nothing are built from identical human beings. The only difference is a condition — sequence plus observation, which together destroy independence (item 21). That is the sign-flip in its purest form, and it is why this chapter opens Book Two.

Everything downstream in this movement is a variation on this fold. When the copied signal is not a restaurant but a claim repeated until it feels true, the cascade becomes an availability cascade (item 27). When the copied action is withdrawing your money before the bank runs dry, it becomes a bank run (item 28), where the payoff of following is not just informational but real. When the copiers are fund managers and analysts watching each other rather than the fundamentals, it becomes institutional herding (item 63). And when the private signal is suppressed not by the mathematics of sequence but by fear — when you fall silent not because two predecessors outweigh your evidence but because dissent is dangerous — the cascade becomes preference falsification (item 34), the special and darkest case, where the hidden signal is a held tongue.

The human weight

Book Two foregrounds a single grim question: how do false consensuses form, and how do they lock? The cascade is the mechanism's clearest answer, and its reach runs from the trivial to the murderous along one unbroken line.

At the light end it is fashion and fad — the hemlines, the baby names, the neighbourhood that becomes fashionable because it became fashionable, the app everyone downloads because everyone downloaded it. One rung up it is the bubble: an asset rising because it is rising, each buyer inferring value from the buying of others, the price a column of copies with no floor of judgement underneath — the manias of item 30 are cascades running in a market. One rung further it is the scientific bandwagon, where a claim is cited because it is cited, and a generation of researchers builds on a foundation few of them independently checked, the private doubts of many never entering the public tally.

But the deepest stakes, the ones this series exists to name, lie where the cascade meets power. An oppressive order is rarely sustained because a majority privately believes in it. It is sustained because each person, looking around, sees a sea of outward compliance and infers from it that resistance is hopeless, that dissenters are few, that perhaps the order is even right — and, so inferring, complies in turn, adding one more compliant body to the tally that the next person will read the same way. This is a cascade of visible conformity, and its terrible efficiency is that it manufactures the appearance of unanimity out of a population of hidden dissenters. Almost no one need believe for everyone to act as though everyone believes. The private signals — the doubt, the refusal, the sense that this is wrong — are all still there, held behind a hundred million screens, transmitting nothing because no one dares announce over their blue ball. This is precisely preference falsification (item 34) and pluralistic ignorance (item 42): a visible cascade of conformity, laid over an invisible reservoir of dissent, and the two can drift so far apart that a society is publicly certain of something almost no one privately accepts.

And here the fragility that fell out of the model becomes the most hopeful sentence in the book. A cascade rests on almost nothing, which is its horror and its weakness both. It stands rigid only until one credible public signal contradicts it — one person who visibly refuses, one leaked truth, one dissenter who does not disappear. Because the whole column is built on two stones, kicking one stone can bring it down all at once. This is why silent orders, so immovable for so long, collapse not gradually but overnight, in the astonished space of a season: the first visible refusal reveals to the second dissenter that they were never alone, and the reservoir of hidden signal, dammed for a generation, breaks through. That first visible refusal has a name and a chapter of its own — the first follower (item 58) — and the collapse it triggers is the recurring miracle that answers the recurring horror of this movement. The same mechanism that locks the false consensus is the one that shatters it.

The critique

The theory is beautiful, and beauty in a model is a reason for suspicion. Three cautions belong on the record.

First, the rationality it assumes is heroic. The agents in Bikhchandani, Hirshleifer, and Welch are perfect Bayesians: they update probabilities flawlessly, know the exact sequence of who moved when, know the reliability of every signal, and reason without error about how much information each predecessor's action carries. Real people do none of this cleanly. They misjudge how informative a crowd is, forget who came first, and fold in emotion and status. The model's elegance is bought by idealisation, and its cleanest confirmations — Anderson and Holt's urns — come from the laboratory, where the sequence is explicit and the signals are calibrated. The lab cascade is a purified thing; the wild cascade is muddied.

Second, and more troubling for anyone who wants to use the idea, information cascades are empirically almost impossible to separate from their cousins. When a real crowd herds, how much is informational copying (I infer you know something), how much is normative conformity pressure (I fear your disapproval — Asch's line, item 35, and the normative conformity of item 41), and how much is payoff externality (I follow because your action changes my incentives, as in a bank run, item 28)? All three produce the same visible herd, and in the field they arrive tangled together. The pure informational cascade may be rarer in the wild than its explanatory popularity suggests.

Third, the framework is dangerously flexible. Because any herd can be narrated after the fact as a cascade — there is always a plausible early mover to blame — the theory risks explaining everything and therefore predicting nothing. Its onset conditions have been tested and hold up; its more striking prediction, fragility — that cascades should shatter easily under a single credible public signal — is far less tested than the claim that they form. We know rather well how these towers rise. We know less than we would like, empirically, about exactly what it takes to make them fall. Which is a humbling place to leave the darkest chapter's brightest hope: the collapse we most need to understand is the part the science has confirmed the least.
