Chapter 100 · The Shape Remade

The epistemics of the field

A book about how crowds fool themselves must, at the end, ask whether the crowd that produced its evidence was fooling itself too — and about whom, exactly, all of it was measured.

The idea

Every claim in this series arrived wearing a citation. A study found this, an experiment showed that, a theorem proves the other. The citations lent the whole edifice its authority — the sense that behind each chapter stood a laboratory, a dataset, a peer-reviewed result, and therefore something firmer than opinion. This final chapter asks the question the citations were quietly borrowing against: how much of it is true, and about whom was it measured?

The question has two edges, and both cut deep. The first is reliability. A large fraction of the findings this series has leaned on — especially the vivid social-psychology results, the ones that make the best anchor examples — turn out to be unstable. When other laboratories repeated the experiments, many of the effects shrank, wobbled, or vanished. This is the replication crisis, and it swept through psychology beginning around 2011, taking down or badly denting several results this very book has presented as though they were bedrock. The second edge is generalisation. Even where a finding does replicate, it was almost always measured on a strange and unrepresentative slice of humanity: Western, educated, industrialised, rich, democratic subjects — very often university undergraduates earning course credit — who happen to be statistical outliers on many of the exact dimensions collective-behaviour science cares about. Psychology built a portrait of "human nature" from the people easiest to recruit, and then forgot to check whether they were typical. They were not.

So the honest capstone to a hundred chapters is not a triumphant restatement. It is an accounting. Some of what you have read is robust, replicated, and cross-culturally supported; hold it firmly. Some of it is a fragile lab effect measured on sophomores in one country in one decade; hold it loosely. The rest of this chapter is about how to tell which is which — and about the uncomfortable discovery that the tools this series built for judging crowds apply, without mercy, to the crowd of scientists who supplied its evidence.

The origin

The reckoning has two founding documents, published five years apart, and between them they name the two diseases.

The first thread began, improbably, with a paper claiming that people can see the future. In 2011 the social psychologist Daryl Bem published, in a respected journal, a series of experiments purporting to show statistical evidence for precognition. The methods were entirely orthodox — the same design choices, the same statistics, the same standard of "significance" that thousands of accepted papers used. That was the scandal. If impeccable standard practice could produce solid-looking evidence for something that cannot be true, then standard practice was broken, and the whole literature built on it was suspect. Later that year, Joseph Simmons, Leif Nelson, and Uri Simonsohn made the point explicit in a paper whose title became a slogan: "False-Positive Psychology" (Psychological Science, 2011). They showed that the ordinary, undramatic freedoms a researcher enjoys — deciding when to stop collecting data, which measures to report, which conditions to compare, whether to control for this or that — could be combined, without any conscious fraud, to produce a "statistically significant" result from pure noise more than half the time. They named the culprit: researcher degrees of freedom. The problem was not a few bad actors. It was the incentive structure working as designed.

The second thread is the one that names whom. In 2010 the anthropologist Joseph Henrich, with the psychologists Steven Heine and Ara Norenzayan, published "The weirdest people in the world?" in Behavioral and Brain Sciences (33:61–83). They coined the acronym that stuck — WEIRD — and marshalled the evidence that the standard experimental subject of behavioural science was drawn overwhelmingly from Western, Educated, Industrialised, Rich, Democratic societies, and disproportionately from the undergraduate wing of even those. Then they delivered the blow: on measure after measure — visual perception, spatial reasoning, notions of fairness, cooperation, individualism, even the susceptibility to certain optical illusions — these subjects were not a representative midpoint of humanity but frequent outliers, sitting at the far end of the human distribution. The discipline had been generalising from the least generalisable people it could have chosen.

Behind both papers stood an earlier, colder warning. In 2005 the physician-researcher John Ioannidis published "Why Most Published Research Findings Are False" (PLoS Medicine), arguing from the mathematics of significance testing, small samples, and publication bias that in many fields the majority of published positive claims should be expected to be wrong. At the time it read as provocation. Within a decade it read as prophecy.

The mechanism

Why does a field herd toward false consensus? Not through conspiracy, but through the accumulation of small, individually reasonable choices — which should sound familiar, because it is the mechanism of every crowd in this book.

Start with what gets published. Journals, editors, and reviewers prefer clean, positive, surprising results. A study that finds an effect is publishable; a study that finds nothing usually is not. This is publication bias, and it means the literature is not a sample of what researchers found — it is a sample of what they found and that came out positive. The failures, the null results, the quiet disconfirmations sit unpublished in what researchers call the file drawer. The published record is a survivorship-biased highlight reel.

Now add the researcher degrees of freedom. Faced with data that do not quite reach significance, a scientist can, without lying, try another analysis, drop an outlier, add a covariate, collect a few more subjects. Each choice is defensible in isolation; together they constitute p-hacking — fishing through the garden of forking paths until something crosses the significance threshold by chance. Add HARKing — hypothesising after the results are known, dressing up a pattern found by luck as a prediction made in advance. Add small samples, which produce wildly unstable estimates and inflate the size of any effect that does clear the bar. Each of these is amplified by an academic incentive structure that rewards publication volume and novelty over reliability: careers, grants, and tenure flow to those who produce striking positive results, not to those who carefully confirm that an old result holds.

The remedy is the mirror image of the disease, and it gives us the field-guide for telling robust findings from fragile ones. Trust large samples over small ones. Trust preregistration — where the hypothesis and analysis plan are locked in, publicly, before the data are seen, closing off the forking paths. Trust replication, especially by independent labs with no stake in the original result, and above all trust the coordinated multi-lab replications like the Many Labs projects (Klein and colleagues), which run the same protocol across dozens of sites and many thousands of subjects. Trust meta-analysis that accounts for publication bias rather than merely averaging the survivors. And trust cross-cultural data over single-population data. A finding that survives all of these is knowledge. A striking result from one small undergraduate sample, never repeated, is a candidate for knowledge — and should be carried as a hypothesis, not a fact.

The anchor example

Two numbers anchor this chapter, and read together they say everything.

The first is roughly one-third. In 2015 the Open Science Collaboration — a consortium of 270 researchers coordinated by Brian Nosek — published in Science the results of an extraordinary undertaking: they had attempted to replicate 100 studies drawn from three leading psychology journals, using large samples and, wherever possible, the original materials and the original authors' review (Open Science Collaboration, Science 2015, 349:aac4716). The results were sobering by any measure. Where 97 of the original 100 studies had reported statistically significant effects, only about 36 percent of the replications did. The average effect size in the replications was roughly half the original. By the various measures they used, only something in the range of a third to two-fifths of the findings clearly held up. These were not fringe papers; they were published results in the discipline's best journals. And some of the casualties are familiar. Ego depletion — the idea that willpower is a finite resource drained by use — has largely failed to replicate at the effect sizes originally claimed. Social priming, including the famous report that reading words about the elderly made people walk more slowly out of the lab, has become one of the crisis's emblematic wreckages. Power posing, the claim that standing in an expansive posture changes your hormones and behaviour, saw its behavioural-hormonal effects retracted in spirit by one of its own authors. And the reputations of the field's grand dramatic set-pieces have taken damage: the Stanford Prison Experiment (item 32) now looks less like a spontaneous descent into brutality than a partly-scripted demonstration, and Milgram's obedience studies (item 37) turn out, from the archives, to be messier and more variable than the clean headline number suggested.

The second number is the WEIRD statistic behind the first. When Henrich, Heine, and Norenzayan surveyed the samples underlying the top psychology journals, they found that the overwhelming majority of subjects came from Western industrialised societies — a survey of the top journals in six subdisciplines found 68 percent of subjects were from the United States and fully 96 percent from Western industrialised nations, which between them hold roughly 12 percent of the world's population — and a large share were psychology undergraduates, a group enormously over-represented relative to their share of the human population. Put the two numbers side by side. Only a third or so of these findings replicate even within the narrow population they were measured on — and that population is a statistical outlier for the species. That is the true state of the evidence. Not "here is what humans do," but "here is what some university students in a handful of rich democracies did, in effects that hold up perhaps a third of the time." How much of this can we trust, and about whom? Now you have the concrete answer, and it is humbling.

The flip

Here is the turn this entire series has been building toward, and it is the only fitting way to end it: science is itself a collective-behaviour system, and everything this book says about crowds applies to it. The replication crisis is not an external audit of the thesis. It is the thesis, playing out inside the institution that supplied the thesis its evidence.

Watch the mechanisms recur, one for one. Publication bias and citation cascades build a scientific bandwagon: a striking early result gets published, cited, cited-for-citing, until "everybody knows" it — an information cascade (item 26) in which later researchers copy the confidence of earlier ones instead of independently checking, and the true evidential weight of a thousand papers is no greater than the shaky first study they all rest on. A fragile finding goes viral in the literature the way a rumour goes viral in a network (item 57), replicated in reviews and textbooks and popular books until sheer repetition makes it feel known — an availability cascade (item 27) dressed in the robes of scholarship. Researchers, being human, face conformity pressure (item 35): it is costly to publish the result that contradicts a famous senior figure, costly to be the null finding at the celebratory conference. And the whole apparatus is driven by incentive-structured herding (item 63) as surely as any market: careers chase the same rewards, so errors correlate, so the field can converge with tremendous confidence on something false — exactly the machinery that Condorcet's second edge (item 18) describes, where a crowd of judges below the competence line does not cancel its errors but stacks them.

Which is to say: the crowd of scientists is wise or foolish under precisely the conditions this book has named throughout. It is wise when it protects independence (labs checking each other without collusion), diversity (different methods, different populations, adversarial priors — the diversity-prediction logic of item 19), and honest aggregation (registered reports, preregistration, adversarial collaboration, the multi-round independence-preserving discipline of the Delphi method in item 22 and the calibrated, track-recorded humility of superforecasting in item 92). It becomes a mob when publication incentives correlate everyone's errors and reward the confident over the careful. The replication crisis is the wise-crowd-versus-mob drama of the whole series, staged one last time on the stage where the series got its script. The book's thesis explains the book's own epistemic risks. There is no cleaner demonstration that the argument is not special pleading: it turns on its own sources, and it holds.

The human weight

Now the frontier thread, the one this series has followed since Book One, closes on its sharpest point.

If the "universal" science of human collective behaviour is really the psychology of a narrow, powerful slice of humanity, then every claim about "human nature" must be held with a specific humility — because such claims have always been load-bearing in the architecture of social arrangements. Assertions about how "people" behave, what "people" want, what human beings are "naturally" like, are never inert. They are used to justify hierarchies, to explain why some are governed and others govern, to naturalise the way things happen to be. And the WEIRD critique lands a devastating blow on that whole enterprise: much of what has been presented as how people behave is really how some people, in specific and unusual conditions, behaved when a researcher paid them in course credit. The individualism, the particular sense of fairness, the specific responses to authority and conformity that populate the classic studies are not the human baseline. They are one culture's reading, taken for the whole. This connects directly to cultural evolution (item 80): the WEIRD mind is not human nature laid bare but a recently and locally evolved package of dispositions, shaped by particular institutions, kinship structures, and histories — a variant, not the default.

Which forces this series to a confession, and it is the moral landing of the entire project. This book rests substantially on WEIRD, sometimes non-replicated research. The vivid experiments, the memorable studies, the anchor examples chosen precisely because they stick — a real share of them come from the fragile, narrow-sample part of the literature. To pretend otherwise would betray the one thing the series has claimed to value: the honesty to turn its own tool on itself. So here is how to hold what you have read. Not as a set of proven universal laws of humanity. As a toolkit of candidate mechanisms and useful lenses — ways of seeing that earn their keep by illuminating cases, not by having been ratified for the entire species. Some of the toolkit is far sturdier than the rest, and you should weight accordingly. The mathematics carries the most weight, because mathematics is not a WEIRD undergraduate: Condorcet's theorem (item 18) is true wherever independent votes and a competence threshold exist. The network results — the topology of contagion, the arithmetic of thresholds, the shape of a cascade — are structural, and travel. Some of the evolutionary logic, where it is truly cross-species and cross-cultural, travels too. The fragile lab effects — the primes, the depletions, the postures, the single-study surprises — travel least, and should be held most loosely of all. Read the whole series again, if you like, with that weighting in hand. That is not a retraction. It is the instruction manual the book owed you.

The critique

And now, one last time, the discipline this series has demanded in every chapter: turn the critique even on the critique. The replication crisis and the WEIRD reckoning are real, but they too can curdle into their own kind of mob if held without balance.

Consider first what the crisis produced. It did not end in nihilism; it ended in reform. Preregistration is now common. Registered reports — where journals accept a study on the strength of its design, before the results exist, so that null findings get published too — have spread across the field. Samples have grown; the Many Labs collaborations run protocols across dozens of sites; whole subfields have rebuilt their methods. This is not science collapsing. This is science working: the crowd of scientists, having correlated its errors, deploying independence and honest aggregation to correct them. Self-correction under scrutiny is the process functioning exactly as advertised. A field that can convict its own famous findings and then change how it works has demonstrated the very wisdom this book prizes.

Consider, too, what survived. Not everything failed — a real portion of the 2015 replications held up cleanly, and the effects that replicate across labs and cultures are more trustworthy now, not less, precisely because they ran the gauntlet. The mathematics stands untouched. The robust, cross-culturally corroborated findings stand. And "it didn't replicate" can itself become a lazy dismissal — a way of waving off any inconvenient result without doing the work, a single failed replication (itself a small study, itself fallible) treated as a verdict. That is just the cascade running in the opposite direction. The WEIRD critique, likewise, does not license the claim that nothing is universal. Some things clearly are: the structural logic of networks, certain features of cognition and cooperation, the basic mathematics of aggregation. WEIRD means check before you generalise, not nothing generalises.

So the series ends where its thesis always pointed, now aimed at itself. The crowd of scientists, like every crowd in this book, is wise or foolish depending on one thing: whether it protects independence and honest correction, or lets its incentives correlate its errors. The Shape — form rising from many local acts, holding or breaking on the conditions beneath it — was never only out there in the juries and markets and mobs. It was also the shape of the knowledge that let us see them, subject to the same laws, redeemed by the same virtues. Carry the ideas of these hundred chapters the way you should carry any finding worth having: as sharp, provisional tools — held loosely, used carefully, revised as the evidence turns. The wise crowd and the mob are the same people. That was true of the ox-guessers and the panicked, the markets and the manias, the swarms and the senates. It is true, too, of the people who studied them, and of the book you are holding. Hold it accordingly.
