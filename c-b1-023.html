Prediction markets

A price is a crowd's probability, made honest by money — the same instrument that reads the future can also stop reading it and start reading itself.

The idea

Suppose you want to know how likely something is — that a particular candidate wins an election, that a drug clears its next trial, that a product ships on time. You could poll people, but people are cheap with opinions they will never pay for. So instead you print a contract: a slip of paper, or a line of code, that pays one dollar if the event happens and nothing if it doesn't. Then you let people buy and sell it. Someone who thinks the event nearly certain will pay almost a dollar for the promise of a dollar. Someone who thinks it a long shot will sell it for a few cents. Trading finds the price at which buyers and sellers balance, and that price — sixty-three cents, say — is not a metaphor. It is the crowd's collective probability, sixty-three percent, read straight off the ticker.

That is a prediction market: an institution whose price is a forecast. What makes it more than a glorified poll is the money. Every trader who prices the contract is staking their own stake on being right. Confidence you must back with cash is confidence weighed differently from confidence you merely announce. The market does not ask what people say they believe; it asks what they will bet, and it weights each belief by how much its holder is willing to lose. The output is a single number that moves in real time, aggregating what thousands of scattered people privately know into one continuously updated probability — a barometer built out of self-interest.

The origin

The intellectual seed is older than any market you can name, and it belongs to an economist arguing about socialism. In 1945 Friedrich Hayek published "The Use of Knowledge in Society" in the American Economic Review, and its argument was that the central problem of economics is not how to allocate known resources but how to use knowledge that no single mind possesses. The relevant facts — who needs what, where a shortage is forming, what a thing is worth to the person holding it — are dispersed across millions of heads, fragmentary, often inarticulate, never available to a planner in one place. Hayek's claim was that the price system solves this by not requiring anyone to gather the knowledge at all. A price is a signal that condenses the private information of everyone acting on it; when tin grows scarce somewhere, its price rises everywhere, and users who never learn why economise all the same. Prices, in his phrase, function as a mechanism for communicating information. A prediction market is that insight turned into an instrument on purpose: build a price whose only job is to communicate what the crowd knows about the odds.

The turning of insight into instrument happened at the University of Iowa. In 1988 economists there launched what became the Iowa Electronic Markets, real-money futures markets on the outcomes of American elections, run by the business school as a research project and permitted to operate as a limited academic exercise. Robert Forsythe, Forrest Nelson, George Neumann, and Jack Wright reported the first results in the American Economic Review in 1992, and the finding was startling: a market of a few hundred self-selected traders, wagering small sums, produced election forecasts that were often closer to the eventual vote share than the major opinion polls. Around the same time the economist Robin Hanson began arguing for "idea futures" — markets in the truth of scientific and policy claims — and later for "decision markets" that would let institutions bet on which choice produced the best outcome. Two decades on, Justin Wolfers and Eric Zitzewitz surveyed the whole field in "Prediction Markets," published in the Journal of Economic Perspectives in 2004, and gave it the name and the framework it now travels under.

The mechanism

The gears are three, and they mesh.

The first is aggregation by price. A contract paying a dollar on an event has a price that, under weak assumptions, equals the market's collective probability of that event. When a trader who thinks the true odds higher than the quoted price buys, and one who thinks them lower sells, their trade nudges the price toward their belief — nudged in proportion to how much they stake. The price that clears the market is a stake-weighted average of everyone's estimate: a live poll in which votes are denominated in dollars and cast by the confident.

The second gear is skin in the game. In a survey, a lie or a lazy guess costs nothing. In a market, a wrong price is money left on the table, and someone will take it. This changes the incentive to reveal. If you privately know something the price does not reflect — that the candidate's campaign is quietly collapsing, that the drug's trial is going badly — you profit precisely by trading on it, and your trade drags the price toward the truth you hold. The market pays you to reveal what you know and pays you again to go and find out more. Truthful revelation is not requested; it is rewarded.

The third gear is arbitrage, the error-correction. Whenever the price drifts away from what the available information supports, it opens a gap between price and value, and a gap is a profit waiting to be taken. Traders competing to take it push the price back. The wrong are penalised automatically — they lose their stake to the better-informed — and over rounds of trading, capital flows from those who price badly to those who price well, so the loudest voices in the market become, on average, the ones who have been right before. In this the prediction market is Surowiecki's aggregation machine (item 20) with an incentive engine bolted on: it does not merely average dispersed judgment, it pays people to supply it and pays them to correct it.

The anchor example

Return to Iowa, and make it concrete. In the summer of a presidential year the Iowa Electronic Markets opens a vote-share market: traders buy and sell two contracts, one for each major-party candidate, each paying out at the end in proportion to that candidate's share of the two-party popular vote. If the Democratic contract trades at fifty-two cents, the market is saying the Democrat will take about fifty-two percent of the two-party vote. As the campaign runs, the price twitches with every debate, gaffe, and jobs report — a debate collapse sends buyers fleeing one contract and the price sliding within the hour, long before any poll can be fielded and reported.

The traders are not a representative sample of the electorate; they skew young, male, educated, and are disproportionately from Iowa. This ought to be fatal to a poll, and it is why the market is not one. A poll needs its respondents to be the electorate in miniature. A market needs only that some of its traders know something and are willing to bet it, because their bets move the price and the unrepresentative rest cannot hold it wrong for long — an overconfident partisan who bids the price above what the evidence supports is simply handing money to whoever sells to him. Across a run of presidential elections the Iowa markets' final prices tracked the actual vote share with an accuracy that matched or beat the contemporaneous polls, and did it earlier, without the machinery of sampling. A few hundred people betting pocket money out-forecast the pollsters — not because they were wiser individuals, but because the institution around them squeezed their scattered knowledge into a price and threw away the noise.

The flip

Here is the move that makes this chapter belong to the wise-crowd movement rather than the mob one: a prediction market is the wise crowd monetised, and the monetising is what enforces the crucial condition. The crowd is wise, this book keeps insisting, when its members judge independently, so that their errors point in random directions and cancel (item 21) rather than lining up and stacking. The trouble is that independence is unnatural — people would rather copy — and most of Book Two is the story of what happens when they do. The prediction market's quiet genius is that it makes independence pay. If you copy another trader and he is wrong, you lose with him; the only reliable way to make money is to know something the price does not already contain — to be independently, privately right. The institution is engineered so that the route to profit runs through exactly the behaviour that keeps the crowd's errors uncorrelated. This is Delphi's ambition (item 22, the non-market cousin, which buys independence with anonymity and structure) achieved instead with cash: two designs for the same scarce thing, keeping judgments apart so aggregation can work.

And here is the hinge, the same one every chapter in this movement turns on: the identical mechanism, under a changed condition, becomes its own dark twin. Everything above assumes traders are pricing the information — the event's real odds. But there is a second thing a trader can price: not the event, but the other traders. Keynes described it in 1936 as a newspaper beauty contest (item 61) in which you win by guessing not which face you find prettiest but which face the average entrant will guess the average entrant finds prettiest — and so on, up a ladder of second-guessing that has left the actual faces behind entirely. When traders start pricing each other rather than the world — buying because the price is rising and others are buying, selling because others sell — the market stops tracking truth and starts tracking what everyone thinks everyone thinks. That is a bubble (item 64). The barometer that read the future now reads only the room. It is the same institution, the same order book, the same profit motive; the sign has flipped because the traders are aggregating one another's guesses instead of independent facts, and the errors that were supposed to cancel are now feeding on themselves. The wise crowd and the mob are, once again, the same people at the same terminals — wisdom or mania set by whether they are pricing information or pricing each other. This is the fault line the efficient-market hypothesis and behavioural finance fight across (item 60), and the whole quarrel is a quarrel about which of these two modes a real market is in.

The human weight

A price that reads the crowd's private knowledge is a machine for surfacing truths that institutions would rather keep buried, and that is why prediction markets are politically fraught wherever they touch power. A company that runs an internal market on whether a project will ship by its deadline is building an instrument that can contradict the executive who promised the board it would. The market aggregates what the engineers quietly know and management does not want said aloud, and prints it as a number no one can deny authoring, because no one did. This is the same reason such markets get quietly killed: they reveal what the hierarchy is organised to conceal.

The starkest case is the one that became a scandal. In 2003 a Pentagon research arm, DARPA, proposed a Policy Analysis Market — part of a programme nicknamed FutureMAP — to trade contracts on political and economic developments in the Middle East, on the theory that a market might forecast instability better than analysts. When it emerged that this could amount to letting people bet on assassinations and terrorist attacks, the reaction was immediate and bipartisan; senators denounced it as grotesque, and the programme was cancelled within days. (The lurid "terrorism futures" framing outran the actual proposal, whose scope is worth checking against primary sources before repeating.) The episode holds the whole moral tension in one place: there is something truly queasy about a market where a death makes someone money, and there is also real forecasting value in the very instrument that queasiness kills. We recoil from betting on catastrophe, and the recoil forecloses a tool that might have seen catastrophe coming.

Yet the same anonymity that makes the price feel cold is also what lets it speak. This series keeps returning to preference falsification (item 34) — the way people publicly affirm what they privately reject, and how an order of imposed silence is held in place by everyone misreading everyone else's compliance for belief. An anonymous price is a designed antidote to exactly that. It cannot be socially punished, because it has no face; it says what the traders will not say aloud, because saying it costs a stake rather than a reputation. In principle a market can register a collapse of confidence that no one dares voice — the number moves before the silence breaks. That is a sober, real promise, and it is not the same as saying markets are wise; it is saying that a well-made price can sometimes hear what a room has agreed not to say.

The critique

The evidence that prediction markets beat other methods is real, and it is oversold. It holds best where the field is thick with traders, the event is well-defined, and the outcome will be settled unambiguously and soon — elections, sports, box-office receipts. It thins out fast everywhere else, and the failures are structural, not incidental.

Markets can be thin. A contract that only a handful of people trade has a price set by that handful, and a thin market is a noisy, easily-distorted one — closer to a rumour than a barometer. Thin markets are also manipulable: a deep-pocketed actor can move the price on purpose, buying not to profit but to plant a signal, and while arbitrageurs are supposed to correct him, correction requires that enough informed traders have enough capital and nerve to take the other side, which in a thin or politically charged market they often do not. Real markets also show a persistent favourite-longshot bias — long shots trade dearer than their true odds and favourites cheaper — so the prices are not the clean probabilities the theory promises.

Worse, prediction markets tend to fail exactly where they would be most useful. The rare, the ambiguous, the far-off, and the self-referential — events with no clean settlement, no deep pool of informed traders, or whose forecast changes the outcome — are precisely the cases the mechanism handles worst, and precisely the cases we most want forecast. And the efficient-market ideal that underwrites the whole enterprise is contradicted by the market's own dark twin: real markets blow bubbles and crash (items 60, 64), proof from the inside that a price does not always track truth. Then there are the walls around the enterprise — gambling law, which makes most real-money markets illegal or barely tolerated, and the moral hazard of a contract that pays out on someone's ruin. The prediction market is a beautiful device for turning scattered private knowledge into a public number. It is also thin where it should be deep, manipulable where the stakes are highest, and blind in exactly the fog it was built to see through — a barometer that works best on the weather you could have guessed anyway.

Confirmed sources

  • Hayek, Friedrich A. "The Use of Knowledge in Society." American Economic Review, vol. 35, no. 4, 1945, pp. 519–530. — The foundational argument that dispersed private knowledge is aggregated by the price system; the intellectual seed of prediction markets and the source of the "communicating information" framing.
  • Forsythe, Robert, Forrest Nelson, George R. Neumann, and Jack Wright. "Anatomy of an Experimental Political Stock Market." American Economic Review, vol. 82, no. 5, 1992, pp. 1142–1161. — First formal report of the Iowa Electronic Markets (launched 1988); documents that market prices often forecast election outcomes more accurately than polls, and analyses trader behaviour and the marginal-trader mechanism.
  • Wolfers, Justin, and Eric Zitzewitz. "Prediction Markets." Journal of Economic Perspectives, vol. 18, no. 2, 2004, pp. 107–126. — Survey defining the field: how contract prices map to probabilities, evidence on accuracy across domains, favourite-longshot bias, manipulation, thinness, and the DARPA episode.
  • Hanson, Robin. "Could Gambling Save Science? Encouraging an Honest Consensus." Social Epistemology, vol. 9, no. 1, 1995, pp. 3–33. — Hanson's "idea futures" proposal; his later work develops decision/decision-conditional markets. Supports the origin beat's account of Hanson.
  • Keynes, John Maynard. The General Theory of Employment, Interest and Money, Macmillan, 1936, ch. 12. — The "beauty contest" analogy for markets pricing other participants' expectations rather than fundamentals; underpins beat 5's account of the bubble mode (cross-ref item 61).
