Chapter 54 · The Shape Flows

Simple versus complex contagion

Some things you catch from a single touch; others you adopt only after several people you trust have shown you it is safe — and the same network wiring that speeds the first can strangle the second.

The idea

Not everything that spreads spreads the same way. A virus needs one contact. So does a rumour, a joke, a piece of breaking news, a catchy tune: a single exposure to a single carrier can be enough to make you a carrier too. Epidemiologists call this simple contagion. One infectious tie, one transmission, and the thing is in you and moving on. The mathematics of it is the mathematics of a wildfire — every spark can start a blaze.

But a great many of the things that actually matter for how societies change do not behave like sparks. Consider adopting a costly new farming technique, joining a strike, wearing an unfamiliar garment in public, quitting a religion, coming out, betting your savings on an untested tool, or standing up in a meeting to say the thing no one has said. Hearing about any of these once, from one person, rarely moves you. You wait. You watch. You want to see whether the second person does it, and the third — whether the people whose judgment you rely on have already crossed the line and come out unharmed. Only when enough independent confirmations accumulate do you move. This is complex contagion: adoption that requires not one exposure but several, from several distinct sources, before the threshold is crossed.

The distinction sounds modest. Its consequences are not. Because the structure of a network that best spreads a simple contagion is close to the opposite of the structure that best spreads a complex one — and once you see that, a whole optimistic story about how good ideas travel through a connected world has to be told again, more carefully.

The origin

The formal split belongs to Damon Centola and Michael Macy, in a 2007 paper in the American Journal of Sociology with a title that was itself an act of provocation: "Complex Contagions and the Weakness of Long Ties." The word weakness is the tell. Mark Granovetter's celebrated 1973 argument had been about the strength of weak ties (item 45): the loose acquaintances who bridge otherwise separate social worlds are precisely the ones who carry novel information across the gaps, because your close friends all already know what you know. Duncan Watts and Steven Strogatz had shown in 1998 that a handful of such long-range shortcuts turn a clustered, provincial network into a small world (item 44), collapsing the distance between any two people. The combined lesson had hardened into common sense: long, weak, bridging ties are what make things spread far and fast.

Centola and Macy asked what happens to that lesson when adoption requires more than one exposure. They ran the models. What they found was that the celebrated long ties, so powerful for simple contagions, become almost useless — sometimes worse than useless — for complex ones. A single distant contact reaching across a shortcut delivers exactly one signal, and one signal is not enough to move someone who needs several. The shortcut fires once and fizzles.

Centola then took the claim out of the simulation and into a controlled experiment, reported in Science in 2010: "The Spread of Behavior in an Online Social Network Experiment." He built artificial online health communities, assigned participants to structurally different networks, and watched a health behaviour diffuse. He gathered the whole argument, with a decade of further work, into How Behavior Spreads: The Science of Complex Contagions (Princeton, 2018). The intellectual debt runs back to Granovetter's own 1978 threshold models of collective behaviour (item 7), in which each person carries a private number — how many others must act before they will — and the fate of the crowd depends on the whole distribution of those numbers.

The mechanism

Start with the threshold. In a simple contagion, the adoption threshold is one: a single active neighbour suffices. In a complex contagion, the threshold is greater than one — you require two, three, four independent confirmations before you move. That single change in a single parameter rewires everything downstream, because it changes what a network tie is worth.

For a simple contagion, every tie is a full channel. Reach one infected person through it and you are infected. Long-range shortcuts are therefore pure gain: each one lets the contagion leap across the network in a single bound, which is exactly why small-world wiring accelerates simple spread.

For a complex contagion, a single tie is worth almost nothing on its own, because a single confirmation cannot clear a threshold above one. What you need is social reinforcement: multiple active neighbours confirming the same behaviour at the same time. And here geometry turns against the shortcut. Picture a bridge between two clusters. If the bridge is narrow — one long tie connecting them — then a person on the far side receives at most one signal across it, never enough. The complex contagion arrives at the bridgehead and stops. What carries a complex contagion across is a wide bridge: several ties running in parallel between the two neighbourhoods, so that someone on the far side is touched by two, three, four adopters at once and finally crosses their threshold.

Wide bridges live in dense, clustered, redundant regions of a network — exactly the tightly-knit local pockets that small-world theory treated as inefficient, provincial, and slow. Your close-knit cluster, where all your friends know each other, is redundant by design: their ties overlap, they see the same things, they reinforce one another. For a simple contagion that redundancy is waste — the same news arriving three times tells you nothing new. For a complex contagion that redundancy is the whole engine, because the point is not novelty of information but weight of confirmation. Reinforcement, legitimation, the felt reduction of personal risk when you can see that others like you have already leapt: these need repetition, and repetition needs density.

So the two contagions want opposite worlds. Simple contagion is carried farthest by long, thin, novel connections. Complex contagion is carried farthest by short, thick, overlapping ones. The very clustering that slows a rumour speeds a revolution.

The anchor example

The cleanest demonstration is Centola's 2010 experiment, precisely because it was engineered to isolate the effect. He recruited participants into an online health-community website and, crucially, controlled the network they inhabited. Some were placed in a clustered-lattice network: many redundant local ties, everyone embedded in a tight neighbourhood, no long shortcuts. Others were placed in a random network with the same number of ties but rewired to include long-range connections — the small-world topology that classical diffusion theory says should spread things farther and faster. Then he introduced a health behaviour: registering for a health-forum feature. Each time one of your network neighbours adopted, you received a signal. The question was where the behaviour would travel farther.

Naive diffusion theory has a confident prediction: the random network with its shortcuts should win, because shortcuts are what carry things across a network quickly. The result was the reverse. The behaviour spread farther and faster in the clustered-lattice network than in the random one with long ties. In the clustered condition, adopting neighbours arrived in reinforcing clumps; a person on the frontier of the behaviour tended to receive several signals from several sources, and that redundancy pushed people over the threshold. In the random network, the same signals were scattered thin across long ties, each one isolated, each one insufficient — so the behaviour repeatedly reached fresh people who received a single ping and did nothing with it. The shortcuts that would have raced a rumour across the graph left the behaviour stranded, because a health decision is not a rumour. It is a complex contagion, and complex contagions need company.

Sociology had already sensed this in the field. Doug McAdam's 1986 study of the 1964 Freedom Summer campaign — young volunteers going to register Black voters in Mississippi, at real risk of beating and murder — found that what separated those who actually went from those who signed up and withdrew was not attitude or ideology, which both groups shared, but embeddedness: the ones who went were bound into dense webs of strong ties to others who were also going. High-risk activism, McAdam showed, travels through thick relational tissue, not thin. The people who leapt did so because the people around them were leaping too.

The flip

This chapter resolves a paradox sitting at the centre of Book Three, and the resolution is the book's argument in miniature. Book Three has spent its chapters celebrating connection — the strength of weak ties (item 45) and the small-world shortcuts (item 44) that let information, and the wisdom it feeds, race across a connected world. But a suspicion has been building: if shortcuts make everything spread, why do so many good, costly, cooperative behaviours spread so slowly, stalling in local pockets while trivia circles the globe overnight?

Complex contagion is the answer, and it is a genuine flip. It tells you when the wise-crowd-enabling weak ties help and when they hurt. Simple things — facts, rumours, viruses, prices, memes — ride the shortcuts, and for them the connected world does exactly what the optimists promised. But the behaviours that matter most for collective change are almost never simple. Adopting a costly cooperative norm, joining a risky movement, abandoning a public consensus you have privately stopped believing — these are complex contagions, and for them the shortcuts are impotent. They need dense local reinforcement. The same network wiring is a highway for the cheap and a wall for the consequential.

This refines the darkest machinery in the series. Preference falsification (item 34) holds an unjust order in place because everyone privately dissents while publicly conforming, each waiting for a sign that others feel the same. A simple-contagion model of dissent would say: one visible defector, glimpsed even across a long tie, should be enough to start the collapse. Complex contagion says no. Dissent is the most complex contagion there is — the most costly, the most risky, the most in need of confirmation that you will not be the only one left exposed. Breaking the silence therefore requires not one distant dissenter but a visible cluster of them, close enough and numerous enough to cross each onlooker's reinforcement threshold. This is also the refinement the first-follower story (item 58) needs: the lone nut and his single imitator make a charming parable, but for anything truly dangerous you do not need one first follower, you need several, reinforcing each other into a critical mass before the third and fourth will move. It is Granovetter's thresholds (item 7) again — individual thresholds greater than one — and it is why social proof (item 39) works by accumulation, not by single instance. The wise crowd and the mob remain the same crowd under different conditions; complex contagion tells you that even the good conditions travel only where the ties are thick.

The human weight

The most useful place this idea reaches is the frontier this series keeps circling: how orders of imposed silence sustain themselves, and how they break. A simple-contagion picture of liberation is seductive and wrong. It says a single brave voice, amplified across the network's long ties — one dissident, one viral post, one distant example — should be enough to topple a false consensus, because courage, like a virus, needs only one carrier to jump. History is littered with the corpses of lone voices that changed nothing.

Complex contagion explains why. Under preference falsification (item 34), each person's threshold for going public with their true belief is high, because the cost of being the isolated dissenter is catastrophic. A single distant example does not clear that threshold; it is one signal where several are needed. What breaks the silence is a dense enough local cluster of visible dissent — enough people, close enough to see, defecting at once, that each onlooker receives the multiple reinforcing confirmations that finally make it safe to move. This is why movements need tight-knit cores and physical co-presence: the square, the campus, the congregation, the factory floor, the shared prison cell. Bodies in a shared space manufacture exactly the redundant, overlapping, reinforcing exposure that a complex contagion requires — you do not merely hear that others dissent, you see them, repeatedly, all around you, and the threshold falls. Tahrir Square, Leipzig's Monday marches, Solidarity's shipyard: the geography is not incidental. Co-presence is the wide bridge.

It also explains the durability of oppression as a matter of network engineering rather than belief. To keep a population quiescent you do not need to change minds; you need only sever dense ties, so that no reinforcing cluster can ever form. Atomise people — scatter them, forbid their assembly, saturate them with surveillance so that no one dares be the second signal to anyone — and dissent becomes a purely simple contagion that no single tie can carry over the threshold. Everyone may privately object; no one receives enough confirmation to move. This connects directly to stratification (item 85): a caste or hierarchy is held not only by force and by falsified preference but by the deliberate thinning of the very connective tissue through which a countervailing complex contagion would have to travel. Break the density and you need not break the will. The order reproduces itself in the silence between severed ties.

The critique

The simple/complex distinction is powerful, but honesty requires marking where it strains. The first problem is classification in advance: it is often truly hard to say whether a given behaviour is simple or complex before you watch it spread. Is adopting a smartphone simple or complex? A hashtag? A vaccine? The answer varies by person, cost, moment, and culture, and the theory can slide toward being unfalsifiable — labelling anything that spread through clusters "complex" and anything that spread through shortcuts "simple" after the fact. The threshold that defines the whole apparatus is, as with all threshold models, hard to measure independently of the outcome it is meant to predict — the same critique that shadows Granovetter's thresholds (item 7). Where do the numbers come from, other than the behaviour they are supposed to explain?

Second, the universality of the effect has been challenged empirically. Some studies of real large-scale networks — online platforms, adoption data — find complex-contagion signatures; others find that long ties do carry many behaviours perfectly well, and that plenty of consequential adoption looks stubbornly simple. Real diffusion very often mixes both modes at once: the awareness that a thing exists spreads simply and fast across the shortcuts, while the decision to adopt it spreads complexly and slowly through the clusters, and disentangling the two in field data is hard. A clean dichotomy may be doing the work of a messy continuum.

Third, the sharpest evidence — Centola's 2010 result — comes from online experiments in artificial communities built for the purpose. That is their strength for causal inference and their weakness for generalisation. Whether behaviour in an engineered health-forum network reproduces the dynamics of a real village, a real workplace, or a real uprising is exactly the question the experiment cannot settle, and the leap from lattice to life should be made with care. Further reading to verify before final publication: the precise magnitudes and network specifications of Centola's 2010 conditions, and the current state of the empirical debate over how widely complex-contagion dynamics generalise beyond experimental and online settings. What survives the caveats is the core reversal, and it is enough: the wiring that speeds what is cheap is not the wiring that spreads what is costly, and the difference decides which silences break.
