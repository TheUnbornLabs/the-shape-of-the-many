# References — 89. Bots, astroturfing & manufactured consensus

## Confirmed sources

- Ferrara, E., Varol, O., Davis, C., Menczer, F., & Flammini, A. "The Rise of Social Bots." *Communications of the ACM*, 59(7):96–104, 2016. — The standard survey of automated social-media accounts: how they imitate humans, why they resist detection, and how they can distort online discourse. Anchors the mechanism and origin beats.
- Woolley, S. C., & Howard, P. N. (eds.). *Computational Propaganda: Political Parties, Politicians, and Political Manipulation on Social Media*. Oxford University Press, 2018. — Documents coordinated bot and troll operations across many countries; source for the "computational propaganda" framing and the Oxford Internet Institute programme.
- Bessi, A., & Ferrara, E. "Social bots distort the 2016 U.S. Presidential election online discussion." *First Monday*, 21(11), 2016. — Establishes measurable bot presence and activity around the 2016 U.S. election; used in the critique to separate documented presence from unproven persuasive effect.
- Rauchfleisch, A., & Kaiser, J. "The False Positive Problem of Automatic Bot Detection in Social Science Research." *PLOS ONE*, 15(10):e0241045, 2020. — Demonstrates Botometer's classification errors, language/time sensitivity, and arbitrary thresholds; underpins the measurement-humility critique.

- Russian Internet Research Agency (IRA) specifics — account counts, impression/reach figures, the scope and even-handedness of its campaigns, and the U.S. indictment details (2018) — widely reported but contested in scope and effect; verify against the Mueller-related indictment documents, Senate Select Committee on Intelligence reports, and the Facebook/Twitter platform disclosures.
- The claim that IRA and similar operations reached large audiences but with unproven effect on votes/beliefs, and that much manufactured content reached already-partisan users — supported by several studies (e.g., work on Twitter exposure and the 2016 election); confirm the specific empirical papers before citing figures.
- The "liar's dividend" concept (as applied here to astroturfing/manufactured consensus) — the term originates in the deepfake/disinformation literature (Chesney & Citron, 2019); verify the citation and adapt the framing accurately if quoted.
