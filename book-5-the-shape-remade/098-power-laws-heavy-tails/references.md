# References — 98. Power laws & heavy tails

## Confirmed sources

- Zipf, George Kingsley. *Human Behavior and the Principle of Least Effort*. Addison-Wesley, 1949. — Source for Zipf's law of word frequencies and city sizes, and the rank–size rule (nth item ≈ largest / n).
- Pareto, Vilfredo. *Cours d'économie politique*. Lausanne, 1896–97. — Original observation of the power-law distribution of income and wealth; origin of the Pareto principle.
- Bak, Per; Tang, Chao; Wiesenfeld, Kurt. "Self-Organized Criticality: An Explanation of 1/f Noise." *Physical Review Letters*, vol. 59, no. 4, 1987, pp. 381–384. — Foundational paper introducing self-organised criticality; the sandpile as the canonical model.
- Gutenberg, Beno; Richter, Charles F. "Frequency of earthquakes in California." *Bulletin of the Seismological Society of America*, vol. 34, 1944, pp. 185–188. — The Gutenberg–Richter law relating earthquake magnitude to frequency (a power law).
- Newman, M. E. J. "Power laws, Pareto distributions and Zipf's law." *Contemporary Physics*, vol. 46, no. 5, 2005, pp. 323–351. — Authoritative review of power-law phenomena, generating mechanisms, and scale-invariance.
- Clauset, Aaron; Shalizi, Cosma Rohilla; Newman, M. E. J. "Power-law distributions in empirical data." *SIAM Review*, vol. 51, no. 4, 2009, pp. 661–703. — The rigorous statistical critique showing many claimed power laws fail proper testing; log-normal and other distributions often fit as well or better. Core of the critique beat.
- Simon, Herbert A. "On a class of skew distribution functions." *Biometrika*, vol. 42, no. 3/4, 1955, pp. 425–440. — The Yule–Simon process; multiplicative/preferential mechanism generating skew (power-law-like) distributions.
- Barabási, Albert-László; Albert, Réka. "Emergence of scaling in random networks." *Science*, vol. 286, no. 5439, 1999, pp. 509–512. — Preferential attachment as generator of scale-free (power-law) degree distributions; underpins cross-reference to item 46.
- Taleb, Nassim Nicholas. *The Black Swan: The Impact of the Highly Improbable*. Random House, 2007. — Popular framing of heavy-tailed extreme events and the systematic underestimation of tail risk.
