# References — 92. Superforecasting & forecasting tournaments

## Confirmed sources

- Tetlock, Philip E. *Expert Political Judgment: How Good Is It? How Can We Know?* Princeton University Press, 2005. — The two-decade study finding that average expert political forecasters barely beat chance, and that fox-like (self-critical, many-ideas) thinkers outperform hedgehog-like (one-big-idea) thinkers.
- Tetlock, Philip E., and Dan Gardner. *Superforecasting: The Art and Science of Prediction.* Crown, 2015. — The trade account of the Good Judgment Project, the IARPA tournament, the superforecasters, and the individual habits (probabilistic thinking, Fermi-ization, base rates, Bayesian updating, seeking contrary views).
- Mellers, Barbara, et al. "Psychological Strategies for Winning a Geopolitical Forecasting Tournament." *Psychological Science*, vol. 25, no. 5, 2014, pp. 1106–1115. — Underlying empirical results from the Good Judgment Project: training, teaming, tracking, and aggregation effects on forecasting accuracy. VERIFIED: Psychological Science 25(5):1106–1115, DOI 10.1177/0956797614524255.
- Mellers, Barbara, et al. "The Psychology of Intelligence Analysis: Drivers of Prediction Accuracy in World Politics." *Journal of Experimental Psychology: Applied*, vol. 21, no. 1, 2015, pp. 1–14. — Further analysis of what drove superforecaster accuracy. VERIFIED: J. Exp. Psychol. Appl. 21(1):1–14, PMID 25581088.
- Taleb, Nassim Nicholas. *The Black Swan: The Impact of the Highly Improbable.* Random House, 2007. — The critique that high-impact, unpredictable, unprecedented events dominate history and lie outside the domain of resolvable forecasting; basis for beat 7's central limit.
- Isaiah Berlin. *The Hedgehog and the Fox.* 1953. — Source of the hedgehog/fox distinction Tetlock adopted.
- IARPA Aggregative Contingent Estimation (ACE) program, 2011–2015. — The U.S. intelligence-community forecasting tournament the Good Judgment Project won.
