# 87. Recommendation algorithms as herding engines

> A recommendation is a machine whispering what everyone like you already chose — and the whisper is what makes them choose it.

## The idea

Somewhere between your last click and your next one, a system made a prediction. It guessed which of a billion possible things — a video, a song, a product, a post — would hold your attention longest, and put that thing in front of you. It did not consult your interests as you would describe them, nor anyone's judgment of what is good for you. It consulted a statistical shadow of you assembled from the behaviour of millions of others, and asked one question: of everything available, what will this person most likely engage with? Then it served the answer.

A recommendation algorithm is, at bottom, a prediction of engagement wired to an act of display. Its raw material is behaviour — what you and everyone else clicked, watched, liked, skipped, bought, lingered over. Its central trick, the one that built the field, is called collaborative filtering: it finds people whose past behaviour resembles yours and shows you what *they* went on to like. *People like you liked X.* This is a phrase you already know, because it is the phrase a friend uses to persuade you, and a crowd uses without meaning to. The algorithm industrialised it.

What makes such a system a *herding engine* rather than a neutral librarian is a loop. Your clicks are not only consumed as private preference; they are fed back as public signal. Each thing you watch nudges the system's estimate of what people-like-you want, which shapes what the next person sees, whose clicks shape the estimate again. Popular items get shown more, and being shown more makes them more popular. The recommendation is a machine-scale form of social proof — the ancient human habit of reading the crowd for what to do — engineered by a corporation and pointed, by default, not at truth or wellbeing but at whatever keeps you scrolling.

## The origin

The mechanism has a clean birth date. In 1994, a group at the University of Minnesota — Paul Resnick, Neophytos Iacovou, Mitesh Suchak, Peter Bergstrom, and John Riedl — published a paper on a system called **GroupLens**, an "open architecture for collaborative filtering of netnews." Usenet newsgroups were drowning readers in articles. GroupLens let users rate what they read, then predicted how much a given user would like an unrated article by weighting the ratings of others who had agreed with that user in the past. It was a modest idea: automate the thing we do when we ask a like-minded friend whether a film is worth our evening. It also named the future field — collaborative filtering meant filtering *by* the collaboration of a crowd's revealed judgments.

The idea then met commerce, and commerce made it enormous. In 2003, three Amazon engineers — Greg Linden, Brent Smith, and Jeremy York — described the engine behind the site's "Customers who bought this item also bought" feature in *IEEE Internet Computing*, in a paper called "Amazon.com Recommendations: Item-to-Item Collaborative Filtering." Their innovation was practical genius: instead of matching a shopper to similar shoppers, which does not scale to tens of millions of users, they matched items to items — computing offline which products tend to be bought together, then surfacing a product's statistical companions instantly. It was fast, it scaled, and it worked; Amazon later credited large fractions of sales to recommendation. The library had become a salesman.

The third and least documented origin is a turn, not a paper: the shift from filtering *for the user* to optimising *for engagement*. As feeds replaced search — YouTube's autoplay, Facebook's News Feed ranking, later TikTok's "For You" page deciding moment to moment what billions saw — the target quietly changed. The systems were increasingly trained to maximise watch time, session length, or interaction, on the reasoning that engagement is the measurable proxy for value. That substitution — engagement standing in for worth — is the hinge on which this whole chapter turns.

## The mechanism

Strip a modern recommender to its gears and you find two turning against each other.

The first gear is **predicted-engagement ranking**. From your history and the crowd's, a model estimates, for each candidate item, the probability you will engage — click, watch through, react, buy. Collaborative filtering supplies much of this: mathematically, it factors the vast, sparse table of who-liked-what into a compact map in which users and items become points, and nearness on the map predicts affinity. The model ranks the candidates by predicted engagement and shows you the top of the list. Nothing in this machinery contains a variable for "true," "kind," or "good for this person." Those quantities are not in the equations. What is in the equations is a proxy for attention.

The second gear is the **feedback loop**, and it is where a filter becomes a herding engine. Your behaviour is both output and input. You act on what the model shows you; your actions become training data that reshape the model; the reshaped model shapes what you and others see next. This is a reflexive loop of exactly the kind item 65 anatomises — the map alters the territory it maps. Two consequences follow with the reliability of arithmetic. First, positive feedback (item 5): an item shown to many is engaged by some, which raises its predicted engagement, which gets it shown to more — a rich-get-richer dynamic that is preferential attachment (item 46) enacted on content, the same scale-free concentration in which a handful of items capture most of the attention. This is the engine under virality (item 57): cumulative advantage, where early luck compounds into dominance and the winner's win is caused, in part, by its being seen to be winning. Second, homogenisation: because the model learns your engagement fastest by feeding you more of what already held you, an unconstrained loop narrows toward the self-reinforcing basins this series names echo chambers (item 50) and filter bubbles.

Crucially, the sign of all this is set by the *objective*. Optimise for a proxy like watch time, and the loop will find whatever content maximises it — which, human attention being what it is, often means the emotionally intense, the novel, the outrageous, the extreme (the link to outrage runs to item 88). The algorithm has no preference for extremity; it has a preference for engagement, and extremity is frequently the cheapest route there. Your clicks are the pheromone; the next user walks the path your attention laid down.

## The anchor example

Picture the "For You" page. You open a short-video app knowing nothing about what it will show, and it knows a great deal about you — not by name but by the geometry of your attention. It shows a clip; you watch four seconds and swipe away. That swipe is data. It shows another; you watch to the end, then again. That replay is louder data. Within a dozen videos the feed has triangulated something you may not have told a friend, because you revealed it not in words but in the involuntary economy of where your thumb paused.

Now widen the frame to the crowd. Each of your lingering seconds is a vote that raises a video's standing in the pool of people who resemble you. A clip that holds the first thousand viewers a beat longer than average gets pushed to ten thousand, whose attention, if it holds, carries it to a million. The hit is not chosen by an editor; it is *grown* by a loop in which every viewer's attention steers the next viewer's feed. This is stigmergy (item 4) in silicon: ants coordinate not by command but by depositing pheromone on paths that others reinforce; here the pheromone is a watch-time signal and the path is a recommendation. No one decided the video should be enormous. The crowd's traces, read and re-served by the machine, decided.

The contested part is the corner this can turn. Critics argue that because engagement often peaks at the emotional and extreme, an unconstrained feed tends to walk a curious viewer down a "rabbit hole" toward ever-more-intense content — one heterodox political clip, then a next recommendation a shade more heterodox, the loop discovering that outrage retains attention. The image is vivid and, in places, has real support. It is also, as the critique beat will insist, far more disputed than the retelling admits. Hold both: the mechanism that grows a harmless dance craze into a global hit is, structurally, the same one that could grow a grievance into a radicalisation — one engine, and the content it amplifies is whatever the crowd's attention rewards.

## The flip

Here is the sentence this chapter exists to write: **a recommendation algorithm is social proof (item 39) and stigmergy (item 4) rebuilt at machine scale and speed.** The trace-following that let ants find food and let a queue become a hit — the reading of others' behaviour as a signal for one's own — has been extracted from human intuition, engineered into an objective function, and run by a corporation across billions of people, faster than any word-of-mouth crowd could manage. The feed is the ancient mechanism industrialised.

And because it is the ancient mechanism, it obeys the thesis of this whole series. **The wise crowd and the mob are the same people; the sign is set by conditions.** The very same collaborative-filtering loop can be an automated Galton's ox for taste (item 17) — aggregating millions of independent judgments to surface the useful book you would never have found, the obscure song that becomes your favourite. Aggregation of preference, done well, is discovery — the wise-crowd pole. But the identical loop, pointed at pure engagement and shorn of independence, becomes a manufacturing plant for herding: homogenisation, runaway concentration, and consensus that feels organic but was compounded by the machine. The condition that flips the sign is twofold and familiar. *What does the algorithm optimise for* — truth and wellbeing, or attention for its own sake? And *what protects the independence* on which any crowd's wisdom depends — or does the loop, by showing you mainly what people-like-you already chose, destroy that independence and turn aggregation into contagion?

The cross-references write themselves. When the loop erodes independence, Condorcet's guarantee (item 18) inverts and the crowd's competence stops cancelling error and starts stacking it; the feed becomes an information cascade (item 26) with a corporate throttle, each user copying the revealed choices of those ahead. When it narrows, it builds echo chambers (item 50); when it compounds a winner, it is virality (item 57) and preferential attachment (item 46). And it recasts the two-step flow (item 55): influence that once passed through human opinion leaders now passes through an algorithm. The recommender is the new opinion leader — except it has no opinions, only an objective, and it whispers a different message to each of a billion ears.

## The human weight

This is a new kind of power over the many, and its novelty is worth naming precisely. A handful of privately owned optimisation systems now shape what billions see, believe, and buy — not by argument, not by visible command, but by the silent arrangement of what appears and what does not. Edward Bernays (item 82), the father of engineered public opinion, dreamed of an "invisible government" that could manufacture consent through mass media. He worked with newspapers and radio — one message, broadcast to all, at least legible enough to be answered. The feed is Bernays's dream surpassed and mutated. It manufactures social proof at a scale he could not imagine, but *opaquely* — its rankings are trade secrets — *personalised*, no two people seeing the same feed, so there is no shared public text to criticise — and *without visible authorship*, the content seeming to have risen on its own, which is exactly what a compounding crowd looks like from inside.

The frontier this series keeps returning to lies here, in a new and unsettling form. The perceived distribution of opinion — how common a view *appears* to be — is the raw material that pluralistic ignorance (item 42) and preference falsification (item 34) both run on. People calibrate what they will say, and even what they let themselves believe, against their read of what everyone else thinks. A feed governs that read. By amplifying a fringe view into apparent ubiquity it can manufacture a false sense of mainstream; by burying dissent beneath an engagement threshold it can perform a suppression no one experiences as censorship, because the suppressed simply never appears. Either way the machine is editing the map of what "everyone" believes — the very map against which private citizens decide whether their doubt is safe to voice. And because the editing is opaque and personalised, it is far harder to resist than a propaganda poster you can point at: mass propaganda had an author and a public; this has neither. It inherits older injustices too — a system trained on the crowd's behaviour reproduces the crowd's biases, so the loop can quietly re-lay the grooves of stratification (item 85), recommending opportunity to some and withholding it from others under the neutral-seeming cover of "relevance." The power is real, concentrated, and nearly invisible — the combination this book has taught us to fear most.

## The critique

Now the discipline. The strong version of the story just told — that algorithms *radicalise*, that the rabbit hole reliably drags ordinary users toward extremity — is deeply contested, and honesty requires saying so plainly rather than riding the vivid image because it is vivid.

Several careful studies find the algorithmic effect smaller than the popular narrative claims. Hosseinmardi and colleagues, in a 2021 *PNAS* study tracing the YouTube consumption of a large panel of users, concluded that consumption of extremist content was driven substantially by user demand and self-selection — people seeking it out, often arriving from off-platform — rather than by the recommender sweeping passive viewers down a chute. Demand, in other words, frequently precedes supply. On the polarisation question, the 2023 suite of studies on Facebook and Instagram run by Guess and colleagues with Meta's cooperation and published in *Science* — experiments that replaced the algorithmic feed with a chronological one for consenting users during the 2020 US election — found the feed's measurable effects on political attitudes and polarisation over the study window smaller than many expected; the chronological feed changed what people saw without reliably changing what they believed. Other researchers have reported similarly heterogeneous, often modest effects.

The "filter bubble" itself — Eli Pariser's 2011 coinage (item 50) — has taken comparable fire: several studies suggest that heavy internet and social-media users encounter *more* diverse sources, not fewer, than light users, complicating the tidy image of each person sealed in a personalised chamber. None of this means the concern is empty. The effects are real; they are simply heterogeneous, contingent, and hard to measure. And three deep problems keep the question open. First, the counterfactual is unclear: to know what the algorithm *did*, we must know what the user would otherwise have seen and chosen, and that ghost is rarely observable. Second, demand and supply are entangled — the loop is reflexive, so the model shapes demand even as demand shapes the model, and clean causal separation is close to impossible in the wild. Third, the data are locked: the systems are proprietary, and independent researchers work with the shadows that leak out. So the fair statement is the uncomfortable one. The mechanism is real and the power is large; the *magnitude* of its harm, especially the radicalisation harm, is not yet settled, and anyone who tells you otherwise — in either direction — is ahead of the evidence.
