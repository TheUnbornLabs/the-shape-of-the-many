# References — 87. Recommendation algorithms as herding engines

## Confirmed sources

- Resnick, P., Iacovou, N., Suchak, M., Bergstrom, P., & Riedl, J. "GroupLens: An Open Architecture for Collaborative Filtering of Netnews." *Proceedings of the 1994 ACM Conference on Computer Supported Cooperative Work (CSCW '94)*, 1994. — Foundational paper naming and demonstrating collaborative filtering; the "people who agreed with you in the past predict what you'll like" mechanism.
- Linden, G., Smith, B., & York, J. "Amazon.com Recommendations: Item-to-Item Collaborative Filtering." *IEEE Internet Computing*, vol. 7, no. 1, 2003, pp. 76–80. — Describes the item-to-item collaborative filtering behind "Customers who bought this item also bought"; the scalability innovation that made recommendation commercially central.
- Hosseinmardi, H., Ghasemian, A., Clauset, A., Mobius, M., Rothschild, D. M., & Watts, D. J. "Examining the consumption of radical content on YouTube." *Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences (PNAS)*, vol. 118, no. 32, 2021. — Large-panel study finding that consumption of radical/extremist content on YouTube is substantially driven by user demand and self-selection rather than primarily by recommendation; central to the contested-radicalisation critique.
- Guess, A. M., et al. Suite of 2023 studies on Facebook and Instagram during the 2020 US election, published in *Science* (and *Nature*) as part of the US 2020 Facebook and Instagram Election Study (in cooperation with Meta). Includes the chronological-vs-algorithmic feed experiment (Guess et al., "How do social media feed algorithms affect attitudes and behavior in an election campaign?", *Science*, 2023). — Found algorithmic-feed effects on political attitudes/polarisation over the study window smaller than widely assumed.
- Pariser, E. *The Filter Bubble: What the Internet Is Hiding from You*. Penguin Press, 2011. — Origin of the "filter bubble" concept (cross-referenced to item 50); the popular framing this chapter both uses and critiques.
- Bernays, E. *Propaganda*. Horace Liveright, 1928. — Source for the "invisible government" / manufacture-of-consent framing referenced in the human-weight beat (cross-reference item 82).
