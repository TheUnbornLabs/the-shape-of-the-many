# References — 94. Agent-based modelling

## Confirmed sources

- Schelling, Thomas C. "Dynamic Models of Segregation." *Journal of Mathematical Sociology*, vol. 1, no. 2, 1971, pp. 143–186. — The founding social agent-based model, run by hand on a checkerboard; supplies the segregation case and the sign-flip on the tolerance threshold (item 67).
- Epstein, Joshua M., and Robert Axtell. *Growing Artificial Societies: Social Science from the Bottom Up*. Brookings Institution Press / MIT Press, 1996. — Introduces Sugarscape; the anchor example (harvesting, emergent inequality, migration, trade) and the "bottom up" framing.
- Epstein, Joshua M. *Generative Social Science: Studies in Agent-Based Computational Modeling*. Princeton University Press, 2006. — Source of "generative social science" and the motto "if you didn't grow it, you didn't explain it"; supports the generativity discussion in beats 1, 4, and 7.
- Wilensky, Uri. *NetLogo*. Center for Connected Learning and Computer-Based Modeling, Northwestern University, 1999. — The modelling environment that popularised ABM; supports the tools/origin material.
- Axelrod, Robert. *The Complexity of Cooperation: Agent-Based Models of Competition and Collaboration*. Princeton University Press, 1997. — Evolutionary computer tournaments as an early ABM tradition (item 96).
- Reynolds, Craig W. "Flocks, Herds and Schools: A Distributed Behavioral Model." *Computer Graphics (SIGGRAPH '87 Proceedings)*, vol. 21, no. 4, 1987, pp. 25–34. — The boids model; three local steering rules producing flocking (item 3).
- Gardner, Martin. "Mathematical Games: The fantastic combinations of John Conway's new solitaire game 'life'." *Scientific American*, vol. 223, no. 4, October 1970, pp. 120–123. — Conway's Game of Life as a cellular-automaton precursor (item 97).
- Granovetter, Mark. "Threshold Models of Collective Behavior." *American Journal of Sociology*, vol. 83, no. 6, 1978, pp. 1420–1443. — The threshold mechanism whose small shifts flip riot vs. restraint, used in the flip beat (item 7).

## Further reading to verify before final publication

- von Neumann's cellular automata as originating in the 1940s and the specific attribution of the theory of self-reproducing automata (edited/completed by Arthur Burks, 1966) — confirm dating and phrasing before publication (item 97).
- The claim that Sugarscape wealth distributions "approach a power law" — VERIFIED as defensible with care. Epstein & Axtell's own text emphasises a strongly right-skewed, highly unequal distribution (contrasted with the initial roughly symmetric one) rather than formally proving a power law; the Pareto/power-law characterisation is standard in the downstream ABM literature (log-log fits yield power-law-like exponents). The chapter's hedged phrasing "approaches a power law" is acceptable; do not assert a clean, proven Pareto exponent as Epstein & Axtell's own claim.
- Specific use of agent-based models in COVID-19 policy (e.g., Imperial College / Ferguson et al. 2020 microsimulation) — the chapter keeps this general; confirm and cite a specific model if the example is made concrete in a later pass.
- The reproducibility/under-documentation critique of ABM — supported by the ODD protocol literature (Grimm et al., "A standard protocol for describing individual-based and agent-based models," *Ecological Modelling*, 2006, and the 2010/2020 updates); confirm citation details before publication.
