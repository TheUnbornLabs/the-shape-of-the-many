# 91. Wikipedia & open source as stigmergy

> Thousands of strangers who never speak build an encyclopedia and an operating system by leaving marks in a shared thing — the same trace-following that a feed uses to herd you, aimed instead at truth.

## The idea

Somewhere in the world, right now, a stranger is fixing a sentence in an article they did not start, about a subject they may never think about again, correcting a fact left wrong by someone they will never meet, in a document that belongs to no one. Multiply that act by hundreds of millions and you have Wikipedia: the largest reference work ever assembled, built without a commissioning editor, without assignments, without anyone deciding who writes what. Do the same for code and you have Linux, the operating system running most of the servers on the planet, written by tens of thousands of programmers most of whom were never introduced.

The puzzle these projects pose is not that volunteers work for free. It is *coordination*. A traditional encyclopedia has an editor-in-chief who parcels out topics; a software company has managers who assign tasks. Wikipedia and Linux have neither, and yet the work does not collapse into chaos or stall for want of direction. Somehow the labour of the many gets organised — gaps get filled, errors get caught, the whole thing accumulates toward quality — without a central authority telling anyone what to do next.

The answer is a mechanism this book named early: **stigmergy** (item 4). Contributors coordinate not by talking to each other but by leaving *traces* in a shared, editable medium — the article, the codebase — and those traces cue the next person's work. A red link marks a page that ought to exist and does not. A "citation needed" tag marks a claim that needs a source. A bug report marks a defect; a TODO marks unfinished business. Nobody decided who would answer these signals. The signal itself recruits its answerer. This is termites building a cathedral out of mud pellets, scaled to civilisation and pointed at knowledge.

## The origin

The idea that people could produce serious work by pooling voluntary, uncoordinated effort predates the web, but three lineages converged to make it real. The oldest is the free-software movement, launched by Richard Stallman in 1983 with the GNU Project and the insistence that software's source code should be open to read, modify, and share. The pivotal event came in 1991, when a Finnish student named Linus Torvalds posted a modest message announcing a hobby operating-system kernel and inviting others to help. Linux grew, by accretion of thousands of contributions, into infrastructure that now underpins the internet.

The movement acquired its self-understanding in 1997, when Eric Raymond delivered an essay later published as *The Cathedral and the Bazaar* (1999). Raymond contrasted two models of software creation: the cathedral, built by a small anointed team in careful isolation, and the bazaar, "a great babbling" of contributors with differing agendas, out of which — improbably — coherent, robust software emerges. His most quoted line, which he named after Torvalds, is **Linus's Law**: "given enough eyeballs, all bugs are shallow." Enough independent people looking at a problem means someone, somewhere, sees the fix. It is the many-eyes principle of collective vigilance (item 12), applied to code.

The second lineage is Wikipedia itself, launched in January 2001 by Jimmy Wales and Larry Sanger, initially as a scratchpad feeding a more traditional expert-written project called Nupedia. The scratchpad swallowed the parent. Anyone could edit; the wiki software kept every version, so nothing was ever truly lost. The third lineage is scholarly: Yochai Benkler's *The Wealth of Networks* (2006) gave the phenomenon a name — **commons-based peer production** — and an argument, that networked individuals could produce information goods outside both the market and the firm. Behind Benkler stands Elinor Ostrom's work on how communities govern shared resources without privatising them or handing them to the state (item 69) — the governance question that, it turns out, decides whether such a commons thrives or rots.

## The mechanism

Strip the mechanism to its parts and five gears are turning.

The first is the **shared editable artifact**. There is one canonical object — this article, this file — that everyone works on directly, not private copies negotiated later. The artifact *is* the medium of coordination. Its current state tells you what has been done and, by implication, what has not.

The second is the **stigmergic cue**. The artifact carries marks that function as tasks without being assigned as tasks. In Wikipedia these are explicit: the red link crying out for a page, the "citation needed" tag, the stub template, the cleanup banner, the talk-page thread flagging a dispute. In open source they are the bug tracker's open issues, the failing test, the `// TODO` in the source, the feature request. Each is a trace left by one contributor that lowers the cost of the next contributor knowing what to do. The work self-organises because the state of the work advertises its own gaps.

The third is **low-cost contribution**. Editing a wiki page or submitting a small patch is cheap and can be tiny — a single corrected date, a one-line fix. This matters because it lets contribution scale to the vast pool of people who have a little knowledge and a little time, rather than restricting it to the few with a lot of both.

The fourth is **reversibility through version control**. Every edit is recorded; any edit can be undone. Wikipedia's page history and Linux's version-control system mean that a bad contribution is not a catastrophe but a temporary state, revertible in seconds. Reversibility is what makes it safe to let strangers touch the artifact at all. The downside of an error is bounded; the upside of an improvement is kept.

The fifth, and the one the naive telling omits, is **norms and governance**. Wikipedia is not a free-for-all. It has policies — neutral point of view, verifiability, no original research — and an elaborate apparatus of administrators, dispute resolution, arbitration, and protection for pages under attack. Open-source projects have maintainers who accept or reject patches, contribution guidelines, and codes of conduct. This governance is precisely what Ostrom (item 69) identified as necessary for any durable commons: clear rules, graduated sanctions, conflict-resolution mechanisms, recognised authority to enforce them.

Put the gears together and you get the outcome: cumulative, high-quality output produced by uncoordinated contributors. And it works for exactly the reasons this book has been circling. The many eyes (item 12) catch errors no single author would. The contributors are *diverse* and relatively *independent*, so their errors and blind spots partly cancel rather than compound — the diversity-prediction effect (item 19). And the aggregation is well engineered, with governance holding the whole thing to its standards. These are Surowiecki's four conditions for a wise crowd (item 20) — diversity, independence, decentralisation, and good aggregation — not stumbled into but, in the best projects, deliberately maintained.

## The anchor example

Watch a single Wikipedia article come into being, because the mechanism is visible in a way it rarely is anywhere else.

It begins as a red link — a mention of some subject, in another article, that points to a page not yet written. The red colour is a stigmergic cue: it advertises an absence. Someone, unbidden, clicks it and writes two sentences. Now it is a stub, marked with a stub template — another cue, saying *this is not finished*. A second contributor, who has never communicated with the first, adds a paragraph but no source, so a third slaps on a "citation needed" tag. A fourth, seeing the tag, supplies the reference. A fifth reorganises the sections; a sixth fixes the grammar; a bot standardises the formatting. None of these people negotiated a division of labour. Each responded to a trace left in the shared artifact by the last. Over months the stub converges, through hundreds of small independent edits, on a coherent and reliably sourced article — the cathedral built by the bazaar, one mud pellet at a time.

Does the process actually produce quality, or only the appearance of it? The most cited early test came in 2005, when the journal *Nature* ran a comparison of science entries in Wikipedia against the *Encyclopaedia Britannica*, sending matched pairs of articles to experts to count errors. Across the forty-two article pairs that could be compared, the reviewers found 162 errors in the Wikipedia entries against 123 in Britannica's — roughly four per article versus three. The widely reported finding was that the two were closer in accuracy than almost anyone expected: Wikipedia's errors were somewhat more numerous, but the gap was narrow, a difference of about one error per article. Britannica disputed the study's methods, and the details deserve care [see flag in the critique beat]. But the direction of the result was the shock: an encyclopedia written by anonymous volunteers, coordinating only through traces in a shared document, had reached the same league as one written by paid experts and refined over two centuries. The bazaar was not merely cheaper. On the evidence, it worked.

## The flip

This is the constructive proof at the heart of Book Five, and the reason the movement does not end in the dark.

Everything catastrophic in the preceding chapters ran on the same machinery. Recommendation algorithms (item 87) are trace-followers: they watch what the many click, dwell on, and share — traces left in a shared medium — and they use those traces to cue the next person's attention, exactly as a red link cues the next editor. Online mobs (item 90) coordinate stigmergically too, each pile-on comment a trace that recruits the next, converging on a target without anyone giving the order. Wikipedia and open source use *the identical mechanism*. Stigmergy (item 4) does not know or care what it builds. Trace-following can assemble an encyclopedia or a manipulation engine, a knowledge commons or a lynch mob, with the same indifference.

So what sets the sign? Three things, and they are the whole argument of this book restated in digital terms. The first is **the optimisation target**. Wikipedia's traces are tuned toward truth and quality — verifiability, neutrality, sourcing. The feed's traces are tuned toward engagement and, behind it, profit. Point the same trace-following at "what is accurate" and you get a commons; point it at "what keeps you scrolling" and you get herding manufactured for someone else's gain. The second is **governance**. Wikipedia deliberately protects Surowiecki's conditions (item 20) — it defends the diversity and relative independence of its editors and enforces standards through Ostrom-style rules (item 69). The engagement machine deliberately *destroys* those conditions, correlating attention and collapsing independence into cascades. The third is **openness and reversibility**. Wikipedia's every edit is public and undoable; the recommender's weights are secret and its nudges leave no revertible trace.

Same mechanism, wise crowd or mob, the sign set by design and governance. That is this book's thesis wearing its most hopeful face. The digital rewiring of collective behaviour is not doomed to manufacture folly. It manufactures whatever it is built to manufacture — and it *can* be built to build a commons.

## The human weight

Wikipedia and open source are the standing answer to a question that has haunted political thought for centuries: can the many coordinate to produce something of value without either the market's price signals or the state's command? For most of the twentieth century the assumed answer was no — you got either firms or governments, capitalism or planning, and nothing durable in between. Ostrom (item 69) had already shown, studying fisheries and forests and irrigation systems, that real communities govern shared resources by their own evolved rules, refuting the fatalism of the tragedy of the commons. Wikipedia and Linux extend her finding to the knowledge commons and prove it at planetary scale: distributed, leaderless, rule-governed collective production works, and it produces goods — a free encyclopedia, free infrastructure — that neither the market nor the state was building.

And it resists capture in ways that hierarchies cannot, through three defences worth naming. Openness means the work is inspectable, so bad actors have nowhere to hide. Transparency of history means every change is auditable. And in open source there is the ultimate exit: the **fork**. If a project's governance goes bad — if a maintainer turns hostile or a corporation tries to seize control — the community can copy the entire codebase and walk away, continuing under new stewardship. The right to fork is a structural check on tyranny that no traditional institution offers its members.

But these commons are not utopias, and the honest frontier note is that they reproduce the very stratifications this series keeps tracking. Wikipedia's editor base is not a representative slice of humanity. It skews heavily male, heavily Western, heavily English-speaking — and because the many-hands mechanism means *who writes* determines *what gets written*, the encyclopedia's coverage inherits those skews. Topics that interest the editor base are deep and polished; topics outside it are thin or missing. This is an emergent stratification (item 85) in who gets to author the shared record of what is known — a silent hierarchy in the very structure of the commons, produced not by anyone's decree but by who shows up. The constructive pole is real. It is also, like every human structure in this book, marked by who is present and who is absent.

## The critique

The success is real and it is bounded, and the reverent telling idealises it in four ways.

First, **quality is uneven**. The bazaar excels where the crowd is large, engaged, and technically literate — popular subjects, science, software, current events. It thins out over obscure topics, where few eyes look, and over contested ones, where eyes look but cannot agree, producing edit wars rather than convergence. The 2005 *Nature* study covered *science* entries specifically, and its scope, methods, and error counts were contested by Britannica and deserve the flag: the details are widely cited but not settled, and no single study establishes Wikipedia's general reliability [Further reading to verify before final publication].

Second, the many are governed by the few. Participation follows a brutal power law (item 98): the oft-quoted **90-9-1 rule** holds that roughly ninety per cent of users only read, nine per cent edit occasionally, and one per cent produce the bulk of the work — though the exact figures vary by study and platform and should be treated as illustrative, not exact [Further reading to verify before final publication]. That tiny core of power-users, plus the administrators drawn from it, wields disproportionate control, a pattern that shades toward Robert Michels's "iron law of oligarchy" — even radically open organisations tend to develop entrenched elites. The mechanism is preferential attachment (item 46): those already central accrue more influence, and the commons stratifies into a governing minority. Joseph Reagle's study of Wikipedia's culture documents both the elaborate governance that makes the project work and the frictions and exclusions that governance produces.

Third, the defences are constant labour, not automatic virtues. Vandalism, spam, edit wars, and coordinated manipulation campaigns are a permanent siege; the "many eyes" that catch a defaced page catch obvious errors far better than subtle or systemic ones. A crude vandalism is reverted in seconds; a plausible falsehood, or a bias shared by the whole editor base, can persist for years precisely because it does not look like an error to the eyes that are looking.

Fourth, open source has its own decay. Much critical global infrastructure rests on tiny numbers of unpaid maintainers, and maintainer burnout and under-resourcing are chronic — a structural fragility dramatised whenever a security flaw is found lurking in some ubiquitous, barely-maintained library that everyone depends on and almost no one funds.

None of this negates the proof. It sharpens it. Wikipedia and open source are not the utopia the boosters describe; they are a real, imperfect, hard-won demonstration that the constructive pole exists and is buildable — that stigmergy, many-eyes, and the wise-crowd conditions, deliberately combined with governance, can produce collective intelligence rather than collective folly. The mechanism is the same one that builds the manipulation engines. The difference is entirely in what we choose to build with it, and whether we govern it well enough to keep it honest.
