# References — 97. Cellular automata

## Confirmed sources

- von Neumann, John (edited and completed by Arthur W. Burks). *Theory of Self-Reproducing Automata*. University of Illinois Press, 1966. — The foundational cellular-automaton work; von Neumann's self-reproducing automaton, developed with Stanislaw Ulam's grid suggestion, completed posthumously by Burks (von Neumann died 1957). Supports the origin beat.
- Gardner, Martin. "Mathematical Games: The fantastic combinations of John Conway's new solitaire game 'life'." *Scientific American*, vol. 223, no. 4 (October 1970), pp. 120–123. — The column that introduced Conway's Game of Life to a mass audience; supports the 1970 date, the rules, and the popularisation via Gardner.
- Berlekamp, Elwyn R., John H. Conway, and Richard K. Guy. *Winning Ways for Your Mathematical Plays*. Academic Press, 1982 (2nd edition, A K Peters, 2001–2004). — Sets out the Game of Life including the Turing-completeness argument and constructions such as the glider gun. Supports the anchor example and the universality claim for Life.
- Wolfram, Stephen. *A New Kind of Science*. Wolfram Media, 2002. — The extended study of elementary (one-dimensional) cellular automata; the four classes of behaviour, Rule 30 as a randomness source, Rule 110, and the concept of computational irreducibility. Supports the origin, flip, human-weight, and critique beats.
- Cook, Matthew. "Universality in Elementary Cellular Automata." *Complex Systems*, vol. 15, no. 1 (2004), pp. 1–40. — The published proof that Rule 110 is Turing-complete. Supports the flip beat's universality claim for Rule 110 and the critique's note on authorship.

## Confirmed factual details

- von Neumann's self-reproducing automaton used **twenty-nine states** per cell — confirmed (two-dimensional, five-neighbour, 29-state cellular automaton; Burks 1966).
- Game of Life **glider**: period 4, moving one cell diagonally every four steps (speed c/4) — confirmed. **Gosper glider gun**: period 30, emitting a new glider every thirty generations — confirmed (Gosper, November 1970).
- Matthew Cook's Rule 110 universality proof — proves a 1985 Wolfram conjecture; circulated in the 1990s, formally published as "Universality in Elementary Cellular Automata," *Complex Systems* 15(1):1–40, 2004 — confirmed.
