# References — 88. Outrage & virality on platforms

## Confirmed sources

- Brady, William J.; Wills, Julian A.; Jost, John T.; Tucker, Joshua A.; Van Bavel, Jay J. "Emotion shapes the diffusion of moralized content in social networks." *Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences* 114(28): 7313–7318, 2017. — The anchor empirical finding: each additional moral-emotional word in a tweet was associated with roughly a 20% increase in diffusion (retweet rate), with the effect stronger within ideological in-groups than across them.
- Crockett, Molly J. "Moral outrage in the digital age." *Nature Human Behaviour* 1: 769–771, 2017. — Supplies the mechanism: digital platforms lower the costs and raise/accelerate the rewards of expressing moral outrage, altering its social dynamics.
- Rathje, Steve; Van Bavel, Jay J.; van der Linden, Sander. "Out-group animosity drives engagement on social media." *Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences* 118(26): e2024292118, 2021. — Out-group animosity was the strongest single predictor of social-media virality; each out-group word was associated with roughly a 67% increase in the odds of a share, exceeding in-group language and positive/negative affect generally.
- Vosoughi, Soroush; Roy, Deb; Aral, Sinan. "The spread of true and false news online." *Science* 359(6380): 1146–1151, 2018. — False news spread significantly farther, faster, and more broadly than the truth on Twitter, especially for false political news; novelty and emotional response are proposed contributors. Supports the "blind to truth" claim in the mechanism beat.

## Further reading to verify before final publication

- The specific claim that named real-world accountability movements "rode exactly this dynamic" is offered in general terms only; before final publication, either cite a specific empirical study of a named movement's diffusion or keep the phrasing generic. — Verify with a concrete diffusion study before naming any case.
- Effect-size figures (~20% per moral-emotional word; ~67% per out-group word) — **confirmed** as the headline associations reported in Brady et al. 2017 (PNAS 114:7313–7318; each additional moral-emotional word ≈ 20% rise in retweet rate) and Rathje et al. 2021 (PNAS 118:e2024292118; each out-group word ≈ 67% increase in odds of a share). The chapter reports both as approximate averages over the specific datasets, which is accurate; the precise per-model coefficients and confidence intervals should still be quoted from the published tables if used as exact numbers rather than round figures.
- The negativity-bias claim (threat/anger holds attention longer than reassurance) is well-supported in psychology generally but is stated here without a specific citation; add a canonical negativity-bias reference (e.g., Rozin & Royzman, or Baumeister et al. 2001) before publication.
- Replication-scrutiny note: Brady et al.'s moral-contagion finding has been the subject of reanalysis and follow-up work in the years since 2017. Confirm the current state of that replication/reanalysis literature and cite the specific critique(s) and response(s) before finalising the critique beat.
- The "platforms reveal vs. manufacture outrage" framing summarises an ongoing scholarly debate; identify and cite representative sources on both sides before publication rather than presenting it as settled either way.
