# 88. Outrage & virality on platforms

> A medium that sells attention will breed the emotion that seizes it — and the emotion that seizes it fastest is anger at the people we already hate.

## The idea

Every medium selects. A printing press favours the pamphlet over the whispered rumour; a telegraph favours the terse over the ornate; a photograph favours the arresting over the dull. The selection is rarely a decision anyone makes. It falls out of what the medium rewards and what it costs, and over time the reward and the cost together decide which messages live and which die. The medium does not care whether the surviving messages are true, or fair, or good for the people passing them along. It cares only about fit.

Social platforms are media with an unusually explicit reward function. They are engineered to maximise engagement — the clicks, shares, comments, and dwell-time that keep a user scrolling and keep the advertising machine fed. And there is now a body of evidence that a particular kind of content fits this reward function better than almost anything else: content that is morally charged and emotionally hot, and above all content that expresses contempt for a political out-group. Post something that names a shared enemy in the language of moral indignation, and the machinery of virality lifts it, boosts it, carries it further than a calm true statement of equal importance would ever travel.

So the claim of this chapter is narrow and specific. It is not that people online are worse than people off it. It is that the medium, by optimising for engagement, imposes a fitness function on the ideas competing to spread — and that fitness function selects for outrage. What survives and multiplies on a platform is not what is most accurate or most useful but what most reliably provokes moral-emotional arousal. The outrage is not a bug the engineers failed to catch. It is the shape the medium was always going to produce once you told it to maximise attention.

## The origin

The empirical spine of this argument was laid down in a compact run of studies beginning in 2017. That year, William Brady, Julian Wills, John Jost, Joshua Tucker, and Jay Van Bavel published "Emotion shapes the diffusion of moralized content in social networks" in the *Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences*. They gathered roughly half a million tweets on three divisive subjects — gun control, same-sex marriage, climate change — and counted, word by word, the moral-emotional language each contained. The finding was clean enough to remember: each additional moral-emotional word in a tweet was associated with a rise of around twenty per cent in its rate of retweeting. The effect held within ideological groups more than across them; moralised outrage travelled through the like-minded, not between them.

The same year, in *Nature Human Behaviour*, the neuroscientist Molly Crockett published "Moral outrage in the digital age" — an essay that supplied the mechanism to Brady's correlation. Outrage, Crockett argued, is an ancient social emotion with real functions: it punishes cheats, signals values, binds groups. But its costs and rewards have always regulated how much of it we express. Digital platforms, she proposed, systematically lower the costs of expressing outrage — you can denounce a stranger from your couch, at no physical risk — while raising and speeding the rewards, delivering likes and shares as near-instant social approval. Change the incentive structure of an emotion and you change how much of it a society produces.

Then, in 2021, Steve Rathje, Jay Van Bavel, and Sander van der Linden closed the loop in another *PNAS* paper. Studying millions of posts from politicians and news outlets on Facebook and Twitter, they asked what best predicted a post's spread. The strongest single predictor was not positive emotion, not negative emotion in general, but *out-group animosity* — language attacking the political opposition. Each word about the out-group raised the odds of a share by around sixty-seven per cent. The medium's favourite food, it turned out, was our contempt for the other side.

## The mechanism

The gears mesh in a particular order, and each is doing real work.

Start with the platform's objective. An engagement-optimising system does not know or care what moral-emotional content is; it observes only that certain posts generate more of the behaviours it counts, and it shows those posts to more people. This is the recommendation engine of item 87, doing exactly what it was built to do.

Feed into that objective a fact about human attention: we are not symmetric. Threat, anger, and moral violation seize us harder and hold us longer than reassurance or agreement — a negativity bias deep enough to look like wiring. Outraged content is, for a human nervous system, unusually sticky. It gets the clicks and the comments because we cannot look away from it, and because moral indignation demands a response in a way that calm rarely does.

Now add the low cost of expression that Crockett named. To voice outrage in person is to risk the face of the person you denounce, the disapproval of bystanders, the possibility of being wrong in front of witnesses. Online, most of those brakes are gone. The target is abstract, the bystanders are allies, and the cost of a hasty denunciation is near zero.

Finally, add the social reward: the like, the share, the follower, arriving fast and quantified. A denunciation that lands well is repaid in visible approval, and the approval is a training signal. This is where the loop closes. The platform amplifies outrage because outrage engages; users, watching which of their posts are rewarded, learn to produce more outrage; the increased supply gives the platform more outrage to amplify. It is a feedback loop of the kind item 5 describes, and like all such loops it does not need a villain. It needs only a metric and a nervous system, iterated at scale.

Two consequences follow that the participants rarely intend. The loop is blind to truth: nothing in it checks whether the moral violation being denounced actually occurred, and there is evidence that falsehood, being freer to be novel and provocative, travels faster than truth on these systems (Vosoughi, Roy, and Aral, 2018). And the loop manufactures a sense of pervasive conflict, because the posts that win the competition to be seen are disproportionately the angriest ones about the enemy — so the feed comes to look like a war even when most people, most of the time, are not fighting. It is emotional contagion (item 33) and the moral panic (item 31) running at machine speed, on rails.

## The anchor example

Hold the Rathje finding in your hand, because it is the sharpest single fact in this literature and the easiest to feel.

Two American political operatives, one on the left and one on the right, each sit down to write a post. Each has the same underlying claim to make about a policy. The first writes it plainly: here is what we believe, here is why, here is the evidence. The second writes it as an attack: here is what *they* — the other party, the enemy, the people you already distrust — are trying to do to you. On the platform's ledger, these are not close. Rathje and colleagues found that each additional word referring to the political out-group was associated with roughly a sixty-seven per cent increase in the odds that a post would be shared. Words about the *in-group* — praise for one's own side — did far less. Positive emotion did little. It was animosity toward the opposition, specifically, that the machine rewarded.

Read that as a selection pressure and its meaning lands. A person running a political account, watching their own analytics, does not need a theory of virality. They need only to notice which posts spread. The ones that spread are the ones about the enemy, written hot. Over months, the rational communicator — the one trying to reach the most people — is *trained by the feedback* to write more about the enemy and less about their own program, more in the register of contempt and less in the register of persuasion. Nobody had to intend a coarsening of political speech. The medium selected for it, one analytics dashboard at a time, and the people producing the content were merely reading the signal the medium sent back.

## The flip

Here is the move this book keeps making, and this is the chapter where it turns on a single hinge. Outrage virality is memetic selection — item 56 — with a fitness function set by engagement metrics. Ideas compete to be transmitted; the platform's reward function decides which traits win; and moral-emotional arousal, especially out-group animosity, is the winning trait. What spreads is not what is true or good but what is *shareable*, and outrage is supremely shareable. The medium is blind to the sign of the thing it amplifies. It will carry a justified alarm and a manufactured panic with the same indifferent efficiency.

And that indifference is precisely where the thesis of this series reappears: **the wise crowd and the mob are the same people, and the condition that flips the sign is not the mechanism but what the mechanism is pointed at.** The very amplification that manufactures perpetual tribal conflict can, under a different condition, mobilise attention against a real wrong at a speed no prior medium allowed. Moral-emotional contagion is the accelerant of accountability: it can take a genuine injustice — a captured abuse, a documented lie, a wrong that would once have died in silence — and carry it past the gatekeepers who used to bury such things, gathering the first followers (item 58) who turn one voice into a movement. Some of the era's real reckonings against powerful wrongdoing rode exactly this dynamic, and rode it because outrage spreads.

That is the constructive pole. The destructive pole is the same machinery with the target changed: the pile-on aimed at a private person over a misread sentence, the manufactured panic over a threat that does not exist, the false consensus in which a loud minority looks like a majority. It is the online mob of item 90 — stigmergy destroying rather than building. The dial that sets which pole you get is not the platform and not the emotion. It is whether the outrage tracks a real wrong or a misdirected, manufactured one. And the terrible feature of an engagement-optimised medium is that it cannot tell the difference, and has no incentive to try. It is the information and availability cascades of items 26 and 27, given a throttle marked *shares* and a foot that never lifts.

## The human weight

Run the loop long enough across a whole population and it stops being a fact about individual posts and becomes a fact about public life.

The first distortion is to the map of opinion itself. Because the feed selects the angriest voices and shows them to everyone, a small, loud, outraged faction can come to look like a majority — manufactured social proof (item 39) feeding pluralistic ignorance (item 42). People infer the distribution of belief from what they see, and what they see is not the distribution of belief; it is the distribution of what the medium chose to amplify. The quiet middle, being quiet, is invisible, and each of its members concludes they are alone. This is affective polarisation manufactured at least in part by the instrument that measures it: we come to feel we live in a country at war with itself partly because the medium that shows us our country is optimised to show us its fights.

The second weight is who profits. The conflict is not a side effect the platform tolerates; it is the product it sells. Attention captured by outrage is attention sold to advertisers. A business model that monetises engagement has a structural interest in whatever engages, and if that is contempt, then the ledger and the public good have quietly parted ways.

The third weight is the one this series watches for, because it is a new dress on an old mechanism. The pile-on is a punishment technology. When any deviation from the group line can be met by a coordinated swarm of denunciation, the rational response for onlookers is silence — not because they agree, but because the cost of visible dissent has been raised past what most will pay. That is preference falsification (item 34) reconstituted for the platform age: an order held in place not by a state but by the credible threat of the mob, enforcing conformity through the chilling of speech. The same tool that broke real silences against real injustice can, turned the other way, manufacture a new silence — and it can be weaponised deliberately, which is where it shades into propaganda (item 82). The frontier double-edge is exact: the instrument that let the powerless be heard is the instrument that lets the swarm punish dissent, and it is the same instrument, and the platform sells both.

## The critique

The findings are real, and they are bounded, and the honesty of this chapter lives in the second clause.

Begin with the data. A great deal of this research runs on Twitter, because Twitter's data was for years the only large behavioural corpus a researcher could actually get. Twitter is not the world. Its users skew younger, more political, more educated, and more prone to public performance than the general population, and generalising from its dynamics to human social behaviour writ large is a leap the samples do not fully license. The effect sizes, too, vary across studies and specifications; the headline numbers — the twenty per cent, the sixty-seven per cent — are averages over particular datasets, not constants of nature.

The designs are mostly observational and correlational. Brady's and Rathje's core findings are associations between the language of a post and its spread. Association is not causation, and there are live confounds: the moral-emotional posts may spread more because they are *about* the most spreadable topics, or because the accounts that write that way have larger, more engaged followings to begin with. Later experimental work has tried to nail the causal arrow, and some of it supports the causal reading, but the strongest evidence in the field is still not as clean as the strong claims made in its name.

Then there is the measurement problem. "Outrage" and "moral-emotional content" are operationalised by dictionaries — lists of words a computer counts — and a word-count is a coarse proxy for a felt emotion. Two posts with identical moral-emotional word tallies can differ utterly in tone, and the instrument cannot see it.

Deepest of all is a rival explanation the field has not laid to rest. The thesis of this chapter is that platforms *make* us more outraged. The competing view is that platforms merely *reveal* an outrage that was always there — that the anger predates the app, and the feed is a mirror, not a furnace. The truth is likely some of both, and the mixture is not settled. Even the foundational moral-contagion result has drawn replication scrutiny and reanalysis, as strong and citable findings should; the effect appears robust in broad outline but softer and more conditional than its first, most quotable form suggested. What survives all of this is the mechanism's plausibility and a real, repeatedly-observed signal — not a law. The medium selects for outrage. By how much, under what conditions, and whether it manufactures the anger or merely magnifies it, are questions the evidence has opened and not yet closed.
